It seems that hardly a show can open in Toronto that isn't a tribute to some troubadour or other. In the past few weeks weve had Sarah-Louise Young celebrating Julie Andrews and Raoul Bhaneja celebrating Raoul Bhaneja. Now comes What Makes a Man, in which two male and two female performers parcel out the words and music of Charles Aznavour.

Lets start at the end. The last thing we see and hear in this Necessary Angel production is the pivotal member of the foursome, Kenny Brawner, thoughtfully reciting the opening words of Tomorrow Is My Turn," an exceptionally buoyant and hopeful Aznavour tune that was memorably recorded by Nina Simone. We wait expectantly for him to get to the meat of the song; by this point of the evening we could use the lift. But no, the intro is all we're given; from there, we go straight to the curtain-call. It's typical of a show that seems fanatically dedicated to denying its audience a good time.

The idea seemed promising enough. If Jacques Brel can have a show, then Aznavour, a somewhat more inventive composer and a far less pretentious lyricist, certainly deserves one. The overture, too, is inviting; a six-piece jazzblues band, stationed by the

side of the acting area, makes an invigorating introductory noise that may not be quite what one expects in a chansonnier context but is probably none the worse for that.

Brawner then appears, announced by the MD as if we were in a nightclub (nobody else in the cast gets that kind of build-up), sits at an onstage piano, and goes into one of Aznavours best-known songs, "Yesterday When I Was Young. It seems a reasonable beginning, contemplative and retrospective; and if your mind runs that way (mine does) you may notice how Herbert Kretzmers portentously melodramatic English lyric anticipates his later work on Les Miserables. You may, though, have to struggle to make the connection, since this is the downstairs Berkeley Street Theatre, a venue whose acoustics have muffled many a play and are plain murder for a musical, however multifarious the mikes.

What Makes a Man is a cabaret. That's fine; so were Ain't Misbehavin and Side by Side by Sondheim, and they worked in the theatre. But I doubt if they would have worked in this theatre, certainly not on a set made up of blue-draped steps and backed by the spaces brick walls, the latter being a feature of which directors seem inexplicably fond and which help mightily to devour the sound. Jennifer Tarver, Necessary Angel's incoming artistic director and this production's originator, doesnt have to direct everything as if it were written by Samuel Beckett. Actually her Beckett productions are more fun.

Brawner, funky throughout and playful when permitted, is followed on stage by Andrew

Penner, whose initial impression is that of a Louisiana busker, a one-man band making simultaneous music with hand, foot and mouth. Later on, he reduces his self-accompaniment to just a guitar. This is altogether as musician-heavy a show as could be wished, with the two actor-guys doubling up on a band that already includes two guitars, and a piano and a keyboard, together with bass and drums. Penner has the most effective number of the evening, by far, and on this he just sings, with touching restraint. Its a song about a gay man who's a transvestite stripper. It's also the title number, though neither the song nor the show answers the title question. They dont even ask it.

First to appear of the two ladies is Saidah Baba Talibah, who sings well, in a jazz-rock fashion, but doesnt manage the essential theatrical trick of turning singing into storytelling. (I was intrigued and impressed, though, to learn from her bio that she's the daughter of Salome Bey, and the niece of Andy Bey, whom she describes as arguably the finest jazz vocalist on the planet." Arguably, she's right.)

Finally, theres Louise Pitre, the only member of the cast who suggests old rather than New Orleans, and whose forte here is barely controlled rage. This works best for her in A Young Girl, about a seduced 16-year old, abandoned and dying by a roadside. This has a fine English lyric, curt and sardonically compassionate, by the great and neglected Oscar Brown Jr. Hes especially neglected by this production's song list, which declines to credit any translators apart from the show's dramaturg Andrew Shaver. For that matter it doesnt let on that not all Aznavours music is by Aznavour. Some of his tunes, including this production's "I'm Gonna Sleep With One Eye Open, are by an American ex-pat named Jeff Davis.

Pitre spends a lot of time sitting at the top of the steps with her back turned to the audience and to the other performers. This isnt because shes rude, or is trying to dissociate herself from the proceedings. It's part of the concept, and it gradually spreads through the entire cast. The show becomes a study in sullen solitudes. Maybe the most dispiriting thing about it is that the four performers, all with excellent voices, are never allowed to sing together. The closest they get is to deliver successive lines of a single song, and even that only happens once.

Indeed, the nearest we get to group action is when Brawner sings Take the Chorus" (a good Shaver lyric) and exhorts the band to do just that; each member gets to play a solo. It makes a welcome change from the other solos, lugubrious vocal ones, that make up the overwhelming bulk of the show. It's just one doom-andgloom thing after another. I remember Aznavour from television appearances as an impish presence with a melancholy streak. The version of him we're offered here is all streak and no presence: not a fair exchange.
