You have to love a show that can send up its own plot synopses. This week's episode of Glee starts with the customary brisk rundown of the story so far. Its exceptionally brisk, finishing off with a cheery and thats what you missed." That isn't quite all. We get some additional narration from the show's two major antagonists. The viperous Sue Sylvester, coach of the high-school cheerleading team known as the Cheerios, reminds us of how much she loathes Will Schuester, director of the school glee club, and of how she intends to take him down. Will, formerly mild-mannered but now beginning to fight back, realizes that "Sues turned me into her and adds, aghast, look at us, were even fighting in our voice-overs."

So the great thing about Glee is that it doesn't take itself seriously, or at least not often. It's that rare if not unprecedented thing: a serial musical comedy. The music of course comes from the glee club itself, a concept that seems to have advanced considerably from the days when club members just stood in rows and came out with barber-shop harmonies enlarged to choral proportions. These kids do numbers. They have an eclectic repertoire, from show tunes to soul, by way of Avril Lavigne and Motown. The songs comment on the action rather than flowing spontaneously out of it since, according to the shows creator, Ryan Murphy, its more realistic that way. That's an interesting view of realism. These characters may be performing because they're performers, but they have an enviable knack for going full-tilt into a routine, with full band accompaniment, without so much as allocating lines or selecting a key.

Glee reinforces the impression, fostered by all previous high-school sitcoms, that U. S. high schools are appalling institutions that place a fanatically high value on developing students self-esteem while creating a peer-pressured system guaranteed to destroy it. Anyway, Will divided his troops into two teams, male and female, for what he delightfully called "a glee-off. In the event, both sides killed. This wasnt surprising, as their high-energy performances were fuelled by decongestant pills supplied by Will's wife Terri, filling in as school nurse despite having had no medical training.

All this was part of a neat, and pretty much self-contained, storyline involving an attraction between Will and Emma, the school guidance counsellor. It was spotted by Cheerio Sue, who set out to destroy it. This wasn't strictly part of her anti-glee campaign; she just likes destroying things. She told Terri and suggested that, in order to keep a closer eye on her husband, she take the nursing job. A split-second flashback informed us that Sue had already disposed of the posts previous occupant: bodily.

Im not accusing anyone of being misogynist, but I have to say that the females on this show are all monsters. Terri (Jessalyn Gilsig) is a control-freak whose pregnancy has turned out to be hysterical, a fact she's hiding from her husband. Emma (Jayma Mays) is a germophobe who's accepted a proposal from a sad-sack football coach on condition that he keep well away from her after theyre married. Of the kids, Quinn (Dianne Agron), the cheerleaders leader, acts the spoiled prima donna both on the field and in the glee club. Yes, she's a double agent, which she confirms by being a) head of the celibacy club, and b) pregnant and not hysterically, at least not in that sense.

Jane Lynch's Sue, of course, with her scheming and her materialism and her sickly-sweet late-night spot on local TV, is off the charts for malevolence. Shes got herself appointed codirector of the glee club, and this week shes reinventing Will's strategy of divide-and-encourage as one of divide-and-destroy. Trying to cream off the best of Will's talent, she briskly calls them out as follows: gay kid, Asian, other Asian, Aretha."

For some reason, she neglects to name-check the paraplegic. You can have too much of a bad thing, and Sue can become not someone you love to hate but someone you just hate. This does allow Matthew Morrison as Will to be likeable without having to work too hard at it. Besides, the writers usually manage to rescue Sue by handing her the very poetry of anti-Will hypocrisy: "Sue Sylvesters rainbow tent will gladly protect you from his storm of racism. The kids arent impressed. The show is post-multicultural; it knows that everyone's a minority. Sure I'm a proud black woman," says Aretha-I-mean-Amber (Mercedes Jones), "but there's more to me than that. If we want to hear mom and dad fight," says one boy as Sue and Will square off at rehearsal, "we could stay home -- those of us who still have two parents. Every so often Glee gets glancingly serious, but so far it hasn't been soggy. Most of the time it is indeed gleeful. It can also, as befits a show about school, be educational. Did you know that after dentists, obstetricians have the highest rate of suicide among medical practitioners"? I heard it on TV.
