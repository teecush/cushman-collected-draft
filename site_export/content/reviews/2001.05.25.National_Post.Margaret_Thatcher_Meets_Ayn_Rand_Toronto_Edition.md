THE MILLIONAIRESS

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

The heroine, for want of a better word, of The Millionairess is named Epifania Fitzfassenden. That, at least, is her married name; her maiden name, by which she prefers to be known, is Italian and even longer. Epifania Ognisanti di Parerga. Her husband is called Alastair Fitzfassenden, her solicitor, Julius Sagamore and her platonic lover, Adrian Blenderbland. All this mirthless elephantiasis is an accurate index to the texture of the play itself, one of Bernard Shaws last. The wheels still turn, and they still give off sparks, but the machinery is getting sluggish and the glow is subdued and intermittent.

Written in 1934, the play reveals the extent to which the 80- year- old Shaw had lost touch with the nature of the world around him. No shame in that, perhaps; except that he still persisted in writing as if he not only understood the questions but had all the answers. Epifania is -- to quote her author's description of one of his earlier and more endearing heroines, Saint Joan -- "a born boss.

The 1930s were a time when born bosses such as Hitler and Mussolini were bossing whole nations. Epifania is, in her own small way, their comic surrogate: a figure through whom we are invited to consider the problem of dictators and what is to be done with them, or how their energies are to be harnessed.

That at least is what Shaw says in his preface. We may note, from the extracts printed in the Shaw Festival program, that he didn't know what he was talking about. He reduces Nazi anti-Semitism to the regrettable failure of one genius to show proper respect for another: Hitlers throwing Einstein to the anti-Semite wolves was an appalling breach of cultural faith." Einstein, of course, was exiled, not murdered; other Jews, less celebrated, were also less lucky -- and this long before the full-blown Holocaust. Shaw may be forgiven for not foreseeing what Hitlers racial policies were to lead to; he cannot be excused for not seeing what they already were, or for trying to transfer the blame to the leader's followers.

The actual play never mentions Hitler or the international situation; Epifania, living in London, operates on a strictly domestic and even romantic front. So it might seem unfair to drag in Shaw's commentary, except that this production does, not only in its program notes but in its use of lines from the preface, transformed into snappy song lyrics to be sung during the scene changes: snappier, actually, than most of the dialogue, and more directly critical of Epifania herself and the money-mad values she represents.

Otherwise, we might theoretically be content to see the play as another comedy about a strong-willed woman who eventually finds happiness with an equally strong-willed, or at least strong- natured, man. In terms of plot, that is what happens here, but there are two obstacles to our enjoying the play on that level. One is that the play is half over before the central relationship is even introduced. The other is Epifania herself.

She begins the play by marching into her lawyers office, demanding a divorce from her husband: a champion athlete, but not in other respects the man she took him for. Her late and revered father, himself the descendant of a long line of millionaires, laid a condition on her future suitors: Like a less idealistic version of Portias dad, he had demanded they transform (ps)150 into (ps)50,000 within six months. Flukily, the bone-headed Alastair pulled this off by going into theatrical production. (The details are complicated, but they sound like The Producers in reverse.)

But the disparity in their minds and manners has led to discord, and to his using his boxing prowess to counter her skills at judo. Most audience members, without endorsing wife-beating as a general practice, are likely to sympathize with Alastair.

He has also acquired a contrastingly mousy girlfriend who has the significantly plain name of Patricia Smith, but who gains honorary entrance to the play's club by being affectionately known as Polly Seedystockings. She has one of the play's better speeches, about the difference between the people anyone can live with, like herself, and the people no one can live with.

Eppy, of course, belongs to the latter group. This makes her feel sorry for herself, and she takes it out on her tame suitor, Blenderbland, beating him rather seriously up. (This sequence is done here to music, which is clever but tends to take the edge off.) A doctor is summoned, and proves to be an unworldly and serenely dedicated Egyptian. She falls instantly in love with him and agrees to a test imposed by his late mother; she will go out into the world, penniless, and survive. So she does, in the process taking over an East End sweatshop and transforming a rundown riverside pub into a tourist attraction hotel, all beam and gleam.

Though admitting some limitations (such as being a lousy scullery maid) Epifania is loathsomely competent and successful, and contemptuous of all who arent. Shaw is still playwright enough to make us see the human costs of her entrepreneurship, or at least to tell us about them second-hand, but basically he wants us not only to admire his protagonist but to like her.

Unfortunately, on her first entrance he has her admit that she has no sense of humour, which makes her an awkward heroine for a Shavian comedy.

Lacking good jokes, successful actresses in the role have relied on ferocious personal charm; this apparently is what Katharine Hepburn did, and its certainly what Sophia Loren, who of course was the right nationality, did in the watered-down film. It's something the usually excellent Sarah Orenstein apparently cannot do, and initially doesn't seem to be trying.

Uniting all the least lovable qualities of Margaret Thatcher and Ayn Rand, she is nothing but bossy, with spasms of self-pity. Wriggle though she may, she cannot later escape from the trap she has dug for herself. She does strike a couple of wittily absurd poses as a rich woman trying to act poor, and conscientiously phrases a long speech about the joys of marriage, which seems like a last-minute attempt to turn the play into something it has not hitherto been.

Allen MacInniss production keeps hinting at uncomfortable contemporary parallels and then shying away from them. It is strange in other respects, too, because though its look, its sense of ensemble and its overall timing are very good, much of its individual acting is pretty bad.

The minimum requirements for Shaw performance, certainly at his own festival, are strong personality and uncluttered, unapologetic vocal line; the heroines uncertainties in both these departments here are reflected and magnified by her colleagues.

Nigel Shawn Williams as the supposedly charismatic Doctor can hardly get a line out without stumbling over it. Peter Krantz, though he has the right beefy demeanour for Alastair, is painfully wooden in any speech containing more than two sentences; and David Schurmann extends the already overlong role of Blenderbland by an unconscionable amount of dithering. This performance is one he has given many times before, though often to better effect.

Peter Millard's sweetly reasonable Sagamore is also an old acquaintance, but in this case it fits perfectly; and Severn Thompson has no problem winning sympathy for the sensible Seedystockings.

William Vickers and Donna Belleville are a welcome breath of stale air as the sweat-shop proprietors; you don't go to Shaw for working-class realism, but their brief scene at least acknowledges and explores how people live and react.

William Schmuck's sets, each first glimpsed through a sort of expanding spy hole, are a treat. Overall, though, the production tries hard to save the play from itself and fails.
