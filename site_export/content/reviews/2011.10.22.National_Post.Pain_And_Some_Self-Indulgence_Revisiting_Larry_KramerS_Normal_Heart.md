For eight years now, Joel Greenberg's Theatre 180 has been bringing us plays on urgent public subjects. They have divided into the documentary or quasi-documentary (The Laramie Project, Stuff Happens, Our Class) and the more conventionally fictionalized. It's telling that the former category has excelled the latter, not only in polemical impact but in human complexity. The Normal Heart, the most famous of all plays about AIDS, belongs in the second group and is very much a case in point.

There's a sense in which Larry Kramer's play may be said to straddle the line between the two camps. It uses real-life characters, but gives them made-up names; the originals are all individually identified in the author's program note for this revival, and one of them is Kramer himself, here called Ned Weeks. Ned is a gay journalist, living and working in New York in the early 1980s when AIDS first hits. He's also one of the first people - the play sometimes makes it seem that he's the only one - to realize that it's a virus that is being sexually transmitted. This, and his passionate campaigning about it, earns him hatred on two fronts. He makes himself unpopular with the straight world, especially the straight political world, which would rather ignore the issue altogether. More woundingly, and something that occupies most of the play's attention, he antagonizes other gays, who feel that they have only just achieved sexual freedom, and who equate liberation with promiscuity. At least some of them do; others are still nervous about coming out. Doing battle on all these fronts, Ned emerges as a beleaguered crusader. He is in fact the hero of his own play, and that's a problem.

The Normal Heart took New York by storm when it was first produced, a theatrical equivalent of Kramer's own off-stage work as an activist. Some of that pain and passion still registers. But it also seems self-indulgent, probably more so than it would if it were more frankly autobiographical and less tied to Broadway tradition. The opening scenes, trying to present the first facts on the ground through the medium of normal dramatic exposition, are full of cardboard characters speaking cardboard lines and receiving, in Greenberg's production, mainly cardboard performances. Both play and production get better as they go on, partly because the story becomes inescapably grimmer and more harrowing, partly because Kramer, like an American John Osborne, seems to have a greater talent for monologue than for dialogue. Most of these tirades go, obviously, to Ned, but others get a turn, too, and the actors rise to the opportunities.

Jonathan Wilson gives an unflagging and compelling performance as Ned himself, scrappy in the best pugnacious sense; the fact that he's physically slight adds if anything to the effect; he's a bantam up against a battalion of Goliaths. The writing, though, is scrappy by a less complimentary definition: elementary and diffuse. The author tries conscientiously to complicate things by having his surrogate admit to being tactless and confrontational, but these are gestures; in essence, Ned is always right. He's also very repetitious; I lost count of the number of times he upbraids a cautious colleague for not living up to his daredevil past as a Green Beret. Certainly we feel for Ned when his intransigence leads to his being effectively ousted from the movement he did more than anyone to create. That's good personal and political theatre. Less so are the attempts to provide Ned, as a good protagonist, with relationships. He has a lover, who dies: horrifyingly and movingly, though no more so than would be the case if he and Ned had no personal involvement. Ned also has a brother, a lawyer, who seems to be standing in for that section of straight society that's sympathetic but uncomprehending (it seems almost too good that the actor who plays him is named John Bourgeois); the production's last image suggests that the fraternal relationship is the central one in the play, though there's very little to justify it.

Brother and lover share a scene, with Ned not present; and it's almost the best in the play. He's also absent from the very best, which is played from opposite corners of a quadrilateral stage, and in which the despairing polio-stricken doctor who first suspected the nature of AIDS and is trying to treat it with pitiful resources, gets a generally hostile response from a medical executive. The latter is played by Mark McGrinder, who has earlier appeared as a similarly unhelpful man from the mayor's office; I thought for a while that they were the same character, and in essence they probably are. Sarah Orenstein is the doctor and the scene affords her one of the play's great outbursts, which she delivers with ferocious intensity, spreading the stage floor with detritus, which, in the evening's chief image, accumulates thereafter. Quieter, but at least as lacerating, is the breakdown speech of Ryan Kelly as an activist cracking under professional pressure and both personal and institutional cruelty. Paul Essiembre traces, with some subtlety, the evolution Will's principal collaborator into his prime antagonist, and Jonathan Seinen brings light and shade, in that order, to a male Southern belle who emerges as a responsible peacemaker.

Caught up in its own moment, the play now seems to leave a lot out. There's the barest mention of infections in Africa or anywhere else outside America or even outside New York; or of heterosexual cases; or of transmission through drug use; or of the helpfulness of condoms. Most of these are covered, admittedly with the benefit of 30 years' hindsight, in Kramer's program note which, in its angry accounting of how little has actually changed, is more challenging and more powerful than his play.
