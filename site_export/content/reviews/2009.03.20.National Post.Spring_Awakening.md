The play Spring Awakening includes a scene at the funeral of Moritz Stiefel, a 19th-century German schoolboy driven to suicide by the demands of a merciless academic curriculum. His disappointed dad proclaims to the world that the boy "was no son of mine," though he's cheered up by a sympathetic teacher who tells him that Moritz's marks probably wouldn't have been good enough to get him into the next grade anyway.

The musical Spring Awakening finds all this heartlessness a bit much to take, and substitutes a father who sobs silently while Melchior Gabor, best friend of the deceased and the show's true hero, sings one of the evening's frequent laments.

Frank Wedekind, the lifelong enfant terrible who wrote the original play in 1891, may have gone a bit far in this scene; even Teutonic parents disappointed by their children's exam results probably weren't that Hunnish. The adult characters in this, his first play, are mainly caricatures. But they're clean-lined efficient caricatures of a powerful social structure that it took some courage to attack.

The musical, more than a hundred years on, is aiming at safe targets, which it's as likely to exaggerate as to soften. The teachers here are not just blinkered and dogmatic, but actively vindictive. A girl in the play tells her friends that her father beats her; in the musical he also subjects her to full-scale sexual abuse, with a song attached. Maybe the authors think that Wedekind meant this but didn't dare say it, though given his explicitness on the subjects of masturbation, teenage homosexuality, pregnancy and abortion, that seems unlikely. Anyway the show appears torn between sensationalism and sentimentality. It's a very confused piece.

Some of the confusion lies in the rock score, which is as anachronistic in its words as its music. Some of the time, the kids in the story seem to be singing about their own desires and frustrations (as in West Side Story, only young people are allowed to sing; unlike in West Side Story, no other topics are allowed); other times, it feels as if the performers are grabbing their mics to offer their own gloss on how they would have reacted in comparable circumstances. The prevailing tone is, "dudes, you wouldn't believe this repression," and no, we don't, not when the lyrics are going on about cellphones and stereos. The show is taking a ride on past pain, and making a factitious parallel between youthful agonies then and now. Yes, adolescence is always a time for feeling misunderstood, but contemporary middle-class kids aren't being victimized like those in the play.

Young Melchior, a freethinking prodigy who's the idol of all the girls and probably great at games as well, blunders into sex with innocent Wendla Bergmann and gets her pregnant. Wendla's mother, whose blushes are here played for cheap laughs, has been too shy to tell her daughter the biological facts. But, on the show's own showing, if she hadn't got them from her mother, she'd have got them from her girlfriends, two of whom had already learned the hardest way. The show, mindful of what becomes a musical, can't resist going all lyrical over the union of Melchior and Wendla, though it's a fumbling, violent thing in the original (and here, until the music starts). The tag line in the ads is "you never forget your first time." Given that the first time in this case leads to death from a botched abortion, the sentiment seems dubious. It's the kind of slogan Max Bialystock might have dreamed up if he'd opted for Spring Awakening instead of Springtime for Hitler.

The cast is loyalty personified. Matt Doyle renders Melchior as a strong full-throated all-American boy, which seems to be what's required; Blake Bashoff is sad-funny as Moritz, who finds his sexual urges even harder to cope with than Latin and math, and also labours under a hair-style apparently modelled on Shockheaded Peter; Christy Altomare is a sweet Wendla, though one who looks better able to take care of herself than her story requires. As for the score:Duncan Sheik's music peaks very early with the poignant opener Mama Who Bore Me, though as a song for the girls when the show is more about the boys, it sends false signals. There are a couple more nice ballads and a lot of musical rough-housing, some of it effective.

Steven Sater's lyrics (he also wrote the book, which is best when closest to the play) at one point find a neat rhyme for "self-destruct" -- sorry about this being a family newspaper -- but are otherwise sloppy on craft and low on content, either narrative or psychological. The songs are interchangeable between characters: Listen to the CD if you don't believe me, and try and work out who's singing.
