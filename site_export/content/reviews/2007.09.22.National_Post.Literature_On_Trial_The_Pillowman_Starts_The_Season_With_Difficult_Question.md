Once upon a time, there was a natural-born playwright whose name was Martin McDonagh. He was Anglo-Irish, and he had an older brother who went off to America to be a screenwriter. Maybe Martin was jealous or something; but anyway, he holed himself up at home for nine months and wrote the drafts of seven plays. The first of them was called The Beauty Queen of Leenane, and when it was staged it knocked its audiences sideways because here, for the first time in generations, was a piece that had them on the edge of their seats, panting to know what would happen next. The author seemed to have dramatic construction in his bones, along with a gift for characters who lived, dialogue that was both taut and flavoursome, humour that was mostly black and action that was largely violent. His other plays turned out to have the same qualities. And the reason this story is told in the past tense is that, at the age of 37, McDonagh has announced that he has quit the theatre for novels and movies, which is frustrating but also sort of fitting. The tale is told.

The Pillowman, which gets the Toronto season off to a blazing start, stands apart from the rest of McDonaghs work in that it isnt set specifically in Ireland and might well be a meditation on his own talent. It's about storytelling and its consequences. Its central character is a writer, largely unpublished, named Katurian Katurian; his parents, he says, had a warped sense of humour. (This turns out to be an understatement.) Katurian claims to despise autobiographical fiction, but he admits that one of his own stories might be taken that way; it's called The Storyteller and the Storytellers Brother. This storytellers own brother is named Michal and is mentally challenged. He loves to hear Katurian's stories; one of his favourites is called The Pillowman, and it's about a fluffy puffed-up figure whose mission is to talk children into committing suicide so as to save them the trouble of doing it later in life when theyre really unhappy. Katurians work is full of abused and murdered children. He now has reason to believe that a series of horrific child-killings have been committed by his brother, acting out the stories Katurian told him.

The brothers have been hauled in for questioning by two policemen, officials of what they themselves cheerfully describe as a totalitarian state." They are certainly freer with the beatings and the electrodes and the threats of summary execution than cops in democratic countries are supposed to be, but any political comment is merely a veneer, a method of pushing the play's themes to extremes. You don't have to be a fascist to deplore child abuse, or even to want to punish it, but Katurians plight is intensified by his living in a society that can routinely censor books and destroy manuscripts. This for him could be a fate worse than death. And he has a point: People die, art has at least a chance of living on. The question -- one that neither liberals nor conservatives have ever conclusively settled -- is whether it should, if it has a demonstrable chance of inspiring harm. That the person committing the harm may, like Michal, not be responsible for his own actions doesnt let literature off the hook: rather the reverse.

These questions are brilliantly dramatized, as opposed to merely discussed, in a scene between the brothers that David Ferry has directed magnificently. Shaun Smyth, who was excellent last year as the tale-teller in Of Mice and Men, is downright superb as this one. He not only brings out Katurian's fear, vanity, protectiveness and flashes of obstinate heroism, but shows how each bleeds into the others. Paul Fauteux's Michal, in whom self-centredness reaches the pitch of virtuous indignation, is his distorted mirror-image.

The two interrogators -- a self-proclaimed good-cop/bad-cop duo - - have their own issues with the issues, both personal and literary They, unlike the brothers, are played American. Oliver Becker, the foot soldier, sounds like Tony Soprano, and, like Tony, the more layers he reveals, the better he gets. Richard Mc-Millan, the guy- in-charge, is more like something out of Guys and Dolls. He does good things, especially in the matter of comic timing, but he undersells the characters intelligence, sophistication and -- above all -- weariness, so a vital thread in the plays texture goes missing. But a huge amount remains, in wit, tension and sheer presence.

The title turns out to have all kinds of resonance, especially for Katurian himself. There's also self-questioning. In The Beauty Queen the audience practically cheered the brutality on; here it appals, wherever it comes from. Which doesn't mean that it doesnt compel. Reconcile that, says the play. Or, thats show business. And they all, or some of them, lived unhappily, ever after.
