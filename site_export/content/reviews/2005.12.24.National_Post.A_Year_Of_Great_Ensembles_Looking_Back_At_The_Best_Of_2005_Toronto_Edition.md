This has been the year of great ensemble productions in and around Toronto. The five most exciting events of the year were A Whistle in the Dark, Half-Life, The Wild Duck, Orpheus Descending and The Last Days of Judas Iscariot. Each of them offered the high theatrical pleasure of a sizeable group of fine actors, all interlocking in a flawlessly orchestrated account a major play. Though I doubt that any of the plays would qualify as tragedies in a classical sense, they all dispensed generous quantities of pity and terror, shot through with laughter.

A Whistle in the Dark was the Canadian premiere of an alarmingly powerful 40-year-old play by the Irish dramatist Thomas Murphy. It was the first offering (there has yet to be a second) of a group called Company Theatre, who imported another Irishman, Jason Byrne, to direct it. He orchestrated a terrifying study of family violence, fuelled by social, sexual and political grudge, building to the extraordinary image of an old man striving to deny the horror that he himself had unleashed.

John Mighton's Half-Life, the year's best new Canadian play -- indeed the only item on the list that wasn't a revival or an import - - was as seemingly gentle as Whistle was aggressive, a study of the middle-aged watching their elders slipping away or, even more disturbingly, refusing to. It was the best offering of the year at Tarragon, though it must strictly count as a visiting production, mounted by Necessary Angel under their new artistic director Daniel Brooks, whose record there, so far, is impeccable.

The Wild Duck showed Soulpepper at its finest. Laszlo Marton stripped all didacticism from Ibsen's play (possibly his masterpiece) to show it as a searching tragi-comic study of self- delusion, which we already knew, and psychological cruelty to children, which we didn't, at least not with this degree of intensity. Some scenes were almost unbearable to watch, and not for sensationalist reasons.

Orpheus Descending had Miles Potter directing an absurdly strong Stratford cast (in the small Tom Patterson space, which made it seem even richer) in a Tennessee Wiliams play whose excesses, initially enjoyable as Southern Gothic, went on to make transfixing sense. It shared with Whistle and The Wild Duck the ability to keep spectators on the edge of their seats, both dreading and willing an inexorable climax.

The Last Days of Judas Iscariot, directed by David Ferry, was a political, religious and anachronistic extravaganza by an off-beat American playwright, Stephen Addy Guirgis, working with a wit and audacity well beyond the refined canons of Broadway or, indeed, off- Broadway. Loose-jointed and confessedly imperfect, mounted in an improvised space in the Distillery for a few performances, it was probably the freest and most exhilarating show of the year for actors and audiences alike (and they were sometimes hard to tell apart).

Part of the fun in this town is in seeing a loose-knit group of exceptional actors forming and re-forming; even those identified with the major companies get to play outside from time to time. Of those five top shows, Diego Matamoros was in three, going from a quiet, alert scientist in Half Life to a glorious, dangerous fool in The Wild Duck to a flamboyant, angry Satan in Judas; no actor can switch more electrically from a purr to a shout. Joseph Ziegler, Alon Nashman and Jonathan Goad were each in two. Nashman in Judas brought off a delightful double of Sigmund Freud and Doubting Thomas; Goad's stud in Orpheus was one of three outstanding performances he gave at Stratford, along with his beleaguered son in Whistle; and Ziegler's paterfamilias in Whistle, a cowering blowhard, was the male performance of the year. The female performance of the year was Seana McKenna's Lady in Orpheus, a superb combination of the passionate and the sensible, the tormented and the outrageously funny.

Just outside the charmed circle was Trout Stanley, outside only because, with a cast of merely three, Eda Holmes's production had to offer a narrower range of kicks. They were superb: Melody Johnson, Michelle Giroux -- two highly distinctive actresses, perfectly cast - - and the protean Gord Rand. (I borrow that description, gratefully, from one of our best playwrights.) Claudia Dey's play was the second best of the year, another stage in the development of a writer who was always wonderful with words and is increasingly good with stories and characters. She confirmed this in miniature with a monologue in the one-night Wrecking Ball, the last show of the year as Trout Stanley was the first. Otherwise, the best new Canadian writing was in Essay, a short piece by a new writer, Hannah Moscovitch, seen at Summerworks and surely to be seen again. After that there's a gulf, which is not too alarming in retrospect (how many good plays do you expect to see in a year?), but can feel so at the time.

There were other golden moments, though. William Hutt, making a graceful, but unsentimental, farewell by finding new and fiercer things to do with Prospero in The Tempest; Albert Schultz, who is some decades off having the same aura, but who brought a comparable skill and freshness to Hamlet; John Fitzgerald Jay giving a stunning wild-eyed performance in Enoch Arden; Mike Shara and David Storch, doing scary and delightful things in Take Me Out, which was also the best of Morris Panych's abundance of productions this year. Factory Theatre had a good year, Buddies in Bad Times had maybe their best times ever; and Theatre Passe Muraille bestirred itself with its Stage 3 season, which brought forward promising voices in Joseph Jomo Pierre and in Nicole Stamp, who was irresistible.

The best visiting show was Billy Crystal's. It was a terrible year for musicals unless they were festival revivals (the Shaw's Gypsy with Nora McLellan coming into her own, Stratford's Into the Woods). But at the big spots in town, Evita was tired, Wicked wicked (and not in any new-fangled positive sense) and Les Miserables miserable. There was far more fun and melody in Jonathan Larson's TickTick Boom at the sadly departed Poor Alex. Actually, the best musical I saw, by far, was the televised version of Jerry Springer: The Opera, which is the boldest and most inventive departure in the genre in years. There seems little chance in the Toronto that rejected Hairspray, Urinetown and even The Producers while embracing Wicked, but can we see it on stage?
