Site-specific theatre comes in two flavours: stationary and Queen In the first kind, the audience is ushered into a space that bears some similarity to the setting of the play -- dingy hotel rooms are a favourite -- and simply sits there watching the play unfold at unusually close quarters. In the second, which these days seems to be the more prevalent, the site in question is a whole building, in which the actors move from room to room with the spectators in dutiful pursuit. The latest of these magical mystery tours is The Turn of the Screw, an adaptation of the Henry James ghost-novella, currently haunting the stately Campbell House at the corner of University. and much

It isn't as logistically complex as, say, Tamara, the forms primary exhibit from some 30 years ago, which derived House of its effect from having different scenes unfolding simultaneously in different rooms. We had to admire the organizers cleverness in keeping their divergent narrative lines from bumping into one another on the stairs; as we did again last year in The Gladstone Variations. The Turn of the Screw is a single tale with only two performers, one of them playing several roles, and everything depends on their skill, and that of the production team, in ratcheting up the tension in a series of confined spaces. They win some, they lose some.

The story is the famous one of an unnamed young governess, late Victorian period, on her first assignment. Her employer, who is both personally and geographically remote, sends her to a house outside of London to look after his orphaned niece and nephew, both of whom are said to look angelic. Flora, the girl, is already in residence, and is shortly joined by her elder brother Miles, expelled from boarding school for behaviour apparently too unspeakable to be described. From Mrs. Grose, the obligingly informative housekeeper, the governess learns of her predecessor, a Miss Jessel, and the groom, the satanic Peter Quint, both now dead but formerly lovers, the details of their liaison also too horrible to be discussed. Miles and Flora, it seems, were their go-betweens, and even in death the guilty and now ghostly couple won't leave them be. The governess, herself the child of a stark and oppressive home with romantic notions derived from reading Jane Eyre, does passionate, even hysterical, battle for the children's bodies and their souls.

Jeffrey Hatchers dramatization calls for a neutral set with minimal furnishings. In accordance with the First Fashionable Law of Creative Direction, which decrees that a play be made either more or less cluttered than its author intended, Vikki Andersons production (for the DVxT company) gives it the kind of naturalistic trappings only available when a room is played by an actual room. Or in this case, by a succession of rooms. Some of this works handsomely. The entrance hall of Campbell me has a sweeping, winding staircase on which actors can cower in terror or cling for comfort to maximum visual effect. The various bedrooms and sitting rooms do their atmospheric bits as well, and John Gzowski has contrived an alarmingly spooky sound-score. But it all gets dissipated whenever a scene ends and we all troop out, transformed from voyeurs to tourists. The fact that we are made very aware of our fellow audience members doesn't hurt much. It isn't as if we can tune them out even in conventional theatres, and in fact their presence is less intrusive in a peripatetic production like this one than in a one-room-only show, where an implicit claim to absolute realism is negated by the presence of a bunch of people wondering whether they should be pretending not to be there. What does get disturbed here, especially in the later scenes, is the rhythm.

The mores the pity, because by this stage the actors are doing an excellent job of terrifying us and one another. Clinton Walker narrates, or at least tops-and-tails, and also plays the absentee uncle, overdoing the vague menace; but his most prominent roles are the housekeeper and the boy. For the former, he intermittently dons an apron (his only costume change) while sliding between prim caricature and genuine feeling; as Miles, hes truly uncanny, sullen and winning and terrorized, a credible boy in the grip of the unthinkable. (Nobody plays Flora who, traumatized into silence, is here invisible as well.) Christine Horne, who earlier this year was a numbingly monotonous Miss Julie, is transformed as the governess; facially and vocally, she transmits rapid shifts of feeling that bear scrutiny in the closest of close-ups. Her delicacy is matched by the force with which she faces disaster and the speed with which she hurtles towards it. The arcane precision of James's language always drives me up the wall, and neither actor can convince location, that this is how anybody, except perhaps James himself, ever actually talked, but they do manage to turn the words, bloodless as they are, into weapons. Horne's performance, for all its nervous energy, gives little support to the idea that the phantoms are merely the projections of the heroines own suppressed passions; shes too sure of herself for that, and Miles' torments are too real. But it's something to think about afterwards.
