Ahearty welcome to Another Africa, which actually is the same Africa we saw last year at Luminato, only shorter. Shorter by a third, since what was previously The Africa Trilogy is now just a double bill, having shed what was generally agreed to be its weakest component.

The strongest, entitled Peggy Pickit Sees the Face of God, remains the strongest, though it may suffer a bit from having only one play rather than two to be better than. Its an unusually compelling piece, given a superbly stringent production. The author, Roland Schimmelpfennig, uses competing and overlapping perspectives in a manner that is, as far as I know, unique. We have two married couples, Carol and Martin, Frank and Liz, all of them past or present medical professionals. Carol and Martin have returned from a few years working in an unnamed African village, and are obviously burnt out. Frank and Liz have stayed home (this location also unspecified though it could easily be Toronto), and they are in a state of burnout, too, though one thats slightly less obvious.

Frank and Liz have invited their old friends over for a reunion dinner. First thing we hear is Frank's confession that the whole evening was a disaster, which makes the rest of the play a flashback. The next thing we see is the two women slapping one another's faces: a flash-forward within the flashback. We watch the main action knowing the slaps are going to happen and waiting to find out exactly when and why, which is suspenseful enough in itself. Only there isnt a why, exactly. The timeslips keep happening. To put it another way: The plays tape keeps running forward, but every so often it stops, freezes frame for a moment, rewinds, and then plays again. During the freezes we hear, briefly, the thoughts or reactions of one or other of the characters. When the action resumes and repeats we, with more information at our disposal, view it differently. This isn't pretentious, and it isn't confusing.

It is like Whos Afraid of Virginia Woolf?, only compressed and turned inside out, since in this case the couples meet as equals. Offstage children bulk large in the conversation, but in this play theyre real. Frank and Liz have a daughter, Carol and Martin virtually adopted a village girl but have left her behind, probably to die. Liz is aghast, since the two girls had been pen pals; what, she asks in what may be the most highly charged line in the play, is she going to tell her daughter? Standing in for the two children onstage are a pair of dolls, their presents to one another. The European doll is the matchstick-thin Peggy Pickit; the African doll has a camera hidden inside it, whose viewpoint is conveyed to the audience via projections. Hence the title, which does seem pretentious, or at least unearned.

Second time around, in fact, the play is more impressive for its technical virtuosity (by which I don't mean the projections) than for anything it tells us about Africa, or even about white reactions to Africa. It tells us more, quite intentionally, about liberal guilt, a subject on which it's trenchant if not exactly revelatory, and which it also of course exemplifies. Frank and Liz by their own account (or at least by Lizs account) would have liked to go to Africa too, but of course family responsibilities kept them home. Carol and Martin (especially Carol) rebut their friends more facile assumptions about a situation of which they know nothing, but they are obviously carrying their own wounds; the possibility that one or both of them may have contracted AIDS looms larger on a second viewing than it did on a first. Both marriages are festering (as what marriages in plays are not), and it's exciting to watch and hear the scabs come off: The German author writes sharp, resonant, sometimes witty English dialogue.

Under Leisl Tommy's direction, which is both pitch-and pace-perfect, the two women give especially fine performances. Kristen Thomsons Liz moves from a precarious comfort zone to total meltdown; Maev Beatys Carol is on crisis alert from the start, but with a top dressing of poise; and both of them can explode, or implode, at an instant's notice and with frightening conviction. The men are less well served; Tony Nappo overdoes Frank's insensitivity, which is already exaggerated as written, though it does give rise to Lizs memorable accusation that hes only present physically - if that. He has the play's last line, which seems not so much a clincher as thunderingly obvious. But there's a lot to enjoy before we get there.

Embedded in Peggy Pickit is a fable, delivered shyly but craftily by Tom Barnett as Martin, about paternalism. Theoretically detachable, it centred the whole trilogy, when it was a trilogy. Now, it seems out on a limb. The preceding play, Shine Your Eye by the Kenyan Binyavanga Wainaina, does tell us more about Africa, if only because its set there, though its a lesser piece in every other way. We're in Lagos, at the epicentre of one of those email scam operations. Here works Gbene (winningly played by Dienye Waboso), a high-flying young geek whose downtime is spent in Skyped correspondence with a Toronto-based lesbian who'd like her to pay a visit; this, even more than Peggy Pickit, is a highly techno-savvy production. One reason everybody wants a piece of Gbene is that she's the daughter of a martyred freedom fighter. The converging pressures eventually lead her to what appears to be a kind of virtual self-immolation; I say "appears" because the process is, surprisingly, harder to follow this time than last. Lucky Onyekachi Ejim is, literally, a towering presence as Gbene's boss, who sees his operation as payback for colonial exploitation, and Muoi Nene is his cheery subordinate, who celebrates graduating to a higher level of scamming by performing a hip-hop number - lively in itself but contributing to the overall impression that this is less a play than a series of devices with a couple of good points to make.

The North American-based play is directed by a South African, and the African one by a Canadian, Ross Manson, whose Volcano company originated the entire project, making an elegant symmetry, ? Another Africa runs through Oct. 22 at the Bluma Appel Theatre
