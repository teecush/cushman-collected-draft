Some people think Cat on a Hot Tin Roof is Tennessee Williams' best play. As of this Shaw Festival revival, I'm one of them.

The play, as has often been noted, is eccentrically constructed. Its first act is basically a duet between Maggie, the title character, and her husband Brick. Or maybe a solo with brief interruptions, since Maggie, who earns her scalded feline status by being passionately frustrated, does most of the talking. She wants a child, preferably male, and Brick, a star athlete whose career ended when he hit the bottle, won't even sleep with her. Her motives are at least as much economic as sexual, since Brick's Big Daddy is, though he doesn't yet know it, dying of cancer, and is far likelier to leave his property - the biggest cotton plantation in the Mississippi Delta - to his unloved but philoprogenitive elder son Gooper than to the favoured but unproductive Brick.

Act Two is another duet: Brick and Big Daddy, a force of nature who, high on his mistaken belief that all that ails him is a li'l ol' spastic colon, doesn't care what he says or who he says it to. His immediate mission is to discover the nature of Brick's relationship with his sporting buddy Skipper, who committed suicide after a failed attempt to prove his manhood by going to bed with Maggie. Brick famously declares his disgust with the "mendacity" he sees all around him, to which Big Daddy replies that there's nothing he himself needs to be taught about that. The scene is a tremendous - and tremendously acted - display of punching and counter-punching, ducking and weaving, that climaxes with father and son shouting goaded unwelcome truths at one another. And Brick, though it doesn't give him much joy, may be said to win this round since what he reveals is scientific and incontrovertible while what he hears is psychological and deniable.

Act Three is a variation on that old staple, the reading of the will, with the twist that the deceased is still around. It is, for a change, an extended ensemble, though it centres on Big Daddy's Big Mama (not his mother, of course, but his wife) whose husband has said repeatedly that he can't stand her but who is still devoted to him and is in denial over his impending demise. At least it centres on her in the play as Williams first wrote it. Elia Kazan, his director, persuaded him to rewrite it; mainly, it seems, so as to include an encore scene for Big Daddy. The urge is understandable, and the revision has become standard; but I still wish Eda Holmes, the Shaw's director, had gone with the original Tennessee waltz.

I have no other reservations about her production, which is a continuous and exhilarating blast. Moya O'Connell's Maggie, sultry and driven, whips the first movement along; so, though as taciturn as she is voluble, does Gray Powell as the psychically and physically wounded Brick. His physical handicap stems from a hurdling accident sustained the previous evening while attempting, he says, to get himself back in shape. He's on crutches, and as he swings himself about on them, usually in the direction of the liquor cabinet, he just about defines the phrase "passive aggression." My feelings at the first intermission were, roughly, "This is magnificent. And Jim Mezon hasn't even come on yet."

When he does, he delivers on a scale that probably no other actor in Canada could match. His Big Daddy is, literally and metaphorically, a towering performance, a bald-headed Rabelaisian giant whose vocal and emotional power seems limitless. He's also delicate. A bullying misogynist he may be, but Big Daddy is the most sympathetic and even admirable person in the play: the creation of a playwright who seems to have consciously set out to write a great character and, damn it all, succeeded. He's been, in his own mind, to death and back; he's also, as he says, bummed all over the country, and the combination has taught him tolerance. He also wants desperately to reach his son who, in turn, is torn between wanting to reciprocate and to remain unreachable. As the father reaches out, the son lashes out; Powell's task, given less colourful ammunition, may well be the harder of the two and his achievement is equally fine.

His anger and bitterness can be elegantly ironic, and they continue to percolate through the final act, which is also where Corrine Koslo's Big Mama, terrified and obstinate, comes memorably into her own, and where O'Connell reasserts herself. Putting this central quartet into action, and building them a world, are the play's great achievements. It also finds a moment of sympathy for Patrick McManus's Gooper, understandably resentful of the love lavished on his football-hero brother and determined ("I'm not a corporate lawyer for nothing") to get even.

His wife Mae, greed incarnate, isn't allowed a case, but Nicole Underhay's unflagging performance lets us know she thinks she has one. Their brood of no- neck monsters is here reduced to two, which is a shame, but their birthday serenading of Big Daddy is authentically horrible. "No-neck monsters" is of course their unloving auntie Maggie's phrase for them; vivid though it is, one wishes she would vary it occasionally.

Holmes' actors have the authority and flexibility to use the Southern accent rather than, as sometimes happens, letting it use them. Williams' language here could never be described as stripped for action, but in this play it's brilliantly armoured for it.
