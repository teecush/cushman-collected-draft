THE SUIT

Wintergarden Theatre

The name Peter Brook sets off literally great expectations. The shows for which he is most famous have large themes, or at least a lot of actors: King Lear, A Midsummer Night's Dream, The Marat-Sade and the Indian epic The Mahabharata.

The Suit, the piece he has brought to the World Stage from his Parisian headquarters, the Theatre Bouffes-du-Nord, is by contrast a domestic drama with only four performers, though between them they play about a dozen roles.

It isnt, in fact, as great a departure as all that. Ever since he set up shop in Paris 30 years ago with an international group of actors, Brooks work has emphasized the charming and the intimate; it has been a blend - sometimes disarming, sometimes disturbing - of outward informality and inward discipline. That isn't a unique combination; it characterizes much good theatre. I don't know if The Suit could have been directed this effectively by anyone else; chances are it could. But as it stands its a beautiful piece of work.

Like much of his Parisian oeuvre, it comes out of Africa, in this case directly. Its based on a story by Can Themba, a black writer from Sophiatown. Sophiatown, a suburb of Johannesburg, was apparently a cultural (and multicultural) oasis in the desert of apartheid.

When the authorities destroyed it, they effectively destroyed Themba too; he died in exile, an alcoholic. (Sophiatown was replaced by Soweto, which says volumes.)

The Suit was adapted to the stage by Barney Simon, a pioneer South African director who founded the famous Market Theatre in Johannesburg, in collaboration with a playwright and journalist, Muthobi Mutloatse. (Both men, like Temba, are now dead.)

Simon's production was a success at home and abroad (it was much- praised when it visited London) and Brook has directed its text in a French translation by Marie Helene Estienne, which, to complete the circle, comes to Toronto equipped with English surtitles.

It's a story of jealousy. Philemon and Matilda have an apparently happy marriage, full of mutual solicitude. One day Philemon is tipped off by an older co-worker that Matilda is having an affair. He rushes home and more or less catches her in the act; the lover runs away but, not having the leisure to get fully dressed, he leaves his suit behind. Philemon takes what the Elizabethans (those specialists in jealousy) would have called a quaint revenge.

He insists that the suit become part of the family.

It takes meals with them, draped carefully over a chair; they take it for walks. Matilda, at first abashed, begins to find her own consolations in the game; when Philemon is out she flirts with the suit; arranges it languorously over her shoulder, strokes it, finally and dreamily dances with it. Its a lovely theatrical image: perhaps the fundamental reason that both Simon and Brook were drawn to the work.

Its matched by the modest but merciless physicality of the play's ending, when Philemon takes his strategy one fatal step too far; if one thinks of Othello (one of the many Shakespeare plays that Brook has never got round to directing) it's not because the jealous husband is black -- so are all the other characters -- but because he is faced at the end with a loss as bleakly irrevocable as Othellos of Desdemona. Its symbolized, like Othello's, by the sight of a woman on a bed.

Then there is the music. Sophiatown was known, among other things, for poetry and jazz, and jazz -- plaintive trumpets and joyful vocals -- fills the air here.

Matilda was once a singer, and dreams of being so again. (Or perhaps -- since she protests so much and so self-consciously about "show business" -- her career was another fantasy; certainly her affair seems like a compensation.)

There is no direct political comment, and very little that's indirect: Philemons pretext for returning home is that hes forgotten his pass (the virtual symbol of oppression) and one of his friends dreams of being able to buy liquor without restrictions. But it is possible to see the marriage and its destruction as an image of Sophiatown itself: a precarious strip of happiness in a rough world.

So a simple story has theatrical levels; and, as in all Brook's best work, the actors flesh it out to the limits. Princess Erika's supple and delicate performance as Matilda is matched by Bakary Sangares open-faced Philemon whose defining quality is his humour; it starts as good humour -- hes a playful, solicitous husband, and modulates into bad; his life becomes the playing of a practical joke, good intentions corroding within him.

Mamadou Fomba and Cyril Guei play narrators, friends and neighbours -- including, memorably, a couple of committee ladies whom Matilda encounters when she turns to social work as therapy. They also give lightning sketches of a rapid succession of guests when Philemon, in the last stages of screw-turning, throws a party.

This isn't one of Brook's extravagantly cross-cultural productions but it has the grace and lightness from which, over the years, he and his players have forged an international style. It breathes trust.
