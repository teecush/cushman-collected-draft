CHEKHOVS SHORTS

Factory Studio Theatre, Toronto

Four actors in rhythmic succession bob up before us on a sparsely lit stage, each of them wearing a dark overcoat and carrying a suitcase. They look very Russian and -- how can I put this? -- very mime.

They are Theatre Smith-Gilmour, a clown troupe consisting on this occasion of founder-directors Dean Gilmour and Michele Smith, plus guest performers Patricia Marceau and Liisa Repo-Martell. The last- named was last seen being wonderful in the Soulpepper production of Platonov, and here she is Chekhovian again. Chekhovs Shorts is not, as you might hope, a saucy comic anecdote about the great Russian author and his underwear. It's a dramatization of four of his short stories, presented within the framework of a fifth, depicting a long journey on a crowded train. The overall tone is one of foreboding farce.

Creating modes of transport without recourse to scenery seems to be one of the basic mimetic skills, but this foursome are exceptionally good at it. Using their bags to form seats or partitions, wielding them as weapons of offence or defence, shutting one another off with them, they perfectly suggest the fellow- travellers' habitual blend of bonhomie and hostility. They can laugh mercilessly at one of their number who has boarded the wrong train but they can also settle down for a cozy game of cards.

They also narrate and enact a series of dovetailing tales, each of them concerned with fear and loneliness. One depicts a schoolmaster whose relation with his colleagues is one of mutual suspicion and derision and who loses his chance at love because of it. Another presents a sleep-deprived nurserymaid, tormented by a crying baby and ceaselessly harrassed by its unsympathetic parents.

We then have the surprisingly similar story of a stray dog who experiences both the glamour and the cruelty of the circus. Finally, there is a coffin-maker, tetchy and self-absorbed, who moonlights as violinist in a Jewish orchestra and who loses the wife he has always taken for granted. (When the doctors refuse to treat her, he puts it down to Russias two-tier medical system; If I were rich, youd bleed her.) He sinks into terminal misanthrophy, from which only his music can rescue him.

All these vignettes are acted out with great quick-change resourcefulness, with the jolting rhythm of the railway always going on behind them; the performers generally look better than they sound but they are all unflappably secure. The mixture of gentleness and ruthlessness is very true to Chekhov, though I'm not sure that the phantasmagoric quality, endemic to this kind of show, really suits him. His poetry was realist, not expressionist.

When it comes to source-material, Theatre Smith-Gilmour doesn't kid around; they have an ongoing Dante project (Hell down, Purgatory to come) and they have done the Bible. They have also had one previous go at their present author, in what sounds like a heavily stylized version of The Cherry Orchard; Chekov admittedly said that his full-length plays were "almost farces" but it's probably imprudent to believe him.

He did, though, write a clutch of one-act farces -- he called them vaudevilles -- which, on the face of it, would suit Theatre S- G perfectly. But maybe a choice that straightforward would cramp their style.
