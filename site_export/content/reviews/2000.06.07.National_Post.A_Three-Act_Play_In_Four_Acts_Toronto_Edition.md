THE IMPORTANCE OF BEING EARNEST

Avon Theatre, Stratford

"I trust," says Lady Brancaster as the final revelations approach, that nothing surprising is about to happen: a forlorn hope, in the circumstances. You don't know Lady Brancaster, at least not by that name. You may well know her, though, under a different title: as Lady Bracknell, the gorgon of the handbag and the cucumber sandwiches, who traditionally dominates The Importance of Being Earnest.

Oscar Wilde wrote his last comedy in the four-act format he had used for its three predecessors. George Alexander, the actor- manager who gave it its premiere in 1895, insisted that it be cut to three, and the result was the play that we have known and loved ever since. You dont argue with success, but Wildes original text survives, in various manuscript forms, and, from time to time, enterprising theatres have given the long version a whirl. Richard Monette's Stratford production is its big-time Canadian premiere.

Earnest's predecessors attained their length by alternating comedy of manners -- what the Elizabethans would have called wit- combats -- with heavy-breathing melodrama. Its an uneasy if sometimes fascinating mixture (as seen in A Woman of No Importance, currently at Shaw), and Wilde finally resolved it, not by jettisoning the melodrama but by turning it on its head and making it farce. The original Earnest is four acts of beautiful nonsense; this is rather too much of a good thing, and Alexander was quite right to cut it. A hundred years on, though, the excised sections prove refreshing. They supply some welcome new jokes to a text that rather too many of us know by heart.

They do other things, as well. They change and elaborate the structure -- the long garden scene that was once Act Two is now bisected -- and they alter the balance of the characters. Lady Bracknell, appearing in two acts out of three, can still be the star part; Lady Brancaster, scoring only two out of four, doesnt have a chance.

The name makes a difference, too; Jack Worthing (his name hasn't changed) complains of Lady B that she's a monster without being a myth, and though there is much that is monstrous about Patricia Collins Lady B, there is indeed nothing that is mythical. She isnt competing with Edith Evans or any other predecessor; and it couldn't matter less how she says "A handbag!" (actually, she throws the line away) when confronted with the information that Jack, her would-be son-in-law, had been discovered in one when a baby.

Collins' matriarch is erect and imperious: a battering-ramrod. With undeviating efficiency she fulfills her social and dramatic function, which is to enforce and enunciate English upper-class prejudices as if they were the decrees of Providence -- as, in this context, they pretty much are.

There is a strain of radical discontent in this ur-text, which the Alexander technique seems to have been at great pains to eradicate. There is also, intentionally or not, a strain of theatrical self-satire; the characters comment on the play as it happens. Lady Brancasters forebodings about the coming denouement are a case in point.

This, of course, is all very post-modern, and some of it is positively cathartic. When Jack, stunned by the relentless stream of paradoxes emanating from his friend Algernon, asks him: Do you ever listen to what youre saying? he voices sentiments that some of us have wanted to scream at Wilde's characters for years.

In this version, the play becomes, as it was probably always intended to be, essentially about Jack and Algy: the two young men about town and country, each leading a double life, each in love with a girl who will only marry him if his name is Ernest.

They are distinctively played here. Donald Carrier's Jack is not, as Algy claims he is, the most earnest-looking person you ever saw in your life, not in that canary waistcoat and wide, permanent smile, but there is an edge beneath the smirk; he is in command, but he is also an outsider, as if the mark of the handbag were on him for life. Graham Abbey, who, as Algy, completes a hair-raising opening week that began with Laertes and DArtagnan, seems less happy fencing with epigrams than with rapiers; but he, too, has depths, suggesting that Algernon still has some growing up to do, and knows it. He also, as the play goes on, becomes increasingly charming.

Claire Jullien, as his beloved Cecily, is as sweetly implacable as she should be, a pink rose with a thorn. Jack fears that his Gwendolyn will "become like her mother in about 150 years time"; Michelle Giroux makes the probability rather too blatant, but there is piquancy in her excited habit of crooking one leg behind her while being kissed, and in the way that she tests the competing vibrations of the names Jack and Ernest as if with an invisible tuning fork.

The most substantial new scene concerns the arrival in the country of a solicitor named Gribsby (Brian Tree, drily obsequious) to arrest Algy for debts that were actually incurred by Jack, living it up at the Savoy Hotel. I am not going to be imprisoned in the suburbs protests Algernon, "for having dined in the West End," but of course it all comes of having two elegant but improvident young men both going around under the name of Ernest.

The scene is watched, with a mixture of pious horror and delighted satisfaction, by the play's very moral senior couple. "As a man sows, so shall he reap" says Miss Prism, the governess; she only says it once in the received text, but here it's a running gag with a payoff. Truthfully, its overdone; but Domini Blythes Prism (not as senior as all that) is an Edinburgh spinster so delectable that I would have been happy to hear her say anything. As her admirer Canon Chasuble puts it, I would hang upon her lips; and, unlike him, I'm not even sure that I would be speaking metaphorically. (His metaphor is drawn from bees.) Bernard Hopkins' desiccated Canon is less original, but agreeable.

The extension of the action means that more eating gets done, in a play that already -- like most comedies -- turns largely on greed; lobster and pate de foie gras sandwiches have now been added to the menu. The production has a fair amount of style and a lot of fun; and due partly, but by no means entirely, to the unfamiliar material, the play comes up fresher than it has a long time.
