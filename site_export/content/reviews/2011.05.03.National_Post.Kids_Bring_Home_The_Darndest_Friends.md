THEATRE REVIEW

Edward the Crazy Man Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

Workman Arts is a company whose initial aim was to use artistic creation as a form of therapy for mental illness. It started as a group of eight people: "individuals," to quote their own literature, with mental illness and limited theatre training.

Over the subsequent 20 years or so, both the size and the professionalism of the company have grown considerably. Edward the "Crazy Man" is one of their flagship productions. It's already been seen in Toronto at the Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People (now, blessedly, to be known again as the Young Peoples Theatre). Its now being given a well-deserved showing to wider audiences at Passe Muraille and on tour.

Emil Sher's play, adapted from a book of the same name by Marie Day, is a portrait of a schizophrenic, and an examination of his relationship with some neighbouring children. Edward, played remarkably by John Cleland, is a homeless man pushing a shopping cart through the streets, laden with shiny things. He has a special fondness for hubcaps, some of which he pinches from 12year-old Charlie. Charlie is initially indignant but is later captivated when he sees how beautifully Edward has decorated them and displayed them on a back wall.

Before, he and his friends had alternately teased Edward and run away from him. Now Charlie forges a friendship with him. He even asks Edward home for supper, specifying to his mother that he likes cheese sandwiches. When the mother actually sets eyes on Edward she is of course both shocked and scared. This is despite her being a social activist when the objects of her concern are a little less close to home. As Charlie points out to her, Edward isn't a bike lane or a pool threatened with closure; hes a person.

Directed by Leah Cherniak, the play is very good as far as it goes, though, at least in narrative terms, it may not go far enough. Perhaps because its based on a book, it's a series of vignettes rather than a developing story. There is an attempt to shape the action towards a finale on Halloween but the characters stay on the same spots.

The two principals, though, are very well drawn, both in writing and performance. Cleland as Edward has found a disconcerting shuffling rhythm, both physically and mentally. He can be very sharp, he can be very absent; always he's in his own world, whose connection to everybody elses is unpredictable. Its a surprise when he turns up to supper on time; it's as much of a surprise when he leaves. Olaf Sham, a 30year-old, manages an equally impressive if less obviously spectacular transformation as Charlie. His home life has its own issues, and he might almost regard Edward as a surrogate father; there's a moment at which he seems as let down by his errant friend as a child might be by a forgetful parent.

Two other actors double as children and adults. Both are fine as Charlies friends and contemporaries, but Faeghan Williams may be too young to convince as his mother, whos an underdeveloped character as written; her fear and protectiveness are certainly credible, and the single-parent and son relationship is truthfully sketched in, but it's only sketched. Charles RevoredCouto has less familiar, more suggestive territory to work as Edward's brother Lewis who barred him from their house when he began fearing for his own wife and child. He retains some guilt and concern, just as Edward remains both resentful and devoted.

Here, writing and acting leave us wanting more in a good way. Two of the actors, it was revealed at a first night talk back, are mentalhealth patients themselves, and one of them is actually a schizophrenic in remission. The quality of their work here shows that a social good can also be an artistic good.
