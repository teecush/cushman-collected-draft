The four shows of the year, the four that left you rubbing your eyes, wondering whether what you saw could really be happening: Company Theatre's Festen, Soulpeppers The Way of the World, Stratfords Hamlet and the Shaw Festival's The President. All were imaginative, fully-fleshed realizations of deeply challenging texts. All were ensemble achievements, with both the Stratford and Shaw shows throwing in a virtuoso performance at the top of the team.

Adrian Noble's production of Hamlet was something we hadnt seen at Stratford in years: a full-blooded main-stage realization of a Shakespeare play that created a world (pre-1914) in almost perfect harmony with the text. Take the one detail of Ophelias piano-playing, something that illuminated her relationship with her family and exploded in her mad-scene. The play was visually beautiful and thrillingly spoken, most of all, by Ben Carlson, whose blend of lucidity, intelligence, passion, wit and speed found their ideal outlet: a performance whose baffled anger haunts the

memory. (He made to be or not to be" sound fresh. And he's young enough to play it again.) Stratford's other great Shakespeare this year was Michael Langhams Loves Labour's Lost. Since Langham is in his 80s and has done the play often before, and since he was doing it now with largely unknown actors, this was routinely dismissed as old-fashioned and undercast. If to make complete intellectual and emotional sense of an elusive play, to look lovely, and to be both heart-tugging and riotously funny is to be old-fashioned, we're in serious trouble.

There were other good things at Stratford: a showmanly Caesar and Cleopatra, fine individual performances in The Trojan Women, the chance to see Fuente Ovejuna and an insanely strong overall company. But it was also a strange-feeling year, with two of its three new artistic directors having resigned before the opening, and one inexplicable choice among the new plays. So, next year, we start again, again.

And, disappointingly, without Peter Hinton, whose two Stratford productions werent among his best, but who more than compensated at Soulpepper with his Way of the World, a scenic update (to the 1950s) that unlocked the play. Traditionally its been about wigs, stars, epigrams and an incomprehensible plot; now it was about driving, sexy, funny and surprisingly young people in a context. Since we seldom see Restoration comedy in Canada, the achievement was taken for granted, but it was extraordinary. One hung on every word. Soulpepper's other big achievement was Weyni Mengesha's production of A Raisin in the Sun, with Alison Sealy-Smith giving the performance of her life. Unlike my big four, this show worked familiar conventions, but it worked them superbly, surmounting any fears that Broadways pioneering black play might now seem worthy but dull. Altogether, this was Soulpeppers Broadway year. The choice of The Odd Couple raised some eyebrows, and one wouldn't want them to make a habit of it, but it was Schultz and Matamoros Inc. in high comic gear, in other words, a treat.

Nothing, though, was as hilarious as the Shaw Festival's The President, Blair Williamss stunning debut production of this wonderfully hardboiled farce (Hungarian-American and combining the best of both). With Lorne Kennedy playing the lead in a perpetual state of fast-forward, it was a lunchtime show, but also the high point of a Shaw season otherwise good without being distinctive.

Festen was Company Theatres follow-up to A Whistle in the Dark. Jason Byrne, who directed both, seems to have an aesthetic that's unique: casual approach, rigorous results, with companies of strongly marked individual actors working in fruitful tension. Second place in Toronto's ensemble stakes goes to Studio 180s Stuff Happens: It didnt have the same ground-breaking feel but it did have super performances and -- courtesy of the Iraq war and the Bush administration -- a mordantly gripping story. Ensemble broke out again at the end of the year, with whole stagefuls of fine actors being better than their material: in the Tarragon's Moliere, CanStage's Its a Wonderful Life, and -- if were going to be stringent about it --in the threehander Someone Who'll Watch over Me at the Berkeley Street Theatre.

Best new Canadian play: Hannah Moscovitch's East of Berlin, whose cast serendipitously included Paul Dunn, author of Offensive Shadows which was the second best, and Brendan Gall, author of Alias Godot which was the third. All three plays witty and blessedly non-parochial, though Moscovitchs carried much the heaviest freight. Dunns might have done better if stripped of its new prologue and paired with his earlier Shakespearean knock-off High Gravel Blind, which some years ago was the inaugural play at Stratford's studio. There was the usual--actually, it seemed worse than usual -- complement of sloppily constructed new plays, dripping with self-importance, at Tarragon, Factory, Passe Muraille and elsewhere. Most disappointing British play: Democracy. (Could a Michael Frayn play really be this dull?) Best imported American play: Sarah Ruhl's The Clean House, though you might not have known it from the CanStage production. On the other hand that much-criticized organization did give us Judith Thompsons Palace at the End (overrated but respectable) and Little Shop of Horrors.

Best musical was, obviously, Jersey Boys. Though hang on, it wasnt: best musical was An Inconvenient Musical, hugely funny, with an original score, and all ours. Finally: I'm too often remiss about crediting costume designers; indeed, one of our leading playwright-directors has been putting it about that I'm fashion-challenged. So, after bows in the direction of Charlotte Dean ( Loves Labours Lost) and Tamara Marie Kucheran ( Intimate Apparel, fittingly) let me conclusively salute Erika Connor, who came up with creations (for The Odd Couple, Little Shop of Horrors, Cinderella) whose magnificence even I had to recognize. And finally finally: it's off my official beat but Tim Albery's COC production of War and Peace was another of the year's triumphs, if also, to my mind, another example of the staging excelling the thing staged.
