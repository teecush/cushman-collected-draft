INSOMNIA

THEATRE REVIEW

INSOMNIA

Friday, May 30, 1997

Written by Daniel Brooks

Directed by Guillermo Verdecchia


  


Starring Daniel Brooks, Tamsin Kelsey,


  


Guillermo Verdecchia and Fiona Highet


  


Rating: ***

High-gloss rant sure to keep you awake

ABOUT two-thirds of the way through Insomnia , Daniel Brooks, reaching a new plateau as a performer, stands at a microphone and delivers an extraordinary long speech. Perhaps I should call it a routine. Anyway, it's a rant, fast but measured, on all the things in modern life that displease or disturb him.

He arraigns the usual suspects: big business, politicians, technology, pollution, both mental and environmental. How, he asks, do we know what invisible substances may be poisoning us in this very room? And he outlines a series of new rules to live by -- he calls then protocols -- rules which, if followed, would transform or even eliminate civilization as we know it: no cars, for a start. He claims that he has summoned this meeting in order to raise the devil.

At this point I should make clear that, although Brooks is the writer of Insomnia as well as its star, he is not, as far as I know, playing himself. He is playing a character called John F, and John F is very likely mad: so much of what he says is persuasive -- and hugely enjoyable -- but you do begin to doubt his motivation. He lives with a woman called Gwen, and they have a child whom they seem to love. Nevertheless the ménage is breaking down, sexually and psychologically. John is part dreamer, part joker, part bully: easy to like but difficult to live with. Gwen is opaque, but thats probably her method of defence: She retreats inside herself. Their deteriorating relationship is shown to us in a series of chill, stylish snippets; the actors communicate their lack of communication quite perfectly. Tamsin Kelsey is very fine and Brooks is superb. As John F, his monologue is wonderful standup comedy -- impeccable in pace, timing and repose -- but the man inside it is plainly going bananas.

John F has a brother, William F, played by Guillermo Verdecchia, who also directs. His direction is terrific, making commanding use of lighting and sound, dividing the stage into new and intriguing shapes, using the space right up to the exit door in the back wall. His performance is less satisfying, or at least, his character is. William is richer than John and more at home in the world but he too has conjugal problems, which arent quite brought to life: No reflection on his acting, or on that of Fiona Highet (who plays his partner, Kate), but when they take the stage the temperature perceptibly drops. Gwen and Kate go off together, at which point the play takes shape as a classic piece of male paranoia, rather like Harold Pinter's Old Times .

As for the title: John can't sleep, and its either a cause or a symptom of his malaise. It may also stand for societys ever-deepening sickness, in which case the full title Insomnia: a Work in Progress may be a philosophical diagnosis rather than a technical description of the work itself, which has the high gloss of a work completed. But if they are still refining it, I'd like them not to worry about the performance-art side, which can take care of itself, but to concentrate instead on the relationships, which at the moment vary from sketchy to compelling. There were moments when I suspected that John and William were meant to be two halves of one personality, but these were blessedly few. In another respect the show earns its forbidding title: It keeps you awake.


  


Insomnia continues at The Theatre Centre until June 1.
