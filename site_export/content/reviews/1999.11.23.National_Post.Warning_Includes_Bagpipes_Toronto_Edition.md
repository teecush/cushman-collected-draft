THE GLACE BAY MINERS MUSEUM

Factory Theatre, Toronto

The great thing about the central performance in The Glace Bay Miners Museum is that it has no truck with worthiness. This is a blight that attacks every other aspect of Wendy Lill's play. Set among the coal miners of Cape Breton during the appalling Depression, it's based on the novel by Sheldon Currie that was also the source of the film Margarets Museum.

The audience arrives to find the stage covered in furniture and the furniture covered in dust sheets. We are plainly in for a domestic reduction of an epic theme; and the heart sinks. It rises, though, when Irene Poole takes the stage to perform -- in quick succession and ascending order of ferocity -- a mournful-sounding song in Gaelic, a bawdy song in English, and an aggressive bit of tap that I can now, thanks to Swingstep, identify as step-dancing. Then she introduces herself.

She is Margaret, daughter of a dead miner, granddaughter of another miner who is alive but severely damaged, and sister of two more miners, one of whom is also dead. She herself is known locally as the slut with a runny nose. She speaks plainly and racingly; she is bursting with intelligence and personality, and is hence very attractive. She may also be a bit crazy.

A table and chairs are unwrapped, and we are transported to a bleak cafe for a nicely crafted scene in which Margaret, suspicious but susceptible, meets the love of her life. He is Neil, who spent one day down the mines and then fled, and is an evangelist for the Capes ancestral Scottish culture. He woos Margaret by playing the bagpipes, and it works.

Everyone to his taste: My own feeling is that the presence of bagpipes in a play should be the subject of a warning notice in the lobby, like the ones they have for cigarettes, strobe lights, sex and swearing. There are no strobes in this play.

Margaret takes Neil home and he more or less moves in. So do we, the table and chairs having re-grouped to create the family kitchen. Poverty, illness, and the threat of unemployment weigh heavy on everyone's spirits, and we hunker down for two acts of bickering and making up, meant to reflect the wider tensions in the world outside.

Through it all, Poole's Margaret gives as good as she gets, generally better. Youre like a perfectly tuned set of pipes," Neil tells her, "you always make the right sound -- it comes from deep inside you. Its the truth. Alone in the cast, she avoids both rhetoric and sentimentality. The play ends with Margaret's three remaining men all dead, and deciding to take a body part from each of them, pickle and preserve it, thus creating the museum of the title. All we have of other people, she says by way of epilogue, is our memories of them.

This presupposes that they are worth remembering. In the theatre, it means that they should have lived while they were alive. This, neither the play, the performances, nor Rosemary Dunsmore's direction, can make happen. The actors are all recognizably talented, but theyre painting by numbers.

Wendy Thatcher as Margarets hard-pressed mother spits nails as required, is intermittently tender as necessary -- but when she harangues her family about their desperate position, a harangue is what it remains, and her shrill efforts to personalize it make it worse. Oliver Becker's Neil starts off cute and ends up wet, perhaps because his character, though certainly voluble, is crucially underwritten. He eventually gives in and returns to the mines, but this traumatic decision seems to leave no mark.

There is a running duel between the apolitical Neil and his kid- brother-in-law (Peter van Gestel), a strong union man who also happens to be carrying on, offstage, with a boss's daughter. Their debate mirrors the bitter paradox of all mining disputes: attempts to keep working in a terrible job. Eventually there is a strike. This too, of course, happens offstage; we learn about it mainly from Margaret, in her periodic capacity as narrator-of-last-resort.

I havent read the book or seen the movie, but I imagine that they got out into the community more. The boxed-in treatment here is not only claustrophobic but counter-productive. It is possible to illuminate big conflicts through small ones. Ibsen did it, Chekhov did it, and the best contemporary plays seen in Toronto this year -- The Drawer Boy, The Beauty Queen of Leenane, Communicating Doors -- have also done it. They summoned history; but not through talking about specific but invisible factual events that were happening on the characters doorsteps.

Onstage symbolism is provided by grandfather (Les Carlson, chairbound) whose lungs have been destroyed in the pits; who has lost the ability, or maybe just the desire, to speak; and who has been denied compensation. The implications are horrific, and all too likely; the presentation is merely irritating. The play harks back to a supposed golden age of Canadian theatre in which telling our story" -- putting bits of it up on the stage and making your audience feel proud or ashamed -- was thought to be enough. It isn't.
