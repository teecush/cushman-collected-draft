FREE AS INJUNS

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

It's a jim-dandy farm, says the sheriff, fulfilling officialdoms customary theatrical task of surveying a scene of physical and psychological wreckage. "Wish I owned it." That line, unfortunately, does not come from Native Earths new production, Free as Injuns, but from Desire Under the Elms, the Eugene O'Neill play on which it is based.

Toronto is having an unaccustomed feast of O'Neill at the moment, what with Long Days Journey into Night at Soulpepper and Hughie at Theatre Centre. Desire is generally reckoned to be the best of ONeill's early plays -- "early" here being anything before the late twin peaks of Long Day's Journey and The Iceman Cometh -- and this adaptation makes one want to see a good production of it. Free as Injuns is far from being that production.

Fair enough: Its not trying to be. But Tara Beagans play borrows enough of the characters and structure of its original for us to be painfully aware of the gap. As in O'Neill we are on the farm of Ephraim Cabot, who has three sons, each by a different wife. He brings home yet another bride, a woman decades younger than himself named Abbie, or in this version simply Be. She catches the eye of Even (Eben in the original), youngest of the Cabot boys and sets about seducing him. He, after some early hesitation, responds with enthusiasm. Other considerations apart, he has a score to settle with his old man for the way in which he treated Even's late mother, with whose ghostly voice he periodically converses.

The source play is a hot brew of physical and territorial lusts, the latter trenchantly summarized in the clinching line quoted up top. This version stirs in a new element. Ephraim, who is white, has built his farm on Native American (or would that be Canadian?) land. He has compounded this act of dispossession by marrying and mistreating a succession of native women -- though one of them, judging by the looks of her son, may have been Asian. (Or is this colour-blind casting? You never know.)

This theme, though, is never actively realized in the text. It merely gives rise to some windy writing, with the occasional stab of genuine poetry, all bound together with more palatable passages of rougher stuff. This admittedly is the mixture ONeill himself favoured when he was straining for tragedy rather than achieving by mining his own past. He was, though, a lot better at storytelling.

The last half-hour or so of Free as Injuns circles endlessly around the same situation. Be has given birth. Ephraim thinks the child is his. Even knows better but is tormented by jealousy. The theory seems to be that the more they all agonize about it, the more that agony will transmit itself to the audience. It doesnt; or at least not in the way intended.

Ruth Madoc-Jones' production makes heavy weather of a heavy script. It's also erratically acted. Old Ephraim is one of those tyrannical patriarchs who are always hard to cast if neither Jim Mezon nor David Fox is available. Here we have Jerry Franken, Foxs old sparring partner from The Drawer Boy and elsewhere, whose performance occasions the kind of pain peculiar to the spectacle of a good actor doing his best with a role to which hes totally unsuited.

Ephraim mercilessly and habitually humiliates all three of his sons, and Franken is neither physically nor vocally imposing enough for us to believe that they, all strapping fellows, would let him get away with it. The stammer that he adopts to indicate failing powers only aggravates the situation.

PJ Prudat's Be mainly projects an effortful idea of sultriness, though James Cade does infuse some raw if intermittent passion into Eben. John Ng and Ash Knight as the other brothers are competent at what they're required to do, which is mainly to leer about the place, long after threatening to leave (which in O'Neill they do). They seem to have been kept on mainly for symbolic purposes; one of them goes around carrying a door frame, which everybody refers to as his gate.

In other respects the show looks good; Andy Moro's set transforms the Buddies main space, the most versatile in Toronto, into a kind of cockpit, lacking only a good fight.
