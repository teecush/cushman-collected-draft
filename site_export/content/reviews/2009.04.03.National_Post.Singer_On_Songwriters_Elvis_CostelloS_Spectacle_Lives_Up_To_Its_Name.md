For a wonder, Spectacle is a show that lives up to its hype. Its full title is Spectacle: Elvis Costello With ..., which sounds both cumbersome and inconclusive, but is at least accurate. The idea is that Costello, the singer-songwriter who gives eclecticism a good name, plays host to a favoured musician or group of musicians. They talk about music, they perform it separately or together. And that's all. The season kicks off with a one-on-one: Costello sits down with Elton John. Next week finds him chatting up the three members of The Police, at the end of their reunion tour (the one that might have been called "the hello again and goodbye forever show). The week after that theres a guitar pull" -- which, as I understand it, differs from a jam session in being sequential rather than collective -- whose participants include Kris Kristofferson, Roseanne Cash and Norah Jones. Future guests range from Lou Reed to classical diva and sometime crosser-over Renee Fleming, via Tony Bennett. Oh yes, and Bill Clinton.

Rufus Wainwright and Mrs. Costello (Diana Krall) will represent Canada, and theres an admiring reference to Ron Sexsmith. We do seem numerically underrepresented (wouldn't Joni Mitchell be a natural? or k. d. lang?), especially as this is a British-Canadian co-production. It's already been shown in the United States, go figure, and it was actually shot in the U. S., at the Apollo Theater in Harlem, than which you could hardly get more legendary, though frankly from all we can see it could be any place with a stage and an audience. Still, its hard to imagine the U. S. coming up with such a project: The nearest equivalent I can think of is Inside the Actors Studio, but there the interviewer is the outsider-as-sycophant. The corresponding danger, when you have peer talking to peers, is that mutual admiration will descend into mutual back-scratching. But, on the evidence of the first two shows, the people concerned are too smart and too funny for that to happen. Costello and John have too much else to talk about, largely their enthusiasm for other performers, while the three Police-men have sharp-tongued reputations to protect.

Each show begins with Costello singing a number associated with his guest or guests. He then slips into host mode, a role to which he brings an unassuming eager-beaverness. As an

interviewer hes unashamedly raw; many TV inquisitors carry clipboards, but it's rare to see one consult his notes as frequently and openly as Costello does. His trademark unshavenness, too, makes him an unlikely prospect to succeed David Letterman or Jay Leno or even Jon Stewart. But he's a natural comic and, most importantly, hes informed and hes enthusiastic. He takes it for granted that his audience will also have those qualities, if not to his degree, and he builds on that.

The first Spectacle might easily have been re-titled in the plural: two pairs of spectacles, in fact, since his glasses and John's -- plain horn-rims against gaudy --make an engaging visual statement in themselves. Another is the way in which the blue-draped set matches the colour of John's shirt.

They talk about perceptions of success and failure. John shares his shock at having, in the first flush of his own success, driven to collect Martha and the Vandellas at a London airport and discovered that these Motown idols "had had to borrow money to get their clothes from the cleaners in order to make the tour at all. They share admiration for the hugely influential Laura Nyro, who may have been ahead of her time by seeming to be behind it; and for David Ackles who ended up as a teacher and who wrote splendid songs, one of which (Down River) they duet as a storming finale. Having decided long ago that Im not an Elton John fan (and his recent theatre scores did nothing to change my mind) I was ignorantly unprepared for the earthiness of his vocals here, or indeed for the information that Leon Russell is Johns biggest idol -- he told me of a great throat remedy."

The Police procedural sensibly brings the trio on separately, for indivdual interviews, before throwing them together for the conversational equivalent of a jam. (This also of course mimics their collective trajectory: dispersal and reunion.) Andy Summers talks about learning new harmonic possibilities on guitar from listening to Jimi Hendrix, and having to un-learn them when playing with punk groups ("you didn't have to be able to play at all). Stewart Copeland defines the split personality of his later career: I'm the composer guy, whos very sophisticated, and the drummer who aint." Sting, announced as "all-round good egg tells us that the bass is a great place to control the band from" and crosstalks with Costello on their songwriter-rivalry. (Costello, placatingly: "You thrive on a degree of competition. Sting, unhesitatingly: Yes, but I'm right.")

Spectacle isn't as pure and unflashy as all that. Its obviously heavily edited, and it breaks things up with irritating cuts to a visual checkerboard motif, backed up by computerised music, apparently designed to favour no known school of composition. It also keeps alluding to exciting performance-clips that then fail to appear. (Both these distractions are especially evident in the John show.)

The great thing, though, is that Costello never stops a conversation because it seems to be getting too serious. Its notable that all the participants in these first two shows are British, immersed in what is after all an American musical culture but still outside of it, and with an amused, articulate perspective. Also that they were part of the second invasion -- the post Beatles-and-Stones wave -- that could accommodate the pre-rock tradition rather than oppose it. From this perspective, Costello's marriage to Krall looks like the symbolic convergence of two musical cultures, with the added reconciling twist that it's the younger singer who does the older music. He duets with Summers on a tune by Charles Mingus (who, he says, "speaks more than other jazz writers to people from rock and roll). He celebrates the 1968-71 heyday of the singer-songwriter (incredibly rich") while lamenting the demise of the songwriter per se: a profession in which he himself could certainly have prospered even if hed never sung a note. He also says that "the quality of the music in the 60s and '70s is much better than what it is today;" he's probably right but, as the son of a British dance-band singer, hes better placed than most to realize that thats every generation's complaint. I look forward to seeing what he'll get out of Tony Bennett, whos been saying the same things in interviews for years, maybe because hes always asked the same questions. Will Costello kick that show off by singing I Left My Heart in San Francisco? And what will be President Clinton's theme song? - Spectacle premieres tonight at 9 p. m. on CTV.
