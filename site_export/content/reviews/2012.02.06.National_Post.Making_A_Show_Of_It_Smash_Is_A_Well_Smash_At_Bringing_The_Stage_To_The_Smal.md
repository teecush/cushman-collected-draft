You do not, even in this age of revivals, expect a touted brand new musical to start with somebody singing Over the Rainbow. That, however, is how Smash begins. It isn't the only instance of false advertising in connection with this show. The advance clips have all made it look like a knock-off of Glee. In fact, it isn't like Glee at all: well, hardly at all. It's better.

It is - the publicity has been accurate about this - a series about the creation of an imaginary Broadway musical about Marilyn Monroe. It has subplots as well, but they sprout naturally, or mostly so, from the main theme, which is tenaciously, and even accurately, pursued. It's also very witty, with the sharpest script this side of Mad Men, and very fast. The scenes are mostly short. The first 20 minutes of the pilot introduce a host of interlinked characters with exemplary crispness.

And that first number is a tease. It's sung by Karen (Katharine McPhee), an aspirant actress who gets as far as "once in a lullaby" before she's cut off. It turns out she's auditioning for a jaded panel that hardly bothers to conceal its indifference. That's the biz, and Smash gets it right. Karen is ushered out, to be replaced by Ivy (Megan Hilty), a more seasoned young chorine who inquires, as provocatively as she knows how, "Do you want the ballad or the up-tempo?" Karen is trim and brunette, Ivy blowsy and blond. Ivy looks more like Monroe, but it's possible that Karen thinks more like her. By the end of the second episode, they'll be the contenders for the role.

The Marilyn musical isn't what they're up for when we first meet them. That show hasn't been conceived of yet, though it will be in the next scene. We meet the composerlyricist team of Tom (Christian Borle) and Julia (Debra Messing), who are allegedly modelled on Betty Comden and Adolph Green, the urbane Broadway and Hollywood legends who wrote Singin' in the Rain and much else, but who seem more like the current partnership of Stephen Flaherty and Lynn Ahrens, who wrote Ragtime. Tom's dangerously eager young assistant Ellis (Jaime Cepero) idly mentions Monroe as a great subject for a musical, a suggestion that will come back to bite everybody round about Episode 3. Tom and Julia pooh-pooh the idea, and then of course get possessed by it, despite the fact that, as somebody mentions, there's already been a musical about Monroe and it was a disaster. In real life, there have been two, and only one was a disaster. The other was merely a flop.

Julia's reluctance is partly connected to her marriage. She and her husband Frank (Broadway musical veteran Brian d'Arcy James, roguishly cast in what, unless things are to take a radical turn, is a non-musical role) are taking time out from their careers in order to pursue the adoption of a child from China, since that's where everything comes from these days. I'm not clear why they should both have to give up work to do this, but chances are that Frank's career as a science teacher is not what's been supporting their lifestyle.

Apart from one funny/harrowing scene involving a focus group, the adoption plot is the least successful part of the show, especially when it brings in the couple's biological son, who seems to have been waiting an awfully long time for his baby sister. (If they could have one kid of their own, why not another?) Tom and Julia have a hit show still running, with the admittedly terrible title of Heaven on Earth; we see a bit of it, and it looks for all the world like the closing number of A Chorus Line.

We meet two Brits. One is Dev (Raza Jaffrey), Karen's highly intelligent and supportive boyfriend, who has snagged himself a job in the mayor's office. The other is Derek (Jack Davenport), a hotshot director whom Tom loathes but who gets attached to the project anyway.

Theresa Rebeck, who conceived the series and wrote the first three episodes, is also the author of the current Broadway play Seminar, in which Alan Rickman plays a role similar enough to Derek to make you wonder which came first; both characters are manipulative, sneery, supposedly brilliant and great believers in the casting couch. Dev and Derek share an especially delightful scene of competitive one-upping, such as only Oxbridge graduates could indulge in.

Davenport and Borle share the acting honours so far. The only jarring performances come from Anjelica Huston, who seems somehow out of focus as the show's putative producer, and from Michael Cristofer, as her nasty estranged marital and professional partner. But I have a bias against that actor, on account of a terrible Pulitzer-winning play he once wrote called The Shadow Box. When Huston threw drinks in his face, as she does with the regularity of a running gag, I silently applauded.

Add McPhee to the standout performers, especially when she gets up on a karaoke stage and does a very un-karaoke rendition of Redneck Woman. Most of the songs, though, are new ones, ghosted so to speak for Tom and Julia by the Hairspray team of Marc Shaiman and Scott Wittman. Their putative score for Marilyn is schizophrenic; much of it is snazzily true to period but there's also the regulation "contemporary" boneless ballad.

The presentation's schizoid, too; rehearsal scenes will sometimes morph into fantasy visions of the finished product. That's fine, but they miss one of the oldest and best tricks in the book: the transition from piano accompaniment to full orchestra, guaranteed to tingle the spine. Here, there seems to be a full band in the rehearsal room, which is too much Glee for comfort or belief. And the show actually is believable, most of the time; it might be darker and racier if it were being done on cable, as was the original intention, but it wouldn't necessarily be better.

The numbers, too, are excellently staged and shot. The presiding director is Michael Mayer, a Broadway regular whose shows aren't my favourites but who certainly knows the territory. Steven Spielberg is the executive producer and apparently had the original idea.

The third episode concludes with an absolute charmer of a number for Monroe and Joe DiMaggio. It makes you wonder if someone is planning a stage spin-off, in the form of an actual Marilyn musical, and also what kind of song-and-dance they could have in store for Arthur Miller.

Karen is the more obviously sympathetic of the two heroines, but you get to appreciate Ivy's point of view as well; that's a real strength, and very handy given that anything can happen on a musical's long and tortuous road to Broadway. Smash deserves to be a smash; it's already, as far as I'm concerned, a date.

- Smash premieres Feb. 6 at 10 p.m. on CTV and NBC. The series also airs on Bravo! beginning Feb. 10 at 9 p.m.
