THE DOMINO HEART

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

Suspense in the theatre comes in many forms. A rarefied example can be found in the first scene of The Domino Heart. This new play by Matthew Edison, previously known and valued as an actor, has a cast of three, of whom two are dimly visible at the start of proceedings. The third is fully lit, and she begins talking to us, or to herself with us as a surrogate. Half an hour later she is still at it, and we, remarkably, are still paying attention. We do wonder, though, whether she will at some point get round to interacting with her companions or whether we are in for a whole evening of monologues.

As it turns out, we get the latter, with trace elements of the former. This is a play that follows its heart: metaphorically, of course, but also literally since it chronicles the transplanting of an organ from a dead body to a live one, which rejects it, with great regret, and thence to another, which accepts it, thank you very much. Structurally, the piece is a cardiovascular La Ronde, right down to a last sequence which brings us back to the character with whom we started.

It isn't her heart of course, at least not technically. It's her husband's. The lady, beautifully played by Rosemary Dunsmore, is Cara, an academic, and she was married to Peter, a writer. For a short time she betrayed him with one of her students. She confessed it and was forgiven. So at least it seemed; Peter made no secret of how much he had hurt her, and he detailed, graphically, how she had wounded his heart. (A metaphor here, of course, but only barely.) Then he died in a car crash, leaving her with guilt, despair and a young daughter.

Also with her husband's organ donor card. The designated host body belongs to Mortimer, a clergyman, elderly but -- since the part is played by David Fox -- vigorous. We meet him on the night before his operation. He is partly engaged in composing a sermon, out of a mixture of habit and enjoyment. As it happens, he has lost his faith, but he still has considerable hope and abundant charity. He too, though, has the abuse of love on his conscience; a lifelong bachelor, he once rejected a female admirer who subsequently killed herself.

We learn, having taken our leave of Mortimer, that there were surgical complications and that he died on the table. The domino heart -- is this really an exact metaphor? -- ends up inside Leo, an advertising high-flyer and part-time insider trader. He is, scarily, only 33 when he receives it. Even more alarmingly, his connections have enabled him to jump the waiting list.

Leo, of course, has been abusing his body for years, in the guise of not denying it anything. When we first see him he is, as near simultaneously as possible, sipping a cognac and smoking a cigar. An early highpoint of Raoul Bhaneja's performance, which is flashy in the best sense, comes when he changes, with visible smugness, from one silk shirt into another.

Leo has also been abusing everybody else. He admits, with what comes over as cheerless bravado, that his profession exists to cheat the public and that he is exceptionally good at it. His cynicism is spiced with self-hatred, a corrosive mixture that burns especially fiercely when he considers his relationship with his mother, a penniless immigrant who was raped at 17 (Leo was the result). In our last sight of him, he seems to be raising a gun to his mouth. It turns out to be a cigarette lighter -- a slower way to go.

So we have one character who may have caused a suicide, another who is quietly committing it, and a third who, as we finally discover, has actually made the attempt. Add them up another way: We have one person who had love but abused it, one who wanted it but refused it when offered, and one who has spent his life denying its existence. The parts don't quite interlock; even in narrative terms, Cara's life is finally affected by Mortimer but not by Leo (though, in fairness, she would like it to be). The asymmetry is frustrating, though admittedly if everything did add up neatly, we would probably be complaining of glibness.

I have a built-in resistance to plays composed of sequential soliloquies, unless they are delivered by Kristen Thomson in masks, or are as potently intercut as in Kennedy's Children years ago. But even setting my prejudices aside, the form lets the subject off the hook. In drama, as in life, love is easier said than done. Edison writes about it eloquently, sometimes movingly, but I still wanted to see his characters slugging it out, one with another, rather than telling us all about it afterward.

On the strength of these monologues, he could probably write excellent dialogue. This is his first play to be produced, and it's a hopeful debut. Nothing sounds forced, and the writing has set off some superb performances.

Dunsmore, whose demeanour suggests the control she's lost, projects a racking bitterness, which breaks down into innumerable fine gradations, and which she finally and inspiringly transcends. Fox's beaming gentleness, which usually serves to mask mountainous reserves of rage, here reveals only more gentleness, which is a treat. Bhaneja has more limited opportunities (it may be a tribute to the author's own moral fibre that this dubious character is the only one who seems to be drawn from the outside) but he takes them with great bite and crackle.

There are no blank spots in any of the performances, a tribute, at the very least, to Michael Kessler's casting, and a strong indication that his direction, though properly invisible, has been both responsive and responsible.
