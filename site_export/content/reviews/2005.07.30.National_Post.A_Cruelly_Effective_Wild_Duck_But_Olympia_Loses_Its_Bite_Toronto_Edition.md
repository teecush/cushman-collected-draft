Ibsen's The Wild Duck is usually described as a play about two kinds of lies -- the high-sounding ones known as ideals, imposed from without and generally fatal, and the more mundane falsehoods that people tell themselves, and hope others will believe, just so their lives will be bearable. "The truth will set you free" goes up against "humankind cannot bear very much reality," and loses. The description's accurate but it sounds, and is, schematic. Laszlo Marton is a director more interested in people than in abstractions, merciless in his eye for detail, charitable in his overall vision. In his production for Soulpepper, one of the finest things he or they have achieved, The Wild Duck is revealed as the most moving play ever to be written on a subject that's painfully specific: cruelty to children, across two generations.

Do what you want to the grown-ups, shouts the play's commentator, Dr. Relling, but leave the child alone. It's a thrilling moment, because Relling, who prefers to remain as drunk as possible for as long as possible, doesn't usually rouse himself to that degree of passion. It's also wrenching, because we have met and fully believe in the child he is trying to protect: Hedvig, the adoring daughter of the feckless photographer Hjalmar Ekdal and his protecting wife, Gina. And it's mordantly ironic, since the man he's cautioning is himself the victim of a kind of child abuse; this is Gregers Werle, who grew up hating his father, a businessman who once engineered the ruin and imprisonment of his partner, Hjalmar's father. Old Werle has, in fairness, supported the Ekdals ever since, but then he may have another motive; Gina once worked for him and he might be Hedvig's father. Certainly Gregers believes he is, and he, the play's idealist, makes it his mission to purify the Ekdal marriage by letting in the truth.

Up until Gregers moves in on them, the Ekdals are a reasonably happy family group, and certainly a tight-knit one; the staging makes that last metaphor tangible, showing the three clinging devotedly together at their first joint appearance and desperately, in a kind of fearful parody, at their last. The heart's blood of the production is Martha MacIsaac who, 20 herself, makes a tearingly convincing 14-year-old, radiating love and trust toward her undeserving dad, hurt but forgiving as children are when he forgets a promised present, devastating when she, afraid she's lost him, is herself devastated. There isn't a trace of winsomeness or false sentiment about this performance, and its closing stages, even if you don't know what's coming, are heartbreaking.

Chekhov may have been laughing at The Wild Duck when he wrote The Seagull, whose heroine, when an admirer gives her the eponymous bird, asks him, "Is this some kind of symbol?" (Well, words to that effect.) Ibsen's duck is several kinds of symbol, and the characters, Gregers especially, are all too aware of it, only their interpretations differ. It could be the truth that needs to be brought to the surface; it could be the lie that needs to be destroyed. Prosaically, it's Hedvig's wounded pet living, along with sundry other creatures, in a surprisingly capacious loft. In Csorsz Khell's set, sparse and starkly lit, this seems to be located beneath the family living quarters, which is even more surprising but allows for some thrilling descents.

There is a creepy bond between Hedvig and Gregers; they are, after all, half-siblings. Brent Carver's Gregers is a haunted fanatic with a residue of charm, a one-man cult who suffers, in Frank McGuinness's adaptation, from "the wrong of believing he's always right"; he can't understand why his nostrums aren't working as prescribed and goes on applying them regardless. The phrase "creeping Jesus" comes to mind, as he insinuates himself through doorways, rubbing his fingers like Uriah Heep. It takes a Hjalmar to see him as a saviour, just as it takes a Gregers to hero-worship Hjalmar. They could be a revisionist Othello and Iago, Gregers a destroyer who means well and Hjalmar an incorrigible self- dramatizer who turns on his daughter -- not his wife -- as savagely and as unbearably as Othello on Desdemona.

Diego Matamoros's hollow Hjalmar, another damaged son and an inventor who hasn't decided what to invent, is disarmingly as well as disturbingly funny at first. Indeed, part of the play's greatness is that he goes on being funny even when the tension is screwed tightest. But his callousness when playing the role of injured father is hard to watch, if only because it's so hard to disown. Matamoros's searching performance could represent any of us at our weakest.

The cast is all-round impregnable, starting with the flunkies (Derek Boyes and Philip Riccio), discoursing in gossipy whispers while preparing a bowl of punch, and moving up through Alon Nashman's deluded demoniac, making a person out of two lines and an alcoholic exit, up to David Hemblen as Werle senior, a tycoon in the quiet twilight of his powers, and Brenda Bazinet as his last mistress. William Webster is magnificent as Old Ekdal, a fantasy monarch in his own domain, a lost and humiliated beggar outside it. (The play may be said to take in elder abuse as well.) Maggie Huculak's Gina is less patient than most; when Hjalmar picks a melodramatic quarrel with his wife, she yells right back. I disagree with the conception but I can't fault the execution. Joseph Ziegler's Relling, angry rather than morose, is irresistible.

Olympia, by the Hungarian Ferenc Molnar, Soulpepper's other new show, starts with a sabre-toothed Brenda Robins and a doleful, pooch- faced Michael Healey (also the adapter) brazenly telling one another what they already know. We've had that joke, in the same author's The Play's the Thing, and here, since the characters aren't working theatricals, it's less thematically relevant.

What they are is Austrian aristocrats relaxing at a spa in the last glow of the Empire. The play was written in 1928 but seems to be set shortly before the First World War put an end to all that. Essential information is that Austrians despise Hungarians, though they sometimes find them useful, and should on no account fall in love with them. So of course Olympia, our heroine, does just that, with a humble cavalryman.

The play sets itself up as a trifle, then shows its teeth. Albert Schultz's production is elegant in looks and movement but the bite is intermittent. Kristin Booth is far more at ease with Olympia's heartache than with her cruelty. Stuart Hughes plays her unintended, who has tricks of his own, and does it well, especially in his earlier, shyer moments, but there needs to be more going on between these two people than is apparent here. Frank Moore appears as a deus ex machina, striking a single beaming note that is highly effective to begin with but whose returns steadily diminish.

There are, though, two riotous performances. One comes from Oliver Dennis, whose accident-prone intelligence officer is high farce. The other belongs to Nancy Palk, whose imperious, if not actually imperial, turn as Olympia's mama is high comedy, which means she puts a shiny gloss on some very base impulses. Apart from being continuously funny, she conveys what the show otherwise lacks: the sense that these people act as if they own the earth, and like it that way. When she utters the best line in the piece, something about the good old days when kings could eviscerate troublesome commoners, no questions asked, she makes Lady Bracknell seem like one of nature's democrats.
