THREE SISTERS

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

The fourth and last act of Chekhov's Three Sisters has been described as the high-water mark of modern drama. I find that accolade a little grudging; I think the summit is occupied by the third and fourth acts together. In Jackie Maxwell's production -- the last of the Shaw Festival's opening week and Maxwell's own first show as artistic director -- this sequence, comprising everything that happens after intermission, is a straight trip up Parnassus.

In the play's first act, we've watched the genteel Prozorov sisters try to maintain a civilized life for themselves in a country town a thousand miles from their longed-for Moscow. Civilization in this case means conducting cultured conversation with the artillery officers stationed nearby.

Act Two has shown the cultured dream thinning out; the women's adored brother, whom both they and he hoped would be a university professor, sinks into the life of a petty civil servant, drunk, henpecked and a cuckold. The third act takes place against the background of a town fire and, under pressure, nearly all the characters look at themselves and see themselves as failures. At its end, Masha, the middle, married sister and Irina, the youngest, each make a bid for a compromised kind of happiness. Masha confesses her love for the battery commander, also married, and Irina decides to accept the proposal of a kindly, homely ex-officer who adores her but whom she doesn't love. The whole long scene is an extraordinary crescendo of desperation.

The last act sees the soldiers ordered to leave town, Masha distraught, Irina's fiance killed in a duel and oldest sister Olga settled into the position of school headmistress, a job she swore she'd never take. It isn't quite tragedy. The cheerful sound of the departing military band spurs Olga to deliver the most measured of the play's many paeans to a happier and less constricting future, and Irina declares her intention to stop feeling sorry for herself and work. That's half the picture. The other half shows brother Andrei pushing a pram, containing a child that probably isn't his, as well as an old army doctor, far gone in vodka and nihilism, declaring everything meaningless.

This production keeps an exemplary balance, with Kelli Fox possibly the best and certainly the strongest Olga I have ever seen. She makes solid sense of every line she utters (a quality not universally shared by her colleagues) and her every reaction is a full one.

It's a truism that any good production of Chekhov makes certain lines strike you as if you'd never heard them before, though I sometimes wonder if this is as true for Russian audiences as for us foreigners, who are continually being exposed to new translations. Susan Coyne's version at Niagara strikes the occasional false note, but it is also full of perfectly chosen revelatory phrases, simple words in which the characters give themselves away for all time. Andrei (magnificently played by Ben Carlson) tries, in that incendiary third act, to tell his sisters what a wonderful woman his wife, Natasha, is. It's an uphill job, because he's lying, to them and to himself. Finally his shame overwhelms him -- "my dear sisters, my sweet sisters, don't believe me, don't" -- and he leaves the stage in something between tears and a cold sweat. Kulygin, the outclassed schoolmaster, says, "I love Masha, she's very special" -- nice greeting-card sentiments to be sure, but he's talking about his wife. And never before have I really appreciated Masha's refusal to enter the house that was her family's inheritance but that has now been colonized by her sister-in-law.

Olga begins the play with a calm celebration of Irina's birthday, and this too carries a lot of weight. The assured staging directs our eye to the happiness at the centre of the stage, and it takes a few moments to register the presence of Masha at the side, with discontent visibly gnawing. After this, the production takes something of a dive, with much of the acting becoming generalized, seemingly aimed more at creating a "Chekhovian" mood -- hesitant enthusiasm undercut by nostalgia -- than at transmitting specific meaning.

Chekhov's characters, especially his men, are notoriously prone to "philosophize" and to mock themselves for doing so, but maybe we should give them more credit for meaning what they say when they say it. Caroline Cave's Irina only achieves full definition when her world falls apart; her understated grief is very well handled. The attraction between Masha and Vershinin (Kevin Bundy) becomes an incoherent subplot; he barely emerges from the uniformed ranks around him.

Tara Rosling's performance of Masha is anchored by her delivery of a snatch of rhyme, idly repeated when first we meet her, desperately reprised at the close, and equally striking at both ends. The abandon of her parting with her lover is very fine, as is the devastation that follows it, and the deadly accuracy with which she lets a laugh break through her torpor when her husband (Douglas E. Hughes) tries to amuse her by putting on a false beard. What passes between these poles, though, is a blur.

The relationship of Andrei and Natasha (Fiona Byrne) comes over, by contrast, as sharp as a boxful of tacks. Cruelly, Chekhov shows us its best and worst moments in direct succession. At the end of Act One Andrei proposes, and at the start of Act Two they are parents, and Natasha is well down the road from shy to shrew.

What sets the production unshakably on its feet is the fight between Natasha and Olga -- insofar as Olga can fight -- over the fate of the superannuated nanny, Anfisa. It's a clash of two world views. Anfisa herself cowers between them, in yet another of Jennifer Phipps' uncannily complete embodiments of a tiny role.

Then comes the first of the parade of self-scourgings: the doctor's, delivered by David Schurmann. This actor's voice is ineradicably placid, more than one expects in this role, and you can feel him placing his words, but he is very powerful, and it's good to be reminded that the doctor was a gentleman before he was a bum. In the last act he gives some curt advice to Andrei, then, as always, gets distracted. You find yourself wishing, for both their sakes, that he could have said more.

Sue LePage's sets, filling the wide stage, make every moment look good. They also echo the play's increasing harshness. The last scene, the only exterior, makes us feel the town's isolation, and its imminent desertion, in our bones. It's only autumn, but there's a cold wind blowing through the farewells. The end, by drying its own tears, should bring on yours: not floods, perhaps, but well- tempered drops. This has been a very good first week.
