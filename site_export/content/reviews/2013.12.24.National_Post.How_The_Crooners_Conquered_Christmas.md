It is, as Andy Williams really should have sung, the Most Repetitive Time of the Year: the time when the same, not necessarily good, old songs go into rapid rotation in every coffee-shop and supermarket. The time when an ersatz carol like The Little Drummer Boy goes around (and around, and around) long after outstaying any welcome it may once have had. The time when Bing Crosby singing White Christmas becomes inescapable. Except that it doesn't, not Bing's recording or anyone else's. Maybe the places I frequent have atypical tastes in muzak, but this year I can't recall hearing it anywhere. Maybe it doesn't like the company it would be keeping. Maybe the programmers feel that it would be just too familiar, though that doesn't seem to stop them when it comes to an ersatz carol like The Little Drummer Boy or even a real one like Silent Night, whose ubiquity has given it the status of an honorary pop song. Or maybe I'm just imagining things. Real or imagined, the absence of White Christmas has set me thinking about what a good, if taken for granted, song it actually is.

Irving Berlin of course wrote it, and Crosby introduced it in the 1942 movie Holiday Inn, a film built around the conceit of a hotel open only on public holidays and staging a separate floorshow for each one of them. This obliged, or enabled, Berlin to come up with themesongs for Lincoln's birthday (Abraham, an earnest anthem of emancipation with Bing in blackface, God forgive him) and for Washington's (Fred Astaire dancing around a cherrytree while cheerfully singing that he can't tell a lie - about being in love). There were also numbers about starting the New Year right and about having plenty to be thankful for (at Thanksgiving).

For Berlin, whose business instincts were as sharp as his verbal and musical ones, the assignment must have seemed heaven sent: an opportunity to write multiple songs with annually recurring hooks. As it turned out, only three of them caught on, One was Easter Parade, recycled from a Broadway revue of a decade before; another was Be Careful, It's My Heart, on which you have to concentrate hard to realize that it's about Valentine's Day; and the third was White Christmas, which also had originally been written for a revue, but an unproduced one, and is one of the soberest celebratory songs ever written. That's its genius and the source, however unexamined, of its lasting appeal. Well, that and Berlin's almost unfailing rightness in matching words to music, so that everything sings naturally, nothing sounds forced. There's an especial grace in the way this song ends, not repeating its title exactly but re-ordering it: "May all your Christmases be white." It's a hope, not a guarantee, sung by a character who at that point in the movie is having a down moment.

As it happens, Crosby's performance in the film, like his own and most other singers' subsequent recordings, is textually incomplete. The full version has an introductory verse:

The sun is shining, The grass is green, The orange and palm trees sway.

There's never been such a dayIn Beverly Hills, L.A. But it 's December the twenty-fourth, And I am longing to be up north.

That localizes and individualizes the song, which dramatically is the right thing to do. But it also diminishes it, makes it less everybody's story. And of course it was wartime, when loss and longing were everybody's story, even if they were spending Christmas at home, even if it were snowing. Even without a war, there's enough yearning in most people's lives for the song to touch a chord. We may think of it as aural greeting-card, but that isn't how we feel it. Cutting the verse was a canny decision, commercially and even artistically.

Curiously, the same thing had already happened in reverse with the other enduring anthem to come out of a Hollywood musical, an immediately pre-war one, The Wizard of Oz.

Over the Rainbow also had a verse, one that generalized the individual yearnings of the girl in the song into a philosophical statement about what happens "when all the world is a hopeless

jumble." In cutting it, the producers, or whoever was responsible, made the song all about Dorothy, eventually all about Judy Garland. Over the Rainbow achieved immortality by emphasizing the personal over the universal, White Christmas by emphasizing the universal over the personal. Each was right for its moment, onscreen and off, and, as it's turned out, for all subsequent moments.

The melancholy - or, heard another way, the dogged against-the-odds optimism - that distinguishes White Christmas also marks the other great seasonal song to come out of wartime Hollywood. I recently saw Have

Yourself a Merry Little Christmas described, disparagingly, as the most depressing of all Christmas songs. The complainant should have heard the lyric that Garland in Meet Me in St. Louis was originally required to sing to her kid sister as they faced being uprooted from their home city: "Have yourself a merry little Christmas, it may be your last ..." She protested that audiences would hate her if they heard her singing that to a child, so the song was rewritten. It still sounds bittersweet: "Some day soon we all may be together if the fates allow/Until then we' ll have to muddle through somehow ..." For commercial (i.e. non-cinematic) publication, that last line was changed to the anodyne "hang a shining star upon the highest bough" and "may" became "will," and that's how the song has mostly been sung since, transforming something touching and individual into just another Christmas number. Hugh Martin and Ralph Blane, who wrote it, were not, as far as I know, Jewish. This of course differentiates them from most classic American songwriters and especially, it seems, from those who wrote Christmas songs. If, as has been claimed, the 19th-century conception of Christmas was invented by Dickens, then the twentieth-century idea might be credited to Berlin.

It's a secular version, of course, though it couldn't exist without some taken-forgranted religious underpinnings. (And White Christmas itself is practically a hymn compared to Easter Parade, in which the holiday is a fashion show.) It's also, though very exportable, an American version. Berlin, the five-year-old Russian immigrant, came by his patriotism naturally; it always sounds honest, which is why it never sounds embarrassing. He wrote, sung, about America even when it wasn't his explicit subject. White Christmas does mention sleigh-bells and glistening treetops, but its central image is that of a man on his own writing Christmas cards and dreaming of family. It's about being lonely but linked; which is a very American thing. And a very wartime thing.

It's significant that none of the enduring American Christmas songs date from before World War Two. The post-war songs, exploiting the market that Berlin opened up, have been loaded with the seasonal cliches that he, and Blane and Martin, avoided. Mel Torme's Christmas Song ("Chestnuts roasting on an open fire") is a catalogue of them, though musically superior and with the grace to be mildly deprecatory about itself. Mitchell Parish's words to Sleigh Ride ("there's a birthday party at the home of Farmer Grey...") - a winter rather than a Christmas song but they're all grist to the seasonal mill - trades on the audience's nostalgia for an Arcadia that never existed. Rudolph the Red Nosed Reindeer (by Johnny Marks, if anyone cares) at least has an original story idea which may explain its survival in the face of words and music of surpassing banality. Though maybe they helped.

Did no writer in the great tradition ever protest? One did: Yip Harburg, the Wizard of Oz lyricist and a Hollywood blacklist victim, wrote a 1951 Broadway show entitled Flahooley, a fantastical satire on consumerism and ... well, just about everything, really. It included a choral number called Sing the Merry: a mock-carol in which the cash-registers musically jingled while the lyrics rhymed off the names of New York's biggest stores, culminating in the exhortation to "for Christ's sake, give Christmas back to Christ."

That final line, penned by a self-proclaimed Jewish atheist, was cut from the stage production. The entire number was cut from the cast album. It seems that all the best Christmas songs have been subject to some kind of censorship, some from their authors.

Of course there are other holidays to be sung about, once this one is over. Frank Loesser, another great wordsand-music man (he wrote Guys and Dolls), knew about that. Baby, it's cold outside. What are you doing New Year's Eve?
