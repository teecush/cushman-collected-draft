A CHRISTMAS CAROL

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

John Huston -- not that John Huston, but another -- is a Saskatchewan actor and director much given to solo performance. His one-man version of Charles Dickens' A Christmas Carol puts him in the line of the late Emlyn Williams, whose evenings of Dickensian recitation were among his greatest successes, and ultimately of Dickens himself, who in his later years gave hugely successful public readings from his works. Unfortunately, the nervous energy he expended on these shows -- he used to drain both himself and his audience with the murder of Nancy from Oliver Twist -- took so much out of him that it may have hastened his death.

Huston's performance is unlikely to get anyone's pulse racing to that fatal extent, but it is an accomplished recital that tightens its grip as the story proceeds. Huston presents us with a costumed approximation of one of Dickens' own personal appearances; we might well be in a draughty hall in the Toronto that he visited on one of his American tours. He faces us across a lectern, bearded, frock- coated, watch-chained and throwing himself enthusiastically into every character who crosses his path.

Slight of build and light of voice, Huston is not the force of nature that presumably was Dickens. He can be fussy in his accumulation of comic detail: sometimes, it seems, assuming as many as half a dozen different voices in the course of a single sentence.

Piling on the humour, he sometimes defuses it, missing out on both the irony and the absurdity. There is nothing especially striking in his characterisation of Scrooge himself; and though there may be no positive reasons against endowing the Spirit of Christmas Present with a strong northern English accent, I cannot see much in its favour either.

It may be, too, that we have had enough over the years of the Carol's more emphatically benevolent characters, its Fezziwigs and Cratchits. The Cratchits' highly calorific Christmas merry-making is actually less effective now than its attendant melodrama, centring on the figure of Tiny Tim. In this account, he is very tiny; Huston indicates his presence by lowering his hand to a couple of feet above the floor. You feel his father's attachment to him, and you understand exactly why Scrooge melts.

Huston doesn't aim for the euphoric heights scaled on film by the late Alastair Sim (who was wonderful at this point, if a somewhat unlikely miser earlier on) but he does very well. And God knows he has stamina.
