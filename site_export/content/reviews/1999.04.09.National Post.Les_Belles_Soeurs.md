LES BELLES SOEURS

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Thirty years ago, Michel Tremblay's Les Belles Soeurs, a group picture of working-class French-Canadian women in a Montreal apartment block, took the Quebec theatre by storm. It subsequently repeated this feat across the country, and now it has been revived, in English, by Canadian Stage. It's possible, with some historical imagination, to appreciate the impact it must have had. Less easy is to accept it now as entertainment.

At the centre of the play is Germaine, who has won a million trading stamps in the lottery, and now enlists her relatives and neighbours to help her paste them into the appropriate little books.

Her helpers are jealous of her good fortune and -- in the intervals of bickering, complaining, and even the odd bout of shared cheerfulness -- they start pocketing the stamps themselves, some with more misgivings than others. Germaine stumbles onto what is happening and unleashes her fury on everyone, including the two or three who tried to put her wise.

The play reaches its climax in a maelstrom of rage, recrimination, and -- the evening's recurring note -- self- righteousness. Tremblay may, as has been asserted, love these women, but nobody would accuse him of sentimentalizing them.

But maybe we should. Rightly, he exposes the drudgery of their lives: an unending round of chores performed for ungrateful children and exploitative men. He also shows their narrowness: the way in which they suffer under stifling social and religious conventions, while turning savagely on any of their number -- an unmarried sister who has dared to set foot in a nightclub, the hostess-prostitute who hardly ever gets out of it, the younger generation in tow -- who threaten to escape.

But his principal method of doing this is to line them up and have them express their grievances in unison. You almost expect them to put on expressionist masks, as befits choral representatives of a social class. (It's alarming to find this supposed new dawn in Canadian drama parading techniques that the rest of the world had outgrown 30 years earlier.) The devices undoubtedly make their point but they do so at the cost of patronizing the characters. This applies in the jollier moments. A maiden lady tells a mildly salacious joke, and we are asked to laugh, not at the joke itself, but at the characters for being titillated by it.

The whole cast erupts into a well-drilled song and dance in tribute to the joys of bingo, whose effect is less celebration than deprecation. Or you may think of it as a detachable bit from an unwritten musical, noting that the cutest high-kicker is a demented 90-year old whose principal function elsewhere is to be repeatedly bopped on the head by an exasperated daughter-in-law.

Tremblay orchestrates his effects -- and an ensemble of 15 characters -- deftly: This was his first play and a considerable technical achievement. Nor is its subject matter all that dated; some things may have moved on but the cruelties and hypocrisies are still with us.

Germaine has two harridan sisters, and a third, named Pierette, routinely referred to as "that whore," who pays a visit (in full tart's gear - a surprise if she's looking for a welcome in this censorious quarter), befriends a tearful pregnant friend of Germaine's daughter, and steers her toward an abortion. Unfortunately, when Tremblay is not writing barbed group scenes, his characters expose their true suffering selves in sensitive soliloquies that force the actors into taking portentous pauses before significant words. (People baring their souls in real life never, ever, talk like that.)

There is, of course, a problem of language. The play was originally acclaimed for its innovative use of Quebecois slang, and the flavour naturally evaporates when it goes into standard English. On the simplest level, vive le Bingo has a zing that "long live bingo" undeniably lacks. I can't think of any solution to this, except perhaps to put the whole thing into Irish. (A couple of the cast did seem to be leaning in this direction.)

And the presentational devices that might have seemed so new, now need recharging. In fact, one would like to let a director as imaginative and as culturally compatible as Robert Lepage loose on this play. The present staging is by Denise Filiatrault, a veteran of the first production, and I would guess that her production is an echo of the original.

The actors go through the more stylized passages with routine devotion, but left to themselves they relax into unsteady naturalism. This means a lot of shouting. Janet Wright, as Germaine, goes through the first 10 minutes of the play on a single strident note, and though she is more palatable when she gets a chance to relax, this doesn't happen often. Most of the others get their turn, with varying degrees of authority. The only one who surprises us is Rose (Nicola Cavendish), the most monstrously unforgiving of the sisters.

After demonizing unwed mothers, and arraigning French films for being "too real" and "too exaggerated," she tells us about her life with the man to whom she said "yes," and who has been legally forcing himself on her ever since.

The imposing set, by Andre Barbe, takes an entire tenement facade and tilts it at a perilously fascinating angle.
