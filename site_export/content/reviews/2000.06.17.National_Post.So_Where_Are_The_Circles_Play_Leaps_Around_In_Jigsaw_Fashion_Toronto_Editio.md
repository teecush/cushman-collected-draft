Circle of Love plays tonight at 8 p.m. ($12 to $15) and tomorrow at 2:30 p.m. (PWYC) at Theatre Passe Muraille, 16 Ryerson Ave. Call (416) 504-7529.

The problem with Circle of Love is one that besets many first plays; the author, writing on subjects close to his own heart and presumably to his own experience, is so caught up in them that he assumes an equal fascination on the part of his audience. It doesn't happen.

Al-Noor Peera's play is set among first- and second-generation Indian immigrants in Toronto. There is Ishwar, a businessman, liberal in his politics (he's an obsessive Trudeau fan) and repressive in his domestic dealings, and Nigar, his submissive homemaking wife. Then there's their son Jamil, who's a student, and who has a gay friend, Rafiq, and a girlfriend, Zuhra, whom he impregnates and, at least for a time, abandons.

It's difficult to know where to start or to finish in detailing the plot because the playwright never quite lets on where the beginning is, while even the end seems more like a comma than a full stop. He tells his story in jigsaw fashion, constantly leaping backwards and forwards in time, bleeding one scene into another. He does this skilfully, and the transitions are fluently handled in Layne Coleman's production. This, though, is about the only saving grace of either the writing or the presentation.

At first, we seem to be watching an intergenerational conflict drama, indistinguishable from a hundred others except by the flowing drapes at the rear of the stage and the sitar music in the background. A few specialized references ("biryani again?") provide a mildly exotic laugh for half the audience and, probably, a ruefully reassuring one for the other half. Things then narrow down a bit. Ishwar turns out to have strictly patriarchal views on the place of a wife, and even of a prospective daughter-in-law. When Jamil brings Zuhra home for an inspection, she declares her expectations that her husband will do his share of the housework, take her to the ballet and provide her with romantic holidays ("in Europe ... and Muskoka"). Nigar leaves the room in silent protest, although whether because she is really shocked or because that's what her husband expects of her remains unclear. This scene, or at least Zuhra's part of it, is the brightest of the evening, but it still seems overly emphatic. She is being tactless so that the author can make a point.

In fact, all the characters are puppets, and little of what they say carries any conviction. Jamil, who seems to be the central figure -- the only one who gets to interact at length with each of the other four -- is an especially loquacious blank. His problem, he tells us, is that he grew up in thrall to his mother, and so is unable to cope with adult relationships, but this isn't dramatized; the scenes he actually has with his mother don't suggest it in the least. (He's also visited, at moments of stress, by frighteningly acute stomach cramps.) He expresses envy of his gay pal Rafiq's promiscuity: a rather slender excuse for protracted conversations of doubtful relevance. The range of cultural reference is refreshingly wide -- from Andrew Marvell's To His Coy Mistress to The Price Is Right -- but the dialogue is clogged and awkward. Male characters, in particular, express their antagonisms by lobbing elaborate metaphors back and forth. It smothers conflict; it also strangles belief.

The production, meanwhile, is lamentable, the actors behaving, as usual in such circumstances, with a sort of reverential defensiveness. They are all Equity members, but they come off like amateurs. The performances are not only low-key -- that can sometimes be very effective as Not Faust is triumphantly demonstrating -- but low-energy, which is fatal. The men -- Ishwar Mooljee, Vijay Mehta, Anand Rajaram -- speak in various tones of apologetic mutter. The women have rather more life, with Misha Vellani as a downtrodden mother with some troubling moments and Goldy Notay conscientiously spunky as Zuhra, though somewhat weighed down by having to express all the right sentiments; she is an assertive victim -- a combination that can certainly exist in real life but needs a few more connections than are supplied here. The underlying pattern seems to be that two generations of straight men are, in some sense, killing their women; but it's all very amorphous.

The author claims in a program note that he "wanted to explore memory." But this would only work if we were clear who was doing the remembering. The actual experience of the play is not of watching people alternately releasing and repressing what's inside them; it's of listening to an author who has all the information but is gratuitously capricious about the order in which he lets it out. Give him his due: Corny though the situations seem, we do go on wanting to know. But then we all have a built-in desire to be told a story, and it takes a lot to discourage us.
