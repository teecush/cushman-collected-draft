There is, despite its title, nothing arboreal about Through the Leaves. The leaves in question are the pages of a diary kept by Martha, one of the play's two characters. Martha is the owner and proprietress of a Bavarian butcher's store or, since she specializes, offal emporium. In her early fifties and unmarried, she starts a relationship with Otto, a factory worker for whom the word "sexist" might have been invented. Younger than her, though not by much, he behaves as though middle age were a pitiable condition peculiar to women. He exploits Martha, sexually and otherwise, and absents himself from her life and her bed for long, unexplained periods, but despite his best or worst efforts, it wouldn't be true to say that he humiliates her. It may be a close call, but the one thing he cannot violate is her intelligence.

The play dates from 1981. Franz Xaver Kroetz, who wrote it, first became famous in the 1980s for a stark series of plays about contemporary German peasantry. (One of them, Stallerhof, is a masterpiece.) They too depicted people grabbing at love, or at least sex, or at least human contact, in circumstances that make those depicted in Through the Leaves seem positively idyllic. At least this one takes place indoors, in Martha's shop and its attached living quarters. (There's a bedroom upstairs, but we never visit it; and Otto doesn't seem too keen on going there either.) It's quite comfortable, as Martha is running a fairly successful business. It's one of the things about her that Otto resents. Another is her beloved dog, whose yapping at intimate moments puts him off his stride. Remembering the well-known Chekhov doctrine -- "never bring a gun onstage unless you intend to use it" -- you may well shudder when Otto, given the run of Martha's premises, starts brandishing a meat cleaver.

The play is brought to us by Company Theatre, in a debut production by Philip Riccio, one of their two artistic directors. More modest in scale and range than this group's previous triumphs, Festen and A Whistle in the Dark, it's up to the same standards of detail and intensity. On Martha's and Otto's first date, or at least the first time she invites Otto over, she feeds him caviar, which he greets with something between suspicion and contempt. That sets the tone. She's always making gestures, or making allowances, without quite succeeding in fooling herself. Maria Vacratsis, in a performance that's at once full-bodied and self-effacing, keeps the balance superbly. We feel for her, but we never despair for her. "Where did it all go wrong?" she asks in her diary, of a liaison that, as far as we can tell, never went right. Those diary entries link the play. She reads them to us at the beginning, at the end, and between the scenes, complete with punctuation: "open brackets ... exclamation marks...close brackets." That semi-comic device helps distance them without, in any sense, alienating us. One of the achievements of the play and of the performance is that, though we are told the story in Martha's words, we don't have to see it through her eyes. We can realize, intellectually, that losing Otto would be the best thing that could happen to her -- so, at some level, does she -- but we can feel the toll it would take. When Vacratsis refers to "my Otto" it sometimes sounds as if she's talking about a car, but otherwise she's perfect.

She isn't, thankfully, a victim. It's likelier that he is: not of her, but of a social and sexual code that's left him adrift in a changing world. Nicholas Campbell's performance, equallyunsparing in its own way, never courts sympathy, but makes us vividly aware of the pressures on Otto, bragging of past and present sexual successes that probably don't exist. When Martha wants to go to a costume ball, he insists on paying for the tickets, because that's what the man does, and then grumbles about the whole experience. Having paid that token debt to chivalry, he settles down to battening off his lady-friend, resenting her offer of a job, accepting her bed and board without actually committing to them, mocking her aging body while availing himself of it, though not as often, and certainly not as tenderly, as she would like. Play and production make painfully clear that, physically and emotionally she wants him more than he wants her, and that they both lose by it. There's one explicit sex scene in the play, and it's crude but clothed: indeed, crude because clothed. Here the production contrasts with one in London a few years ago that did have nudity, and also had Simon Callow as Otto: aggressively miscast in a working-class role and compensating with studied brutality and a touch of the grotesque. That gave the play more suspense than it has here, but less inexorability. Here things work out, in terms of emotion if not of incident, as you know they'll have to.

John Thompson has designed a brightly lit set in which the clinical butcher's store at the back provides a striking ironic counterpoint to the spar-tan domestic chintz that occupies what's left. There's something tragic in the air here, at least in textbook terms: a lot of pity, a little terror and no sentimentality. (Martha can be sentimental, but the play isn't; besides, she sees through herself.) The play's view of life may be offal, but it's red meat.
