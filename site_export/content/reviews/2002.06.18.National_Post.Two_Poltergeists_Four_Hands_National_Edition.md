THE TURN OF THE SCREW

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

Henry James, that most resistible of great novelists (O the thinness of blood! O the long parenthesis-jammed sentences! O look who's talking!), had a relationship with the theatre that was loving but strained. He was a fan and he wrote plays of his own, all flops. But there have been many successful dramatizations of his novels and stories by other hands. The shorter ones, naturally, have been the more amenable, and The Turn of the Screw, that ineffably chilling haunted-house novella, has inspired several plays, at least one first-rate film and a Benjamin Britten opera.

A sheltered young governess, toward the end of the 19th century, is dispatched by a suave swell to his house outside London in order to take charge of his young niece and nephew. One of the terms of employment is that she shall take all responsibility for them, and never trouble him in any way.

The children turn out to be beautiful and charming. The only worrying factor is that the older of the two, Miles, has just been expelled from school for reasons that even his headmasters letter leaves uncomfortably obscure. The girl, Flora, is too young to have been sent away, and besides, in those days, girls werent.

The governess, whose name we never learn, sees two sinister figures around the place, appearing and disappearing, and she learns from the housekeeper, Mrs. Grose, that these must be her predecessor, Miss Jessel, and the master's former valet, Peter Quint, who had been lovers and who had apparently exercised an unhealthy influence on the children. To make things worse, both Quint and Miss Jessel are dead. The governess is convinced they have returned from beyond the grave to complete their corruption of Miles and Flora. All ends horrifically.

It is possible to read the book on two consecutive occasions and come to two diametrically opposed conclusions. The first is that the governess, who is also the narrator, really sees what she says she sees and is correct in her interpretation of it. The other is that she is a hysteric, besotted with the handsome employer, whom she only met twice, and determined to bring herself to his attention. She imagines the spirits (or, as she comes to think of them, demons) and misinterprets everything the children say and do, overrating their innocence and inventing their fall. Horrified by any manifestation of sexuality, she calls Quint a hound (not to mention a "base menial") and Miss Jessel a fiend, without actually knowing the first thing about either of them. The housekeeper who seems to be corroborating her suspicions, and whom she patronizes as an ignorant but honest soul, is actually just humouring her.

Keeping this uncertainty going throughout is a high achievement on James's part. I find his achingly fastidious style, which never makes a statement without qualifying it seven ways, a form of elegant hell, but I have to admit that he does actually achieve the subtleties he aims for. When a climax comes he is able to present it with comparative simplicity and to shattering effect. Reproducing this theatrically is, of course, a severe challenge.

Jeffrey Hatchers version, bought to Toronto by Theatre Kingston, has been praised (by its director) for preserving the ambiguities that previous versions have sacrificed. You could have fooled me. The governesss interview here ends with the employer silkily enquiring, "Have I seduced you?" This is an elaboration and a coarsening of a word used glancingly in the novel, and the motif recurs throughout the script. The governess's neurosis is stripped pretty bare in the play, and she is far more explicit about what the ghosts actually want of the children than her fictional inspiration ever was.

Meanwhile, Hatcher has jettisoned James's dialogue almost completely. His own substitute chat includes a long series of riddle games, not as tantalizing as they are obviously meant to be. He varies the story and even adds a coda, telling us -- as James never did -- what happened to the characters afterwards. (It isnt good news.)

It is probably impossible to put this material on a stage without coarsening it, and Hatcher has done a reasonable job, but where James created suspense, he creates impatience. We dont revel in the build-up; we just want to cut to the chase.

There is one central device that bestows a kind of claustrophobic sheen. The cast is confined to two actors. Michelle Mallen plays the governess, and gives a conscientious performance that sometimes buckles; at moments of severest crisis her passion descends into generalized urgency, as if she were defending herself against charges that nobody has actually made. Craig Walker, who is also the director, plays all the other roles: man, woman or child.

Actually he does one of each, with no change of costume; voice and stance are all. First he is the employer. Then he is the housekeeper, whom he plays as an old Scots biddy out of some old BBC television series, a choice that adds a note of feyness to the character who least needs it. From this he leaps back and forth into the character of young Miles, whom he plays uncannily well.

Little Flora we never see -- she is an invisible presence whom we imagine trailing along holding one of her elders' hands -- and we never hear her either; she is said to have been struck dumb by the traumatic events that preceded the play. This is not in the book. It's presumably been done in the interests of theatrical economy, and in the hope that it may provide some additional spookiness on the side.

But it's a mistake. We need to believe in the house and the children as real and normal if we are to be appalled by their supernatural takeover. A book can more easily get the best of both worlds, this and the next; a play really has to work at it.
