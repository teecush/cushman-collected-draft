On my left a green patio, on my right a yellow kitchen, each of them occupied by a woman dressed to match. Between them the facade of a house, picked out in blue with square black holes for windows. Above and around a dark sky decorated with fluffy white clouds, the contrast may be indicating that happiness is possible but may also be illusory.

Rose Cullis's play is called The Happy Woman, and taking into account the expected doses of irony, the decor is very appropriate. It's also very stylish, which befits a script that's accomplished, penetrating but somewhat frustrating. The title figure, though not exactly the central one (this is part of the frustration), is the yellow lady, Margaret, a widow who reveres the memory of her recently departed: a reverence not shared by others, notably her grown-up son and daughter. Barbara Gordon plays Margaret: providential casting since no actress is better, or even conceivably as good, at probing the horizons of the upper-middle-aged middlemiddle-class. Margaret is sunny without being silly, but she is only able to maintain her serene practicality by shutting out unwelcome facts.

Her polar opposite is her daughter Cassie, a barmaid and performance artist (not, she insists and despite her brother's insinuations, a stripper), who wears her heart not so much on her sleeve as on her entire body. As played by Maev Beaty, she's alight with wit, warmth, pain and anger; this actress's ability to take instant command of both stage and character has never been more electrically on display. Cassie is widely suspected of carrying on with the married man across the street, an allegation that shocks her mother though, where her daughter's demons are concerned, she doesn't know the half. In the play's showpiece scene, Cassie confronts Margaret with things that have been going on in her own life and under their own roof. Interestingly, Margaret is shocked but not outraged. Her composure cracks, but not irreparably.

The play is not, though, just about the two of them. There's Christian, Cassie's brother, whose wife Stasia is terrified that she is about to give birth to a monster and, after delivery, is convinced that she's done so. There's some scarifying writing here; scarifying acting, too, by Ingrid Rae Doucet. The remaining character is the woman in green, named (in the program, though mercifully seldom in the text) BellaDonna, and appropriately venomous. She, too, is perfectly played by Maria Vacratsis, sitting on her porch and commenting, with censorious prurience, on the impending end of the world and the assorted fecklessness of her neighbours. She may have a point, and she certainly seems to enjoy making it. She could be a happy woman, too. Indeed, the play's title might be generic, with an implied question mark on the end; Cassie may be en route to her own kind of happiness, and even Stasia gets some, at a price. There's a pattern here, but there isn't much of a climax and the strands are only loosely tied together. The link between all the women is Christian (the name presumably means something) but the play doesn't seem much interested in him, or in men generally. Martin Happer plays him strongly, but is principally confined to a note of vexed cheerfulness. Whatever: Kelly Thornton's production is the second consecutive triumph, following The Penelopiad, for her and for Nightwood Theatre, while the colour co-ordination of Denyse Karn's set and costumes takes in the smallest details. The play does haunt the mind, and may even invade your dreams. The work of a new playwright (to me, anyway), it stands out in a season that's been notably short on good new plays.

A comparable stand-out in its way is The Never Ending Story, adapted and directed by David S. Craig from the children's classic by Michael Ende. The adventure story itself is fine enough, centring on Adamo Ruggiero as an engaging boy-hero and featuring a throng of actors in multiple roles among whom Charlotte Moore is a sequential delight. But what really counts is the involvement of the bullied boy Bastian, played with wonderful freshness and conviction by Natasha Greenblatt, who steals the story from a mysterious bookstore and finds himself progressively drawn into it. It's an essay on imagination that only disappoints at the close; this never-ending story ends all too abruptly. Up until then, it's a resourcefully staged delight. It may not be as spectacular as War Horse, but it's every bit as stirring. And its horse talks.
