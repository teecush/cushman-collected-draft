The first thing to be said of the Shaw Festivals production of My Fair Lady is that it's a very good production of Pygmalion. That isn't the back-hander it might seem. A lot of Shaws play remains in the Lerner and Loewe musical, however trimmed and romanticized. The romance, in fact, was there all the time, just waiting to be brought forward - which is one reason the show is so strong; it establishes its own identity while respecting that of its source. In this revival, Deborah Hay as Eliza Doolittle and Benedict Campbell as Professor Higgins do especially well at exploring the penultimate stage of their relationship: when she declares her independence from him and he grudgingly admits to having become attached to her. Theres much backand-forth role reversal in this scene, and both actors explore and expose every moment of it. They do so, incidentally, more economically than would be possible in the play, which at the equivalent point has the two characters going round and round in repetitive circles.

Hay's Eliza is enchanting throughout, and a more credible denizen of the Covent Garden flower market than most. She gives very good, very funny and sensitive cockney, and her performance of Wouldn't It Be Loverly is, well, loverly; the staging involves her being pulled around in a barrow thats an impromptu carriage, from which she transmits a delightful mixture of pride and apprehension. Rather the same qualities infuse her first entry into the Higgins home asking for speech lessons; shes on her best behaviour and on her guard. There's a superb moment at which, battered by his brusqueness, she not only threatens to leave but makes, wounded, for the door. The lessons themselves could hardly be more amusing or more touching, and her timing and phrasing of the transplanted tea party scene (which Kenneth Tynan rightly called Shaw's peak of pure, as opposed to applied, comedy") are gorgeous. At the ball shes a vision, and she pretty much remains so. The musical Eliza is a terror of a role, especially for a nonBritish actress: Shes required to get two English accents exactly right as that's what the story's about, to act a full-blown Shavian heroine and, especially in the later stages, to soar in song like Julie Andrews. Hay doesnt quite bring off the last part; I Could Have Danced All Night and the climax of Without You are a bit of a strain. But shes a believable, irresistible woman and that's what counts. It's probably heretical but I loved her more in this role than I did in Born Yesterday.

Campbell breaks with the tradition established by Rex Harrison and followed by all subsequent Higginses by singing rather than talking almost every line of his songs. Ive often wondered what the melody line of Why Cant the English might be; now I know. The treatment reveals an unsuspected astringency in the music, and in no way weakens the wit of the lyrics. In fact, Campbell acts Higgins more pointedly in the songs than in some of the dialogue, where he tends to play the man's rudeness as calculated rather than automatic. He has, though, a fascinating look. He wears glasses almost throughout, which at first suggest scholastic gentleness but turn out to be a defence against feeling. The one and only time he takes them off is in the last section of I've Grown Accustomed to Her Face, his one and only love song; and its like a thunderclap of vulnerability. (He replaces them immediately afterwards: another kind of thunderclap.)

Molly Smiths production perpetuates some of the American musical's sentimental misconceptions about the English class system: that a professor of phonetics would live in a mansion with an entire brigade of liveried servants, and that London's lower orders would exist in a perpetual state of jolly costermongering that isnt even laid on for the benefit of the tourists. The first of these stereotypes is built inescapably into the show (though not the play); the second doesnt have to be. Nor does the musical need not one but two jolly cockney knees-ups. Neil Barclay, who as Alfred Doolittle is at the centre of them, certainly doesn't; he sings with point and gusto, dances with the delicacy of great bulk, and in speech conveys the less attractive aspects of Eliza's absentee dad without rubbing our faces in them.

Daniel Pelzigs choreography is actually most valuable not in the big concerted numbers but in the way it navigates the actors through the smaller ones. The Rain in Spain regains the joyous impact that this impromptu tango must have had in the far-off days when nobody knew it was coming. The Ascot Gavotte has gone Latin in less felicitous ways, since the female members of the Edwardian aristocracy seem from their headgear to be prophetically channelling Carmen Miranda. Another oddity of Judith Bowdens costuming is that Higgins, admittedly a social eccentric, appears to be attending the embassy ball in his very best dressing gown. At home he lives, courtesy of Ken MacDonald's sets, surrounded by giant symbolic bird cages, in addition to the tiny real one mandated by the text. It's a striking and not unhelpful environment, though the recurring projections of actual birds in flight seem excessive, at least in a production as otherwise straightforward as this one.

Theres gracious support from Sharry Fletts Mrs. Higgins, Patrick Galligan's Colonel Pickering and from Mark Uhre's Freddy, who sings his one song beautifully and is hardly called on to do much else. The show's vexed ending seems here not so much ambiguous as undecided. For the overwhelming majority of the time, though, this is a touching, intelligent and generally magical evening.
