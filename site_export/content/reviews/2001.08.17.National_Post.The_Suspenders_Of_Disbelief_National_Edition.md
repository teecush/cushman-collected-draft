INHERIT THE WIND

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

I have never previously seen or read Inherit the Wind, but you develop a feel for these things. I arrived at Stratford expecting something gripping but hollow, and that is what I got.

The play is a 50-year-old dramatization, by one of those anonymous teams of Broadway writers, of a crucial trial in the American South of the 1920s -- one that pitted the advocates of Darwinian evolution against the defenders of Biblical creationism. A teacher, accused of contravening state law by mentioning Darwin in the classroom, found himself in court with his pious fellow citizens baying for his blood, or at least for a good stiff jail sentence. The teacher's name was John Scopes, and his case became famous as "the Tennessee (or the Scopes) monkey trial."

Prosecuting him, to the applause of the town, was William Jennings Bryan, three-time presidential candidate (unsuccessful) and a passionate fundamentalist. Ranged against him was the legendary Clarence Darrow, champion of unpopular causes, fresh from defending the accused in the sensational Leopold-Loeb murder case. The fate of the accused became overshadowed by the courtroom duel between these two men.

Some of the dialogue of Inherit the Wind is drawn directly from the court-record but the names - of the characters and even of the town itself - have been changed. That is the measure of how compromised and superficial the play is. It wants the privileges of fiction and the benefits of documentary without committing itself to either.

Darrow is renamed Henry Drummond, Bryan becomes William Harrison Brady. By any names, they are a couple of extremely juicy roles, and the performances here, by William Hutt and James Blendick, are super. Hutt gives Drummond an infallible dry wit, with an angry moral passion crackling beneath it. Its a relief to see the Darrow figure played thin and stylish, which he historically was, rather than with the sweaty bulk of an Orson Welles or a Spencer Tracy.

Its Brady, the feted local hero with a heart condition, who is the bulky, brow-mopping one, and Blendick's deep sonic boom suits him to perfection. He gives the man decency and simplicity as well as complacency, and makes a pretty distinction between self- righteousness, which Brady has, and self-satisfaction, which he hasn't: His past failures are visibly gnawing away at him. Because of the heat, those in court are permitted to remove their jackets, so we are treated to the rare spectacle of a couple of titans squaring off in their suspenders.

Their battle reaches its height when Drummond, denied the right to call expert witnesses in favour of Darwinism (since that doctrine is illegal, it cannot be heard in court), puts Bryan himself on the stand and cross-examines him about possible absurdities in his own beliefs. He brings up the usual problems. How come that the world didnt descend into chaos when Joshua stopped the sun coming up over Jericho? Where did Cains wife spring from? (As a Jew, I can't help resenting that when people go hunting for scriptural impalusibilities, they always turn to the Old Testament rather than the New.) But he only really gets Bryan on the ropes when he forces him to admit that the first day of creation might not, since it pre- dated the invention of light, have lasted the standard 24 hours. Maybe, suggests Drummond, it lasted millions of years.

Not for the first time when listening to a debate -- live, staged or televised -- you want to jump in and point out that the opponents have more in common than they might think. If they could both just agree that the Bible story is a metaphor, honour would be satisfied on both sides and civilization could move on. But of course life isn't like that, and drama cant afford to be.

Darrow-Drummonds role-reversing tactics must have been very dramatic then and remain highly theatrical now. But when the authors, Jerome Lawrence and Robert E. Lee, get away from the facts of the case and fall back on their own devices, those devices turn out to be very tired indeed.

Very much downplayed is the fact that the accused, here called Bertram Cates, actually sought prosecution. His was a test case, specifically engineered by the American Civil Liberties Union to expose the absurdities of creationism and the menaces of censorship. Cates here is a bewildered innocent, surprised to find everybody hates him and half-inclined to give up. He also has a romantic interest, a fellow-teacher who is also -- get this -- the daughter of the town preacher, a man whose virulent bigotry gives even Bryan pause.

Tim MacDonald and Claire Jullien, who play these two young puppets, understandably fail to bring their early encounters to anything like life. Only when she is forced to testify for the prosecution, and when he awaits the court verdict, do they seem like people.

Much else is left cloudy. The jury, traditionalists to a man, convict Cates (they have to -- he broke the law) but impose a minimal fine. What changed their minds? If it was the judge's unheard summing-up (C. David Johnson plays this part with dignity, which is all he's allowed), then what changed his mind, since he seemed as dead set against the prisoner as they were? Bryan is no Neanderthal. How does he reconcile his stance here with his earlier fight for womens suffrage?

There is a cynical journalist, modelled on H. L. Mencken, who goes around making free-thinking wisecracks that are initially very funny (Peter Hutt, who plays him with sour excellence, has the Hutt family timing), but get gradually less sympathetic. When he turns on Bryan in his hour of defeat, the chivalrous Drummond turns on him and calls him a vicious smart-alec attacking a noble man. As if we werent capable of noticing these things for ourselves.

When the schoolteacher prepares to skip town with her beloved, she says she has now read Darwin, and though she doesn't like what she's read she will defend -- well, you get the picture. Drummond nods his wise old head and gives her an atheists blessing. We know these issues are still alive, and we may amuse ourselves with fantasies of what Drummond might do to Stockwell Day if he got him up there, but really there is nothing in this play to disturb any audience liberal enough to go to a theatre and see it -- now, or any time in the last half-century.

Richard Monette has given it a full-bodied staging (i.e. a lot of actors well manoeuvred), on a set cunningly designed by Peter Hartwell to combine the spiritual with the topographic. Through stained glass, we glimpse townscape and landscape. In the early scenes, the townsfolk tend to stand around in a semi-circle, like the chorus in a musical. They seem to be waiting for Robert Preston to arrive and tell them they got trouble. When Bryan arrives instead, they do break into song: Hymns, of course, which they sing even on their way to prayer meetings. The point -- that this is a God-fearin community -- is made rather quickly, but they go on doing it, and a male trio singing an especially accusing spiritual at the start of Act II is very powerful. The population includes a Bible salesman, who is very ragged, and no wonder: There can't be anybody in town who doesn't already have what he's selling.

I have always been curious to see Inherit the Wind, so I can hardly knock Stratford for giving me the opportunity. They have the talent and resources to do it justice, and they have done so. But nobody had better take it for serious drama.
