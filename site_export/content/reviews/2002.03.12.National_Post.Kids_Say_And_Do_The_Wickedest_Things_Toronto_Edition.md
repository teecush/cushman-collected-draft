BUNCHA' YOUNG ARTISTS FESTIVAL

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

How do you dramatize an actual, recent and bloody event and explore rather than exploit it? Adam Pettle, who made an auspicious debut last year as the author of Zadie's Shoes, tackles that question in his new short play, Misha. Until about halfway through I doubted he'd cracked it. Then he does -- and rides triumphantly home.

Misha must have been at least inspired, if that's the word, by the Toronto murder of 15-year-old Matti Baranovsky, swarmed and killed when he refused to surrender a packet of cigarettes. It's presented as part of the Buncha' Young Artists Festival, a season of one-act plays presented by Theatre Direct, which specializes in plays by youthful writers aimed at yet more youthful audiences and which alternates between taking plays into schools and having the schools come to them. Not enough people get to see their shows. Their best work is excellent by any standard (I have to declare an interest: My wife is president of the board), and this play belongs with their best.

The author seizes on the fact that Matti, his assailants and the friends who were with him and were scared away were all Jewish. Misha is now the victim's name. This is a three-character play, with friends and killers reduced to one each.

Max, the friend, is also the narrator, plagued by nightmares ever since the event. Max recalls how Misha, a Russian immigrant, kept silent for his first three years in Canadian school, how he learned English from videos of Hollywood musicals, how he loved to play Shostakovich on the piano, how he was nervously preparing for a belated bar mitzvah (the earliest his parents could afford) and how he enthusiastically looked forward to losing his virginity on the weekend that proved to be his last.

How much of this is rooted in fact I don't know, but much of it seemed forced. We know, as we watch him in flashback, that he is going to die; and in the face of that brute certainty, psychology and human interest feel irrelevant, just as Max's traumas seem initially beside the point, the kind of thing you invent to fill out a play. We take Max's word that Misha was a nice, funny, intelligent boy, but would it have been any less horrific had he been none of these things?

The temperature changes when a Hebrew prayer is spoken and all three characters chime in on the "Amen." There is a chilling bond here. Its other face is Jewish anti-Semitism, expressed humorously by Max and Misha -- like many Jewish kids, they're embarrassed by their relatives -- and murderously by Aaron, the killer. He has fought in the Israeli army, (as did one of the youths in the Baranovsky case) and Misha attracts his animosity by seeming to personify Jewish submissiveness. In fact, he stands his ground; he has done enough running. It's Max, the streetwise Max, who runs and suffers for it ever after. (It's never explained why, having escaped, he didn't call 911.)

Max reflects that there is just one thing about Aaron he can respect: He didn't go on television and seek absolution through repentance. Neither did Max himself; but apparently everybody else in the school who had so much as clapped eyes on the deceased lined up to regale the chat shows with their memories. ("Misha once held open the cafeteria door for me." Cool.)

Pettle has a brilliant handle on grief culture, and the more the play opens up to wider issues, the more incisively the personal details strike home. Misha is not the central character -- Max is -- but he earns his titular position by being the figure in relation to whom all the other characters, seen and unseen, define themselves. The play takes on an independent life that is more than topical, more than anecdotal.

Zadie's Shoes showed Pettle's mastery of Jewish humour; the jokes here jar to begin with, but they get truly funnier as the play gets darker. (The bar mitzvah stories are a riot.) The closing sections of the play fall like hammer-blows, Max replaying the fatal encounter in his mind, over and over, trying to make it come out differently and always being brought up against the brick-wall truth.

Richard Greenblatt's production matches the tautness of the play, and the performances of Miklos Perlus (Max) and Daniel Stemer (Misha) grow with the writing. Aaron is hulkingly, loomingly embodied by Jacob Barker, who makes his first entrance swinging on a crossbar, commando-style. The play accords him a certain pity and a certain respect, and in so doing says something about the nature of terrorism and of counterterrorism; it may be timelier now than when it was first conceived. It does not dignify history -- what, in this case, could? -- but it does not degrade it either.

I saw one other Buncha' play last week: Martian Summer, by a Quebec writer, Nathalie Boisvert, translated (though it never sounds like a translation) by Bobby Theodore. This is another play about two kids growing up in shared solitude and another play that crucially involves smoking, though not of tobacco.

Peanut, a loner because she wears bifocals, and Kiko, an outcast because of a home life that is desperate to non-existent, are initially drawn together by a common hatred of dodgeball. Peanut is scared of the world, Kiko is mad at it; their friendship develops around a tabloid story they have read about an imminent landing by aliens.

They determine to meet the ship, reasoning that life in a distant galaxy has got to be better than here; in the linear, literal way in which they regard their fantasies, they might be an updated, transgendered Tom and Huck.

Peanut has a severely handicapped sister -- "a vegetable" -- and the two girls drag her with them to the meeting place, with predictably dire results.

This play, too, grows as it goes. It starts as just another rite- of-passage piece -- we are inured to plays about disadvantaged youngsters pooling their physical and psychological resources, and there is generally something voyeuristic about them -- but it becomes altogether richer and stranger. You get caught up in it. The wilder and faster the action becomes, the more believable it seems, both in writing and performance.

Soraya Peerbaye, gentle as Peanut, and Michelle Polak, driven as Kiko, complement one another admirably.

Jennifer Tarver's production handles some sizeable logistic challenges with impressive ease. The ending is beautifully, rackingly ambiguous.
