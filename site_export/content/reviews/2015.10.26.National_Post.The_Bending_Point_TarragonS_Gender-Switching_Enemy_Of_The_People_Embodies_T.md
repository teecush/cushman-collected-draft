Theatre Review

Enemy of the People Revisited

A year ago Toronto's Tarragon Theatre presented an adaptation of Henrik Ibsen's 1882 play An Enemy of the People. Actually it was twice adapted: a Canadian version of a German production that had already taken considerable liberties with the Norwegian original. It was a deserved success, and is now back for a second run with an additional transformation. The principal role and its performer, both formerly male, are now female.

Ibsen's central character is, or was, Dr. Thomas Stockmann, medical consultant for a spa town that has staked its economic future on the tourists who will come to take its curative waters. He discovers that those waters are far from curative; that they are in fact fatally contaminated by industrial waste. He's naive enough to think that his fellow citizens will thank him for this information. Initially some of them do; the local paper sees it as great ammunition to use against its ideological foes. But when the councillor-in-chief, who happens to be the doctor's brother, points out to them how long it will take to fix the problem, and how much it will cost, and how taxes will have to go up to pay for it, tunes get changed. Stockmann, branded a public enemy, is hounded out of a public meeting and out of his livelihood.

All of this still happens in Richard Rose's Tarragon production, except that Thomas is now Thomasina, played - very well indeed - by Laura Condlln. Thomas' straight marriage is now Thomasina's gay one. There have been several other cast changes, but the role of the wife is played by the same actress as before, Tamara Podemski, whose performance I liked much better now than I did last year.

How much difference does the gender-switch make? Surprisingly little. The play isn't about the Stockmanns' marriage. Their domestic life was always the least interesting part of Ibsen's original (though a production some years ago at Britain's National Theatre did more with it than I would have thought possible) and the German-Canadian script reduces it still further. Ibsen gave the Stockmanns a grown daughter and two school-age sons; at the Tarragon this time around they have a single baby and - courtesy of Podemski's actual pregnancy - another on the way. But the family relationship that counts is the one outside the household. I wrote last year that the production showed the play to be the best ever written about sibling rivalry. It somehow seems even more acute when the siblings, the physician and the politician, are brother and sister. Condolln and Rick Roberts, repeating his performance as the dyspeptic Peter Stockmann, summon up a whole lifetime's fractious relationship, one of wary affection gone irredeemably, even hysterically, sour. Condlln's body language is remarkable, rangy and cheerful in her initial innocence, hunched and clenched and incredulous in her angry disillusion. There are, obviously, two kinds of cross-gender casting. One, as here, is to change the sex of the character or characters. The other is to leave them as written, but to cast across the line. Generally, not always, this involves having either an all-male or all-female cast. This of course is how Shakespeare's plays were first performed; actresses weren't allowed on the Elizabethan stage and the women's roles were played by boys.

This tradition has been revived from time to time at the reconstructed Globe Theatre in London, with the obvious difference that the female impersonators are now grown men. Mark Rylance played Cleopatra there, and he was the best I've ever seen. Freed from the female obligation to look like the most beautiful woman in the world he could concentrate on playing the character, to its uncompromising limits; he made Cleopatra's unpredictable rages more frightening, and more believable, than any actress has managed or perhaps would have dared. I was far less charmed by Rylance's Olivia in the Globe's all-male Twelfth Night when I caught it in New York; rapturously received and technically brilliant, this was an elaborate piece of female impersonation that unbalanced the play, a star turn in a less than star role.

Sometimes conventions can be mixed. Sarah Bernhardt used to play Hamlet, in productions otherwise conventionally cast. She was the most famous actress of her time, and it was accepted as her prerogative. (It may have helped that she did it in French.) Rather more recently Seana McKenna at the Stratford Festival played Richard III, in a production in which the other men were men, fastening on Richard's obsession with his own deformity to present him as asexual, a monstrous imp. It worked. So, some years ago in Toronto, did a much-underrated production of A Midsummer Night's Dream, directed by Kate Lynch with an all-female cast. It established the convention that the play was being performed by a troupe of victorious Amazons and followed it through, shining an ironic but not hostile light on the comedy's sexual politics. It stayed faithful to its own terms which, it turned out, were perfectly compatible with Shakespeare's.

Chris Abraham, directing the Dream at Stratford two years ago, offered three different varieties of what might be called intersexuality in one production. Maybe four, since he too had a frame; the play became the entertainment at a gay wedding. Male actors alternated as Oberon and Titania, but played them traditionally as male and female. This worked beautifully. The squabbling quartet of young lovers were re-imagined as a straight couple and a lesbian one; this sort of worked, but not without strain. Peter Quince, moving spirit of the Athenian workers' amateur dramatic society, was played by a woman, as a woman.

This didn't work at all. The dynamic of half-a-dozen men, buddies at that, is different from that of five men and one woman. The scenes went flat. The same is true of a string of well-intentioned open-air productions in Toronto, including a Merchant of Venice in which Shylock was transformed into a Jewish mother. Neither the family nor the commercial relationships made any sense.

A dead author's intentions may be unknowable but a play's logic isn't - or at least it makes audible creaking sounds when it's contradicted. But its identity can be surprisingly flexible. And it's not just a matter of gender. I read recently of a production of Othello in which Iago, as well as Othello, was black. It sounds crazy. But when you think about it: Iago says many vicious things about "the black Othello" but he never claims that he himself is white. You can play him as jealous of his compatriot's success in the white world without altering a line of the text or, more importantly, without in any way changing the relationship between them, which is what drives the play. It may even make more credible Othello's credulity. What this suggests is that the term "colour-blind casting" - as opposed to the idea behind it - is meaningless, and indeed seems to have fallen into disuse. An audience can no more fail to notice whether actors are black or white than whether they are tall, short, fat or thin. How much it matters, in a given play, is something infinitely variable. Often it doesn't matter at all. But if a production of Othello were to cast a black actress as Desdemona, it would be in trouble. The play would be pointless.

As, to take an example unrelated to race or gender, Sophocles' Oedipus Rex became in Daniel Brooks' Stratford production this year. The key scene in the tragedy is the one in which Oedipus extracts from an old shepherd the truth about his own origins. He's been warned that finding out will be fatal, but he has to know. The shepherd wants to spare him, but Oedipus' insistence, his sheer strength of will, is too much for him. In this production, Oedipus has a couple of goons beat it out of him. The play ceases to be about a man digging his own grave, from a mixture of pride and genuine nobility, and becomes about how big and tough the director is.

An Enemy of the People never makes this kind of mistake. It makes changes: "ch-ch-ch-changes" to quote the David Bowie song that the characters have an irritating habit of singing. It has a darker ending than the one Ibsen provided, and in this it follows its own logic. But that turns out to be a variation on Ibsen's, not a contradiction of it. The essential drama is there: the feuding of brothers, or in this case of brother and sister; the shifting tides of personal and political interest, coming to a thrilling head in the newspaper office. In fact the only time in which invention becomes distortion is in what the production plainly intends to be its innovative high point. This is the meeting scene, performed with the house lights up and the audience invited to join in. Trying to underline the play's perennial topicality, it actually dilutes it. On the first night Rick Roberts - who, as councillor Peter, was giving his best Stephen Harper - was peppered with questions that seemed to have less to do with the story than with the election, then five days away. He coped brilliantly, though I have to wonder if some of the fire may not have gone out of this scene since Monday.

Inhibited by being a critic, or maybe just inhibited, I failed to ask the question on my mind, which was just how the town expected to stave off financial ruin when people started dropping dead from the poisoned water. Roberts would probably have replied that it wouldn't happen and his sister's charges were unproven. But we knew that they were proven. We'd been watching the play. The pretence that we hadn't, or that we were characters in it, was even phonier than audience participation usually is. But the production picked up in the last scene, with Condolln and Roberts and Tom Barnett as the weasel publisher all desperately fine. Last year this was an excellent production. This year it's the most exciting show in town.
