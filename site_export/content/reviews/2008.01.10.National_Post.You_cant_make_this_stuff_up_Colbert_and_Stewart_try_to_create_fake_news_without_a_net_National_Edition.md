Jon Stewart had an otherwise empty box on his screen occupied by the words "Space Reserved for Clever Pun." Stephen Colbert called as of old for The Word to appear and no word was there. As of Monday, the late-night twin-pins of faux news and comment have been back, bucking the writers' strike. Or maybe being bucked by it.

In practical terms, Stewart and Colbert without writers -- living, we have to assume, on their own wits -- has meant shows reduced to monologues and interviews. So far, this has worked out better for Colbert, who is essentially a one-man band, than for Stewart, who has always relied on a cohort of comic correspondents to whom he can act as incredulous straight man. (The best of them, before he struck out on his own, was Colbert.) At their best, Stewart's monologues have been great, but when they have fallen flat there has generally been something funny -- and transparently scripted--from the supporting corps to pick up the slack. The interviews have always been chancy. They have worked when political; depending on the guest pundit's affiliation, Stewart has been able to share his liberal outrage with the pundits with whom he agreed, or raise incredulous eyebrows at those with whom he didn't. When some showbiz figure has wandered by to promote a new movie, he has seemed at a loss; on these occasions The Daily Show with Jon Stewart has stopped being a parody of a chat-show and turned into an uninspired example of the real thing.

There were no visiting movie stars this Monday or Tuesday, though I suspect the show may have to fall back on them fairly quickly. Monday found our boy just a little too convincingly nervous as he acknowledged his new solitude, made passing reference to the Iowa and New Hampshire primaries that were the day's main news story, and then proceeded to the industrial action unfolding -- at least in theory -- just underneath his own window.

There's a nice irony about his position -- a boss who must have crossed a picket line of which he was an ideological component -- but he didn't make much of it. Stewart's most treasurable talent has always been stripping the surface absurdity from a situation to reveal the absurdity underneath. This time, though, he managed it only once, when suggesting that the $1.99 exacted for downloading his own show from iTunes had nothing to do with making a profit and was just "a shipping and handling charge."

He seemed relieved to get to the interview, which was with Ron Seeber, a very tough-looking professor of labour relations at Cornell.

Having established that they were both, guardedly, on the writers' side, Stewart was able to get to the funny stuff, to the jargon of it all ("They've walked away from the table; is it a nice table?") and, by some mysterious logical process, to anti-Semitism ("the whole reason I got into this business was I thought we controlled it"). He signed off with, by my watch, a good five minutes to spare, time that he effectively bequeathed to his friend, colleague and employee Colbert.

Tuesday found Stewart in much better shape, acknowledging the limitations of the unscripted life ("any movie reference I make is going to be from the '80s") and getting down to the impending presidentials.

This involved an aghast look when calling all the candidates, especially Mitt Romney, on the changes in their invocations of "change," and re-running footage to expose the gap between the stuff that happens and the stuff that gets said.

This has always been his greatest public service, and he was at his best showing us Hillary Clinton's supposed meltdown and then asking, "Was that all?" (Even the BBC had been taking the pool-of- tears stuff seriously.) He even managed to get one of his own team back: John Oliver, the snippy Englishman, insisting that every thing he was saying was strictly off the cuff, while standing before a projection of himself, in his writing persona, walking in a protest- circle, and also insisting that as a foreigner, he wasn't affected by Guild regulations anyway.

"My brain's not on strike, brother," said Stewart, interviewing David Frum on his disillusion with the Bush administration of which he was once a booster. Here Stewart, playing the standard interview game, was at a disadvantage compared with Colbert when speaking to the similarly disenchanted Andrew Sullivan, worried about America's standing in the eyes of the world.

"You care what the world thinks?" Colbert asked, sounding generally incredulous. Colbert's trick, in his persona of self- intoxicated right-wing blowhard, is to play devil's advocate so well that the devil feels embarrassed to have shown up.

Without unions, Stephen correctly pointed out, we could go right back to the good old days of child labour. Colbert is now so thoroughly within his role, and can think against himself so well on his feet, that he maybe can do without literary help. Noting petulantly that there was nothing on his auto-prompter, he turned it into a comment not only on the studio situation but on himself: "I always thought the machine read my thoughts and played them back." (I paraphrase, but that was the thought.)

The Colbert Report pronounced its title in English fashion on Monday, but on Tuesday it had regained its confidence and gone back to Gallic. One almost trembles for the day when the White House shifts leftward, as it may make Colbert's pose as voice of America untenable. Still, I don't suppose Limbaugh and O'Reilly are going to fade away when that day comes, so we may still need Colbert to expose them by saying the same things with more panache. And if we don't -- well, he's a talent; he'll survive. And he could always go back to supporting Jon Stewart. Maybe they could be a double act.
