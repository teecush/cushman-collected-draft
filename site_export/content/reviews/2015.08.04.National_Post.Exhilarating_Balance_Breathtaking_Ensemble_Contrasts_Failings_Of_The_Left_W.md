If you want to get high on acting, make tracks for the Shaw Festival's studio where the Tony Kushner play with the space-devouring title is given a breathtaking ensemble rendition, studded with great solos. The flawless direction is by Eda Holmes.

The play covers three days in 2007. Jim Mezon gives one of his patriarchal performances, mournfully quiet but with power in reserve, as Gus Marcantonio. He's a seventy-year old retired longshoreman who comes from a long line of Italian anarchists and who all his life has been in and out of the American Communist Party. He is also a self-taught classics scholar, engaged in translating Horace's Epistles. He feels that all the protests, all the strikes, all the past victories, have been for nothing, merely leading to "the prison rebuilding itself." He also feels that, through one compromising agreement, he betrayed his colleagues. For these reasons, which may be more theatrical than believable, he wants to commit suicide. He offers additional motives: he claims to have Alzheimer's, though his long and short term memories both seem in excellent order, and he wants to sell his big Brooklyn brownstone, so that his three grown children may divide the million or so it will fetch. Rich people's problems, leftwing division.

The children of course want none of it. The closest to Gus, certainly politically, is Empty (long for M.T., which is short for Maria Theresa), medical student turned nurse turned labour lawyer, who wants her father to keep living and keep fighting, while he wants her to help him kill himself. Kelli Fox plays her with an edge of common sense humour and an apparently inexhaustible supply of passion; I know of no other actress who can play anger so long and so strong, without ever appearing to force it, and without ever seeming self-indulgent. Empty has other concerns; she's in a lesbian relationship and her partner is about to give birth. But she still sees and sleeps with her ex-husband, who lives in the brownstone's basement.

Her elder brother Pill is also gay, and unequivocally so; the play's title is probably generic, but if it's specific it could mean him. Steven Sutcliffe plays him with quivering intensity, again leavened by humour. Pill is a teacher, academically unfulfilled, which seems to run in the family, and emotionally torn between the man he's married to and the young hustler with whom he's obsessed. The latter relationship occasions much debate on the socio-political implications of selling one's body. Pill's sexuality is now accepted by his father but previously rejected, which left scars. He also kicks the play off by talking about a production he's seen of George Bernard Shaw's Major Barbara, a play in which a father has to dispose of an inheritance, intellectual and industrial. Kushner's title is a riff on a nondramatic Shaw work, The Intelligent is compared By Robert Cushman Woman's Guide to Socialism, Capitalism, Sovietism and Fascism, and it could be a counter blast to the ageing Shaw's fascination with dictatorship.

Gray Powell, with refreshing roughness easily provoked to rage, plays younger brother Vito. He talks less than his relatives, preferring to bust a hole in a door by hurling a bust of Garibaldi through it. Fiona Reid gives an astonishing performance as Gus's sister Clio, grey of hair and also face, communicating much by simply sitting with her hands hanging lifelessly in her lap, looking on, drained but involved. As she says herself, she's gone from one cult to another, from a convent to Maoism to, currently, Christian Science.

Thom Marriott is, both sensitively and uproariously, Empty's ex, an estate-broker who at one point climbs drunkenly on a table to compare himself to Lopahin, the property-buying peasant of The Cherry Orchard. Diana Donnelly has some very funny moments as Empty's extremely pregnant partner, otherwise occupied as a theology student and nursing a grudge against a former professor, who happens to be Pill's other half. He's played by Andre Sills, at first with a stridency that's the only tear in the production's fabric, but with more reasonable forthrightness later on. Jasmine Chen is Vito's Korean wife, almost drowned out by her in-laws. Julie Martell, quieter than everybody else, makes a striking late appearance as a dedicated suicide counselor. Ben Sanders, the Shaw's latest major young actor, is magnetic as Pill's crush. He has the first speech, which seems odd as he's a minor figure but it is beautifully resolved in the closing scene which, unexpectedly and ominously, he shares with Gus. That's not to say that everything in the play adds up. It bulges. It covers a lot of intellectual ground and the plot doesn't always accommodate it. "When did you get so glib?" somebody asks; everyone talks with phenomenal fluency and Kushner, unlike Shaw, doesn't create a dramatic world in which this becomes credible. But there are no dull moments; and there's an honest attempt to balance the historic sins and personal failings of the left against its virtues, aspirations and abiding necessities. And it means something, a lot, that Kushner's plays, certainly in Canada, consistently elicit magnificent performances, of which these may be the collective best.

Shouting in plays usually yields rapidly diminishing returns, but there's an extended scene in this production in which everybody is yelling at once, with the occasional pause for a pointed solo interjection, and it's exhilarating: beautifully orchestrated by the actors, the director and, one assumes, the author. Paul Sportelli's musical score - Gershwin on acid - is invaluable in moving the production along. Move it does; the whole thing takes four hours, and I would sit through all four again in a heartbeat.
