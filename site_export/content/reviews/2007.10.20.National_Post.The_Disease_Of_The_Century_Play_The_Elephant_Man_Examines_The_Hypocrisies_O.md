The Canadian Stage Company -- no longer, it seems, to be known as CanStage -- often does revivals but seldom does classics, which puts it in awkward territory. If the company were in the music business, you'd say it does high-class pop covers; how long before a new production of Equus? If the plays are British, they tend to fall into the category that William Goldman identified in his Broadway chronicle The Season as the Snob Hit. The Elephant Man, a play whose author is American but that got its start in London and is set there, definitely qualifies. It has a period setting and is based on real events. It also counts as a Disease of the Week Play, or, bearing in mind the extremity of its central character's affliction, a Disease of the Century Play.

Bernard Pomerance's piece takes the story of John Merrick, the hideously deformed young man whose face and body were covered by masses of bony protuberances, and makes of it both a poignant personal history and a lecture-demonstration on the hypocrisies of Victorian, and by possible extension modern, philanthropy. It works better in the first aspect than in the second, especially in this production with Brent Carver playing Merrick.

The play's convention -- self-evidently a wise one -- is to use no make-up or prosthetics to portray the character's plight; everything is conveyed by the actor's stance and the amount of inner pain he can convey. I used to think that any competent actor obeying these rules could play Merrick with the identical results: a conclusion I drew from having seen both the original London actor and the original London understudy and finding them effectively indistinguishable, as well as indistinguishably effective. Carver proves me wrong. There is an ironic tilt to his head, as well as a resigned slump in his frame, as he is stood up in front of a hospital faculty to have his condition described. (It's known now as Proteus syndrome.) Paring everything down, he achieves a grace of body, and of soul. In Pomerance's account, Merrick relishes beauty, longs for love and worries about those whom he believes, extraordinarily, to be even worse off than he is. He whimsically ascribes his own evil fortune to his mother, a beautiful woman, having been frightened by an elephant when she was pregnant. Carver abandons the beauty-of-suffering quaverings that have marred some of his recent performances and asks hard questions in a soft voice. He brings a fastidious comic sensibility up to the edge of tragedy.

All the same, we don't learn much about Merrick, biography apart, except that he was sensitive. Against him, the play sets Frederick Treves, an ambitious young surgeon who rescues him from a brutalized existence as a fairground freak and, with the aid of public subscription, sets him up in relative comfort at the London Hospital. This benevolent act also turns out to be a shrewd career move for Treves and in the second act, the author, in an oblique way, sets about flaying him --and us --for it.

Treves is played by Geraint Wyn Davies, last seen in a Canadian theatre as that other sculptor of human material, Professor Henry Higgins. He is excellent in the first half as businesslike baby- faced saviour and forgivably at sea later on when beset by financial and moral crises that are neither explored nor explained.

Under Treves's protection, Merrick, who at first repelled all who looked upon him, becomes a pet of society: a frillier freak in a fancier fairground. In a sequence that plays like a high-school idea of irony, lords and ladies are paraded before us, each exclaiming that they see reflected in Merrick the best aspects of themselves. We too, are implicitly arraigned for congratulating ourselves on our compassion for his compassion. That is not, in fact, a bad index of how this kind of play works, but I don't know where it leaves the author, who is profiting from the situation on his own account.

In the play as written, and originally produced, there is a kind of rigour to his approach, with each scene prefaced by Brechtian subtitles. Robin Phillips's production tastefully dispenses with these, but is left with short scenes that refuse to meld. His staging is elegant, to no particular purpose; the wrought-iron set, which he seems to have designed himself, is impressive when you enter the theatre but unhelpfully vague once the action starts. Phillips has steered the two central roles well but can't do much with the lesser ones. There's a baffling bit of sub-plot in which Jack Wetherall gets to describe Barry MacGregor as "a moral swamp"; Michael Spencer-Davis makes sadly little of the bullying showman who exploits Merrick and later comes cringing back but compensates as a sleek and pompous prelate. The key-role of a star actress who, more reasonably than most, finds in Merrick a kindred spirit is played by Kate Trotter who makes her as grandiloquent off stage as she may have been on: a pity since the vocal posturings seem to be swathing a decently sympathetic performance

- The Elephant Man plays through Nov. 3 at the Bluma Appel Theatre, 416-368-3110.
