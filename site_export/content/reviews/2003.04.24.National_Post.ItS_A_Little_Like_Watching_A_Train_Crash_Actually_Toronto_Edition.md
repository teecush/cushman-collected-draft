RUSSELL HILL

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Chris Earle's Russell Hill takes its title from a grassy spot by a Toronto ravine. Several subway passengers clambered to safety there after a crash in August, 1995, in which one train rammed into another at Dupont station. Three passengers were killed and many others injured. It was the worst accident in TTC history. I had completely forgotten about it.

The fact that I could may be part of the play's point, though both play" and "point are dubious words in this context. Earle, whose roots as both author and performer are in comedy and revue, has here written a collection of speeches and sketches. The presumption is that they have some connection, metaphorical rather than factual, to the disaster, and the basic set is a subway platform: An imposingly realistic design by John Thompson that also turns out to be remarkably versatile. It needs to be, since the scenes we witness happen all over town and beyond.

The best is a chance encounter between two former lovers who reminisce about their break-up. He, who initiated it, wants to be told that he didnt cause her any pain. She begins by saying sweetly that he didnt, and then goes on, at ruthlessly hilarious length, to prove to him that he did. This is a virtuoso speech, both in the writing and in Shari Hollett's performance, which is both unhurried and unstoppable. When it does stop, the stage, with appealing incongruity, is taken over by Frank Moore, playing a crooner who seems to have jetted in from Las Vegas, or at least motored down from Casino Rama. Tuxedoed and with a hand microphone, he unleashes the full lounge treatment on a honeyed anthem to Toronto, leaving us to ponder yet again why lyrics that would pass without question if they celebrated New York or Chicago should sound so absurd when aimed at home.

It's just as well this sequence exists because the rest of the show, despite being exceedingly well acted and directed, is dire. Its the second Tarragon play in succession to begin with the author chatting up the audience. Susan Coyne, in Kingfisher Days, got away with this by pretending, charmingly, to be embarrassed; Earle blows it by pretending, embarrassingly, to be charming. We know he can be charming in other contexts, but here his very skills betray him; hes too confident for words. Of the items that follow, one, a young woman's letter to a penpal, is dully written but redeemed by the intensity of Mary Francis Moore's delivery. Others dont seem to be redeemable at all. They include a commuters address to the lucky beggar" in whose basket he regularly drops a coin; a thwarted tryst between two women lingering in a pool at a Mexican resort; and a number in a massage parlour. This last item comes early enough for us still to expect some connection between what we see and what we've been told the evening is about. Our confusion at there being no such connection is exploited by having Earle himself say what we're thinking. This may be meant to be disarming. It isnt.

Theres a late scene in which a young film assistant comes to the door of a local householder, explaining that a picture about the accident is to be shot on his street: a movie-of-the-week, apparently, with oodles of suspense and a heroic young doctor in the starring role. One welcomes this segment for its humble relevance, and for its touch of satire, but it has other merits, too. An unspoken sense of loss makes itself felt behind the politeness of the resident (Frank Moore again), while Earle does his best acting work of the evening as the gofer, trying to be brash and polite at the same time.

But he also has a monologue, in what we take to be his own person. He tells about waiting for a streetcar at 1 a.m. and being shouted at by a gang of youths in an SUV to "get a f---ing car. After initial fury, he decides this admonition hides great wisdom, presumably about the impending death of public transport. Earle can't be blamed if his story reminded me of Alan Bennett's vicar in Beyond the Fringe, recalling the employee -- of, coincidentally, the London underground -- who stopped him when he was entering an area marked No Entry by saying hey, Jack, where do you think youre going?" and caused him to ponder on where, indeed, do any of us think were going.

Earle also treats us to some whimsical musings on the modern impossibility of separating tragedy from comedy, apparently under the impression that is some revelatory idea. He talks about the escaped passengers emerging at Russell Hill on a summer evening, and of the sense of relief, and even of well-being, they must have felt, and how illusory such a feeling must be, in the grand scheme of things. Because the thing about disasters is, you never know when they're going to happen. Tell me something I don't know.

And presumably the point of all the disconnected vignettes is they each carry the possibility of catastrophe. Only they dont: Unless you take the view that any human story, taken far enough, will explode, in which case any selection is arbitrary and, almost by definition, boring. Or maybe the whole fragmented structure of the show is meant in itself to be an example of dicing with disaster. Or, to switch cliches, of skating on thin ice. But it doesnt cut any.

The invented scenes are intercut with extracts from the train driver's testimony at the inquest. Earle plays the questioning lawyer, and the others take turns at playing the driver. Of them all Sam Earle, aged nine, is the simplest and most effective. Earle senior has decently forsworn any intention of dramatizing, or otherwise sensationalizing, the crash itself. But he's still invoking documented suffering, and in that light the games he plays are every bit as cheap. Chris Abrahams direction gets everything possible out of the text, and is very slick technically. The one cast-member I havent yet mentioned is Robert Smith; he's good too.
