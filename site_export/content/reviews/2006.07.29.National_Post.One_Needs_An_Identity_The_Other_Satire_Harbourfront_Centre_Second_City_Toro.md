robertcushman@lycos.com; I Am Not a Dinner Mint closes tomorrow at Harbourfront Centre Theatre, 416-973-4000. Bird Flu Over the Cuckoo's Nest runs indefinitely at Second City, 416-343-0011.

Trey Anthony, who wrote Da Kink in My Hair, has said that one of the most gratifying aspects of its success was the way it cut across race and colour lines. White women came up to her and told her that her play, set in a hairdressing salon with an exclusively black staff and clientele, expressed their thoughts and feelings as well. With her new play, I Am Not a Dinner Mint, written in collaboration with Rachel-Lea Rickards, Anthony has taken the logical next step. She has a cast of five women, of whom three are black and two white. The step after that, of course, would be to open up the stage to the other half of the human race. But one thing at a time.

Of course, no play is under any obligation to be about everybody. What it does have to be, though, is about somebody, and here I Am Not a Dinner Mint falls down. It doesnt have the unity of setting, or the vestiges of plot, that helped give Da Kink an identity. Its mostly a series of self-contained soliloquies, whose only formal unity comes from the title, a dinner mint being defined as the small refreshing afterthought one enjoys after a really good meal." This apparently is how men think of women; even how some women think of women. Whatever the underlying truth of that, the image itself is far-fetched and when the performers invoke it at the beginning and end of the show, it sounds forced.

We have a series of complaints ("I have been treated like a dinner mint) bookended by affirmations (I am not going to be treated like a dinner mint"). The play is subtitled "the crap women swallow to stay in a relationship, and much of it, as you would expect, deals with the speakers' experiences with their partners, male or female. But the show's view of what constitutes a relationship turns out to be remarkably, even meaninglessly wide. It can encompass a womans relationship to her employers, or even to the work itself, not to mention the loss of it. But theres nothing specifically female about being fired, or even about hating your job while you have it.

The show certainly isn't whining or self-pitying. It is, most of the time, very good-humoured, not often funny but still vivaciously cheerful. There's some music, and one of the acting quintet, Graziella Mastrangelo, is also a singer who can hit some startling high notes. But the two most powerful items, the two after which you feel that you have met a person, are the starkest. In the first, Aktina Stathaki plays an overburdened mother who, all too believably, loses it. The second gives us Rickards, the co-author, as half of the only black couple on the Bridle Path, caught in a confounding vortex of privilege and abuse; shes a trophy whos also a punching bag, and she projects her rage and confusion outward. Some of the show's earlier takes on women and men have incorporated self-deprecation and made it amusing; Rickards' turn explores its consequences, and makes them kink-in-my-hair-raising.

The shows current run is a brief one with no trappings, obviously intended to lead, as happened with its predecessor, to greater things. There are certainly scenes in it worth developing. At the moment, though, its less a play than a cross between a cheerleaders' rally and a consciousness-raising session.

Or a revue. Which brings us to that form's leading local practitioners, Second City. Their latest show is called Bird Flu Over the Cuckoos Nest: one in a long line of groan-provoking titles, though this one has a modicum of relevance. The show does contain a number about avian flu. Its a calypso, sung -- in what may be a historic fusion of West Indian rhythm and East Indian voice -- by Anand Rajaram, a performer who excels at being simultaneously woeful and forceful. He sings it to a very aggrieved chicken, a non- verbal role that nonetheless gives the tousle-haired and impeccably beaky Matt Baram his second-finest hour. His finest comes as a scootered cop, berating stationary members of the audience for exceeding the speed limit and answering a call to an incident at Jane and Finch with a flat I don't go there."

The show's opening promises political satire and its closing behaves as if theres actually been some. In between, though, it delivers nothing of the kind -- also par for the course at Second City -- except for a long Bush-Harper sketch that stubbornly refuses to tell us anything we havent heard before. In general, this show is a steepish step down from the standard of its immediate predecessor, but it does have its share of goodies. Best of all, it has Naomi Snieckus, a comedienne with a face that sumptuously accommodates every variety of shock and delight, as a mother hacking into her son's MSN account to converse with two of his friends; for once a revue number doesn't run out of surprises, for us or for her. Snieckus also shines as a woman who answers a telephone survey about eggs, opts to answer her questions in French and lives to regret it. There were two understudies on at the show I saw, and they got to share a sketch, about two people who share a cab, post office- party. They did very well: David Raitt as an embarrassed underling desperately making conversation as if his life, not just his livelihood, depended on it ("There are many movies. Some with Dudley Moore. Most without."); Ashley Botting shooting him stony glares. She also sang the best of the few songs, something called I'm Getting More Like My Mother Every Day. It might, with a rhythmic makeover, fit very neatly into I Am Not a Dinner Mint.
