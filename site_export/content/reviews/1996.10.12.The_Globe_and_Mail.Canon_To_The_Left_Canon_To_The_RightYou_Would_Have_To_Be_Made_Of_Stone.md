Canon to the left, canon to the right

You would have to be made of stone not to be moved by at least one of the books in Lit Hum, said one of critic David Denby's youthful classmates. GREAT BOOKS: My Adventures with Homer, Rousseau, Woolf and Other Indestructible Writers of the Western World

Saturday, October 12, 1996

By David Denby

Simon & Schuster, 492 pages, $40

DAVID Denby is the film critic for New York magazine. His stuff there comes off as mimicry of the great Pauline Kael, of The New Yorker fame. However, when Denby himself writes in The New Yorker, as a general cultural commentator, he is his own man: an easy stylist with a concerned and generous temperament serviced by a quizzical, burrowing intelligence. These qualities come to brilliant flower in his new book, the story of a middle-aged quest.

Around 1990, Denby grew dissatisfied with his milieu: not bored, exactly, but hemmed in. "A member of the media, he says, I was also tired of the media . . . like many others, I was jaded yet still hungry." His hunger took him back to college, to the same university, Columbia, at which he had been an undergraduate 30 years earlier. In 1991 he re-enrolled in two "core curriculum courses that had been mandatory for all Columbia students in his day and still are. One was Literature Humanities (or Lit Hum), a survey of imaginative literature from Homer to, this time around, Virginia Woolf.

The other was Contemporary Civilization (or C.C.), encompassing major works of political philosophy, Plato to Simone de Beauvoir. They are familiarly known as Great Books courses. Denby wanted to see what the books could do for him now, and also to observe what they could do for the freshman students with whom he read and discussed them.

He tells three interwoven stories. One concerns his own encounters with the texts: the pleasure they gave or failed to give him, the reflections and the memories they stirred. King Lear -- this may sound crass but isn't -- reminds him of his own powerful mother and the guilt and confusion he felt during her last years. Hobbes on human greed and aggression and Hegel on the confrontation of opposites call up the morning he was mugged in Times Square. He retreats defeated before Dante and Goethe, revels in the cheerful sexuality of Boccaccio and the bracing political realism of Machiavelli, and is swept away by the greatness of Virginia Woolf, an author he had once thought dismissible. (With most of these experiences, I empathize.)

Foolhardily, he takes an exam (being examined is one of the things you become an adult to avoid") and has a violent reaction. ("Gee, I said to myself, after all these years its nice to find out what a nervous breakdown is.) He tries to teach a class himself and discovers, in a lovely comic build-up, how hard it is to make end-of-the-century American adolescents share his passion for Jane Austen.

These students are the books second story. Sometimes they remind Denby of his own youth, other times they seem like an alien species. Grappling with, or buffeted by, these demanding, unfamiliar texts, they move him -- and the reader -- to affection, exasperation and, ultimately, pride.

There are of course people who doubt the wisdom of putting them through it at all. Lit Hum and C.C. are embattled courses, the idea of great books" under constant fire. This, the book's third theme, is also its sexiest, and although Denby calls the chapters he devotes to it "interludes, they are obviously central. Harold Bloom, having expressed in The Western Canon his contemptuous indifference to the School of Resentment (his name for the anti-canonical critics), proceeded to yap at their heels on almost every page. Denby, who seems preternaturally decent and fair-minded, takes them steadily on.

Is the choice of texts arbitrary? Yes, but so is every syllabus; it has to be. Is too much being asked of students who have been under-educated in high school and don't know the contexts? Perhaps, but when the books were first written, people just read them. The notion of context is . . . academic."

This is a bit disingenuous (the original readers were the context), but essentially true; if the term "great book means anything, it means a work that continues to have an impact irrespective of specialized knowledge. Anybody who has taught will have seen this happening and been moved by it. As Denby says, the opposing logic seems to be, [The students] havent been educated properly; therefore lets not educate them properly."

As for the prescribed authors (Dead White European Males, plus a few propitiatory females) being "unrepresentative, the students don't seem to mind. A black undergraduate says, You would have to be made of stone not to be moved by at least one of the books in Lit Hum." Nor do the books make up a solid wall of repressive thinking; they constantly contradict one another. Many of them are still subversive. As Denby discovers, Marxism may be dead but Marx ("stern, logical, inexorable, implacably moral) lives. And even if you consider European civilization the enemy, getting to know it seems only common sense.

Denby's touchstone, carried over from his day job, is pleasure," the more complex the better. His bitterest quarrel with the "academic left is that they deny pleasure. I dont quite agree; in my experience, theyre eager to proclaim their enjoyment of movies and rock music, supposedly democratic forms that are actually the most commercialized on Earth. But when it comes to the art on which they make their living, Denby is right: They do not love literature." You can tell from the way they write about it. They read books, if at all, with distaste, to dredge up further evidence for opinions they already hold.

Denby is equally repelled by the conservatives' appropriation of famous authors as "bulwarks of democracy. Could such classic works actually be as boring as the right -- or as wicked as the left -- was making them sound?" He set himself to find out, and he makes his discoveries joyously real. In defending the humanities, he asserts his own humanity. This is a magnificent book.


  


When he reviewed The Western Canon, Toronto critic Robert Cushman was trying to catch up on his reading. He still is.
