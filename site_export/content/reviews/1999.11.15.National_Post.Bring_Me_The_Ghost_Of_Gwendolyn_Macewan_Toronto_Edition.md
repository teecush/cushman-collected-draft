Dont Sylvia Plath me, says Linda Griffiths at the beginning of her theatrical summoning of Gwendolyn MacEwen, "and I won't Sylvia Plath you."

This was encouraging; it confronted the primal fears of any audience about to experience a solo performance based on the life, works and personality of a female poet with a self-destructive streak. In fact, Griffiths-as-MacEwen is quick to point out that she didn't commit suicide: I drank myself to death -- theres a difference.

Rather a subtle one, I think, and not enough to prevent the built- in conventions of such an enterprise from exacting their toll. By the end, despite all her best intentions and even a few of my own, I felt thoroughly Plathed.

Griffiths has said that she came to MacEwen through working on a TV show about her and through reading what she refers to in a program note as a "biography of her life." (Those do tend to be the best kind.) The piece itself arranges its biographical information in a series of free-floating, non-chronological chunks; if you go in, as I did, with no more than a vague idea of the subjects notoriety you will be only a little wiser by the time you come out.

We learn that she was a fashionable and prolific writer in the 1970s when it was great to be young, and went into a steeply spiralling decline in the '80s when it was hell to be middle-aged, especially if youth was what you had had to sell in the first place. The piece is confessedly less about the woman herself than about a subjective conflation of performer and performed: what-Gwendolyn- means-to-me.

Very little of MacEwen's actual verse is included. This may be a prudent decision; poetry recitals are usually tedious, especially for an audience that has gone expecting a play, but removing the evidence of the subjects talent means removing the spectators main incentive for being interested in her in the first place.

In their place we have, from time to time, what Griffiths describes in the program as her own theatre pieces: little jingles that feature a recurrent obsession with nouns, mainly medical, ending in "-osis." At one point she falls back, with a great show of self-deprecation, on noses, leaving you wondering why nobody informed her of the existence of the word "proboscis."

Non-comic solo plays of a literary bent are generally boring; and my attention certainly wandered during this one. We are stuck with one person and with no chance of an alternate perspective; and the more hard-luck the story, the more we need an opposing voice to lift the curse of self-pity.

MacEwen herself was probably not self-pitying; the play presents her surveying the wreckage of her life with an air of mordant jocularity directed equally at herself and the shrinking world around her. (I didn't mean to go over the top. It's just that the top is so low nowadays.)

But the frame that it erects around her is ineluctably self- regarding; the tone may not be reverential but the event is. As for the occasional other people in the picture -- alcoholic father, crazed and verbally abusive mother, pungently malodorous first husband, unhelpful bank manager -- they do come momentarily to life, but only, and inevitably, as things in her dream. Reducing them to that level reduces her as well.

The show has considerable visual appeal in an austere way. MacEwen spent her later years in a succession of basement apartments; Jan Komarek, designing both set and lighting, evokes them with three variously shaded brick walls and a high bare window, which also suggest a prison cell. There is some play with the idea of MacEwen as a magician; the image is made flesh by having her perform a series of conjuring tricks, smoothly inserted by director Simon Heath and niftily executed by Griffiths.

She herself is a compelling sight: dark-haired and draped in a flowing, diaphanous red-and-white number, the love goddess of the Toronto book scene. As a performer she has improved enormously since her acclaimed debut in Maggie and Pierre, an atrociously smug show that must rank, despite severe competition, as the most overrated in the history of the Canadian theatre. She no longer scampers around the stage while gabbling her lines like a berserk ventriloquist; she now has stillness and pace and timing and command. She also, though she sometimes seems dangerously hung up on her own adjectives, lets her text breathe.

Griffiths does not, though, make it or her character live: not, at least, in terms that I could recognize or that could make me care. (Others apparently could; there were the regulation cheers at the end.)

The play is subtitled "a visitation" but it seems more like a rarefied seance.
