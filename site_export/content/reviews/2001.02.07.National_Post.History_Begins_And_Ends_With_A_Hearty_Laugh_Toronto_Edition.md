KEN CAMPBELLS HISTORY OF COMEDY (PART ONE: VENTRILOQUISM)

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

Ken Campbell has been functioning in and around the British theatre for several decades now as an actor, playwright and director, though none of those classifications quite suits him. To the observer of his work they seem confining -- and they seem to seem like that to him, too. He still works with other people, but he may well have found himself as a solo performer, a raconteur who tells tales of the occult and bizarre with a gleeful gurgle in his voice and an air of casual conviction that is modestly spellbinding. The self-styled History of Comedy (Part One: Ventriloquism) that he brought to the Toronto Six Stages Festival shows him at his peak. Be advised, though, that it is not so much a history of comedy as a history of Campbell.

For as long as I can remember (which, where Campbell is concerned, is 30 years) he has been balding; he now has preternaturally bushy eyebrows to match the grey thatch. He walks briskly but unhurriedly onstage, giving the (grammatical erroneous) impression of being already in mid-sentence.

He starts talking about a Festival of Radical Ventriloquists that he attended in Kentucky, and he does so in the hypnotic tones that the late Peter Cook used in his immortal Beyond the Fringe monologue about working down the mines and not having the Latin to be a judge. The difference is that Cook was distancing himself from his character, obviously putting on a voice and somewhat sinister, while Campbell is affably himself.

He comes from a long tradition of British comedy. His roots are in the more eccentric aspects of the music hall, and I find it hard to describe him without invoking comedians who are not very well known in Canada. So indulge me. Like the melancholy-mad Max Wall, Campbell beckons us into a world of his own, though the welcome he extends is cheery rather than doleful. His voice can rise to pitches of outraged incredulity, expressed in the southeast London vowels, and the southeast London flamboyance, of the supreme British stand- up artist Frankie Howard. And he seizes on unlikely pieces of information and holds them up to the light with a schoolboyish enthusiasm that reminds me of my good friend Rob Buckman before he gave up comedy and moved to Toronto.

His act is in fact a collection of objets trouves, physical and intellectual. Behind him are two tables piled high with stuff: ventriloquists dummies (obviously -- and he tells us that in professional circles the word dummy" is frowned upon, the favoured term being "knee pal), pictures from his past, books including one virtuosically free of all words including the letter e." He tells us about his own life, concentrating on school days just after the Second World War when he and his contemporaries wrote away for teach- yourself-ventriloquism books and used the techniques they had learned to invent a universal non-verbal language with which to baffle the crazed war veterans who taught them and incidentally to bring about the destruction of the universe. He acknowledges that in the latter aim they were unsuccessful.

He also tells us about friends of his, the Plashwit brothers, who were nursed by dogs, and of the intense canine attachment developed by their kid sister, Nina (apparently the love of his life), and by how unshocked his own mother was when he told her. ("Remember Tim, she said mysteriously.) He tells us many details about these friends' father, who was probably an octuple agent," and about a 19th-century U.S. attorney who won hundreds of 19th-century dollars in damages for a dog-deprived client by delivering a passionate speech so moving that the recorder had to stop recording it. What the recorder did get down became the basis of the young Campbell's first professional ventriloquial act, a funeral oration that unfortunately drove the son of the deceased absolutely mad. He did, however, recover, and Campbell was eventually to re-encounter him, and the long-lost Nina, at the very same Kentucky festival that he began by telling us about.

You see how it goes. Campbell goes off at enormously lengthy detailed tangents (his act alludes, historically and geographically, to much of the known world and even more of the unknown) but he always comes back to where he left off, and then comes back to where he had left off before that. Its one thing inside another, like taking a Russian doll out of a Chinese box. Its amazingly fluent -- a great feat of memory, construction and conceivably spontaneous invention. I don't know if he makes any of it up as he goes along (he went on at my visit for 2 3/4 hours, which is excessive, though it was rarely boring), but he is never at a loss for well-chosen words. For an apostle of the non-verbal, he is a wiz with language.

Of course, we don't believe much of what he tells us. But then again, all the things he says that I can check up on are actually true. He did the eastern hinterlands of London -- what they used to call the Essex marshes -- and has the accent to prove it. He did, in the early 70s, found a troupe called the Ken Campbell Road Show -- I saw them. (Bob Hoskins was in it). He did write, direct and appear in epic day-or-week-long shows for a company he called the Science Fiction Theatre of Liverpool -- The Warp, which I didnt like but is being revived, and Illuminatus!, based on a cult book, which was amazing. So why shouldn't the rest be real? He seems to believe them.

He is fascinated by things -- the more arcane the better -- and the fascination is infectious. He talks of the psychic experience of "going through the wall on the other side of which everything seems funny; and I seem to remember reading that in real life he had to give up acting in plays because he found himself too amused by his colleagues' performances to concentrate on his own. He also tells us, with reference to performance conditions in primitive cultures, that you might say plush seats were the last nail in the coffin of real comedy." Well, that wasn't a problem in the back room at Passe Muraille. Believe me. Despite his logical mind, Campbell is not averse to the odd long-sequitur, so I doubt that his History of Comedy will have a Part Two, but if it does I hope we get to see it. If it doesn't, we could take this one again.
