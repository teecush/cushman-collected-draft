ERIC IDLE EXPLOITS MONTY PYTHON

Hummingbird Centre

Vikings were visible in the lobby before the show started -- or at least men in funny helmets with horns sticking out of them. They weren't performers; they were members of the audience. Once inside, though, they and a host of other kindred spirits -- some with knotted handkerchiefs on their heads, others dressed in the red robes of the Spanish Inquisition -- became part of the show, which would indeed have been impossible without them.

Eric Idle Exploits Monty Python is sing-along, talk-along and dress-along entertainment: the apotheosis of audience participation.

As a comic event, this makes it difficult to judge. Comedy, after all, is supposed to have an element of danger about it, and this tends to vanish when the audience is there, not to be surprised by laughter, but to indulge its nostalgia and pledge allegiance to a cult.

Still, if anyone has the right to preside over such a feast of indulgence, its Eric Idle, who is among us as sole theatrical proprietor of Monty Pythons Flying Circus. The Pythons were a hilarious, cleansing comic force in the '1960s, and their impact, on those of us who were there first time round -- and, as I can now testify, on our offspring -- remains profound.

As a performer, Idle always struck me as the second funniest Python (no prize for guessing the first) and he takes the stage here at the head of a freshly recruited crew of singers, dancers and impersonators of the original sextet. Hearing other people trying to say "Nobody expects the Spanish Inquisition" as if they meant it is admittedly somewhat taxing; it's as if the cast, too, were members of the audience, acting out their fantasies.

But the show isnt just an anthology of Python. Some of it comes from Idles post-Circus career and from his impressive talents as a comic songwriter. Every Sperm is Sacred comes over especially strong; so does A Man Called Brian, the movie theme song that should have ended them all but didn't. Not to mention the unforgettable song about the uniformly drunken habits of the world's greatest philosophers.

Idle himself doesnt so much alternate between self-deprecation and self-aggrandizement as overlap. He has matured into a stand-up comic in the classic English tradition; he does a devastating impression of a sadistically smarmy master of ceremonies, in gold- lame jacket and what looked like a tinfoil waistcoat (though occasionally he loses the balance and comes uncomfortably close to the real thing).

He goes through his blazered saloon-bar (nudge, nudge, wink, wink) routine with impressive devotion, if with an inevitable air of doing it because its expected of him. On the other hand, a lesser- known routine -- an unstoppable catalogue of the horrors of an English holidaymaker's package tour -- is a tour de force, as impressive on stage as on TV.

Idle excels at carrying certain aspects of the English character, meticulously observed, to the fantastic limits; with him it's the futility and genial bloody-mindedness of the lower middle class. Maybe you have to be English and of a certain age to get the full acrid flavour, but it certainly seems to travel in time and in space.

Which reminds me: Why, given that this is Python and this is Canada, did the audience not appear to contain a single Mountie?
