RECORDINGS"Performances of Cole Porter's work are going in opposing directions: a search for authenticity and attempts at new interpretations. Both schools are represented among these albums A stocking full of Cole

Saturday, December 22, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

RED, HOT AND BLUE, Chrysalis VKS 41799 DIONNE WARWICK SINGS COLE PORTER ARISTA AL 8573 JULIE WILSON SINGS THE COLE PORTER SONGBOOK DRG CDSL 5208 JOAN MORRIS, NIGHT AND DAY, THE COLE PORTER ALBUM OMEGA OCD 3002 THE COLE PORTER SONGBOOK VOLUME ONE ELLA FITZGERALD Verve 821 989-2 BY ROBERT CUSHMAN The Globe and Mail COLE Porter used to fire off telegrams to the likes of Bing Crosby and Frank Sinatra, tetchily inquiring why they botherd to sing his songs when, as written, they obviously didn't like them.

An aging songwriter is generally delighted to hear his songs sung by anybody and Porter changed, so to speak, his tune. But his earlier intransigence is a reminder that what most of us regard as the right and obvious way to arrange and perform a Porter song - the fifties' way of Crosby and Sinatra and, pre-eminently, Ella Fitzgerald - is as much an interpretation as anything that has come later.

Performances of Porter, and of the other standard American composers, have recently gone in two opposing directions. One is the search for authenticity, assisted by the rediscovery of original thirties orchestrations and their use in recordings of complete scores. The other is represented by Red, Hot and Blue: 20 current pop acts charitably doing it for AIDS relief and research.

Red, Hot and Blue annotator-producer John Carlin tells us that Porter's "music presents love as a personal, almost subversive, force, that enables us to transcend life's hardships" and that Porter was rebelling against a "repressive" Victorian hangover that feared and distrusted love. Actually the Victorians were all in favour of love, an opiate second only to religion: read their novels. What they distrusted, some of them, was sex.

Carlin draws a disingenuous parallel between Porter's Let's Do It and John Lennon's All You Need Is Love. Now when Lennon's song first came out in the late sixties I took it to be a satire on the sentimentalities of flower-power. There seemed no kinder way of accounting for its banality. Subsequent history suggests that Lennon was in earnest, and that when he said love, he meant love. Porter meant copulation. Birds do it. Bees do it. Sweet guinea-pigs do it. Buy a couple and wait.

"Use your mentality, wake up to reality." Those words, plus the title, are all that remain of the original lyric in Neneh Cherry's rap version of I've Got You Under My Skin, the album's opening cut. But Porter was no preacher, and he undercut that exhortation in his very next line: "but each time I do, just the thought of you makes me stop before I begin" which in this context would translate as "I'm far too excited to put on a condom." I am not decrying safe sex; I merely question the efficacy of pop rhythms, from Porter's time or ours, in pleading its case. The medium and the message are in flagrant contradiction.

The point is not that these tracks don't honor Porter's originals but that they don't make sense in themselves. And they are in close enough proximity to the originals for their verbal sloppiness to be doubly painful. Frank Loesser, spoofing calypso in the forties, was prophetic: "We put the accent upon the wrong syllable, and we Sing a Tropical Song." Or, here, a Topical Song.

Rock has systematically devalued the word (never more so than when its lyricists have been touted as poets) and it may be too late for recovery. These triumphantly conversational songs are mostly delivered in a series of bleached monotones.

Debbie Harry's Well Did You Evah suggests that she is about to be sick, which may be a legitimate extension of Porter's jaundiced insider's view of the high life but is a lot less pleasant. I Love Paris is deservedly (but is it intentionally?) parodied by Les Negresses Vertes, droning accordion to the fore. On the other hand, Aztec Camera's Do I Love You is haunting, even moving, and k. d. lang's So in Love is triumphant, plumbing the song's masochistic depths like no other singer I have heard. Lang has been threatening for some time now to move into jazz, which I take to mean that she intends to sing some decent songs. Here she serves notice that, when she does, the competition will have to look out.

She even makes the song seem relevant to AIDS, simply by singing it with so much conviction that we are are forced to consider its sentiments in the context of our own lives. Annie Lennox's Ev'ry Time We Say Goodbye also benefits from the fortuitous but shocking impact of its key phrases - "I die a little . . . I wonder why a litle". Which, by the way, is all that the updating of any performed work, from Shakespeare down, can hope to achieve.

Red, Hot and Blue is a more stimulating item than the competition, Dionne Warwick Sings Cole Porter. Here is a once distinctive if limited singer, trapped within her packaging. The album jacket promises within not merely a disc but rave reviews of it from Sinatra and Quincy Jones. Advertising the advertising may represent a new frontier in hype. The arrangements are lush disco, down to the extended fades in which a single boring riff is endlessly repeated. The vocals, including a bowdlerized I Get a Kick (1990 is a little late to deny the existence of cocaine) are clear but distant. Porter was worldly; Warwick is other- worldly. Only on a post-Charleston Anything Goes are there signs of life, and even here the production effort is out of all proportion to the result. A closing "jazz version" of Night and Day is a joke; a squad of fusionists noodle innocuously while Warwick delivers a vocal identical to her opening "straight version."

Those wishing to celebrate Porter's approaching centenary more traditionally already have a fair choice. Julie Wilson, the reigning diva of New York cabaret, growls through an adventurous choice of songs. She sometimes underlines the jokes, but she does appreciate them; and so will you. Joan Morris, whose roots are in art-song, vaudeville and operetta, is also good at comedy, though less so on heavy Porter drama. Her sparkling My Heart Belongs to Daddy is a lot better than her earnest Night and Day.

Ella's 1956 set, blessedly available on CD, shows why fifties style remains the benchmark. Its best practitioners had an assurance, and a reservoir of repertoire, unmatched by any pop singers before or since. Though some degrees away from Porter's theatrical manner they were in touch with it; had in fact grown up with it. Ella's songbooks (19 of which this was the first) are a bother to jazz and Broadway purists but a delight to the rest of us. True, she doesn't improvise much, but with that gentle voice caressing the melodies, and that gutsy voice attacking the rhythms, who needs it? Nor is she an actress, but she lays out the text so firmly that you can build a theatre in your head. These are not definitive interpretations, but they are essential education. Cole, unless he were having an extremely bad day, would surely have agreed.
