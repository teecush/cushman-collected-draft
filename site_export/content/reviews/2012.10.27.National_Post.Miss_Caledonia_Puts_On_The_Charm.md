Its worth going to Miss Caledonia just to experience Melody A. Johnson singing I've Been Everywhere while twirling a baton, and doing both at ever-increasing speeds. As she performs it, it's the climax of the beauty competition that gives her play its title. Its also the theatrical climax of the show, though it isnt quite the dramatic climax of the story.

Therein lies a flaw, though certainly not a disabling one. I referred to Miss Caledonia as her play" with reason; Johnson wrote it, she performs it all on her own, and at a certain level it seems to be about her. At a more literal level, it's about her mother, born Peggy Douglas, whom Johnson presents as a 1950s teenager, growing up in Ontario farm country, at driveable distance from the bright lights of Hamilton. Peggy is a movie fan who likes Roy Rogers, fantasizes about getting advice from Bing Crosby, and is especially stuck on Debbie Reynolds and on how she was "discovered." She sees her own path to similar fortune as leading through the plentiful array of local beauty pageants. She fails miserably in her first attempt, coming in 11th in a field of 12. However, Peggy perseveres, enrols in a local charm school and proceeds to the biggest contest in the region. Her ambitions meet firm opposition from her farmer-father, but are supported - morally, financially, logistically and semi-secretly - by her mother.

Miss Caledonia was first seen at Summerworks two years ago. It returns to Toronto, expanded and strengthened. The additions and improvements, if memory can be trusted, largely concern the character of Peggy's mother who reacts to the constricting circumstances of her life and home by plastering the walls of the living room with newspaper clippings, carefully selected to happy events. She especially dreams of having an indoor toilet and, when she and her daughter visit a prosperous neighbour, spends an inordinate amount of time wallowing in the well-appointed bathroom. Theres a lovely payoff to this in the plays final section.

As a performer, Johnson has brought self-deprecation to the sharpest comic level. She's a counter-charmer, inspecting the world with a deadpan scowl that insulates her against any suspicions of sentimentality. It's especially effective here where shes playing a character young in years but a veteran in disappointment. Peggy has her dreams, and pursues them, and we root for the pursuit. Determinedly downbeat, shes the last person you'd expect to play a cheerleader; which is why, when she gets to the routine, it's such a joy.

Along the way, she plays other people as well. Its impossible, seeing a self-authored one-woman play in the Tarragons Extra Space, not to make comparisons with Kristen Thomson's I Claudia. Miss Caledonia isn't in that league; it doesnt probe as deeply, or at such length, into as many characters. The farmland setting also prompts associations with the Beattie brothers Wingfield cycle, but Johnson isn't the wizard of vocal versatility that Rod Beattie is. She's a versatile actress, as witness her array of brilliant performances in Trout Stanley, 7 Stories and I Think I Can, but none of those were quick-change operations.

Minor individuals may be faintly presented, but the overall atmosphere comes over very strong. It all comes together at the crucial contest when Peggy is asked to recall a memorable incident from her life and comes up with one that effectively binds self, family and community together. It even suggests, without overstressing, a rapprochement with her father.

What, we wonder but are not told, happened to Peggy once the pageant was done? Well, we know one thing that happened. The play, in effect, spans three generations; Johnson plays her mother telling us about her mother. And yet it feels directly autobiographical. You have to keep reminding yourself that the girl it depicts didnt grow up to be Melody Johnson; she grew up to be Melody Johnsons mother, which on this and other showings, must be a proud accomplishment. The play was directed by Rick Roberts and Aaron Willis, with a choreographic assist from Jane Johan-son; one wonders if Johnson, even wearing two hats, didn't sometimes feel outnumbered at rehearsals. After the Toronto run, she's taking the show to British Columbia, Alberta, and three more locations in Ontario. She's going everywhere, man.
