THEATRE REVIEW

THEATRE PASSE MURAILLE BACKSPACE

---

Layne Coleman's Tijuana Cure is a first-person solo piece that prompts both empathy and embarrassment, with the former winning out. Coleman, best known as an actor and director (and also as former artistic director of Passe Muraille, whose Backspace is hosting this Smash Theatre production) is writing about his journey with his late wife, the novelist and critic Carole Corbeil, to a "last hope" clinic in Mexico. Their purpose was to try a diet cure for what proved to be Corbeil's fatal cancer. Coleman doesn't perform the piece himself, and the actor who stands in for him is in fact a woman, Ieva Lucs.

The Colemans' ordeal has already been the subject of a full-scale play, Scratch, by their daughter Charlotte Corbeil-Coleman (who is a momentary character in this one). That, though, changed the names and many of the details and emerged as an imaginative work in its own right. Tijuana Cure sticks, we must assume, to the facts, physical and mental. It's already been published in The Walrus; I didn't know this while I was watching it, but I did find myself thinking that the recurring queasiness I felt would not have been present if I had been reading the same material as a printed memoir. Live performance, and especially a live audience, change the equation. We're being asked to applaud someone, right then and there, on the quality of his grief.

The saving grace here is that we're also being asked to consider the quality of his happiness, and it's easier to rejoice in that. We are presented with what was plainly a lovely, loving marriage -- something that the theatre, for obvious reasons concerned with dramatic conflict, doesn't often ask us to contemplate. The title seems at first blush ironic, as we know that Carole wasn't cured and didn't survive. The play doesn't take us up to her death; it confines itself to the trip, in the course of which Layne's love for his wife wasn't exactly rediscovered -- it hardly seems to have gone away -- as reaffirmed. To that extent their pilgrimage produced, if not a cure, then a Tijuana tonic.

This of course is very moving, not least because the accompanying spectres of pain and loss must connect to the worst nightmares, imagined or lived, of everybody watching. These factors aren't obsessively dwelt on but they are always present, not least in the constant references to Carole's need for painkillers. We also partake as Layne girds himself--his phrase -- for an expanded role as his wife's mental and physical support. (The diet is an onerous one.) Somewhat more light-heartedly, we watch him doing battle with the rigours and uncertainties of travelling from Toronto to Tijuana via San Diego, and with his relishing of the local colour. Encouraged by Carole, who urges him to enjoy himself as much as possible, he inspects the local red-light district. This in turn connects with amorous memories of his youth: his falling in love with his wife, and some less satisfactory encounters earlier on, at least one of which is excruciatingly entertaining. These reminiscences and others, such as his excitement at seeing The Beatles on Ed Sullivan and the disgust they aroused in his puritanical father, are justified as having run through his head while he was in Mexico. But it seems a dubious peg. It's as if the author is trying to cram into the piece material that, however engaging in itself, doesn't belong there. Again, it would work fine in print. In the less forgiving medium of drama, it's padding.

The casting must be intended to provide a distancing effect, to avoid theobvious danger of self-exploitation. Up to a point it succeeds. Lucs, under Ashlie Corcoran's direction, makes no attempt to imitate Coleman, or indeed to do any kind of male impersonation at all. She's an actress with a strong physical and vocal presence that quite often goes into overdrive: I've never known so much juice to be squeezed from simple place-names. This wide-eyed over-emphasis, pervasive in the earlier parts of the evening, is the direct opposite of the discretion the play needs. It's the wrong kind of alienation. Later on, the performance settles down. A woman's body takes the curse off, while adding a comedic edge to, the narrator's rueful preoccupation with his own physicality. Lucs also of course gets to play all the women in the story, including Carole herself, whose spoken contributions are significantly minimal. That's a tactful decision on the author's part, and it communicates itself to the actress who becomes both simpler and more affecting as the play's emotions take hold. Naked autobiography in the theatre always seems to me like special pleading, sometimes amounting to emotional blackmail. This production, which I saw at a preview, can't avoid the first charge but keeps honourably clear of the second. - Tijuana Cure is at the Passe Muraille Backspace until March 28.
