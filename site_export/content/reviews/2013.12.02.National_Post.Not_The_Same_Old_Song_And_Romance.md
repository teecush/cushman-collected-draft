THEATRE REVIEW

Once

Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

There's a party going on when you enter the theatre to see Once, and it's irresistible. The stage is crowded with people singing, playing, drinking, jigging about if not actually dancing. It all looks very Irish as, given the subject matter to come, it should. Some of the celebrants, it seems, are audience members who got there earlier than you did. They return to the auditorium but the party onstage continues, with cast members boisterously performing a couple of traditional, or at least unattributed, songs. Then one of them is cajoled into singing something that he himself has supposedly written. And this, as they used to say, is where the story really starts. We've been eased, delightfully, into the show proper. It isn't a wholly original idea, but it's brilliant.

From this point on, all the songs in Once, with a couple of broadly parodied exceptions, are the fictional creations of this same Dublin guy, whom the program identifies only as Guy and to whom the script gives no name at all. Guy meets Girl, who's a Czech immigrant with a talent for the piano, and who declares Guy's compositions to be masterpieces, just waiting to be snapped up by some fortunate record company; and indeed in real life they've made it as far as Broadway, where Once has been a great and deserved success. Even before that, they won an Oscar. As they come off the stage, though, the songs are pretty much indistinguishable, with keening and largely incomprehensible words, and tunes that typically start attractively and then wander off into inconsequence. The paradox of Once is that it's a very good musical with a poor score. A further paradox is that the songs, in their way, work. They're hopeless at advancing the story or, except at the most rudimentary level, depicting the characters, but they do create a mood. It's a mood that's superbly reinforced by the show's main attractions: its script and especially its staging.

Last week I described Aladdin as the best stage musical of a movie. I should have stressed that I meant the best stage musical of a movie that was already a musical. Once is adapted from a movie (hence the Oscar), one that I haven't seen; however, I trust the word of people who have, plus my own instincts, that it wasn't a musical but a film with songs. There is a difference: something in the way they move.

The stage show moves beautifully. There's nothing in John Tiffany's production that suggests another medium is being imitated or reproduced; it all feels warmly and indigenously theatrical.It follows in the path of John Doyle's Sondheim revivals by having its actors double as musicians (or vice versa). It departs from it by having them not just play their instruments but dance with them.

Steven Hoggett's choreography moves us rhythmically and enchantingly from scene to scene, with steps that have nothing to do with conventional Broadway stepping, everything with everyday body language.

In this way, it resembles the choreography of Spring Awakening, except that its prevailing mood is tender rather than tough. It's capable of sweeping, stagefilling collective gestures, and also of the most delicate detail. There's a small heartstopping moment in which an older woman sweeps a boy, who's just received crushingly bad news, up into her comforting arms.

It's emblematic of a show with its own flavour of bittersweet. Guy and Girl (whose characters apparently owe something to the show's actual composers, Glen Hansard and Marketa Irglova) look on track to be the predestined romantic couple: ill-sorted perhaps, but what would be the fun if they weren't? Girl has a child, whose absent father may or may not be wholly out of the picture. Guy is writing his songs in memory of a former girlfriend who left to go to New York, and with whom he may or may not wish to be reconciled. Guy comes on to Girl; Girl rebuffs him, but is obviously still interested.

Meanwhile she encourages him to follow his dreams, musical and romantic, all the way to America. That's all there is really; and it necessitates a fair amount of secondact padding. But the suspense of wondering which way they'll jump is enough to buoy the story up; and beyond that there's the city itself.

The show is in part a love letter to Dublin, and the musical's book (by the Irish playwright Enda Walsh) keeps it salty, funny and largely unsentimental. The songs are tied to the lamentable singer-songwriter principle that autobiography counts for more than craft or content, but they both suit and enhance the atmosphere, especially when Guy, Girl and a homemade band get to put them down at a 24-hour recording session.

Bob Crowley' designs are as magical as, though quite different from, his sets from Aladdin; they encase the whole roving action within an Irish snug with a long mirror on its back wall. Everything and everybody gets reflected, but there's nothing hectoring or pretentious about the device; it simply resonates.

Guy is played with casual charm by Stuart Ward and Girl with spiky, complex appeal by Dani de Waal. The supporting cast is splendid, not to mention multi-functional, with specially endearing work from Benjamin Magnuson as a bank manager rendered susceptible to the financial propositions of hero and heroine by the fact that he himself plays guitar (and, as it turns out, doubles on cello). The all-embracing musical metaphor does much to carry the evening. The show's good beginning is matched by its good ending: both logical and surprising - logical because surprising - as good endings should be.
