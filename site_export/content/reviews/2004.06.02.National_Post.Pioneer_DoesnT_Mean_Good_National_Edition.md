PAL JOEY

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

Richard Rodgers, composer, and Lorenz Hart, lyricist, were the greatest songwriting partnership of the 20th century. Pal Joey is probably the most revivable of their musicals and certainly the most revived. A moderate success in its first Broadway outing in 1940, it came back in triumph in 1952, causing many people to claim that it had been ahead of its time. It has, from the outset, had the reputation of being pioneer work, and that reputation is deserved. But pioneer is not necessarily synonymous with good, and the show, in my experience, never works as well as one wants it to. The new Shaw Festival production, though it certainly has its merits, is another in the line of disappointments.

Pal Joey was the first realistic musical comedy. (There had been realistic operettas, notably Show Boat.) Realistic is a relative term in musical theatre, since real people don't sing, but what it means here is that script and score combine to tell a coherent story about believable characters. To adapt a Kenneth Tynan definition of serious drama that I have always liked, they present a view of life from which the pain is not left out. Musical comedies of the 30s, notably those written by Rodgers and Hart themselves, had left the pain almost entirely out; when it crept in, in the superbly despondent lyrics Hart wrote for the teams best ballads, it never had much to do with the rest of the show. And now here, suddenly, was, of all unprecedented things, a musical about a failure.

Pal Joey is based on a series of vignettes by John O'Hara, about a small-time singer and nightclub emcee. They are presented, in arresting syntax and even more arresting spelling, as letters written by "yr old pal Joey to a bandleader-friend, or former friend, whose star is rising as inexorably as Joey's is standing still. Bitterness and jealousy, submerged at the outset, take increasing command of the correspondence.

The musical supplies a plot. Joey, whose looks and charm are both his blessing and his curse, can never keep his hands off an available broad (in his vernacular, mouse"); he is also a compulsive fantasist, whose pick-up line consists of spinning yarns about an imaginary country-club childhood. Newly arrived in Chicago, he meets Vera Simpson, a rich married woman who doesnt believe a word he says but likes everything he does.

She stakes him to his own club, but ends the affair when the going gets risky. Its a serviceable story, though not enough happens in the first act and too much in the second, which involves a confusing set of blackmailing schemes -- a structural imbalance, by the way, that's the precise opposite of the one you find in most musicals.

The strength of the show's book is its consistency of tone, rooted in the seedy clubland it depicts. Every character in it is unashamedly venal, apart from one innocent. ("And she, Rodgers liked to point out, was stupid." I dont want to impugn the shows integrity, but actually she emerges as rather smart.) It must have had quite an impact in 1940; musical audiences were used to dirt, but not to dirt with a purpose.

In one vital respect, though, the musical's script betrays the spirit of O'Hara -- even though OHara himself wrote it. (Or co- wrote it. Much of the work seems to have been done, uncredited, by the original director, George Abbott. That gives Abbott, also the co- author of Three Men on a Horse, two shows in this years Festival. I'm surprised more fuss hasn't been made of this.) We lose Joeys narrating voice. Maybe Broadway wasnt ready for a first-person musical, but without that unifying consciousness Pal Joey becomes just another show, scene into song into scene.

Which brings us to the great uncomfortable secret. Because Pal Joey is Rodgers and Hart's most adventurous work, it has generally been rated as their best. But it is, by their standards, a second- rate score. Yes, it has Bewitched, whose velvety candour ("Vexed again, perplexed again/Thank God I can be over-sexed again) always sounds bracing, especially if you grew up with the bowdlerized pop version. It also has I Could Write a Book, but I suspect this song's popularity derives from being the nearest thing in the show to a conventional love song. Its really rather ordinary. It also has nothing to do with the people who are supposed to be singing it, being the same kind of precocious boy-girl duet the pair had been turning out since Manhattan, 15 years earlier.

None of the songs fit very well with the dialogue (and the transitions are usually awkward); OHara's characters speak with their own voices; Hart's sing with Harts. In his earlier shows this was a joy; here its a liability. He knew what was needed, and he tried, but he tried, as in the self-consciously frank" numbers like What Is a Man and Den of Iniquity, too obviously hard. That leaves the nightclub numbers, brassy parodies that Rodgers, in particular, seems to have enjoyed writing. They sound, in retrospect, like his farewell to jazz; three years later he would, professionally speaking, have divorced Hart, married Oscar Hammerstein, given birth to Oklahoma! and embarked on a career of respectability.

The best of all the spoofs, an arch horticultural parade called Flower Garden of My Heart, is ruined here by having its performers portrayed as tuneless incompetents; it's only funny if it's done solemnly. Most of the time, though, Alisa Palmers production is solid and Amy Walshs choreography lively. William Schmuck has designed some economically stylish sets, one of which seems to be paying tribute to the original, or at least to a widely reproduced photograph of it.

There are good performances, with Adam Brazier -- who acts, sings and dances extremely well -- playing Joey as his own worst enemy, loud of mouth but essentially innocent, "fresh" in a double sense. This is original and plausible, at least until Vera throws him out, when the script gives him the cue to turn venomous, and he passes it up. As his mistress, Laurie Paton looks stunning, speaks her lines with unfailing wit and joins the swelling ranks of fine actors you never imagined could sing so well. Her acting and singing, though, haven't fused; and her rendition of Bewitched is undercut by restless staging. Vera is supposed to be a woman of complete social and sexual assurance; she shouldn't be breaking up her reverie by wandering aimlessly around the room

Lorne Kennedy is too effortful a tough guy as Mike, the club owner; Neil Barclay, by contrast, is effortlessly in style, more so, indeed, than anyone in the show, as the gangster Ludlow Lowell. With Jenny L. Wright as his moll, he does right by both his scenes and his songs. Whether this character should have songs is another matter. The fact that he has shows how much this musical is stuck in an older tradition in which anyone could sing anything. Most revivals have assigned his numbers to somebody else.

They would actually sit rather well on Joey himself: not as a performer but as a character. One of the more tantalizing things about Pal Joey is that so many of the numbers almost work as commentary, as if the show were a concept musical waiting to happen. Joey's brash audition song You Mustn't Kick It Around happens to be my favourite thing in the score, but I still found myself wishing someone would ignore his advice and kick it in precisely that direction, even at the cost of wholesale rewriting. As it is, when Joey does the boastful Happy Hunting Horn, a long number that could be commentary, as if it were part of the plot, I get nervous. I remember that Mrs. Simpson is expecting him to join her in the car outside; and I am sure she is one dame who just hates to be kept waiting: a real Uptown Mouse.
