SIDE BY SIDE BY SONDHEIM

Bluma Appel Theatre

- - -

I spent some of my time at Side by Side by Sondheim smiling and tapping my feet, but rather more of it groaning and grinding my teeth. At moments I even averted my eyes. This 1976 revue, designed to make audiences more aware of the music and lyrics of Stephen Sondheim, may have outlived its historical purpose. Its subject has become a cultural icon; people who don't like his funny, beautiful, probing and unnerving songs by now are never going to like them. And, as things have turned out, he's written nearly as many musicals since this retrospective as he had before it.

None of this, of course, need prevent Side by Side from furnishing a decent evening's entertainment. This CanStage revival, though, arrives loaded with handicaps. Some of them are inherent in the piece itself. One is the opening, a coy and genteel arrangement of Comedy Tonight. (The song is meant to be a celebration of low comedy, for Jupiter's sake.) Another is the closing, a medley of, basically, song titles that's equally cloying and even more insufferably knowing. The mandated two- piano accompaniment, though perfectly well executed, imposes a conservatory sound that knocks the guts out of the music

Then there's the matter of casting. The first London-New York production was built around and sparked by two first-rate singing comediennes, Millicent Martin and Julia McKenzie. None of the four Toronto performers is in that league; they obviously get the wit of the lyrics, but they are very self-conscious about putting it over. The original was held together by the dapper, deadpan narration of Ned Sherrin, who wrote and delivered disposable jokes that nobody else seems able to get away with.

Eda Holmes' production dispenses with a separate narrator, turning her cast into a quartet of singer-dancer-talkers. I confess that when I heard about this I thought it a good idea, a way of bestowing unity and fluidity on what could easily become a stop-and- go succession of disconnected songs. I had reckoned without the discomfort level of actors, as opposed to raconteurs or stand-up comics, asked to deposit uncharacterized material in the audience's laps. It's even worse when the banter is divided between them, and they have to pretend that it's spontaneous. And when new and supposedly topical lines are shoved in among the old, it becomes positively painful.

John Thompson has designed a Shakespearean history kind of set, full of steps and darkness and varied by some sharply etched projections. There are a few other visual effects; I liked the strip of blue that unfolded when the I Remember singer got to the word "sky" and folded up again at the song's regretful end, but I was totally baffled by the snowfall that accompanied Send in the Clowns.

The costumes are uniformly unbecoming. A few of the ensemble numbers give Holmes a chance to show her skill at directing actors and text; the magnificent Someone in a Tree, a combined history- philosophy lesson set to a steadily climbing beat, is done with an exemplary blend of delicacy and force. (But how one misses a full band at the finish.)

Most of the conversational bite of You Must Meet My Wife, a barbed duet from A Little Night Music, comes through. I wish they hadn't cut Barcelona, a post-coital number that's one of the rare theatre songs that does what we are always told they should do, i.e. the work of a one-act play. Sondheim in the '60s and '70s wrote many songs about discordant marriages, and this show makes a theme of them. Holmes ends several of them by bringing on a third, silent partner at the end. She must be trying for a through-line, but it comes off as just a mannerism.

I wonder, rather than employing a full-time choreographer, Jane Johanson, if Holmes might not have done better by devising her own, as she did this year for Floyd Collins. The result would probably have been homelier, but it might have been tighter, and this material, in this setting, doesn't really need the old razzle- dazzle, which in any case here seems like enthusiastic winsomeness.

There's a paradox about taking Sondheim's songs out of their original settings; they might seem less equipped for independent life than the self-sufficient tunes of, say, Cole Porter, but they can sometimes drag enough of their contexts with them to give the actors something to act. The corresponding danger, especially in the solo numbers, is that the performers will try too hard.

Here they all do. Dan Chameroy does it least and comes off best. His seasons at Stratford, mainly in plays, have sharpened his interpretive skills; he does a stabbing Could I Leave You, here transformed into a song about a literally gay divorce, a passionate Marry Me a Little and a yearning Anyone Can Whistle, topped by a pardonably flaunted falsetto.

Jay Turvey, so good in Floyd Collins, seems lost without a consistent role to play, but he does manage a lovely I Remember. The allocation of songs is hard on him, and harder still on Julain Molnar, who only comes into her own in The Boy From ..., a hilarious bossa nova spoof that she does toothy and bespectacled. Mary Ann McDonald is the most effortful, and sometimes her efforts pay off; she strains every nerve to make I'm Still Here the show-stopping climax of the evening, but the song only works if the singer is a star, or at least a former star.

An evening like this should leave you wanting more. There is, after all, far more good Sondheim available, even within this show's foreshortened time frame. But, in fact, you want less. It all feels long, and that's wrong. Very wrong.
