There was once--and still is, of course, in reruns -- a delightful musical number on The Simp-sons in which the kids at Springfield Elementary pay tribute to the Mediocre Presidents of the U.S.A. (William Henry Harrison! I died in 30 days!") John Adams didn't make that cut, nor should he. All the same, when you think about what the same song calls "caretaker presidents, its hard not to think of Adams, Americas second president (one term only) and first vice-president. Also, one might have thought that he was the Famous Founding Father least likely to get his own miniseries. But he has one now, and it's good.

This is Adams' second brush with showbiz stardom. He was one of the delegates to the Continental Congress whose deliberations were the subject of the musical 1776; and if not quite the leading man -- the show didnt have one -- he could be counted first among the casts equals. He was the prickly commentator at the Congress, impatient with his fellows' reluctance to get on with it and declare independence already. When it came time to prepare the document, he stepped down, knowing his own unpopularity, and -- with the encouragement of the canny and persistent Benjamin Franklin -- handed the job to the more personable Tom Jefferson.

The TV series takes much the same view of all these characters, and its second episode covers almost exactly the same events as the musical. But, with seven hours to fill instead of two, the series can afford to be more leisurely; it starts earlier in Adams' life and goes on much later. This allows Paul Giamatti to play an Adams whose resentments run far deeper than those of his singing counterpart but who also has more of a sense of humour -- about himself if about nobody else.

Hes surrounded by other illustrious Oscar losers: the luminous Laura Linney as his wife, Abigail, Tom Wilkinson stealing all available scenes as a peg-legged Franklin who seems to be auditioning for Long John Silver, and Sarah Polley, who emerges at the end of Episode Four as the grown-up version of the Adams only daughter, Nabby. We also get Stephen Dillane as Jefferson, presented here as a romantic malcontent with what seems to be an unrequited eye for Abigail, the ubiquitous Zeljko Ivanek as the recalcitrant delegate Dickinson; and David Morse, a George Washington who, with the aid of a prosthetic nose, is physically right on the money: He looks, in fact, as if he's stepped right off it.

I cannot tell a lie: In this version, Washington's elevation to the presidency comes as rather a surprise, because we have seen so little of him. Hes been at war while Adams battles have been fought in council, first at home, then -- on diplomatic missions to France, Holland and England -- abroad. Many of the textbook highlights of the American revolution -- the Boston Tea Party, the British capture of Philadelphia, Valley Forge, the British surrender -- have taken place offscreen. The show partakes of its hero's integrity by keeping him, and us, well away from them. Can you imagine old Hollywood being so scrupulous?

We began with Adams, lawyer and farmer, trudging through the merciless Boston snow. (A later episode will find him reassuring his 14-year-old son John Quincy, who's been posted to St. Petersburg, that the Russian winter has nothing to teach a Massachusetts man.) Almost the first thing he does on arriving home is to take off his wig, to reveal a perfect egg head; the key to Giamattis excellent performance is that, though gentlemanly fashion obliges him to wear those hairpieces (and he gets through a lot of them), he really cant bear them.

The main event of the first episode is his defence -- against all his political preferences but in line with his professional and personal ethics -- of British soldiers falsely accused of mowing down local townsmen. The trial was presented at possibly excessive length, but it's vital in setting Adams' character note; hes always prepared to do the unpopular thing. Theres a side of him that revels in it. Abigail has to advise him to shorten some of his learned courtroom rhetoric and, as usual, she's right. Theirs was a famously long and devoted marriage; Linney's role for the first two weeks seems confined to saying yes, John" and -- at moments of exceptionally high drama -- "no, John, which seems a waste. But she has more chances later.

While John is away at the congress, she runs the home and the farm, apparently (though it seems historically unlikely) without any hired help. By way of compensation, U.S. history develops an appealing habit of parading past her door, on its way to and from the war. When Nabby asks, Why do boys have all the fun?" Abigail pauses in her hoeing to reply grimly, "Because we let them.

She does get to join John in Paris and London, though even here her fun is limited. John proves no diplomat, and smarts when his efforts to get the French onside are slighted in favour of the smoother, more flirtatious Franklin. Ah, to be 70 again," says Ben, ogling the Versailles beauties. (Thats prior to being discovered playing chess in the bath with his Parisian mistress, who may seem 70 again, but not 60.)

John is secure with Abigail, however jealous and uneasy with everyone else. He knows his own worth, and the show persuades us that hes mostly right; there's an interesting contrast between Giamatti's way of worrying at his lines and the legato delivery favoured by his British co-stars. Theres too much heart-pumping music, a cheapening I thought HBO had outgrown; even the opening theme seems to think its The Magnificent Seven. Sets and costumes are sumptuous, as is the camerawork; the Dutch scenes look like Vermeers. As historical dramas go, this is less fun than Rome but more trustworthy. In both respects, it leaves The Tudors in the dust.

-Episode Four of John Adams airs April 6 on TMN. Episodes Five to Seven air on the following Sundays.
