DOC

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

---

The best scenes of Doc are also the best to have appeared in Soulepper's entire season of Canadian collectables. (They must be collectables because they've been published in collections.)

In Diana Leblanc's finely acted production of Sharon Pollock's 1984 play, R.H. Thomson plays the doc of the title, whose name is Ev. Ev, a character based on the author's father, is a family physician whose own family is the first casualty of his devotion to his work.

The play is a memory piece, juggling periods, and Thomson switches with absolute authority between at least three incarnations: passionate young visionary, dedicated but weary professional, aging grouch, all united by a bracing sharpness of tongue and mind and a crippling incapacity for expressing tenderness, or even perhaps for feeling it. For Ev, charity ends at home.

He's a budding specialist, diverted into general practice, presumably in Pollock's own province of New Brunswick, though I don't remember hearing it specified. He fights fiercely and profanely to improve the local health facilities, and ends up with a hospital named after him. His concern for his patients is passionate and personal; he's forever leaving home at unsocial hours to make house calls.

You may wonder whether the play has to show quite so many times that Ev is a raging workaholic who neglects his wife and children, but Ev's drive is unrelenting, so it figures that the scenes depicting him should be unrelenting as well.

In order to attend an old patient's son who's fallen under a thresher, he absents himself from the birth of his own son. It's monstrous, but he does have his own kind of point. Anyway, you can't argue with him. No one can.

The chief casualty is of course his wife, Bob (actual name, Eloise Roberts). When they first met, she was a feisty and, by her own believable account, super-competent nurse. After their marriage, and for reasons that are never completely explained, he forbade her to work. (He says that having her in his operating theatre would create conflict, but that doesn't account for why he won't let her work anywhere else.) So she vegetates, neglects her two children, or so her husband says, and becomes an alcoholic. Jane Spidell gives a harrowing, emotionally naked performance as Bob, grabbing your attention with her throaty vocal attack and pinioning it with an unsparing descent through anger, loss and despair.

For solace, strictly psychological, Bob turns to Oscar, Ev's lifelong friend, who has drifted into doctoring and whom Ev uses, with mostly unthinking patronage, to pick up his own slack. Oscar is a go-along smiler who can be pushed too far, and Derek Boyes captures both sides of him admirably. He is, of course, in love with Bob, though Ev won't even pay him the compliment of being jealous. Bob, though, is good and jealous of Ev's office nurse Valma on whom he is completely reliant, both at work and, in extreme circumstances, at home. This, too, is a platonic relationship, though Bob understandably doesn't believe it. We get quite a picture of Valma, considering that she doesn't actually appear, the play being not a triangle but a quadrilateral with one invisible side.

There are, though, two other characters -- or rather two other roles, since the author has written herself into the play in two separate guises: as Catherine, a woman in her thirties, and as Katie, who is Catherine entering her teens.

This is where the play stops being good. It's framed as Catherine's return to see her dad, after years of semi-estrangement that she's spent being a writer; but their relationship, and Catherine's whole persona, remain shadowy. The play's admirers enthuse about how its fluid structure permits Catherine and Katie to address one another across the years. But they have remarkably little of interest to say, and the play would work as well if there were only one of them. (For a witty and moving use of this elder-meets-younger device, see -- if we're ever allowed to see it -- Tom Stoppard's The Invention of Love.)

Hannah Gross makes a singularly assured debut as Katie, and Carmen Grant does what she can with the ungrateful role of her older ego. Ev's mother committed suicide, and this is also meant to hang more significantly over the evening than it actually does. In trying to give these matters greater weight, Leblanc makes the play's last scene overly sentimental and its first one overly portentous. The show begins with the characters picturesquely posed on Astrid Janson's set, a perspex structure punched with actor-sized holes, and presumably meant as a metaphor for memory; it looks impressive, but it's hard initially to make out who's who and what they're talking about. Once they start getting to grips with one another, the production itself gets a grip and it's a fierce one.
