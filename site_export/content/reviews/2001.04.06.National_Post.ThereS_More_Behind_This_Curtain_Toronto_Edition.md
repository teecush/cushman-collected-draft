SO IT'S COME TO THIS

Tallulah's Cabaret, Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

So what it's come to is an amiable evening of raconteuring that could only with the most elaborate courtesy be described as a play, or even -- so low-key is it -- as a show. Emmett Foster, who wrote and performed it, describes himself at one point as an old man, though in fact he looks youthful but creased. Let's settle for well preserved. He has a lot to have been preserved from, including a Mormon upbringing, a mother who married nine times, major addictions to alcohol and cocaine, a wild gay life in Manhattan, and 18 years as personal assistant to legendary New York impresario Joseph Papp.

Foster's major asset in telling us about all this is his absolute naturalness. He walks on stage in black pants and sweater, sits on a stool at a microphone, and just talks. His major liability is that he seems to assume everything he talks about is intrinsically interesting simply because it happened to him, and it isn't. A woman with nine husbands certainly ought to be fascinating, if only as a case study in optimism, but we need to know more about her as a person than Foster seems willing to vouchsafe until late in the proceedings. Then he does start filling us in on some of her eccentricities, and especially on her cantankerous impatience when interrogated by doctors, but at this point he is talking about something else and he only brings her in as a reference.

We do gather that Foster's multiplicity of stepfathers only left him looking for a real one. He seems to have found a temporary paternal figure in a rich Californian who, without making demands on his body, financed him through university where he found a vocation in theatre. But the major force in his life turned out to be Joe Papp, for whom he functioned as chauffeur and gofer, confidant, minder and -- in the painful months leading up to Papp's death from cancer -- unofficial nurse.

When Foster talks about his boss, the piece comes to life. We get a clear picture of Papp as a driven egocentric who used people ruthlessly but did not manipulate or exploit them, much. In some ways he seems to have been rather naive. (Maybe egomaniacs have to be; they certainly can't afford a sense of irony.) He honestly could not see why Foster should not be at his disposal 24 hours a day, and really he was right; Foster connived at it and thrived on it. As he says himself, it gave his life purpose. He loved Joe and Joe maybe loved him -- he certainly protected and respected him. (The relationship was non-sexual. Foster praises Papp as a straight man who, before it became fashionable, took a stand on gay rights.)

His most revealing anecdote tells how, in the course of an extensive trawl through the city's sleazier hot spots, he lost Papp's passport the night before an international trip. The next day he tried ineffectually to cover up but soon had to confess. Papp made a swift phone call for emergency documentation, reassured Foster that he did not feel lied to ("a lie is when you say something to hurt people") and advised his protege, for his own good, to cut back on the drugs and booze. Papp literally had no time for other people but when he did find some, he used it. A man like that inspires loyalty, especially in a boy like that. The two ended up making a suicide pact: If either became incapacitated through illness, the other would arrange for euthanasia. This was to cause problems later. Foster tells us enough about Papp for his own purposes, but maybe not enough for ours.

For years, after all, Papp was maybe the most powerful figure in New York theatre. He started free seasons in Central Park, and had them institutionalized as the New York Shakespeare Festival. He parlayed that into the powerhouse downtown multiplex known as the Public Theater. For a time, he ran the theatrical component of Lincoln Center. He produced Hair, one of the most successful American musicals, and A Chorus Line, the most successful of all. He had a missionary enthusiasm for bringing theatre to audiences and players who might otherwise be excluded from it, for reasons of cash or culture or both.

He maintained his passion for Shakespeare and had lucid, often unconventional opinions about him, especially when it came to Troilus and Cressida. (When I met him, I expected to find a hustler. Instead I found myself talking to a scholar. That's not to say he wasn't a hustler as well.) I went to Foster's show hoping to discover more about Papp's theatre from somebody who had been there; at the least, more backstage stories that actually had something to do with what went on backstage.

Foster only mentions one actor, Meryl Streep, and one show, A Chorus Line; he paints a vivid picture of the off-Broadway first night, and how the entire audience went crazy and stood and cheered when the key changed and the brass blared on the closing number. This figures, not just because it was a great show and Papp's biggest hit (for years it funded the rest of his operation), but because Foster himself is temperamentally a Broadway baby, in love with the glitziest musicals. One would like to know how this sat with Papp, who often expressed disdain for Broadway but was not above making common cause with it.

To hear some of this, I would be prepared to sacrifice Foster's own thoughts on life, though to be fair these do become more compelling when he moves into his post-Papp period; if that relationship made him a person, then the person he is automatically becomes more worth listening to.

He laughs at his own coke-induced paranoia that led him to fantasize about being stalked by the authorities because of his homosexuality. (The joke proves double-edged; his Toronto audience seemed inclined to take the conspiracy seriously.) He mentions his own incredulity at not contracting AIDS, not to mention that of his friends. He also tells us he has given up on all his addictions including sex, the latter on hold until he finds someone he can commit to, which, he implies, he is "too chicken" to contemplate. He can be ruefully amusing, though his background and demeanour (I don't mean the Mormons) lead us to anticipate a higher standard of one-liners than he actually delivers.

His message to us is that we should be honest with ourselves, which is not likely to start any fights. There are both worse and better ways of spending an evening.
