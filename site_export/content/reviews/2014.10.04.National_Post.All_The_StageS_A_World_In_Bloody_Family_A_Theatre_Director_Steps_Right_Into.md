Clytemnestra is doing her ironing, patiently by the look of it. There's a knock at the door, and she admits an earnest young man who would like to interview her. He turns out to be a theatre director who wants to tell her story yet again, this time from her point of view. He thinks she's got a raw deal from previous playwrights. Surprisingly, she doesn't altogether agree.

So begins Bloody Family, a theatrical meditation on the roots of Western drama and indeed of Western civilization. Clytemnestra is the only character to appear in all three parts of Aeschylus' Oresteia, the one Greek tragic trilogy to have survived in complete form. In the first play she murders her husband Agamemnon; in the second she herself is killed by her avenging son Orestes; in the third she's a ghost, blaming the Furies for falling down on the job of tormenting her tormentor. The apparently endless chain of human and superhuman vengeance is only broken when the gods step in and establish the first ever courts of law. (Orestes gets off, with an Olympian caution.)

The story has been re-told and re-interpreted by countless other playwrights, beginning with Aeschylus' own near-contemporaries. In Bloody Family, we get to hear a scene from a play by Euripides, Iphigenia in Aulis (in, as it happens, a free translation by a couple of British guys who had their own agenda). It tells how, on the eve of the Trojan war, Agamemnon sacrificed his daughter Iphigenia to the gods, so that the winds might blow and allow his ships to sail to Troy. Clytemnestra never forgave him for allowing their daughter to be killed. According to Bloody Family, which simplifies the issues a bit, this was her main motive for killing her husband.

The show doesn't list a director, but it was "created" by Philip McKee and Tanja Jacobs. Jacobs plays Clytemnestra, and is wondrously enigmatic. Emotions pour out of her, and yet she maintains an outward serenity. (Ironing boards will do that to you; cf. Look Back in Anger). Answering her visitor's questions, or reading the Euripides scene with the actress slated to play her daughter, she gives full vent to the mother's terror and anger. And yet, when asked whether her husband's conduct was justified, she calmly replies that of course it was. There was a war on. And, by the way, she doesn't believe in art as an instrument of social change. And she doesn't like the theatre, because it isn't real.

McKee, a director by trade, here plays the director, and brings off a very entertaining piece of what I take to be selfsatire: an eager-beaver theorist, proudly brandishing his iPhone, and taken aback when his enterprising research project fails to yield the results he expected. We then shift into a different mode, one favoured by French dramatists of the last century; a situation from Greek myth is re-imagined in disarmingly domestic terms. Orestes, following a trail of bloodstains on the floor, comes home to kill his mother; she asks him to stay for a few days in hopes he'll change his mind. He can have his old room. The loving look she bestows on him while he's sleeping is a peak moment. She makes him sandwiches; he makes her a coffin. He doesn't like the theatre either, which surprises her given that he acts so dramatic. In the end, the story takes its predestined course.

We switch modes again. Director McKee calls his Orestes (Ishan Dave) in for a special rehearsal, despite the fact that said actor is feeling awful. We're at the last part of the Oresteia; Orestes has to be worked into an extreme state of mind and body befitting a victim of the furies whose fate can only be determined by the invention of democratic justice. The director subjects him to rigorous exercises ("do pushups during this speech ... I'll do them with you") and we laugh at the pretentiousness of it all; we also sympathize when the actress who read Iphigenia (Norah Sadava) arrives in a state of high indignation to rescue her colleague. And yet ... it does seem to work. When Orestes says "pain has taught me the wisdom of rituals," we believe him.

Bloody Family is partly the latest re-telling of an old story, and partly a commentary on current theatrical practice. Both aspects are fascinating, but they don't often mesh. When they do, though, the stage resonates; the justification for re-living the old saga of endless retribution is "these things don't stop happening." The project was partly prompted, in 2011, by a radio program about Afghanistan. It almost feels like a simpler time.

Life, Death and the Blues might well be an alternate title for Bloody Family (and for many other plays as well). In fact it belongs to an autobiographical show by Raoul Bhaneja, actor, writer and - we now learn - musician. It too is a piece with a double identity; the publicity calls it a "concert/theatre hybrid" and the concert part is much the better - great, in fact. The theatre, or drama, section begins enjoyably with Bhaneja in shades, as a composite of every gutturaltalking bluesman that was ever cartooned. He then tells us that that's quite enough of stereotypes and proceeds to tell us his own story: that of a part-Indian, part-Irish, North-of-England -born Canadian, who grew up with a passion for the blues but initially felt abashed about singing and playing them.

Quite why is never really clear, though he's given an on-stage antagonist in Divine Brown, a fine lady singer who tells him that he'll never sing the blues until he gets in touch with his "roots." This strikes me as malarkey, especially given how tangled his roots actually are. He does, with her prompting, sing a traditional tune from India, and he does it beautifully, but no more so than when he's singing black American music. He pays tribute to blues musicians who have helped or influenced him, though his historical memory seems to be short-term. (How do you chronicle blues singers without mentioning Bessie Smith? And was T-Bone Walker the first to blend blues and jazz? Would have been news to Louis Armstrong.)

Eda Holmes directs, and has staged the material better than she's shaped it. Projection design, by Cameron Davis, is excellent. But in the end, it's all about the music. The divine Divine enchants the joint, especially when singing gospel. Bhaneja is a commanding singer, and a demon of a harmonica-player. A terrific three-man band backs him up, and there's a guest musician winding up every show. On the first night, it was Chris Whiteley, whose guitarplaying could get you high.

Bloody Family runs through October 5 at the Theatre Centre; Life, Death and the Blues runs through October 19 at Theatre Passe Muraille
