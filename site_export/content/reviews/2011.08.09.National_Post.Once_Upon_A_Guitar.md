THEATRE REVIEW

Maria Severa Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

Maria Severa Onofriana sang the blues, in Portuguese. More exactly, she was an early exponent - some say the inventor - of the guitar-based form known as fado. She was born in Lisbon in 1820, and died 26 years later. She was also a prostitute and her mother owned a tavern in one of the city's more colourful neighbourhoods. Her lovers included the Count of Vimioso, aristocrat and bullfighter.

Those, which seem to be almost the only known facts about Maria, are faithfully incorporated in Maria Severa, the new musical at the Shaw Festival, with book, music and lyrics by Jay Turvey and Paul Sportelli. The music, the actual sound and flavour of fado is, by the authors' own account, what attracted them to their subject, and they communicate that attraction powerfully to the audience. The songs they've written for Maria herself, who's given a spitfire performance by Julie Martell, are melodically moody and rhythmically complex; a number that she shares with a street-walker friend, titled Bread and Butter, comes over like Portugal's answer to Brecht and Weill. Sometimes the score strays further afield, or maybe that should be closer to home; the ensemble number At the Bullfight, is high-end contemporary Broadway, and so, even more, is the beautiful love-duet I Can't Touch You with Words. The lyrics can be pungent, though they can also, too often, sound earnestly explanatory. The book is strong on atmosphere, short on story, and confusing on structure.

The main focus is on the doomed passion of Maria and her high-born matador, here called Armando, who first picks her up, or is picked up by her, when out slumming with his younger brother. They fall in love, to the fury of Armando's mother, who wants to repair the family fortunes by marrying him off to an heiress. (Though if he's such a star of the bullring, couldn't he repair them himself? But maybe sporting economics were different back then.) Clara, the young lady in question, is a rather subtly drawn character, a sheltered drawing-room pianist who is fascinated by Maria and her songs of love and social protest and who doesn't actually want to marry Armando. He therefore seems no end of a wimp for giving in to the maternal demands. For all that the actor, Mark Uhre, can do for him in the way of voice, presence and even wit, he's an unsatisfactory hero. And the show seems to realize this because towards the end it gives increasing attention to Carlos (Jeff Irving), Maria's accompanist and companion-since-childhood, who nurses a hopeless love for her. Carlos actually starts the show off as a narrator ("There is a song/There is a singer/There's a guitar/and there's a player"), and the switch from commentator to protagonist is hard to accommodate. If it's really all about Maria and Carlos, then why have we been spending so much time with Armando?

There are nine characters, and though the dialogue serves them well, the narrative runs out of things for them to do. That opening number has most of them saying in turn that "it was my fault."

"It" must refer to Maria's fate, but it's never quite stated what that was, which makes responsibility rather hard to apportion. (In actual fact, she died unromantically of tuberculosis.) Anyway, the guilt thing has been forgotten by the last number, even though in staging it's a mirror-image of the first. The second act features a succession of solo turns, for the cast members who haven't had one yet. One of them, for Maria's friend Jasmine (Saccha Dennis), works better on the cast CD than on the stage, even though it has a big dance component. It's a strong character piece in itself, but it's a distraction (and the preceding dialogue tells us in advance what it's going to say). Jenny L. Wright has been bringing down the house all evening as Maria's lovably caustic mama who, rather like Mama Brice in Funny Girl, fancies that she knows better than her daughter what the customers really want; giving her a number to prove it seems somehow anti-climactic. The only one of these songs that both relates to and advances the plot is The Money Tree, in which Clara (a lovely performance by Jacqueline Thair) describes her pretty, wealthy, cloistered and frustrated existence. It stops the show without even trying.

Sharry Flett, whom I never knew could sing, gives an icily commanding account of the kind of scheming matriarch whom you expect to encounter in the Shaw's non-musical repertoire, her deadest recent ringer being the mother in The Cassilis Engagement. Jonathan Gould plays her other son, a nasty bit of wastrel, and Neil Barclay is a kindly priest, blackmailed by the prospect of being sent to do missionary work in Paraguay and, for some reason, the only person with an accent. There are no weak performing links, and Martell's Maria is a triumph; musically and dramatically she burns up the stage. Jackie Maxwell's direction fits everything smoothly together, Eda Holmes has devised choreography that's alternately quirky and sinuous, and Judith Bowden's set is cleverly shaped to suggest a guitar. The show teaches one unlooked-for lesson: It's acceptable in opera for a character to sing while dying but in a musical - well, that's why musicals have books. More generally, this is a talented evening that hasn't decided whether to be a portrait or a whole canvas.
