AN INSPECTOR CALLS

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

J. B. Priestley's An Inspector Calls can be played quite effectively in a realistic mode, as a detective drama with sociological overtones and a delayed metaphysical twist.

The Shaw production, the first of its season, brings the play literally, down to earth. Peter Hartwell's set, one of his best, is a colonnade with an alarming elevator door at its back and a couple of adjoining planes -- tectonic plates, perhaps --jutting out in front.

On these sits the posh family dining room in which the play happens. Those lift doors clank like prison gates, and there seem to be chains hanging down from the ceiling. (This last detail struck me as a bit much.) The production was to have been directed by Neil Munro, and there is an identifiably Munrovian air of High Concept about it.

Jim Mezon took over when Munro fell ill, and he has done a first-rate job. The play has been beautifully choreographed to reflect the guilt-trips and power struggles that make up its action, and the excitement is all but continuous.

Benedict Campbell plays Inspector Goole, and he doesn't shout; he's a quiet but implacable interrogator (and apparently self-appointed judge) pressing insistently on the nerve of his subjects' collective conscience -- a commodity in which some of them have a greater share than others. He's calling on the Birlings, a prosperous family living in the north of England just before the start of the First World War. (The play was written just before the end of the Second.)

A young woman has killed herself by the excruciating method of swallowing disinfectant, and all present turn out to have had, unknown to one another, a hand in her downfall. Mr. Birling, one of the town's most prominent industrialists, started it off by firing her from his factory, when she helped lead a strike for higher wages.

Daughter Sheila had her dismissed from her next job in a dress shop, for seeming to laugh at her when she tried on something unflattering. Sheila's fiance Gerald Croft (an upwardly mobile match for the Birlings) kept the girl as his mistress, treated her well, but then cut her loose. Mrs. Birling and son Eric contributed as well, but since their guilts are only revealed after the intermission, it would be breaching the critical code to describe them here.

Whatever his stature as a moralist, Priestley at his best was a superb dramatic craftsman. Judicial process proverbially make for terrific theatre, and this play is one long trial-scene, with the witnesses varied enough and the story grim enough, to stop any threat of monotony in its tracks.

If there's a heroine it's Sheila, who fiercely admits her responsibility and is both agonized and repulsed by her relatives' ability to slip out of theirs; Moya O'Connell plays her with the same force and delicacy that lit up her debut last year in The Circle. Does the Shaw need another actress who can play blazing moral fervour and make us laugh? (Sheila has the play's few witty lines, mainly of the corrosive variety.) Well, never mind: It's got one now.

The shiftier young males, weak and drunken Eric and smug and sober Gerald, are well done by Andrew Bunker and Graeme Somerville; both men are capable of remorse, as long as there's no other option.

As for their elders: Mary Haney overdoes the ramrod aspect of Mrs. Birling, one of those committee ladies who give charity a bad name, but Peter Hutt is a persuasively oily and evasive Mr. B., a self-proclaimed, self-satisfied, self-made man.

This is the second suicide play to arrive on out stages in as many nights -- the first was Soulpepper's 'Night, Mother -- and it strikes me as a more persuasive, and certainly a livelier, account both of what might lead to such an event and of its effect on the survivors.

Like 'Night, Mother it has an ending -- in Priestley's case shamelessly borrowed from Gogol's similarly-titled Government Inspector -- that you can see coming from a mile off and that still pins you to your seat when it does. - An Inspector Calls runs until Nov. 2.
