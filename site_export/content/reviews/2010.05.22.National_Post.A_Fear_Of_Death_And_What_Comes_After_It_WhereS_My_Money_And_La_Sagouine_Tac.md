John Patrick Shanleys play Wheres My Money? is a dark comedy of Manhattan manners, befitting the screenwriter of Moonstruck. Shanley is also the author of Doubt, one of those pseudo-serious dramas beloved of both Broadway and Hollywood, but the present piece is far more entertaining, and also more searching.

It's constructed somewhat on the model of Arthur Schnitzler's La Ronde, though only somewhat. Its a succession of duologues in which a character from one scene will be paired with a different partner in the next. It does not, however, adhere to this pattern with the same geometric purity as its predecessor. A couple of the couples get to play return matches, and a few scenes even involve a third party, though admittedly under peculiar circumstances: To make no bones about it, the third person is always a ghost. The difference that makes all the difference is that most of Schnitzlers scenes depict one-night stands while Shanley's characters are all -- I nearly said trapped but anyway, all in relationships.

It goes like this. In Scene One, Celeste, a deeply unsuccessful actress, confides in her friend Natalie about the two men in her life: her loser druggie boyfriend and her older S&M lover. Natalie tells her, very emphatically, to get a life, preferably one like her own: She married a lawyer. However, that marriage, when we follow Natalie home, turns out to have its own problems, mainly of non-communication. Natalie's husband, Henry, takes those problems to the office; he confides in his boss, Sidney, whose experience of wedlock is even bleaker than his own, but who is taking traditional means of escaping from it. He expounds on these, at frequently hilarious length: Monogamy," he says in what may not be the plays best line but is certainly its most quotable, "is like a 40-watt bulb; it works, but its not enough." We follow him home, too, and find his wife, Marcia Marie, seething equally with resentment and possessiveness. Theirs is a love-hate relationship, like all those in the play, but it's important to note that in all of them the love exists. Sidney and Natalie are the characters who get the most stage time, and each of them is haunted, literally, by the ghost of a dead lover. Tommy, Natalie's ex, makes threatening appearances from beyond, demanding repayment of $3,000 -- her price for leaving poor him and marrying rich Henry. Just about everyone in the play claims to have been financially shafted by his or her partner, hence the title. This theme, though, is not well integrated into the rest of the play, and David Ferrys production (for Alley Co-op) cannot bridge the gap. Ferry is our most undervalued director, but this is not his best work.

He deals well with the spookiness, bringing the cast before us in separate columns of eerie light, singing. When he repeats the effect at the end, though, it effectively defuses the authors own denouement. The performances, meanwhile, have largely failed to connect. The play's structure sets its own acting problems. We are all, to some extent, different people, depending on whom we're talking to, so most of these characters have two personalities. The trick is to make each of them add up to a person, and the only actor who really brings this off is Ingrid Rae Doucet as the woebegone Celeste. But then she doesnt have anything to reconcile; having made the first scene work superbly, she all but disappears -- for which, I have to say, she has a first-class alibi. None of the others seem to have quite the skills. Michael Kash brings Catskill-comic bravado to Sidney, but lacks weight. John Clelands Henry is amorphous, Mary Francis Moore's Marcia Marie biting in her bitterness but unvaried, while as Natalie, the closest thing to a central figure, Anna Hardwick delivers an excessive amount of vocal finger-wagging.

From duologues to monologues. La Sagouine gathers five of them, the reminiscences of an Acadian floor-scrubber and fisherman's wife as imagined, originally for radio, by Antonine Maillet and as performed, for almost 40 years, by the now octogenarian Viola Leger, who has taken such complete possession of them that its impossible to imagine anyone else doing them, or her playing anybody else. Though Im assured, on excellent authority, that she most certainly can. The writing is weakest when anecdotal, strongest when reflective to the point of being metaphysical. La Saguoine reflects, like Hamlet but more cheerfully, on why she should fear death, or something after it, when it could hardly be worse than her own hardest of hard-scrabble lives. She muses on how unfair it is that, when the doctors tell you that you've got something, it's you and not they who have to live with it. She looks back nostalgically on the war, as the best thing to have happened since the Depression; they were the only times when the government felt obliged to do something for you. And she welcomes spring and does indeed seem, quite unsentimentally, transfigured by it. The cracks in her face light up. Shod in plimsolls (Act One) or boots (Act Two), her defining gesture a stern strong shake of the head, Leger summons both a harshly particular way of life and a universal spirit of transcendence.
