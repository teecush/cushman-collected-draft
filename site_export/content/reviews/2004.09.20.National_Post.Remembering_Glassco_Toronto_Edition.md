Obituary of Bill Glassco.

The reviews will be universally positive for founder of the Tarragon Theatre

Bill Glassco, who died last Monday of throat cancer at the age of 69, had one more year of life than he had expected. His doctors had predicted his death a year ago, but his illness went into remission, bestowing on him what he described to friends as a miraculous bonus. It was typical of him that he could sound genuinely joyful when saying that, with no hint of either sanctimony or martyrdom.

As has been said many times during the past week, Bill Glassco was one of the first gentlemen of the Canadian theatre. He was also truly a gentle man, and possibly never more so than during those last years. His place is secure in Canadian theatre history as one of the architects of the 1970s revolution, which combined the creation of our first school of indigenous playwrights with that of theatres and companies dedicated to their performance. In 1972, with his then-wife Jane Gordon, he founded the Tarragon Theatre, of which he remained artistic director for a decade, and which became Torontos flagship house for new plays.

Among the playwrights he championed were John Murrell, Judith Thompson, James Reaney and David French. He directed every one of French's plays, from the first, Leaving Home, which inaugurated both the Tarragon and its author's cycle of plays about the Mercer family and their emblematic progression from Newfoundland to Toronto, down to the most recent, Soldiers Heart, which brought both author and director back to the Tarragon after a long absence. The list also includes Jitters, the backstage comedy that was the Tarragons greatest financial success in Glassco's time, in which he himself is generally reckoned to be one of the characters. His reign at the Tarragon was before my time in Canada. When I came here he was helping to form what we now know as CanStage, a company formed by the merger of CentreStage (which he had also directed) and Toronto Free Theatre. It was not initially a very happy marriage, and both Glassco and Toronto Free's Guy Sprung had left before the new entity achieved its own identity. Anyway, I thought of Glassco principally as a nurturer of dramatists. He was certainly that, but what I have learned from talking to his colleagues -- and just about everybody in Ontario theatre was a colleague of Bills at some point, as were many beyond -- is that he was a nurturer of all theatrical talent.

On consecutive days following his death, I found myself talking about him to Andrey Tarasiuk, head of new play development at the Stratford Festival, and to actor and director David Ferry; casual conversations that turned into impromptu interviews. The stories they told me -- even the words they used -- were eerily similar. Both credited Glassco with starting their careers; both had been spotted by him when they were students at the National Theatre School in Montreal. Tarasiuk, who thought he wanted just to direct plays, was informed that he was going to run theatres -- as he has done, notably, before going to Stratford, the peripatetic young peoples group known as Theatre Direct. Glassco took him on at the Tarragon and, as he says, made him do everything: production, stage management, directing and associate producing.

One of Bill's great strengths said Tarasiuk, using an acting metaphor but not just talking about actors, "was to cast an individual and encourage him on a certain path. He also had the ability to work in a team with all his collaborators -- not just with the playwright and the actors, but also with the designer, the stage management ... he worked on a basis of friendship with all the production personnel. He believed that success depends equally on all the parts. It was an illustration in mentorship."

It obviously wasn't planned that way, but next year at Stratford, Tarasiuk will in effect be paying tribute to his mentor by directing James Reaneys Sticks and Stones, the first play in his Donnelly trilogy -- a bold project to which Glassco gave a home at the Tarragon 31 years ago, after seeing a workshop of it in Halifax. Among the actors involved in it, in both Halifax and Toronto, was one to whom Glassco had already given a break, Ferry.

It might be truer to say that Ferry spotted Glassco than that Glassco spotted him. In 1972, when the aspiring actor was in his third year at the NTS, the Tarragon production of Leaving Home came to Montreal. Ferry remembers being mesmerized ... by the recognition I felt. The young lead was Frank Moore (who, 32 years on, has just finished playing the big, bad businessman in Urinetown), and Ferry, a Newfie himself, found "his energy, presence and Canadian sound, electrifying. I wanted to be an actor like that right away. There was also an electric feel of commitment to the telling of Canadian stories that was reflected in the talk that Bill gave to the audience after."

A few weeks later Ferry was visiting friends in Toronto, and espied -- outside a butchers shop in Rosedale -- a Volkswagen with a bumper sticker: Have You Been To Tarragon Lately? "This long drink of water with a Buddha on a chain round his neck gets out, and I recognize him as Bill Glassco. Without even thinking, I went right up to him and gushed about the French play and told him that I thought it would be a good thing if I went to work for him at Tarragon. Of course, I was clueless as to the etiquette of the business, but he was generous enough a man to say he would consider it." The audition Ferry eventually got (for the title-role in David Freeman's You're Gonna Be Alright, Jamie Boy) was unconventional. Glassco took him and his girlfriend out for a drink: I remember going on passionately about her at one point while she was away from the table, and Bill asked me to immediately do my audition piece right there in the bar with the passion I was feeling about my girlfriend. The punchline? "He cast me right there."

Ferry confirms Tarasiuks description of the Tarragon atmosphere in those days: The thing that one felt working for Bill in those days, for better or worse, was that you became part of a family. It may seem simple now, but walking in the front door of Tarragon to be greeted by all those photos of Canadian playwrights on the walls was to give us a sense of being home. He adds that "the place was very late-60s in its ambience." Though I can't speak to that, I can testify that in his later years -- the sage, white-bearded years -- there was always a touch of the ex-hippie about Bill. He seems, indeed, to have been quite the sage even in his younger days.

It wasn't only anglophone dramatists whom he championed in the 70s; he also introduced the plays of Michel Tremblay to the world outside Quebec. He translated them, in collaboration with John Van Burek, and he staged them at the Tarragon. Strangely, though Bill himself was from Quebec, his command of French was not at this time especially good; before embarking on the translations, he had to take language lessons from his schoolteacher-cousin, Jane (the other Jane Glassco in his life, and someone to whom I owe much of my own friendship with him). Quebec, too, became his chosen home in his last years; it was there that he embarked on his last major enterprise. This was the Montreal Young Company, which he started in 1999, at the Saidye Bronfman Theatre, as an opportunity for emerging professional actors (especially anglophone Quebecers, who tended to get lost after emerging from the NTS) to play the classics. The company played two interesting seasons and then went into hiatus, a state from which it was set to return -- and still is -- next year. (Glassco was a genius at fundraising). His continuing commitment to young talent was additionally evident in his choice of the outstanding young director, Chris Abrahams, as his deputy. I remember his incredulous delight when he told me that Abrahams, who directed a stern version of Brechts Fears and Miseries of the Third Reich in the first Montreal season, had fallen in love with a neglected play by Terence Rattigan, of all old-fashioned dramatists, and was bent on staging it in the second; the play was After the Dance and it proved excellent.

It's an open secret that Bill himself was not an especially inspired director of plays (though I have good memories of his Montreal Measure for Measure) but it's more important that he was an inspired and inspiring director of theatres -- and a great and apparently selfless facilitator of talent. Ferry remarked to me that Bills uncle (or was it his great-uncle?) was a poet and added some of that poets heart was definitely deposited in Bill. Bill was also a musician. I used to run into him into CD and sheet-music stores in Toronto, and we had talked about doing a show together on Harold Arlen. He was an accomplished pianist, and in the last few years he recorded a couple of homemade CDs, to be distributed among friends. One of them -- with his daughter, the actress Briony Glassco, supplying some vocals -- was devoted to Gershwin. His funeral (at Hart House last Thursday) ended, unconventionally but surely very movingly, with everyone present singing They Can't Take That Away from Me. It's a great epitaph, but even better is the one with which his close friend Mary Kerr relayed the news in an e- mail: "Dear Bill G. died today. He is free and through and clear. I am very sad."
