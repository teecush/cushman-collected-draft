Perhaps the best thing about United States of Tara is that the title isn't the best thing about it. Good, teasing pun though it is, the show itself more than lives up to it.

Tara, in this new joint offering from Dreamworks and Showtime, is a suburban lady in her late thirties. In Kansas, I think; the geography isnt stressed, though the character certainly is. Besides having a career, a husband and two teenage children, she also plays host to three alternate personalities. She has been able to keep them at bay through medication but now, distressed by side-effects, she has gone off her meds, and her alters, as everybody here calls them, are invading her life and her familys. The show, I should perhaps make clear at this point, is a comedy.

Which raises a ticklish question. Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID), from which Tara suffers, is a real and distressing condition, often stemming from childhood abuse. The show, from an idea by Steven Spielberg no less, is actually written by Diablo Cody, screenwriter of Juno, who is famous or notorious for not doing research. She does not examine the real-life implications of her premise in anything approaching clinical detail. She uses it as a device. But I don't think that constitutes exploitation. The show doesn't laugh at Taras condition. It uses it as a way of laughing at other things.

Ive seen three episodes. The first two both begin with Toni Collette's Tara talking to camera; the third doesn't, but I hope these prologues will return -- because they are, in a nervously informal way, smashing. She tells us, hand on chin ("while Im still lucid"), that shes an interior designer, currently fashioning a 17th-century rococo nursery for a rich client. ("I create opulent environments for women who hemorrhage money.") She has an extremely understanding husband, Max; a 14-year old prodigy of a son, Marshall; and a daughter, Kate, who is commencing being sexually active, maybe not as much as she'd like people to think but enough to provoke very mixed feelings in her mother. And it's primarily to deal with these feelings that Tara calls upon the services of her other selves. Or sacrifices herself on her alters.

The three are: T, a gum-chewing aggressive flirt of her daughters own age; Buck, a foul-mouthed jock with a different brand of aggression, which he puts down to service in Vietnam; and Alice, a picture-perfect housewife from televisions idea of the 1950s. Each comes complete with wardrobe and coiffure. Whether they could really manifest themselves so neatly is open to doubt. But I'm prepared to go with it, especially with Collette being splendid in quadruplicate. She's fine as T and also as Buck, but its Alice -- whom we dont meet until Episode Two-- who's the killer.

She represents old-style sitcom intruding on new-style, and leaving it largely speechless. (That makes her the comic photo-negative of January Jones' wife in Mad Men, a 50s figure engulfed by the onrushing 60s.) In one of the best scenes from any TV show in recent memory, Alice imposes her old-world wisdom, and impregnable sweetness, on an insecure teacher who's been giving her too-bright son a hard time in literature class. She also, literally, washes Kate's mouth out with soap. This doesnt go as well; its Kate, as you might expect, who has the hardest time with her mothers. Why, she asks, "can't mom just be manic-depressive like all the other moms?" Actually, special problems apart, this family doesn't seem any more dysfunctional than what TV has trained us to regard as the norm. Both Max and Marshall think that Tara is, or are, pretty neat.

Codys writing is razor-sharp in both speech and silence , the smart-ass quality less irritating than it sometimes was in Juno, or at least more evenly spread around. Brie Larsons Kate is something of a blur, especially vocally, but John Corbett is delightfully placid as Max, trying to sort out the various sexual demands and opportunities afforded by three women in the same body. (Buck doesn't turn him on.) There is an added attraction -- for viewers, I mean -- in Tara's sister Charmaine, played by Rosemary DeWitt who was Rachel in Rachel Getting Married, so may be making a career out of being the crazy ladys sensible sibling. (They dont even want me around when I'm me, complains Tara of the people she lives with. "Please," says Charmaine, it's all you.)

But the prize performance, Collette apart, comes from Keir Gilchrist (British-born, Canadian-based, if you care) as Marshall, with his Pabst movies and Thelonious Monk records, and his developing crush on a slightly older boy who involves him, to his dismay, in a church basement morality play ("We need an AIDS patient being flogged in Hell") that he advertises as cutting-edge.

And so it may be, compared to the school production of Grease, whose auditions provoke an especially memorable comment from Marshalls earnest would-be girlfriend: Even something mainstream like Miller or, God forbid, Wilder would be preferable to this --its not even good on an ironic level.

United States of Tara is good on an ironic level, and on other levels as well, levels more truly entertaining. "I'm not schizophrenic," says Tara, "I don't hear voices." She knows best. But, if I were being sloppy about it, I could say that this show does for schizophrenia what Weeds does for drug-dealing. And, in the patented cable TV game of subverting suburbia, it's the best thing since. - United States of Tara premieres tonight at 9 p. m. on The Movie Network.
