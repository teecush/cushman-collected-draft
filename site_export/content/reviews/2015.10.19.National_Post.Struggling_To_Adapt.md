Let us begin by admitting that Shakespeare's plays are not absolutely dependent on their original words for their effect. If they were, they wouldn't work in translation; and the histories of the French, German and Russian theatres offer plenty of evidence that they do.

Still, a play in translation is a different animal from a play in its original language. It doesn't have the original breathing down its neck. If you're re-writing Shakespeare in English, then you're going into competition with him. And you're going to lose. So the shivers that ran down James Shapiro's spine (The Dumbing Down of the Bard, Oct. 8) when he learned that the Oregon Shakespeare Festival was commissioning modernized versions of all 36 plays are fully justified.

The Oregon festival is a long-established event - older by 20 years than our own festival at Stratford -and to see a place turn its back on so much history is kind of depressing. It's also, as Prof. Shapiro describes it, funny. Apparently Oregon will continue to mount the plays in the authorized versions alongside the new revised texts. So, in a given season, one production or another will have the status of a freak-show, though I'm not sure which.

There are of course different kinds of adaptation. You can rewrite every single line of the original, in effect coming up with a new play on the same subject. Or you can just try to tidy up the hard parts. This seems, to judge from Shapiro's description of a specimen adaptation of Timon of Athens, to be what Oregon has in mind. The example he gives actually involves the addition of a single letter: the word "erection," which in context could be social or sexual or ambiguous, becomes "erections" which removes the ambiguity and leaves us with a dirty joke. In all fairness, that's probably how the line in question would strike a modern audience even if left unchanged. But we don't actually gain anything from the change, except a possible laugh in a context where it isn't needed.

Shapiro is right that, even if imperfectly understood, the original is more resonant, especially when that one example is multiplied over the course of an entire evening.

Still, there's a difference between ambiguity and obscurity. Words and phrases that simply get in the way of plain meaning can legitimately be altered. Stratford has been doing it for years. The late Michael Langham, as sensitive a Shakespeare director as you could hope to find, was ruthless about making small changes in the cause of clarity, and they were never objectionable. (Well, hardly ever.) It goes on in England too. I recently watched the video of what I still believe to be the best Shakespeare production I've ever seen: Macbeth, directed by Trevor Nunn in a small shed in Stratfordon-Avon in 1976, with Ian McKellen and Judi Dench in the leads. I remembered it as a model of making conversational sense of the language while still suggesting hidden depths beneath every line. I hadn't remembered that one or two of those lines had been changed. When Macbeth meets the witches on the blasted heath and they salute him with his present and future titles he says "by Sinel's death, I know I am Thane of Glamis." Who's Sinel? We never hear of him again. Presumably he was Macbeth's predecessor in the thanedom (if that's a word and even if it isn't). McKellen changed the line to "by my father's death" which makes sense. I doubt if anyone minded.

I have to admit, too, that another of my great memories - and of a production universally considered a landmark - involved adaptation on a large scale. This was an earlier Royal Shakespeare Company production, The Wars of the Roses: a tumultuous trilogy in which the three parts of Henry VI were reduced to two and then topped off with a full-length Richard III. It was a triumphant evocation of a medieval world in chaos, and the cuts to these very early plays could be justified on both artistic and financial grounds. They necessitated, though, the writing of new transition scenes, and John Barton, the scholar-director who prepared the text, duly supplied them. He was a master of pastiche, but he didn't just edit, he editorialized; his additions told us what to think; they had the feel of sore thumbs. A few years later Barton took this to extremes with a version of King John whose title might have referred to the director himself; power had gone to his head. Trying to make the Middle Ages seem contemporary, he added lines like "the price of goods soars, meteor-like, to heaven." He almost wrote a new play; but unfortunately not quite.

That's the problem with juxtaposing Shakespeare's words, even his second-best words, with your own; they make yours sound silly. Shapiro's article originally appeared in the New York Times, and it prompted a response in the New Yorker, pointing out that "authentic" Shakespeare is a comparatively new idea, and that from the 17th century to the 19th, the plays were heavily adapted and re-written, to fit "contemporary" tastes. That's true, but nobody now thinks those adaptations were a good idea, and even at the time some of the best minds rebelled against them. Charles Lamb lambasted Nahum Tate's happy-ending version of King Lear which effectively was a new play, though not a good one; staged today, it would seem like a parody.

Adaptations are sometimes justified on the grounds that Shakespeare himself was a shameless reworker of other men's works, but three things distinguish his rewrites from those who would rewrite him: a) he wrote better than his sources; b) with a couple of exceptions, those sources were not other plays but chronicles and prose fictions; c) his plays were offered on their own merits, rather than as parasites on the backs of famous originals. I don't see the Oregon trail-blazers beating those odds.

I quarrel with Shapiro's claim that "directors and actors ... too frequently offer up Shakespeare's plays without themselves having a firm enough grasp of what his words mean." At the literal level, whether in Britain, America or Canada, they usually do; and if they don't they're willing and able to look it up. Shapiro laments that programs list "a fight director, a dramaturge ... but rarely an expert steeped in Shakespeare's language and culture." I don't know if he's applying for the job, but I thought that's what a dramaturge on a Shakespeare production was supposed to be. He can hardly workshop the script with the author.

Shapiro may well be right about how the words sound; when it comes to counting the beats in a blank-verse line a generation, certainly of British actors, seems to have gone deaf. It is true that actors and audiences don't have the educational equipment they once had. I referred above to the "authorized version" and the scriptural echo was intentional. I'm all in favour of secular education but a generation raised on the King James Bible (my own was probably the last) was off to a flying start in understanding the Jacobean Shakespeare. In my school, we started reading the plays at 12 or 13. Not everybody enjoyed them; some were bored; but nobody was baffled.
