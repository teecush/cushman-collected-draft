THEATRE REVIEW

Beauty and the Beast

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

In their ceaseless quest for new but old source-material, Ross Petty's family musicals -- British Christmas pantomimes, slightly Canadianized -- have reached Beauty and the Beast. I've never known this tale to be used before as panto material. Previous authors must have thought it wouldn't work. The current version suggests that they were right.

The writers, Lorna Wright and Nicholas Hune-Brown, have detached the story so far from its original moorings that it doesn't even qualify as parody. It does have a Beauty: a village maiden who, in deference to the Disney movie, is called Bella. It does have a Beast, sort of, but he's a wannabe Beast, as opposed to the original who was very much a don't-wannabe Beast. This guy is a bashful, awkward Prince named Zack, who is ineffectually practising to be a mean ol' rock star so that Bella will notice him, though in fact she's quite prepared to love him for his nerdy self. It takes a full half-hour for the show to set up even this much of a situation, and it has no idea where to go from there. The Prince does get to be a Beast through the wiles of a nefarious magician who wants the kingdom for himself (and is played, of course, by Petty himself ); but it's a half-hearted kind of transformation. By way of a twist, Beauty becomes a bit of a beast herself, courtesy of the villain's villainous iScroll, a device whose apps function and malfunction with baffling irregularity, even when backed up by their owner's most malevolent snarl. There's nothing here of Beauty tending to the Beast and recognizing his better qualities, on account of she's already done that before the story even starts. But if you remove that element there really is nothing left, nothing even to make fun of. First law of comedy: set up your target before trying to knock it down. Wright and Hune-Brown are best known as the authors of Lord of the Rings: The Musical: The Musical!, a show on whose quality I cannot comment because I never saw it, but whose nature seems pretty clearly indicated by its title. This Beauty and the Beast comes with a prologue in which performers audition hopeless ideas for family musicals. It's travesty

at one remove: not so much a genre as a disease.

The show does have another quarry in its sights. Petty's character is called Baron Barnum von Cowell, and the show spends as much time, and rather more energy, spoofing the American/ Canadian Idol thing as it does on its ostensible subject. Bella is played by Melissa O'Neil, a former Idol herself. The Baron has a nasty singing protege called Buskin Beaver; he also manages to lure away Bella's faithful sheep (are we in the right story?) and rebrand her as Lady Baa Baa. Goodies and baddies finally face off in a battle of the bands, an idea that's turned up at this address at least once before. The amount of humour the production doesn't manage to extract from all this is amazing. The underlying conviction that kids can't be made to care about anything beyond TV, pop music and computer games is depressing.

Petty himself goes through his usual routine (villain as smarmy narcissist, with a dash of hysterical paranoia) with his usual aplomb, but his material gives way beneath him, and I suspect he knows it. This isn't one of the boss's years for going into drag; that chore is bequeathed to Scott Thompson, who plays Bella's man-crazy aunt (in this kind of show, there's no other kind) and who doesn't even pretend that his lines are funny. The ultra-reliable Eddie Glen, playing one of Zach's two sidekicks/bandmates, makes an effort in that direction but is defeated; Jake Simons, mustachioed, Hispanified and playing the other (who plays the guitar) fares even worse.

Jake Epstein's gormless Zack does have a spark, and his shy courtship with Bella in the castle library -- two bookworms in their natural habitat -- is one of the show's candidates for Redeeming Feature. Another -- actually the only other -- is the standard and staging of the musical numbers (choreographer Tracey Flye, musical director Mark Camilleri). When the music isn't playing, Ted Dykstra's direction just gives in and slouches. Am I taking a children's show too seriously? I don't think so. For one thing, many of the names that get dropped in place of jokes are there for the grown-ups. (Des McAnuff? Christopher Plummer? Or -- not named but pictured -- Rob Ford?) For another, the kids deserve far better and there are at least two other seasonal shows in town that give it to them. As, in years gone by, Petty himself has done. His shows have fluctuated wildly in quality, but this one is the worst. Oh no, it isn't? Oh yes, it is.
