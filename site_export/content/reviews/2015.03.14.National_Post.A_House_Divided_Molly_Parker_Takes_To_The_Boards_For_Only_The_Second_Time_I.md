I don't know what Harper Regan is about. That doesn't stop it from being both interesting and entertaining. But it does make it frustrating.

I know who it's about. It's about a young-middle-aged Englishwoman, the oddly named title figure, who takes unheralded leave of her home and immediate family to attend the deathbed of her father, in another town; and who during her absence discovers things about herself and others, some of which she would rather not have known.

The play's production by CanadianStage is also about introducing us to the work of Simon Stephens, the prolific young British dramatist who here demonstrates an impressive ability to construct tense encounters between characters who always seem to be at rightangles with one another; they oppose, sometimes violently, but they never quite confront. It's also about CanStage's repatriation, to Canada and to the stage, of Molly Parker, celebrated for her role in House of Cards, except in my house ,where she's celebrated for her role in Deadwood.

This, we are told, is only the second time in a 25-year career that Parker has appeared on a stage. I'm hidebound enough to find it shocking that a serious actor should become a star without a thorough theatrical grounding; but Parker takes to the boards as if she'd spent her whole career there. She does so, too, in a particularly demanding role. Most of the play's scenes are duologues, and Harper is in every one of them. But she seldom takes the initiative. She reacts, and Parker, apart from proving excellent at wry vocal colouring, imposes herself through stillness. At least she does after an opening scene in which she

doesn't seem to know what to do with her hands. But even this could be characterization; Harper is in an embarrassing situation, asking for compassionate leave from a boss who refuses to grant it. Hardee T. Lineham plays him with a manic stridency that seems excessive at the time but is justified in retrospect when someone describes him as "weird." His weirdness includes hardly listening to Harper, but instead rattling on about his own obsessions, which include young people who live their lives on the Internet or in their headphones. Or of course both. We seem to be set up for two well-worn British theatrical forms: the drama of noncommunication and the stateof-the-nation play. Matthew Jocelyn's production honours the first category; he gives us a stark and stylized staging in which actors sit in the halflight between their scenes, the scenes themselves being played on a newly constructed forestage - for which relief from the Bluma Appel's customary remoteness, much thanks. The nation, though, is nowhere to be seen, or rather nowhere to be heard; Canadian actors are generally wizards at English accents (see the Shaw Festival, any time) but they make no attempts at them here.

This is especially damaging, since the play switches between two distinct parts of the country. Harper lives, with her husband Seth and their daughter Sarah, in the extreme west end of London, at Uxbridge (one of those place-names guaranteed to get a laugh from an English audience for reasons incomprehensible to everyone else). They moved there after some scandalous allegations about Seth made their original home-life intolerable. They used to live up north, in Stockport near Manchester, and it's to there that Harper, risking unemployment, repairs to see her father (she's too late) and to confront her mother, who tells her things about both their partners that she would prefer not to believe but cannot dismiss.

The lack of local flavour has an unfortunate effect on the mother (Lynne Griffin) who comes off as a non-specific nag. It also turns Sarah (Vivien Endicott-Douglas) into a stereotypical bratty student, though the same actress does better as a tactlessly well-intentioned nurse. Philip Riccio is excellent as a slimy journalist who makes an ill-fated pass at Harper, and whose leather jacket she makes off with, feeling herself much empowered. Izaak Smith is a real find, bringing one major and one minor character to vibrant life; the major is a boy, another student, to whom Harper finds herself blamelessly attracted. Alex Poch-Goldin doubles as Seth and as a man with whom Harper hooks up, via the dreaded Internet, for a hotel tryst, and makes both of them ambiguously sympathetic. As Seth he has, rather surprisingly, the play's last speech, outlining an improbable retirement fantasy (he doesn't have a job); Harper, as usual, just listens, we imagine skeptically.

Jocelyn's program-note suggests a parallel between Harper's quest for the truth and that of a Greek tragic hero; this seems very far-fetched and besides, with the Greeks, we always find out what the truth was. Harper remains inscrutable, even to herself, and the play is similarly sphinx-like,

hinting at themes without developing them. The parts, or many of them, are good; the whole, not so much.

Daniel MacIvor's Cake and Dirt is a mere doodle. Its main point of interest is its construction: two cryptic scenes are followed by a flashback that explains some things. We then proceed to a final episode which makes everything that has gone before, and most of the characters, irrelevant.

The main point at issue is the destruction of a Toronto park; the blame goes to a man dubbed Councillor Flip-Flop, for his fickleness and his footwear. The other people are mostly spoiled and mostly drunk. In Amiel Gladstone's production, most of the actors start strong and then amp themselves wearyingly and repetitively up. The exceptions are Maria Vacratsis, who stays on an even keel throughout, and Patrick Kwok-Choon, who is unbelievable from first to last. In this play had any other author's mane on it, I doubt if it would have got on.

Harper Regan runs through March 22 at the Bluma Appel Theatre; Cake and Dirt runs through April 15 at the Tarragon.
