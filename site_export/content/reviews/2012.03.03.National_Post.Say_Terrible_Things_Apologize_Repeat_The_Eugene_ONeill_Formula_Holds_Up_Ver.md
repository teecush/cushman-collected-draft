'If you didn't make allowances in this family, you'd go nuts." Those words, spoken by James Tyrone Jr. in Eugene O'Neill's Long Day's Journey into Night, could probably apply in some degree to any family anywhere, but Jamie's has to be accounted an extreme case. He's the self-hating, alcoholic wastrel son of a successful but pathologically stingy actor-manager father and a morphine-addicted mother who owes her condition to pain-medication administered to her after the birth of Jamie's younger brother Edmund. The long day in the life of the Tyrones is the one on which Mary, after a period of remission, relapses into hallucinatory oblivion, and on which Edmund, an aspiring writer with a tiring cough, learns that he has tuberculosis.

O'Neill himself was a consumptive, and the play, as much of the world knows, is barely disguised autobiography. Only the names have been changed, and a couple of those have merely been fudged; a third brother who died in infancy and was actually called Edmund is here referred to as Eugene, an exchange that would make a field day's journey for any psychoanalyst. The memory of that child is another of the curses hanging around the family's necks. Long Day's Journey is perhaps the most special of special case dramas. Part of its power to move derives from our awareness that the author was writing about himself, which seems rather unfair; but we respond also to the unsparingness of the drama, and in the end to its charity. O'Neill forgets nothing and, it seems to me, forgives everything.

In being so specific, he outlines a universal dance. Four people, inextricably locked together, say terrible things to one another, feel instantly ashamed, apologize, and then say them again. It's amazing the number of variations O'Neill can play on this single tune, but then the compulsiveness is the point. Nearly 20 years ago, Diana Leblanc directed a legendary production at Stratford that stressed the love between the characters as well as the pain. Her new production for Soulpepper takes the same tack; it doesn't unfold with the same seamless inevitability, but it's equally affecting. One of the play's key moments is James Sr.'s appeal to his wife when he starts to feel her slipping back: "Mary, won't you stop now?" Laurence Olivier at the Old Vic made it a great theatrical howl; maybe he was showing us Tyrone the actor playing for effect, but it sounded more as if he himself was. William Hutt made it a cry wrenched from the heart; even on a second viewing, it took you by world-rocking surprise. Joseph Ziegler accompanies it with a desperate embrace, and is less startling but even more moving. Ziegler showed in A Whistle in the Dark how superbly he could play a monstrous hypocrite of a father (transplanted Irish, too, like Tyrone); if he doesn't do the same here, that could be because Tyrone is not, in fact, a monster. He's a limited but loving man, whose good intentions are undercut by a tragic flaw that's also a running gag. He can't stop himself from crying poorhouse (literally), from measuring the whisky in the bottle (everybody in this play is a drinker, including the maid), from railing at his sons to switch off the lights; more darkly, his miserliness has caused him to stint on proper treatment for his wife and, incipiently, his son. He also knows it, though he has to be pressed into admitting it. Ziegler's performance has been criticized as insufficiently actorish and insufficiently Irish, but there's nothing in the text that suggests he should be either.

It's Mary whose accusations are the cruellest; even her apologies, as the drugs anaesthetize her, draw blood. Nancy Palk brilliantly charts her growing estrangement from reality, along with the retreat into her convent girlhood, a fantasy that by the final curtain has reached the stage of total immersion. It's unfortunate, though, that she has no staircase to descend for her final speech; she has to sidle on, which blunts the impact. The play, by the way, follows High Life as Soulpepper's second opening in one week involving morphine addiction, taking thematic programming to new levels of specialization. The two sons are terrifically played. Evan Buliung's Jamie has a tormented bravura that declares itself on its first appearance, and pays off wonderfully in his last-act return from an evening of sousing and whoring; vicious one moment, shamefaced the next, driving and driven throughout, he's a one-man summary of the entire play. Gregory Prest's watchful Edmund, by contrast, bides his time; he declares himself, both tolerant and triumphant, in the marvellous final scene where he trades bittersweet burnt-ember memories with his father and brother. As the maid, alternately solicitous and supercilious, Krystin Pellerin is, not for the first time, superb, which here includes being funny. The play is not a tragedy but a tragicomedy, and no less moving for that. I watched a goodly proportion of this production through tears.

I didn't cry at The Big Smoke, but I was both impressed and transfixed. This import features a British based Canadian-born performer, Amy Nostbakken, as a blocked young painter trapped in depression in both countries. Her solo performance, of a script written by herself and her director Nir Paldi (with acknowledgments to Anne Sexton, Virginia Woolf and Sylvia Plath, which should give you an idea) is mostly sung, a capella. This turns out to be an extraordinary vehicle for portraying a mind on the edge, with time out for instant portraits, mostly satiric, of less encumbered people encountered on the way down. Technically, the performance is a marvel; emotionally, it's a disturbance. It closes tomorrow, and is well worth grabbing.
