THEATRE REVIEW ECHO

Monday, February 05, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written and directed by Robert Lepage Starring members of Thatre 1774 God takes a wife and He sings in Lepage's dynamic play Echoes of enthusiastic atheism BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail In Echo, God sings. It seems to have been a lifetime's ambition. He stands erect at the mike and, with the falsetto fervor of a paid-up Mills Brother or Inkspot, gives out with a couple of choruses of You Always Hurt the One You Love. A suitable number for Him. If He does love us, what other excuse could He have?

Echo is Robert Lepage's adaptation, apparently a very free one, of Ann Diamond's prose fiction A Nun's Diary. It has been brought to Toronto's Theatre Passe Muraille by the Montreal group Thatre 1774. Like the novels of Joyce or the films of Bunuel, it comes across as a work of enthusiastic atheism, the kind that blooms by reaction in a Catholic culture.

Take another example. Take another song. (The only other song. Nobody would describe Echo as a musical.) Disinterred, like the previous exhibit, from among the lachrymose laments of World War Two is We Three, subtitled My Echo, My Shadow and Me. This has all kinds of resonances. One is historical; if the show has any time-frame it is that of the war. It refers us back to - well, it echoes - the show's title, which might otherwise seem a bit capricious. Which still does seem a bit capricious. It reminds us of Lepage's own directorial virtuosity with light and sound. Nobody uses echoes or shadows more expressively than he does. But really it's the show's mocking acknowledgement, disaffected but still affectionate, of the Trinity. God is a character in the play. This is how He introduces Themselves.

Lepage's set, impeccable as expected and predominantly white, is a high, narrow box within which nestles a bed, on which nestles a married couple. A preliminary voice-over has informed us of their condition and made it sound desolate. They bear this out by indulging in the most loveless love-making yet simulated on a stage. Or he indulges; she endures. For better or worse, it's soon over. What happens next is a dream. This is only revealed at the end. Normally I hate giving things away, but in this case I think you're better off having the information beforehand.

War has broken out, and he dreams of falling in with the German army. The ensuing brutality is tactfully depicted, but still predictably disturbing. The planks of the bed are pulled up, and prove capable of instantaneous transformation from splinters of the cross to splinters of machine-gun and back again. It is all beautifully dexterous but finally not too interesting. The soul of the show is in what happens to her.

She dreams of becoming a nun, in a medieval French convent, she afterward says, but it could just as easily be a modern one. After all, habits die hard. At first she finds the routine mortifyingly congenial, or congenially mortifying, but later she rebels. The Mother Superior, who has literary aspirations, is a particular pain.

This is where God comes in. He is not dead, He has merely got Him to this nunnery. Old and bearded, of course, but still hale and very hearty, He wants a wife. And though nobody explicitly points this out, He has come to the right place. Everybody on the premises is theoretically His bride. So at the show's climax He and the heroine are married, maybe in the convent chapel. And then, as the groom enthusiastically strips and begins vigorously to enjoy the bride, a kind of sinister soot descends from the ceiling, engulfing everyone.

Alerted by the publicity to expect Holocaust imagery, I took it that we were now transported to the gas chambers. The final solution is surely the ultimate question that anyone would wish to put to the Deity. Nothing else here qualifies. If "holocaust imagery" means merely corpses and green uniforms, then terms are being thrown around with culpable looseness.

God has the last, echoing words, peering over the rim of the box at the human cauldron: "I just wanted to see how they were made." Hence His interest in sex: the summation of an attitude to humanity that can most charitably be described as detached curiosity. War, sex and religion make in this piece an unholy trinity. When the male and female dreams intersect, a nun is raped. During the initial coupling on the marital bed (surveyed, of course, by a crucifix), the wife's rosary beads fall one by one to the floor, a recurring note in Lepage's sound-score. The themes are not new, and only Catholics, present or lapsed, are likely to find them shocking, or even particularly provocative.

For the rest of us the fascination lies in Lepage's stagecraft, in the measured, unfaltering accumulation of images, and the inexorable (and truly dream-like) merging of each with the next. Not in the same league as the mammoth, exuberant Tectonic Plates, this is still the minor work of a master. Nobody else in Canada is doing this kind of world-class work. (And nobody in the United States or Britain either. A few in those places are doing other kinds of world-class work but not this kind.) What distinguishes him from the self-appointed prophets of Performance Art is his interest in acting, and his ability to help it happen. Opportunities here are more spectral than in his other works, but they are mostly well taken by a mixed anglophone and francophone cast working in English, some of them doubling across the gender line as nun and Hun. God is good.
