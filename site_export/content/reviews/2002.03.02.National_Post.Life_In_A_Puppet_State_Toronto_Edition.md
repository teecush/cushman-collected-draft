Until March 10. (416) 531-1827

THE PIRATE WIDOW CHENG

Tarragon Extra Space

- - -

The magnificent Ronnie Burkett has raised the bar dauntingly high for practitioners of puppet plays. That's puppet plays as opposed to puppet shows. Burkett's achievements as a maker and manipulator of dolls are breathtaking, but they are not what you think about when you watch him at it. You concentrate on his accomplishments as actor and playwright.

In The Pirate Widow Cheng, the brother-and-sister team of Ann and David Powell enter these challenging lists and are defeated. Their previous productions have been for children, but they are attempting here to tell a grownup story with historical roots. Their technical skills are undeniable, though what they do here comes over as expert rather than exciting. Besides, it isn't the focus of attention. In this show the puppeteers are as much in view as the puppets, and -- as immediately becomes clear -- their talents do not extend to acting.

Nor, apparently, to writing. The script they have provided is sketchy in outline, cliched in detail. But we can't just ignore it and concentrate on the visuals. There is the outline of a story here, and -- like any audience given a sniff of a narrative -- we want to follow it and would love to be interested in it.

It certainly looks good on paper. The future Widow Cheng was a 19th-century Chinese entrepreneur who was sold into prostitution as a child but whose lot in life dramatically improved when she married a pirate. (Or, more accurately perhaps, when he married her.) He was good at his job but she proved better. Mr. Cheng's pirate fleet seems already to have ruled the waves, even in the face of the Imperial Navy itself. Mrs. Cheng turned it into big business, an armed extortion racket that earned the tacit co-operation of the Chinese government and may well have been less corrupt. Like a model capitalist, the widow retired while still at the top of her game and devoted herself to good works.

The most interesting thing about the Powells' presentation is their use of space. They occupy the whole stage floor. Black-clad, they move between an array of tables on which their wooden company - - who run into dozens -- are displayed. Two of these tables are later pushed together to create a ship: sort of like the forming of the barricade in Les Miserables, but with less fanfare.

Like Japanese Bunraku masters, the Powells let us see them manipulating their characters, but they are not purists. They employ a variety of techniques: at one point a whole sea battle is fought in shadow-play. Naturally this is impressive; or at least you feel it ought to be. But then it's over and you're stuck again listening to the presenters' voices.

Obviously, you don't go to a puppet show in order to listen. Except that, even at the humblest level, you do. Anyone who has been to a Punch and Judy show ("that's the way to do it") can vouch for that. And the Powells have a lot to say.

There is a framework in which, with miniature full-length suits hung from their necks like vests, they impersonate two published experts: Jorge Luis Borges, who turned the widow's tale into a short story and is played earnestly effete, and a Professor Dian H. Murray, who put him right on his departures from historical fact and is played anemically stern. (I don't know about Professor Murray but Borges certainly deserves better.)

They have no more authority when they turn to impersonating the pirates; the Powells, who apparently are British, play them in a kind of denatured Cockney accent. There are rude words aplenty, but the rhythm and spirit of slang are missing.

The same goes for the displays of violence (Mr. Punch still rules) and the cheerful acknowledgement, and occasional demonstration, of homosexuality below decks: The creators seem to be enjoying their sabbatical from playing for kids, but they still seem like pedagogues. It's very BBC.

So the characters never come to life. The principal puppets are striking enough at first glance -- both Orientally and marionettishly inscrutable -- but they don't grow in our imaginations; they never achieve the state of grace in which puppets seem to change expression. The performers exude a sense of unearned cheeriness; a story that might be worth hearing remains, for practical purposes, untold.
