THEATRE REVIEW

Toronto the Good

Factory Theatre

Despite some appearances, the title of Andrew Moodie's Toronto the Good may not be altogether sarcastic . Its true that the plays cityscape includes random shooting, racial profiling, irresponsible journalism, overworked teachers of under-parented children, marital tension and a husband nerving himself to be unfaithful, but nearly everybody in it has good intentions and is drawn with sympathy and wit. Whether their personal and professional lives all fit neatly onto the one stage is another matter.

The play begins with a youngish man named Thomas Matthews being pulled over by a cop for no apparent reason other than he's black. It turns out that he's a Crown lawyer who--justice in this play being not so much poetic as ironic -- is engaged in defending a white policewoman accused of just such an act of discrimination. Politically, Thomas might be called centrist. For the prosecution is Simon Phillip, white and vociferously left. Multiplying the contrasts and correspondences, Thomas and his French-Canadian schoolteacher spouse are expecting their first child while Simon, in his wifes brief absence, is playing single father to his own two kids. Additional stresses in their respective lives include the Matthews fights over who renews the licence on the car and who gets to drive it (she's the one who ends up taking the TTC where, despite her condition, no one offers her a seat), and Simon's attempts to exploit his temporary freedom with a succession of strippers and hookers.

Its a lively, fractious, funny play that might be Torontos answer to The Wire, with the same cross-currents of black and white, conscience and compromise, public and private, office and street; and the same refusal of the characters to stay in their own docketed boxes. It even features the same professional groupings as The Wire: police, the law, the press, the schools. (No dockers, though; and only the merest whiff of drugs.)What it inevitably lacks is the length and breadth of The Wire. There's no time to let the characters' on-and off-duty lives breathe and develop and enrich one another. We end up with alternating slabs of, for want of better terms, social significance and human interest.

Both elements have their attractions. The legal procedurals are initially the more thrilling, milking all the paradoxes of black and white lining up against preconceptions. The scenes with the accused Officer McGill (Sandra Forsell), who initially talks a very convincing game, are especially gripping. Her victim/accuser (Marcel Stewart, who strengthens the Wire connection by looking like DAngelo Barksdale) fares less well. He disappears from the action before we even hear him state his case, which accordingly ceases to be of more than theoretical interest. Even the reversals begin to seem schematic. The rival attorneys wheel and deal, threaten and compromise, and end up in a shouting match, each brandishing the names of un-favourite politicians. (Ill raise you one Jack Layton for a Mike Harris.)

It's odd how false this scene sounds, given that another kind of specificity is one of the play's greatest strengths Its an acute joy to sit in a Toronto theatre and listen to characters literally mapping their daily lives, with actual street names and landmarks and intersections. This may be the first play to which audiences should bring a GPS. Both Thomas and Simon engage us with monologues, and Simons, addressing the getting of breakfast and the daycare run and the race to the courts, is a particular treat. (Though if, from what I could work out, he lives in East York and works downtown -- what's he doing later on the Gardiner?) His attempted dalliances -- all with black sex workers, unless I'm confused by colour-blind casting -- are meant, I suppose, to complicate his idealism, just as the snapping of patience in Thomass occasionalimpatience with his white wife is supposed to show that assimilation can have sharp edges. He gets interviewed by a Toronto Star reporter who gets very aggressive about what she sees as his privileged refusal to rock any boats. "My name," he says angrily, "is Thomas, not Tom;" but she misquotes and crucifies him anyway. The whole incident is incredible (and crudely played): the plays lowest point. Better, but still only sketched in, are the attempts of Thomas's wife to get through to a student with just too many strikes against her, and a hip-hop chorus that's a complete break in style and barely comprehensible.

Xuan Fraser gives a beautifully controlled performance as Thomas, urbane witheruptions, and Brian Marler a winning one as Simon, appealing with complications. Philip Akins production is handicapped by a playground set that serves little practical or metaphorical purpose, but it brings out all the scripts excitements. It starts like the urban play we've all been waiting for. It can't sustain that, but it ends on an oddly hopeful note thats both touching and persuasive. The character whos been keenest to leave town is resigned, maybe even reconciled, to staying. The city may not be Toronto the good, but it isn't all bad either. - Toronto the Good runs until March 1.
