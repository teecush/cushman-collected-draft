How I Learned to Drive

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

A woman in her forties looks back on the older male relative who sexually abused her throughout her teens, and wonders about his own formative years. "What," she asks "did they do to you, Uncle Peck?" That is a stunningly generous way for her to put it -- and she is not a sentimental person.

I am not a Christian, and I don't imagine that Paula Vogel is either, but her play How I Learned to Drive does seem to me to exemplify what I believe to be one of the principles of Christian charity: Hate the sin, love the sinner. Or, if not love, then understand. Understand, but don't presume to explain.

How I Learned to Drive has been mounted by the Canadian Stage Company after a lengthy run off-Broadway and a heap of regional productions. It also won a Pulitzer Prize which, for once, seems well-deserved.

The narrator-heroine grew up in rural Maryland, in an oppressively close family that gave its members nicknames inspired by their genitalia; hers is Li'l Bit. (We never do learn her real name.)

Mocked for her academic ambitions and literary inclinations -- how, asks her grandfather, will Shakespeare help her to lie on her back in the dark? -- she turns to Uncle Peck, who is married to her mother's sister, and who is courteous and sympathetic and helps with the dishes. He gives her driving lessons, starting when she's 13, and he proves an excellent and conscientious instructor. She passes her test first time, and in later life driving through the countryside is her main pleasure.

The rest of Uncle Peck's legacy is less benevolent. He uses their time in the car to seduce her. He also photographs her, and teaches her to drink. Confused but not exactly innocent, she, in her own words, "draws a line."

As far as we can see, he crosses that line only twice -- on their first and last encounters -- but those are enough to traumatize her; and her trauma, really, is the play. From a chilly, detached middle age (she's going through life with the brake on) she recalls, in scrambled order, the crucial events of her youth.

The scrambling provides a pleasurable puzzle for the audience. It also allows us to track the ebb and flow of her memories, and the way in which each contributes to the final picture.

Fiona Reid plays Li'l Bit with a wary spirit and an open face: The perfect counterpoint for a dual role. On this basic distinction she builds a more complex layering; she distinguishes not only between the girl and the woman, but between the girl at 16 and at 17, a bit more brazen, a bit less happy.

Jim Mezon's soft-spoken Uncle Peck, by contrast, comes to us fully formed, or fully malformed: Desperation kept in check (he's a reformed alcoholic), always patient, always polite, always, somehow, pink.

It would be difficult, by the end, to say which of them is the greater victim; though, mercifully, neither of them acts the part. Only one scene reveals how calculating, and how indiscriminately predatory, Peck actually is: One in which he takes a young nephew angling in a Southern stream. The boy is unseen, but he might have grown up to figure in an even more disturbing play called How I Learned to Fish.

Those supporting characters who do appear are divided between three actors, referred to by the author -- with needless pretention - - as a Greek chorus. In fact they aren't commentators; they merely announce the scenes with spoken captions drawn from a driving manual: "Reverse gear" means a flashback.

They are cast without reference to age: A device that levels the playing-field for the chronological leaps required of the leading actress. It also keeps the tone light; as played by two youngsters, Kevin Bundy and Cara Pifko, the scenes with Li'l Bit's grandparents are neat rather than gross. (He is unwaveringly horny, she unwaveringly censorious, memorably announcing "if women would learn to keep their knickers on, maybe somebody in this family would finish high school.") They also play Li'l Bit's schoolmates: Female and jealous, male and geeky.

Susan Coyne doubles as the heroine's mother and aunt. The first is indeterminately written and played, existing mainly for a dissertation on a lady's rules for drinking that is all too obviously a set-piece. The second -- Uncle Peck's wife -- is also defined by a single speech: A defence of her husband that's a beautiful blend of anger and protectiveness.

Joseph Ziegler's production equals the New York version on acting, and beats it visually. The original was not so much sparse as impoverished. This one, in sets by Peter Hartwell, lets us in on back-road, cocktail bar, motel-room America: Rather like Lolita, which apparently inspired the author. (Though in her version it's Lolita, not Humbert, doing the telling -- and she who's the intellectual.)

A British director once offered the best diagnosis I know of the sickness of modern drama: Its failure to create characters that actors of the future might conceivably want to play. This play offers two of them, and this production has the performances to prove it.
