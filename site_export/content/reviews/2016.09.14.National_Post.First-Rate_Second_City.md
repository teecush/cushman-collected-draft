THEATRE REVIEW

Come What Mayhem Second City, Toronto

Come What Mayhem!, Second City's 78th revue hits its peak, as a good revue should, at the end of its first half. The number in question is that hardy perennial, a game-show sketch. The game thats being played is called You Oughta Know. A hyper host puts questions to three contestants, two of them his fellow cast-members, the third a volunteer (well, she didnt actually say no) from the audience. Unusually for this kind of number, the questions are about what we may loosely call Current Affairs: fairly basic ones like "who is the president of Turkey?" or what do the initials of ISIS stand for. You can feel the theatre audience guiltily racking its own brains over that one.

The rehearsed performers, of course, come up with answers of inspired ineptitude, like identifying the capital of Syria as S. Their impromptu colleague naturally cannot be relied upon to get things so hilariously wrong but I have to say that the gallant lady on my night got thoroughly into the spirit. She did best of all, though, when on her last go-round she got an answer right. The question was "who is Kanye West married to?" At which point the host, who had reached increasing heights of righteous hysteria at each successive display of ignorance, finally exploded. Why he screamed, in the vocal equivalent of italics and capital letters combined "is that what you know?" He could have been indicting all of us.

Roger Bainbridge, a newcomer to Second City's mainstage, plays the demented quizmaster, and he's a find. He brings a comparable, though quieter, moral-satiric edge to the show's closing number which, again in the best revue tradition, represents a second peak. It has him speculating on what a terrorist attack might be like if it happened, say, close to where we were sitting. Or exactly where we were sitting. The show makes a good running gag out of ISIS. That is not a sentence I ever expected to write.

You may deduce that the title has more relevance than the pointless pun that generally adorns the Second City marquee. Not that every sketch is apocalyptic, at least not on the grand scale. The show can narrow its sights without blunting its aim.

One thing this revue does better than most is punchlines. Lindsay Mullan is a versatile performer who does a fine improv bit as a daydreaming checkout clerk, autobiographically riffing off minimal information proffered by yet another intrepid audience participant. This number is educational: it may make you better understand why you have to line up so long at Shoppers Drug Mart. Kyle Dooley and Brandon Hackett provide what is known in the trade as solid male support.

This isn't the most spectacularly performed Second City show ever, but it may be the best written, at least in recent years. Not all the material is great, but none of it is bad, which in a revue is remarkable. And since at this address the performers are the writers, they earn on the swings any bouquets that they may have lost on the roundabouts. Bouquets also go to Carly Heffernan who has directed with style and speed.
