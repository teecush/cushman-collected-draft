With a mildly rude song giving its ironic all on the soundtrack, a comfortable-looking middle-aged lady rides a bike with a basket along a provincial English street, looking like a benign version of Miss Gulch from The Wizard of Oz. She is actually Mrs. Mary Whitehouse, a morality crusader who flourished in Britain from the early 1960s right through until her death in 2001. And her opponents did indeed regard her as the reincarnation of the Wicked Witch of the West. Or, more accurately, of the Midlands.

The chief object of the Whitehouse wrath, at least in her earlier years, was the BBC. The Beeb, in what looks initially like a classic example of turning the other cheek but turns out not to be, has made her the subject of a dramatized film, cheerily entitled Filth. "This story took place," say the titles, "only with more swearing and less nudity." That's a teaser in more senses than one; the actual show has no nudity but it does have swearing. It begins pretty much as a cartoon: Mary exchanging friendly words with the local vicar, Mary cycling home, Mary pouring tea for her family when an early-evening discussion of pre-marital sex comes on the TV, taking her so completely by surprise that she drops the pot. At this point the show seems to be both trivializing and patronizing Whitehouse, treating her as a dear out-of-touch old thing, thereby underrating both her and the forces she opposed and provoked.

But it soon begins to dig deeper. We see Mary as a teacher (quite a good one, it seems) genuinely concerned for her students, upset that they're upset. Mary and her husband, a postman named Ernest, are played by Julie Walters and Alun Armstrong, two of the most endearing actors on God's Earth. Armstrong, especially, is a treasure, as a loving, supportive, quietly humorous partner both spurring his wife on and reining her in. (He points out that Clean Up National Television, her original name for her campaign, could give rise to an acrimonious acronym.) The Whitehouses are the only characters whom we actually see having sex: a joke that resonates on any number of levels. They certainly have a better marriage than their archenemy Sir Hugh Carleton Greene (Hugh Bonneville). Sir Hugh, brother of Graham (the novelist, not the actor), director general of the BBC at its most permissive period, treats his family with foul-mouthed casualness and ends up divorced. He's no more endearing at the office, ogling two successive secretaries and greeting their departures to get married as tiresome interruptions of his routine and without even token words of congratulation.

He refers to Mrs. Whitehouse as "that woman" and refuses to meet with her: a tactical error at the very least, since it quickly becomes obvious

that she has a sizeable constituency. He also refuses to allow her air time, though it's pointed out to him that if you're against censorship, you really shouldn't be censoring your opponents. His social life revolves around clubs and cricket matches. He is the cultural elitist of Stephen Harper's dreams.

But not so fast. The cunning of Amanda Coe's script is that, though it gives us plenty of reason to like Mrs. Whitehouse and plenty of reason to dislike Sir Hugh, it ends up leaving us in no doubt that she was wrong and he was right. The turning point for her is a public meeting at Birmingham Town Hall that she approaches in terror and exits in triumph. Present at the meeting is a young playwright whose radio play she's attacked. He asks for right of reply. She says that he's already had his time (fair point) by writing his filthy play (unfair abuse, shutting down debate). He responds by savagely lampooning her in a TV series called Swizzlewick. "Good show" says Sir Hugh, adding sotto voce, "pity it wasn't funny."

And so, for Mary, it hubristically goes: What starts as a reasonable desire to protect children from subject matter for which they may not be ready progresses into a desire to eradicate the mention, if not the actual existence, of any word or idea she finds personally disagreeable. (She asks Ernest, incredulously, if he can imagine such a thing as oral sex. His wistful silence suggests that he can.) She keeps tabs on the number of times the word "bloody" occurs in Till Death Us Do Part (progenitor of All in the Family). And it isn't just sex and language. It is, as always, religion and politics. She can't abide to see Christianity mocked or even criticized; she's shocked that the satirical show That Was the Week that Was ( The Daily Show without the underlying moral fervour but incredibly liberating in its day) should be lampooning the (Conservative) prime minister. She also gets around to attacking sci-fi violence in Dr. Who and -- in a semi-apocryphal incident that anticipates James Dobson's attack on Teletubbies -- deploring the lack of respect shown by the puppets on a kids' show towards their human co-stars. Generations of ventriloquists oppressed by their dummies must have risen up and cheered.

When Filth was aired in the U. K., a poster on the Times website summed Mary up perfectly: "too proper to enjoy vulgarity and too vulgar to enjoy anything else." And the film doesn't have to defend Greene personally: Merely by mentioning some of the shows that she attacked and he championed (there were many others) it reminds us that he presided over a golden age of television. Other responses to the show have said that Whitehouse would have been appalled by today's television, the game shows and the degradation. So she would; and so would Greene. But it's far more her legacy than his. It's the free market in action. The film concludes with Mary standing triumphantly outside the hallowed portals of Broadcasting House, the BBC's Central London HQ, about to receive an audience with a new director general who recognizes her existence. It's the beginning of an end.
