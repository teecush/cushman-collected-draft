THEATRE REVIEW

Soulseek

Walter Centre, Toronto

Zorana Kydd, the power behind BirdLand Theatre, has put us all in her debt as the producer of two of the most exciting shows seen in Toronto in recent years ( Assassins and The Last Days of Judas Iscariot) and of another ( The Pillowman) that was substantial, if not quite in the same league. But there is another side to the ledger, dating back to Bird-Lands first two productions, which were of noted if not especially notable American plays ( The Ride Down Mount Morgan, Frankie and Johnny in the Clair de Lune), both of which featured Kydd as an actress, in roles for which she was under-equipped.

Those memories, much magnified, have now come back to haunt us in Ognen Georgievski's Soulseek, a new play in which Kydd once again stars and which seems to have been written especially for her. It's one of those occasions in which text and performance keep luring one another down into new circles of unhappiness.

That image was probably suggested to me by the play itself, which takes place in the underworld. Kydd plays an actress whose husband has died of cancer. She has either committed or is contemplating suicide, with the second possibility meaning that every-things a hallucination, which is by no means unlikely.

On arriving at the crossroads of the afterlife, shes greeted by Morpheus, best-known as the Greek god of sleep and still in the soporific business. ( I'm Morpheus he says, "I give you morphine." Well, that's etymologically fair enough.) His duties, though, seem to have extended to being Kydds guide through her posthumous existence; mixing mythologies, he plays Virgil to her Dante. For a slumber merchant hes very voluble and, in a performance by Andre Sills stronger on force than lucidity, very loud. Also snippy: He tells his charge, apparently speaking for the whole hellish and heavenly hierarchy, that he doesn't much like the arts. Nor does he think that she was very good at them. It seems that the traumatic event of her professional life was appearing in a production of The Tempest and blanking out on an important speech. As far as I could tell (neither actor is very distinct) she was playing Miranda, but the speech that gets quoted -- actually misquoted, twice -- is one of Prospero's: our revels now are ended no less. The humiliation apparently sent her fleeing into reality TV.

The play seems to be making up its own iconography as it goes along. Projected titles inform us that we have moved from one stage of the protagonists journey to the next, but its impossible to distinguish any progression or even any distinction, given that both script and performance have the texture of sludge, faintly leavened with whimsy. As much as she talks, we never learn enough about Kydd's character or her predicament to figure out why we're supposed to be interested. And at the risk of sounding xenophobic: Her Serbian accent doesnt do anything for her expressiveness.

What comes over is not pretentiousness, since one cant make out what the pretensions might be, so much as self-importance. A television news-reader brings reports of seismic ructions in the nether regions, but whether these are connected with Kydd's presence or just a coincidence is something else left undefined.

Talking of ferrymen: David Ferry is in the play too, sort of. His name in the program threw me a bit, since I didn't think that even an actor of his talent and energy could appear here while still giving his devastating performance of what King Lear called "unaccommodated man" in Blasted. Turns out hes only here on video, speaking presumably wise and comforting words as the heroines deceased partner. That means he's currently in two plays during which, though for rather different reasons, I sometimes found it hard to look at the stage. Even dead and phoning it in, he gives the liveliest performance in this one. Also technically, or technologically, present is Janet Porter as the TV anchor, who resembles Samantha Bee on an off-night. The video and audio effects, plus Camilla Koo's set, are the most striking things about Stefan Dzeparoski's production. Of the live human action he cannot, or at any rate does not, make any sense.
