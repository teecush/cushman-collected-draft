For the third time in recent months, a character in a TV action drama finds himself, or in this case herself, standing, petrified, atop an unexploded bomb. This scenario should be more gripping in ZOS: Zone of Separation, which is set against a real and recent conflict, than it was amid the fantasy heroics of Prison Break or 24. But in fact it's less, precisely because the stakes and therefore the expectations are higher and they have not been met.

The person on the mine is Captain Sean Kovacs (Michelle Nolden), commander of an unarmed team of United Nations military observers in a Balkan town called Jadac, fictitious but resembling Sarajevo. Her job and her team's is to keep the town's Muslim and Christian populations from one another's throats; it isn't easy since just about everyone we meet from either side is either a fanatic or a racketeer or both. Kovacs is Canadian and a self-hating Albertan. (A rare breed as far as I know; anyway she doesn't like other Albertans.)The ante in the minefield incident is upped, theoretically, by the presence of two other potential victims, both children, and of a pair of distraught parents. On top of that, the entrapped Kovacs keeps having flashbacks, in hallucinatory black-and-white, to an earlier incident that left her somewhat traumatized and a male colleague almost completely so. And on top of that, high-pitched music on the soundtrack keeps alerting us to how tense and potentially pitiful it all is, though the tension is somewhat eased by our knowledge that the tough and tender Nolden has the show's top billing, and so is hardly likely to perish in the first episode. I know I must sound like a broken record about this, but the use of music to manipulate our emotions -- to do, in fact, what the writing and performance should be doing -- has become the sign that separates the serious TV shows from the soaps. ZOS, which has aspirations on the level of The Sopranos, Deadwood and The Wire, winds up instead in the semi-serious world of Flashpoint and The Border. (This may reflect its mixed parentage; ZOS is a show with CBC involvement that has ended up premiering in the tougher cable environment of The Movie Network.)

As it happens, Malcolm MacRury, the show's principal writer and one of its executive producers (Paul Gross is another), has a Deadwood past, and he's quoted as seeing the new show as a homage to that one. Both depict a frontier civilization, or lack of it, with a fragile rule of law. Notably, both feature a transplanted Englishman, an outwardly genial brute ruling the local roost as saloon-keeper, brothel-owner and gangster. Ian McShane's Al Swearengen, though, is a hard act to follow, and ZOS's George Titac, a Muslim from Manchester, doesn't have the flavour, the charm or, judging from the first two episodes, the complexity. He's played, forcefully, by the aptly named and cauliflower-faced Colm Meaney, who is in fact Irish. You've heard of colour-blind casting; this show specializes in what I'd be tempted to call accent-deaf casting, were it not that all the actors do the voices so well. Thus Allan Hawco, certainly Canadian, does a flawless cockney chancer as the new second-in-command of Kovacs' multicultural corps, with what seem to be nefarious sidelines that will doubtless be clarified later. Enrico Colantoni, such a nice if troubled guy on Flashpoint, is startlingly transformed into a bare-chested Christian paramilitary chief with rock-star ambitions and accoutrements. (This does take some swallowing.) His partner in -- almost exclusively -- crime is Lolita Davidovich, the cast's nearest approach to Hollywood celebrity, playing the glamorous widow of a Christian warlord.

Cast not against ethnicity, but rewardingly against type, is Rick Roberts as Major Hart, who is Canadian like Kovacs and who is also her UN opposite number, professionally and psychologically; he commands a newly-arrived force of definitely armed peacekeepers. In contrast to his predecessor, an accommodating officer from Azerbaijan (smoothly played by Hrant Alianak, whom I hope it's not unkind to describe as Canadian drama's all-purpose Eastern European), and despite bearing an occasional disconcerting resemblance to the chicken-shooting captain from Air Farce, he ain't about to take no guff from nobody. Indeed, part of his aim, and maybe the show's, seems to be to dispel all the polite cliches about Canada. Promotional material for the show suggests that some of his acerbity will turn out to be due to family problems back home. But that's rather how the show operates. It hands out neat backstories to its characters and gets them to act up. None of it feels organic or original. And the political background-- the element that's supposed to distinguish the show from other shows -- remains just that, a background. Only the most sensational aspects are allowed in.

Much of the first episode is devoted to a potentially provocative march by the Christian side of town, who wish to rebury their dead. It's a tinderbox waiting to happen, but when it does, it's dramatically ineffective. Maybe there's just too much else to be set up. Openers tend to be confusing; the show does get clearer and more purposeful in its second week. The principal conflict seems to be not between sects but between people: the battle of Kovacs and Hart who, put like that, sound rather like a comedy team. That may not turn out to be inapposite. The show's ambitions seem to include absurdist military satire on the order of M*A*S*H. But it hasn't happened yet. - ZOS: Zone of Separation airs Mondays at 10 p. m. on The Movie Network.
