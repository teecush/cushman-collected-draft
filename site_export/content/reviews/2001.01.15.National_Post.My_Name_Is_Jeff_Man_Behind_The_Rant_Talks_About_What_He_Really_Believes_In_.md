His name is Jeff, and hes Canadian, and I bet you never want to read another opening sentence like that as long as you live.

Jeff probably doesn't either, though his feelings on the matter can hardly help being mixed. Jeff is Jeff Douglas, a 29-year-old actor based in Toronto, who has now been famous twice: a remarkable achievement considering that very few people actually know his name.

For them, Jeff is Joe, the guy who gave the patriotic speech in praise of Molson's beer in last years most celebrated TV commercial. It caught on; people would watch it again and again, for the pleasure -- especially when Joes voice hit screaming-pitch on the line about the beaver being a proud and noble animal -- of wondering, is this for real?"

Politicians, whose senses of humour tend not to be of the sharpest, decided that it was and, for them at least, Joe became an icon, giving Jeff his proverbially guaranteed 15 minutes of celebrity.

And the iconic status seems to have taken, because the news that Douglas was leaving Canada for Los Angeles has led to anguished questions about our national identity. We let a man star in a widely- applauded beer commercial with patriotic implications, and we still can't keep him? Let's sell the country now.

Carrie Fallis, Douglass Toronto agent, is bemused. "Jeff, she told me on Friday, is an actor; hes doing what actors do. Heading for Hollywood in the pilot season. What's the big deal? Still, any publicity is good publicity."

Douglas himself evinced the same mixture of gratitude and surprise when he spoke to me on Saturday from L.A. (Yes, he's there already, he just couldnt wait, get over it ... and anyway, my prestige has shot up in my neighbourhood since my kids friends learned that Joe called the house, though they had to be persuaded of it because who the hell is Jeff Douglas?)

"Movie and TV work, he told me, is slow in Canada in January and February. In L.A. it's the hottest time." Well, it would have to be hotter than Canada, but I appreciate that he wasn't referring to the climate. Actors are nomadic workers, and the way Douglas tells it, TV projects with an eye on the coming season, and even feature films, are falling off the trees this time of the year. They dont, of course, drop on everybody, but it makes sense for an actor with any kind of visibility to place himself in their path.

Douglas has that visibility, and it isnt all a matter of beer and beavers. He spent three years appearing for Disney in The Famous Jett Jackson (shown here on the Family Channel). No call seems to have gone out in Hollywood for ambulant Canadian icons, and Douglas has not been summoned south, like Paul Gross's Mountie, to demonstrate Canadian grit or even to provide a focus for Canadian jokes, though with the number of sitcoms that use the word "Canada as a knee-jerk cue for laughter, it can only be a matter of time before one of our own becomes the permanent fall guy in his own series.

When Douglas sits across the desk from a potential Californian employer the conversation will apparently go something like this:

So, your manager tells me you did some commercial. Who was it for?"

"Molson.

What's that?"

"Its a beer.

Oh."

No resonance, it seems. On the other hand, "its received with less prejudice down here. Nobody pigeon-holes me through it.

Actually, the commercial did lead to him being interviewed on American TV, and he thinks the mere fact that executives were seeing him and not knowing who he was or what he was talking about may have worked in his favour. They felt they ought to know, that something was happening that they didn't understand, and that they'd better grab a piece of it.

Douglas has a recall tomorrow for an independent feature called The Good Girl, movies being what he wants to do. Traditionally youre supposed to avoid the small screen if you want to work on the big screen -- why should people pay for you when they can get you at home for free? -- but that seems to be changing. TV shows are aimed a lot at people in their teens and early twenties, and thats the movie audience too. And I could end up doing a couple of pilots before I come back to Canada in March."

Well, that's news. We'd all thought hed left for good. Whats even more interesting is what he's coming back to do, what it is in his diary that Hollywood will have to arrange its schedules around. He's going to Edmonton to play Romeo in a new production at the Citadel Theatre. ("My name is Ro, and Im from Verona. I believe in peacekeeping, not swordfights. I want to marry my worst enemys daughter. Hey, I'm in love.)

It will be his second Shakespeare; he practised last year as Macduff, in a shoestring self-started actors' collective production of Macbeth, in an obscure theatre at Parliament and Yonge in Toronto. We got terrible reviews" he says cheerfully. "It was like they were trying to humiliate us. I got blamed for doing exactly what I was going for. But I was happy with it. The story got told.

So it turns out that Joe Canadian is really a theatre guy. (That should stand him in good stead in L.A., by the way; theres a lot of good theatre there. Well, think of the pool of unemployed actors they have to draw on.) And its obvious, even within the strict confines of a TV commercial, that Douglas is a good stage actor. Think of the way he modulates, before that screaming crowd, from shyness to hysteria.

Did he think it was funny? Yeah, I thought so. The tone is humorous. I'm not sure I have a clear understanding of the term 'irony" (would that everyone were so honest) "but I tried to make it funny. That doesnt mean I think everything he said is a joke. I believe in peacekeeping. I believe in diversity.

Another question I had to ask: We all know the cultural impression the ad made, but how much beer did it sell?

I read that Molson gained two market points as a result of the ad. I think that translates into between 20 and 40 million bucks." A financially savvy pause, because these days every actor has to be his own packager. "I wish I'd gone for a percentage."
