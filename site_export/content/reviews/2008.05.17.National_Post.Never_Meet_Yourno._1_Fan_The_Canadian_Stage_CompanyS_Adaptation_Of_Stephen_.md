It may be every writer's worst nightmare to be corralled for an indefinite period by his self-described No. 1 fan. Then again it may not. Let's admit, though, that if the writer has been knocked out in a car accident and wakes up to find himself incapacitated and at the fan's mercy, then that raises the stakes considerably. They go even higher when the fan is an ex-nurse with access to pain-killing drugs that give her traumatized patient a choice between agony and addiction.

Those who have read Stephen King's novel Misery and/or seen the film that was based on it will recognize the situation. Those who, like me, have done neither may still find it striking a chord since you can't help hearing things, especially about the movie. My ignorant impression is that Simon Moore's stage adaptation is a faithful one. And not knowing what happens once the basic set-up has been set up probably helped me to enjoy the Canadian Stage production, which I found thoroughly gripping, even though it moves slowly (this actually has its advantages) and lays on its effects with a trowel (this doesn't).

For my fellow innocents: Paul Sheldon is a best-selling novelist who has hit it particularly big with a sequence of decorously sexy period romances (what the trade used to call bodice-rippers) about a heroine whimsically named Misery. We first see him accepting an award, prior to taking off to his mountain hideaway to work on his latest. We next see him, book written and car crashed, pinned to a bed in the home of his ardent admirer Annie Wilkes. She has read his new manuscript, which he thinks his best, and she does not approve. She finds it pretentious ("stream of consciousness" he explains, breaking no ice whatsoever), and full of rude words. Worst of all, it has no Misery. But this is nothing compared to her wrath on going into town, buying a paperback of Paul's last Misery book (she can't afford hard covers), and finding that it really is his last Misery book. He's killed her. Annie isn't having this, any of it, and she forces Paul to: a) tear up his just-completed masterpiece and b) write a new story, Misery Returns, in which she does. It's hard to tell which of these two humiliations he finds more agonizing; though if Conan Doyle could yield to readers' entreaties and bring Sherlock Holmes back to life after pushing him over the Reichenbach Falls, Paul has at least the consolation of being in good company. (The company, perhaps, that Misery loves.) There are rumours, too, that the upcoming seasons of 24 and Prison Break will revive characters thought deceased; one wonders whose beds their authors have been strapped to.

So, Annie gets Paul off his back and into a wheelchair, and buys him a second-hand typewriter. (I'm not sure when the play is set, but it certainly wouldn't work if Paul had a computer. Though, of course, going for a hard drive is what got him into trouble in the first place.) Once partially mobile, Paul naturally sets about trying to escape, thereby getting Annie very upset. Reaching back into her experience on surgical wards, she ... I'll spare you the gory details, though David Storch's production doesn't. It gets very literal, inducing incredulous gasps from the audience that turn rather too speedily to giggles. There really are things you shouldn't do in a theatre, at least not without an anaesthetic. Anyway, if you had begun to wish that the play would stir its stumps a little, the action around here may make you regret your choice of metaphor.

In fact, the show's leisurely nature is what makes it work. We see everything from Paul's point of view, hear him screaming when his medication is withheld, share his musings on his helplessness and on his hopes of outwitting his captor. He's immersed, in fact, in his own stream of consciousness and we're in there with him, splashing for our lives. Tom McCamus makes the identification seductively easy; he's completely convincing, right from his calculated blend of modesty and arrogance when accepting his prize. His charm and suavity never quite leave him, even at his most desperate as he counts off the moments that separate him from final disaster. Annie is the showier role and Nicola Cavendish seizes it, right from the sugar-sweet smile with which she greets her awakening idol, a moment at least as scary as any of the more overtly menacing things she does later. She digs deep into Annie's memories of a twisted childhood, but she's less powerful when she switches into villainess mode, which she's either allowed or encouraged to do too early and too loudly; I wanted her to cut the cackle, literally.

Where the play and the actors excel is in giving life to the usually invisible bond between the oblivious entertainer and the audience who, in a mixture of grudge and gratitude, feels that she owns him and that he owes her. She, who for years has paid the piper, can finally make him dance to her tune. Each of them is comically oblivious of the other's values and modes of operation; they do know that they're in a life-and-death struggle. Rather less subtle are the setting that Britta Gerecke has dreamed up for Annie's stronghold, full of poles but short on walls, and James Fisher's supposedly spooky sound-score, which seems to think that the play is still a movie. The two forms have different ways of creating suspense, and what's effective in one can be ludicrous in the other. Which reminds me: The CanStage program note says Misery is the only work of King's to have made the journey from page to screen to stage. The writer must have forgotten the Broadway musical Carrie, a superflop so legendary a book was named after it. - Misery runs through May 31 at the Bluma Appel Theatre.
