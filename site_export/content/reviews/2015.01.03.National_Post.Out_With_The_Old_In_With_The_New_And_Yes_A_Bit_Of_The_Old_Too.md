Im looking forward to Infinity. Thats the title of a new play by Hannah Moscovitch, due at the Tarragon Theatre this March. Moscovitch is one of the two best playwrights now working in Canada. The other is Michael Healey, who doesn't have anything coming up, or at least nothing that I've been told about. Theyre opposites. Healeys plays are always identifiably Canadian; their themes and settings may be domestic or political, but they're always very much here. Moscovitch's interests, and her sensibility, are cosmopolitan. Her first success was The Russian Play; her greatest was East of Berlin, which despite its title was set largely in South America. Her most recent was This Is War, in which all the characters were Canadian, but which took place in Afghanistan.

I dont know where Infinity is set, but its press release, which is all I have to go on, suggests that its venturing into Tom Stoppard/John Mighton/Michael Frayn territory: physics and metaphysics. I quote: How does a new theory of time change everything we know about ourselves? A brilliant young woman and her parents smash together - like colliding particles in an accelerator." Well, that sounds like fun.

Preceding Moscovitch into the Tarragon are Waiting Room, a new play by Diane Flacks, and Abyss, the Englishlanguage premiere of a piece by German writer Maria Milisavljevic. Don't let anybody tell you that female playwrights are under-represented on Toronto's stages. That hasnt been the case for years. The Factory has two new plays coming up, one of them by Charlotte Corbeil-Coleman, the other by Yvette Nolan. The Factory-in-exile, also known as the Canadian Rep Theatre, has three plays lined up, two by women.

One is How Do I Love Thee, which, as might be guessed, is about the courtship of Robert and Elizabeth Browning, a fittingly poetic subject for Florence Gibson MacDonald; the other, by Colleen Murphy, is a two-hander on an African war theme, in fact entitled Armstrongs War. Theatre Passe Muraille, the remaining house devoted to new Canadian work, has R-E-B-E-C-C-A, the playwriting debut of Sara Farb.

Three years ago Passe Muraille gave us Crash, a prizewinning first play by another actress-playwright, Pamela Sinha. This year her second play Happy Place is being produced by Soulpepper. It isn't just the first new play by a woman the company has presented; it's the first new play. When Soulpepper started out, it was focused on the classics; it has drifted from that policy, re-defining it as "plays that have already been done somewhere else.

Even the successful Kims Convenience was the development of a piece that had premiered on the Fringe. Shows newly created - most recently Of Human Bondage and Spoon River Anthology - have still been adaptations of pre-existing texts. Meanwhile, theres been a heavy reliance on established plays from the American repertoire (Miller, Mamet and even Neil Simon) while - with its revivals of David French, John Murrell, Judith Thompson and David Young - the company has practically invented the category of Canadian classic." So Happy Place is a departure, though a logical one.

The Canadian male playwrights haven't disappeared. There's a new Daniel MacIvor at Tarragon, Cake and Dirt, whose publicity makes it sound as if its author is breaking fresh sociological ground while delving into familiar themes. The third production in Canadian Reps lineup is a revival of George F. Walkers Nothing Sacred, which is another adaptation of a literary classic, in this case the Turgenev novel Fathers and Sons. It also happens to be my favourite Walker, due to a production years ago at the Shaw Festival.

The Shaw's own bill this year includes tempting American morsels: Sweet Charity, maybe the last of the great unselfconscious showbiz musicals; Light Up the Sky, a rare piece by Moss Hart; and, trailing clouds of more recent New York glory, Peter and the Starcatcher and Tony Kushner's The Intelligent Homosexuals Guide to Capitalism and Socialism with a Key to the Scriptures.

It looks like a good year for catching up with recent American stuff. Soulpepper has Sarah Ruhls Eurydice. Acting UpStage are giving us one of the two American musicals of The Wild Party (and it's the more interesting of the two, with a score by Michael John LaChiusa). Company Theatre's residency at CanStage will yield Domesticated by Bruce Norris, whose Clybourne Park raised hopes that deep satire might not be dead on Broadway. Can-Stage have also programmed Harper Regan, which, alas, is not a lampoon of Canadian and American political leaders.

Stratfords season is built around the theme of discovery. This seems to mean plays about science, from the Jacobean charlatans of Ben Jonsons The Alchemist to the pretenders of Friedrich Durrenmatt's The Physicists, a play from the early 1960s that's been crying out for revival: the implications of its theme - was has been thought can never be unthought - grow more terrifying by the year. But it also takes in Sophocles Oedipus, whose hero certainly discovered stuff, and which marks an overdue Stratford debut for director Daniel Brooks.

The characters in the festivals four Shakespeares also make discoveries about themselves and each other; it's what drama's about. And, just to bring a few wheels full circle: the Tarragon season with which I began kicked off last year with An Enemy of the People, a free adaptation of a classic, and it ends with another, a "Bollywood version" of Much Ado About Nothing.

I dont know whether Im looking forward to that. But I can testify that the best Much Ado I've ever seen (by the Royal Shakespeare Company) was set in the British Raj, with Judi Dench and Donald Sinden as Beatrice and Benedick going around the block for the last time, and a Dogberry whose malapropisms were the result of his imperfect relationship with the English language. You couldn't get away with that now.
