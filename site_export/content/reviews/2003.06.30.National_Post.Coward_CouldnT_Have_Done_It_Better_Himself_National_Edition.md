PRESENT LAUGHTER

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

Stratford, Ont. - Garry Essendine, the protagonist of Noel Coward's Present Laughter, is not a playwright. That is one thing that differentiates him from his author. Another is that, though Coward himself tended to see the outside world in terms of the theatre, he did at least see it. Garry seems unaware that it exists at all. Otherwise, as has been recognized ever since the original 1942 production with Coward in the lead, the play is autobiographical, not as a literal record of events but as the distillation of a small but seductive world.

I doubt it has ever been recreated more completely or more scintillatingly than it is now at Stratford, where Brian Bedford gives it direction that is nearly impeccable and a central performance that is absolutely so. Garry is a London matinee idol, whose success has enabled him to build a personal and professional empire. Its wheels are turned by two men -- his manager and his producer, and two women -- his secretary and his not-quite-ex wife. They have to attend to his ego and, quite often, his love life as well as his career, and they frequently complain. However, as he never tires of reminding them, without his talent there would be no machine to maintain. Garry has achieved, purely as an actor (and probably, though we never really hear about it, as a director), the kind of eminence that it took his creator the exercise of half-a- dozen talents to attain.

His charm and his susceptibility to flattery expose him to the attentions of a succession of younger admirers (female, to protect the innocent). They arrive at night on his doorstep, claiming to have lost their latch keys, and emerge from the spare room the following morning, demanding breakfast from the resident domestics and collapsing into tears when their host and lover turns out to have only the haziest recollection of their existence. They can be dealt with.

A more serious threat emerges in the person of Joanna, who is the wife of one of Garry's male associates and the mistress of the other. (It's impossible to remember which relationship is which. The two men are indistinguishable, even when they're on stage together.) Joanna, as the Essendine entourage all agree, "is not one of us," but this is not a fate she seems at all willing to accept.

There's a relaxed quality about Present Laughter that makes it Coward's most attractive comedy. This is especially true of its first act, which sets up Garry and his lifestyle with no apparent signs of haste, even though a plot-line is already ticking meticulously away underneath. In its second act, with characters threatening to burst inopportunely out of rooms, the play becomes explicitly farcical, but it works throughout as a subtler kind of farce, one in which it's not the hero's reputation or physical well- being that is in jeopardy, but his place in his own mental world. Garry is about to embark on a taxing tour of Africa (nobody denies that he's a hard worker) and, as people never cease to remind him, he's getting older; the play's title comes from the Shakespeare song that tells us "youth's a stuff will not endure." He needs to be admired, needs to be in control, and though he may be the most intelligent person on stage, he is not necessarily the cleverest. He does, though, have untouchable style.

Bedford, from his first dressing-gowned entrance (descending the staircase that naturally dominates Michael Yeargan's beautifully lined Art Deco set), holds all the contradictions in balance. He is graciously tetchy, well-meaningly self-absorbed. As in Private Lives, he plays the Coward style without once aping the staccato Coward voice, thereby doing the play the greatest of services. Every line, whether a direct thrust or a glancing blow, is flawlessly timed, but the technique always illuminates the character.

He makes us love Garry (and like him, which is not the same thing) but he never sentimentalizes. The play is, in fact, a more unsparing self-portrait than can be found in Coward's diaries, autobiographies or other published pronouncements. People actually get to attack Garry for his self-centredness; he even gets rebuked for his "finger-wagging tirades," which sound very Cowardian. Which only reinforces the cardinal tenet of Coward's, and Garry's, own faith: that the theatre is all-important and therefore, of necessity, the place in which you tell the truth.

Seana McKenna is in dagger-sharp form as the perfect secretary, and Domini Blythe charming as the presumably perfect wife, though she is best when she too gets down to business. (I wish she didn't keep her hat on so much; it makes her look temporary.) The live-in staff are of golden quality, with Brian Tree imperturbably pertinacious as Garry's valet, and Patricia Collins something near sublime as his housekeeper who is a sloucher and a chain-smoker and a spiritualist and Scandinavian -- almost an embarrassment of riches for any actress.

There are a couple of flaws: Sara Botsford's Joanna is merely haughty, not much of a threat, and Michelle Giroux has an exaggerated initial idea of what a star-struck English deb might sound like, though she straightens out later on. Tim MacDonald is frighteningly accurate as her male counterpart, a solemn young playwright who comes to attack and stays to worship, and who has travelled all the way from Uckfield. (This is one feature of the play that defies export; to the English, English place names are hilarious. Just take it from me: Uckfield is a scream.) This character strikingly exemplifies the play's double nature. He allows Coward to let off steam, via Garry, with a cri de coeur about theatrical pretension, but is then slotted smartly into the structure, right up to the final curtain.

Which reminds me: All of Coward's major comedies -- this one, Hay Fever, Private Lives and Blithe Spirit -- end with a character or characters tip-toeing off the scene, leaving others to face some outstandingly cacophonous music. This is what a later dramatist, in a different connection, called "withdrawing with style from the chaos." In its way, it's a philosophy.
