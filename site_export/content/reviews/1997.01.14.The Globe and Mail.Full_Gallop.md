Diana Vreeland -- unzipped

Spectator

Tuesday, January 14, 1997

MY ideal Sunday kicks into gear around noon, with eggs Benedict and continues with a matinee. This only works if Im in New York. Toronto has both brunch and Sunday theatre, but not on the same scale or with the same feeling. London has never taken to the idea of Sunday theatre; the place's weekend rhythm is too sluggish without being -- as New York manages once a week -- relaxed.

The relaxation part is important, and to preserve it I go, ideally, to an off-Broadway show. Broadway is too frenetic, too big a deal, for a Sunday afternoon. It needs to be a night out; or, failing that, it should be taken in at a weekday matinee, where the routine unfolds with clockwork regularity. West 44th and 45th Streets, between 1:30 and 1:45 on a Wednesday or Saturday afternoon, are scenes of well-orchestrated frenzy as tourists and suburbanites crowd out of restaurants and into lobbies.

The 3 p.m. start is far more relaxing, a chance to be invited into somebody's world, rather than drummed into it as I was last Sunday at Full Gallop at the Westside theatre. Full Gallop is a one-woman show that invites you, rather imperiously, into the world of Diana Vreeland, first into her apartment and then into her head. Vreeland, as an editor at Harpers Bazaar and then as the editor at Vogue, presided over New York fashion from 1937 until being summarily fired in 1971. This is not in itself an achievement that interests me very much, and the snobbery that goes with the territory was as likely to repel as to intrigue me. On the other hand, I do like sacred monsters, at least from a distance. An unusual number of people had assured me that I would enjoy the show and I believed them. But I was curious as to how Mary Louise Wilson, performer and co-author, was going to bring it about.

She has done it, first of all, by looking outrageous. Her cheeks are rouged. In fact most of her face is rouged, all the way up to the hair line. (It actually looks like a wig line, but it could just be that her black locks have been spectacularly lacquered.) She matches her room, which contains one tiny expanse of white but is otherwise -- wall, ceiling, chairs, carpet and pillars -- all red. This is called practicing what you preach, since Vreeland was the Apostle of Red. (She was the inspiration for the fashion editor played by Kay Thompson in the Fred Astaire movie Funny Face . Thompsons big number in the film was called Think Pink , which seems appropriate since, for all her zing, her character was a pale reflection of the authentic Diana.) Early on in the play our hostess -- speaking in what I can best describe as a passionate drawl -- instructs us in her preferences and their attendant paradoxes: "I hate red with orange in it -- but I love orange with red in it." It's fascinating.

It's vital that a solo performer have someone to talk to. Otherwise you get stuck with watching them stumble around their homes, inexplicably talking to themselves or making marathon expository phone calls.

Vreeland talks to us because shes a compulsive chatterer, and well do. We have somehow to believe that we are simultaneously in a theatre and in her living room, but -- as always in the theatre -- that's all right with us if it is with her. Wilson and her collaborator, Mark Hampton, have plundered their subject's memoirs for bon mots, which she delivers with the swacked authority, the decisive delivery and abrupt changes of tack that constitute an American female comic tradition in themselves. It is, of course, the screwball style. Try to imagine a less fastidious Katharine Hepburn. Hepburn, even in the craziness of Bringing Up Baby , always sounded tinglingly sane. Wilson as Vreeland suspects that she might be what the world calls crazy, but doesnt care. Both are totally convinced of their own rightness.

Full Gallop , though it seems random, is rather cunningly shaped, filling us in alternately on her protagonists past and present. We actually meet her when she should be at the end of her rope, in the aftermath of being sacked. She is planning for a dinner party, despite having nothing in the house, a refractory staff, and the dimmest idea of where the kitchen might be. (She goes in search of it during the intermission, and returns from her expedition, shocked and blinking at its unconscionable whiteness.) A bill arrives, and is stuffed into an overflowing box of other bills. We sense -- she does make some phone calls -- that she is losing her social as well as her professional position. She ought to be falling to pieces, and after one call to her family in London, she does break down. Then we discover that she's crying not for herself but for her grandson: not that he's dead or anything, hes become a Buddhist and shaved his head. No one she cries "had hair like that," and its obviously heart-felt agony: a truth to her own principles more moving than any self-pity could be. That's what I liked best about the play; it could have been a tear-jerker and wasn't. And in fact Vreeland picks herself up at the end and accepts a job in the costume department of the Metropolitan Museum: a job that she has earlier scorned (who wants to work in the past?), but of which she apparently made a great success.

Vreelands mental, and certainly political, horizons were limited. One could wish that she had found something more to say about Hitler than that his mustache was unbelievable -- it was just wrong . On the other hand, given her delivery, it does sound like the last word in damnation. And when she says that "the best thing about life in London was Paris" or recalls New York frothing at the mouth at its first Balenciaga show, or declares that the twenties were "the whole beginning of the 20th century" (equalled, apparently, by the sixties), or proudly recalls that her husband ironed his shoelaces until the day he died you wouldnt dream of arguing with her. Actually you wouldn't dare, but more to the point is that you wouldn't want to. She can even take somebody elses line, acknowledge it as such, and still make it work for her. "As Joan Crawford said, if you want the girl next door, go next door." That got the kind of laugh that ripples round the auditorium, then starts all over again, louder.

One usually ends a piece like this saying how grateful one was to have been ushered into the presence of a rare spirit. I didnt feel that. I felt I had seen a woman who may have been entertaining brought to life by one who unquestionably was. I had recently made Wilson's acquaintance on, so to speak, the other side of the barricades, in an old Broadway musical called Flora, the Red Menace -- or, to be exact, on its original cast recording which I was reviewing for a book. She played a Communist agitator with a heavy schedule, and she brought the same fervour to that as she is doing now to Manhattan's queen of the transient but opulent. She makes you laugh with the woman, and, therefore, accept her. It's perfect for a Sunday afternoon.
