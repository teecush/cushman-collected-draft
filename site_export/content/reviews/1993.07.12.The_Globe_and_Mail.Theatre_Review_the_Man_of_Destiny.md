THEATRE REVIEW THE MAN OF DESTINY

Monday, July 12, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY GEORGE BERNARD SHAW Directed by Miles Potter Starring Stuart Hughes and Deborah Lambie At the Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the Lake Rating: 0 An incoherent Man of Destiny BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail SHAW'S earliest one-act play, this year's lunchtime show at Niagara, is also his first stab at history. He presents the young Napoleon, billeted at a wayside inn while campaigning in Italy and on the verge of decisive military success. Like other Shavian powerhouses, he talks a good game and prides himself on his freedom fron cant and illusion. Also like them, he is outmanoeuvred by a woman,one who acts instinctively on the principles - or lack of them - that he has had to work out intellectually.

Not much is at stake. The lady in the case is an Austrian spy who has defrauded Bonaparte of dispatches that he can live without anyway and secreted them, as he immediately guesses, in her bosom. (In which movie did Groucho Marx say: There must be a way of getting at that letter without upsetting the censor?) Among them is a piece of mail that could compromise Mrs. Bonaparte ("tonight, Josephine") but the play - a self- confessed trifle that is more trifling than its author may have acknowledged - cannot make this matter either, to the characters or to us. The plotting is irritatingly weak, but the dialogue is reasonably cheerful and the play should pass a painless hour.

The director, Miles Potter, muses in the program on Shaw's fascination with "great men" (and women) and sadly connects it to his infatuation, some 40 years later, with Stalin: "I like to think that if Shaw had lived to know the 'destiny' of Stalin as he did that of Napoleon or Saint Joan, he would have abandoned the character." What character? Shaw never wrote a play about Stalin. And it was a little late for him to tear up his earlier chronicle plays. Anyway, the truth about Stalin - as about Hitler, on whom Shaw was embarrassingly equivocal - was amply available during Shaw's lifetime, for those who cared to know it. And as far as it goes, his early view of Napoleon seems tenable enough. It's one of cynical admiration, and the play's title - slapped on to a virtual burlesque - is coolly ironic.

A director may be forgiven an incoherent program note, but not an incoherent production: certainly not of a play as slight as this one, which depends entirely on poised flamboyance. Shaw called it a "display of my knowledge of stage tricks," as opposed to its immediate predecessor Candida, of which he said - I'm plundering the program again - that when he read it to people "they can be heard sobbing three streets off." Which reminds me, I'm afraid, of his comments on a French troupe that came to London with productions of Ibsen that were said to have made their authour cry; "That's nothing; I've seen performances by English companies that would have driven him to suicide." I don't suggest that a bad production of The Man of Destiny would have impaired Shaw's own life-force, but he might have felt inclined - his words again - to rush upon the stage and rend the actors limb from limb.

Robert Benson, who plays the innkeeper, would probably have got off with a mild dislocation; he is not sufficiently ripe or olive-oily but he can time his lines. Peter Wilds as a blustering blockhead of a sub- lieutenant substitutes his own unreadiness for the character's and keeps his hat on in the presence of his general, which is not only insubordinate but untidy. It is also in flat contradiction of Shaw's own stage directions, which are not sacrosanct, but will do until something better comes along. The play needs a clean line, and this is not achieved by having the actors perch with attempted nonchalance on all available bits of furniture. Deborah Lambie's Strange Lady, substituting simpers for wit, is intolerable (and most unbecomingly costumed). Napoloeon, whom Shaw specified as having "no revolutionary untidiness about his dress or person" is loaded with it in Stuart Hughes's performance, which reeks, physically and vocally, of the post-hippy 1970s. He pulls himself together, though, to deliver a next-to-closing set-piece about the invulnerably hypocritical English. Shamelessly unmotivated, this speech plays triumphantly to a stereotype that Shaw himself did much to create. With England in tatters and the Empire a joke, it should now seem hopelessly dated, but some kind of race-memory sees it through.
