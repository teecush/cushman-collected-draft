Musicals are in the air at the moment, even more than usual.

Hamilton's success and its prestige continue undiminished, despite the illadvised mixtape. (Ironic, considering that is what the stage show was originally intended to be.) Meanwhile, in the movies, everyone's raving about La La Land. I'm eager to see it, though I can't help noticing that no review I have read has had much to say about the actual songs. I had to go to IMDb to discover their titles and who had written them. But the fact that it's an original, rather than an adaptation of a stage show is encouraging, after the false musical dawns of Chicago and Mamma Mia. Well, I must confess: I haven't seen the movie of Mamma Mia. The stage version was more than enough. Much more.

And of course there are musicals lining up for the coming year in Toronto and environs. Here are some of the more intriguing: Passing Strange is a Tony-winner from 2008 (though set in the 1970s). It's described as "a coming-of-age story told through sex, drugs, and rock 'n' roll." I can see how it might be told through the last of those, but it's surely being told about the first two. Anyway, it has a score by Mark Stewart (a.k.a Stew) that got a lot of attention at the time. It reaches us this month in a co-production by Obsidian who have collaborated on a few musicals in their time, and Studio 180 who haven't - though their director, Joel Greenberg, staged a lot of them in a former life. The venue is an unaccustomed one: the Opera House at Queen and Broadview.

The Elgin Theatre is the more familiar setting for the muchawaited Sousatzka, marking the return to producing of Garth Drabinsky. Confession of interest: I worked for Drabinsky's Livent organization for 18 months, and had a great time. My biased opinion is that the best of Livent's work (Kiss of the Spider Woman, Show Boat, Ragtime, Parade) constitute a track record unmatched by any commercial theatre organization since, or indeed for some time before.

The new show's director is Adrian Noble, former head of the Royal Shakespeare Company and responsible here for a memorable Stratford Hamlet; the choreographer is Graciela Daniele of Ragtime; and the cast includes Victoria Clark, renowned on Broadway for her performances in The Light in the Piazza and other things, and Montego Glover, who did a great turn here a few years ago in an otherwise misbegotten musical that claimed to be about Alberta Hunter.

However, the names that interest me most are those of Richard Maltby Jr. and David Shire, lyricist and composer respectively. They have been writing together since they were students in the late 1950s, and were primed to be the great American team of the post-Sondheim generation. Shire's music being both melodic and adventurous and Maltby's words sharp and character-driven. They certainly haven't lacked for success: Shire as a composer of film scores and Maltby as director of Ain't Misbehavin' and English lyricist of Miss Saigon. Together they wrote Baby, one of the few good musicals of the 1980s, and two whole heaps of wonderful songs collected in the anthology shows Starting Here, Starting Now and Closer Than Ever. But they've never had a Broadway hit together.

Sousatzka is set mainly in London but with tentacles spreading outwards in both place and time. It's based on a novel by Ruth Prawer Jabvala that also gave birth to the Shirley MacLaine movie Madame Sousatzka. Both book and film portray a Russian piano teacher instructing a gifted student from India. The show makes one major alteration: the young prodigy is now African. This was a great relief to Maltby and Shire, who didn't relish the idea of writing an Indian-flavoured score; they had attended a sitar concert in preparation, and Maltby told me that as they emerged he said to his collaborator, "Let's go back to your place and modulate." Now they can have jazz.

I confess - this seems to be my confessional week - that I have doubts about the title. The source material doesn't have great name recognition and, to quote a lyric from the musical Fiorello!, "Nobody likes a candidate whose name they can't spell." Yes, I too wish there were more Canadians in the cast of this "Broadway tryout," especially given the number of first-rate actor-singers we have on tap.

Meanwhile, we can also look forward to a couple of classic British shows: the Shaw Festival's Me and My Girl, from the 1930s (but an even bigger success in the 1980s) and, from the 1870s, the Stratford Festival's H.M.S. Pinafore, which in its day was the grand-daddy of all hit transatlantic transplants. Also at Stratford is Guys and Dolls, for the third time (and it cropped up not too long ago at the Shaw). No question that it deserves the exposure - it's the greatest of all musicals. But it does point up how few titles count as guaranteed cash-cows.

One wishes they could take a chance on something less familiar. I mentioned Fiorello!. A Pulitzer winner in the 1950s, when that award was rare for a musical, it deals with New York's fabled crusading Mayor LaGuardia. The line I quoted comes in a song called "Politics and Poker," Other shows have taken us into those legendary smoke-filled rooms, but it was left to the team of Jerry Bock and Sheldon Harnick, later of She Loves Me and Fiddler on the Roof, to give us a song you can really choke on. It might be a good choice for the Shaw, probably in one of their smaller theatres. I yearn for a staging of an earlier political show, the Gershwin brothers' Strike Up the Band, a satire on warmongers much harder-hitting and perennially pertinent than their more famous election lampoon Of Thee I Sing. It exists in two versions, both given sumptuous studio recordings in recent years. It must be possible to combine the best of both.

And of course there are current Broadway shows we will be itching to see. Natasha and Pierre and the Great Comet of 1812 (Great Comet for the listings) is already the hottest Broadway thing since Hamilton. The irresistible long-form title clues us in that this is a musical of War and Peace. And to judge from the CD of the original off-Broadway production, it's a good one, combining irreverent comment ("It's a complicated Russian novel/Everyone's got nine different names") with truthful, touching adaptation. Less lauded but still running is Something Rotten, a romp about a couple of Elizabethan theatrical wannabes, trying haplessly to compete with an apparently unstoppable phenomenon. Judging again from the recording, some of it is crass but some of it, especially when the man himself appears rhyming off his best-selling quotations, is truly funny. Stratford might have a ball with it.
