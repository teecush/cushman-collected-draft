LESLIE ARDEN AND FRIENDS

Canadian Stage Theatre, Toronto

I laughed twice during the opening number, which is a self- deprecating, audience-welcoming thing called Don't Expect Too Much. They werent belly laughs, more warmly appreciative chuckles, but two laughs of any kind are a lot for any song.

And Leslie Arden is not primarily a comic writer. She is the composer-lyricist whose uneven but impressive The House of Martin Guerre was staged in Toronto last year. Her current show, subtitled A Meeting of Minds, is a kind of modified anthology. Assisted by three singers and three musicians, she presents a cavalcade of songs from her works in progress. (Any musical is a work in progress until it gets staged, and frequently afterwards as well.) The concluding sequence is composed of Guerre songs, and they stand up very well. They end the show on a note of justified triumph.

The songs are arranged in groups, but there are no announcements, and only a few minimal passages of dialogue. We can tell from internal evidence that Ardens portfolio includes a musical about 18th-century Canadian pioneer women of Scottish extraction, and another based on the fairy tale about the peasant-girl who could weave straw into gold.

You can usually tell how the various sections fit together but you can't always work out when one has come to an end. I was still mentally caught up with the pioneer ladies when a wistful song called Robert Dreams came along; even with my special interest, it took me some time to realize that Robert was not one of their husbands, off in a world of his own, but somebody modern. I assume now that he belongs to the same project -- something, I guess, modestly satirical about Toronto yuppies -- as the three females who follow him onto the stage to sing The Ladies' List, which is about love and friendship and shopping.

Arden was a student in the musical-theatre course that Stephen Sondheim taught for a year at Oxford, and she seems to be drawn to similar subject matter. The listing ladies come over as gentler descendants of the ones who lunched in Company, a show whose central character was also called Robert. The fairy-tale bits, also including a Hansel and Gretel witch who sings a cheerful confessional called I Eat Children, wake obvious echoes of Into the Woods, though their affectionate jocularity is even more reminiscent of Mary Rodgers Once Upon a Mattress. The imagery of Demons Fly suggests a downright homage to a song in Sweeney Todd.

Arden lacks Sondheims all-pervasive irony, and she is more inclined to straightforward affirmation. When she has a man sing The World is Changing he means it positively and so does she. Sometimes her awareness and her idealism clash, as in Happy New Year,which is about counting our individual blessings despite massacres abroad and poverty at home; one knows what she means, but as a dramatic statement it doesn't really balance.

Her great lyrical strengths, though (other than the rare but basic knowledge of what to do with rhyme and metre), are that she can handle conversation in song and that, when she writes a solo, she charts the developing awareness of the character singing it. She rarely just takes an emotion and has it jump up and down on the spot.

There are Sondheim touches, too, in the nervous energy of her accompanying figures. (She does her own orchestrations, on top of everything else; in fact, she has five separate credits on the program's title page.) Her melodies, though, are two generations further on, which makes them two generations removed from the last period at which theatre music was pop music.

Unlike some of her American contemporaries, she hasnt gone art- song; she tends to fall back on folk and soft-rock rhythms, which sap her strength and make too many of her tunes, at least on first hearing, sound the same. Shes also capable, though, of writing a blues-drenched secular spiritual that galvanizes both the audience and the performers.

I'm not sure that her present format shows her wares off to best advantage. The individual mini-musicals tend to end just when you're getting involved in them, and though Allison Grant's staging has elegance, it lacks shape.

The singers -- Glynis Ranney, Jay Turvey and Julain Molnar -- are not natural revuers; they have fine voices but not especially winning personalities: not, at least, without consistent characters through which to filter them. Arden herself, safe behind her keyboard, sings comparatively little; but when she does give out, she does it with more warmth and colour than anyone else on stage.

Down in the forest of Arden, something stirs, something ambitious and intelligent and -- to a Canadian musical theatre whose aspirations have previously been defined by Anne of Green Gables and Billy Bishop Goes to War -- it may be the best thing to happen, ever.
