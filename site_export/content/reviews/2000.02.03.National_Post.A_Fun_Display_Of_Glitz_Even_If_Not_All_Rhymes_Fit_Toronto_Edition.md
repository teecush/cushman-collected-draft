JOSEPH AND THE AMAZING TECHNICOLOR DREAMCOAT

Pantages Theatre

Musical theatre partnerships -- Gilbert and Sullivan, Rodgers and Hart -- have traditionally been made up of opposites, each of whom could supply the qualities the other lacked. The word on Tim Rice and Andrew Lloyd Webber used to be that they complemented one another perfectly, since Webber didn't have a sense of humour while Rice had nothing but.

How true this may be of them, I know little (and what I do know I'm not telling), but it seems to work for their work. You can see it operating on a sliding scale, with Webber taking increasing control as their shows get more ambitious and less entertaining. But Joseph and the Amazing Technicolor Dreamcoat, their first musical -- a genesis based on Genesis -- is the one in which Rice is in charge.

The words in Joseph, though generally delightful, are not above criticism. In London last summer I witnessed civilization take another step backwards when a TV host, flourishing a cherished copy of the original 1970s LP, enthused about Rice's rhyming of "biscuit" with "district": "You won't," he cried, "find that in a rhyming dictionary!" (No, you won't; and for very good reason.)

I even have problems with the celebrated couplet in which Joseph offers his meteorological interpretation of Pharaoh's dreams: "All those things you saw in your pyjamas / Were a long-range forecast for your farmers." The idea is witty, and the images superb, but try singing "long-range forecast" so the syllables hit the beats.

All the same, there's a flavour to those lines -- one of amiable disrespect -- that typifies the whole piece. Joseph is a very English show (American performers don't always get it), and when Rice invokes P. G. Wodehouse to describe Pharaoh's butler ("the Jeeves of his time") he is acknowledging a cultural parent.

Like a Wodehouse novel or, indeed, a Wodehouse musical, Joseph tells its story straight while looking at it askance. Characters are cut off-handedly down to size, and anachronism is used not to patronize but to humanize. "I don't speak Egyptian very well" the hero sensibly laments, as he's dragged off into slavery in a foreign land. "No one comes to dinner now / We'd only eat them anyhow," sing his brothers in the throes of famine.

The score is enchantingly eclectic; basically rock 'n' roll, with Pharaoh a born-again Elvis (or maybe vice versa), it also takes in country, chanson and calypso, each accorded a full-length number whose insouciant irrelevance is its charm. Rice not only writes funny lyrics, he gets Webber writing funny music.

I guess it's facile to say that this first score is its composer's best, but if it hasn't all been downhill since, then it's certainly been a very strenuous road up. Strangely, though, Rice without Webber has proved even more turgid than Webber without Rice; the composer has seemed far happier abandoning his roots and writing mid-Atlantic than the lyricist, who maybe had more roots to lose.

The show famously started as a 40-minute school entertainment; it was subsequently presented at a London's leading youth theatre, the Young Vic, in a double bill with extracts from the mediaeval cycles of Biblical mystery plays, which, as it happens, also thrive on anchronism.

That was the best production of the show I've seen. The problem since has always been to make a brief but obvious hit last a whole evening. Numbers have been extended; having wrung every drop of juice from the parody songs, productions have tried to squeeze the pulp as well. There is an epilogue in the shape of a dance-mix finale that amounts to a miniature recap of the entire score. A show that used pop is being used by it.

This is the formula bequeathed us by the production that criss- crossed Canada and parts of the U.S. for years, usually with Donny Osmond as Joseph. It was outrageously glitzy, but it winked at its own outrageousness, and was imaginative with it. It was crammed with visual jokes that matched the verbal and musical ones. It was a great deal of fun.

The present version is an economy-size derivative of that one, with sets that are cut-down cut-outs. It's a fair amount of fun.

Some things are far too literal; doing the C&W song in cowboy gear kills the joke by underlining it. Patrick Cassidy (as Joseph) is conceivably a better actor than Osmond, and he does some ringing singing, but he, too, lacks wit.

Joseph's prophetic smugness, which used to be guilelessly amusing, is now simply smug. (He tells the royal baker whose death he's foretold not to worry, but "it's just that I was never wrong before." One thing missing from this staging is the subsequent sight of the baker's head brought to Pharaoh on a platter.)

Like his predecessors, Cassidy only gets to wear his dreamcoat on very special occasions, going through most of the evening bare- chested; I suppose somebody's already called the show The Loin King.

In ancient days, children, the part of the Narrator in Joseph was always played by a man, and I don't know that having it done by a woman is a good idea, at least not the way it actually works out. What happens is that a lady who was once a pop star (Deborah Gibson this time round) descends to Earth and sings with a determined smirk, as if instructing the kids in Biblical history was a career move undertaken on advice of the highest authority, whose wisdom she was beginning to doubt.

The kids, naturally, are locals and are worked very hard. The grown-up ensemble are billed as The Osmond Brothers Second Generation, though if you look at the small print, only two of them seem actually to be called Osmond; as in the source material, it's difficult to keep track of who begat whom. But they all dance energetically and seem to be enjoying it, which is what counts.

One credit to Dallet Norris' production; for the first time I can remember, I was touched when Joseph was reunited with his brothers. And as musical-theatre messages go, this one's casual benediction (Any Dream Will Do) is remarkably palatable.
