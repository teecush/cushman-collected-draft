PETER PAN

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

Productions of J.M. Barrie's Peter Pan fall into categories: those traditional and still in the majority, in which a lady plays Peter, and those gaining ground in recent years, in which a young gentleman does. No production, to my knowledge, has gone the whole hog, or perhaps the whole piglet, and cast an actual boy in the role, but a young actor who can pass for a boy can still automatically place the play in a more disturbing light. The boy may still refuse to grow up, but his play no longer has that option.

Dylan Trowbridge, who plays Peter at Shaw in the best all-round production I have encountered, gives us both the look and the essence of boyhood: vigorous, clear, energetic and sublimely selfish. Peter is the boy who runs away from his mother but still clings to her: The boy was heartbroken when he found that his real mother had locked the nursery window against him, so now he makes equivalent demands on the surrogate Wendy.

In the play's last section, when all but Peter have abandoned Neverland but when Wendy has promised to make annual trips to do his spring cleaning, Peter can forget to come for her for years on end but he will always do so eventually; he's still tied. She, though theoretically remembering, has forgotten in essence and in perpetuity simply by becoming a woman and having a daughter of her own. That daughter, a temporary substitute, will forget in her turn, and so will all their female descendants, "so long," Barrie's narrator tells us, "as children are young and innocent and heartless."

The catch is that Barrie himself, at sea with grown-up relationships, preferred children that way while knowing, Lewis Carroll-like, that he was going to be hurt by it. He was himself the Peter who refused to grow up and the parent (in his case an adoptive one) who was wounded when his children passed beyond him. The repeated questions of Fiona Byrne, this production's Wendy, as to what she really means to Peter, carry more than usual weight. They are not arch; they are potentially passionate and increasingly frustrated.

You didn't use to get that from productions of Peter Pan when I was a boy, but they were carefree cut-price affairs that didn't include the play's epilogue, which tells us most of this. That, as Captain Hook would say, is where the canker gnaws. (He does not, in this production, pronounce the "g" in gnaws, and personally I missed it.)

Barrie seems never to have stopped revising Peter Pan, whether as story, play or novel, and Christopher Newton has assembled his own text from the available variants. As a director, he is in top form; nobody stages literate spectacle better. Sue LePage designed the sets, and Kevin Lamotte the lighting.Very little is stinted. The flying is unprecedentedly prolonged and acrobatic, and only at one moment can you see the wires.

The crocodile (the one who swallowed a clock: We know what makes him tick) is of prehistoric proportions -- a veritable Moby-Dick to Hook's Ahab. The pirate ship is everything it should be, and for their ventures on land the buccaneers have a motorized sledge that might have been designed by Heath Robinson. The mermaids dance to the sound of Fred Astaire at his absolute vocal best. I did wonder, though, why Peter, at the end of the lagoon scene, uttered his famous line about dying being an awfully big adventure only after he had secured his escape.

There is acting going on, too. The lost boys are a varied and meticulously orchestrated ensemble, their scenes governed and taken to a new dimension by the inspired casting of the plumply mature Neil Barclay as Slightly Soiled, whom he makes a wonderful prep- school know-it-all. The pirates are less rewarding -- possibly less well-served by their author -- but they too have a champion in Bernard Behrens' divine Smee, though his role has been cruelly truncated. (The scene in which he and his shipmate Starkey meet the crocodile with their captain inside him has gone. No great literary loss perhaps, but why does the narrator bother to tell us that two of the pirates survived their defeat if we are never to learn which two?)

Jim Mezon's Hook lingers in the memory, as this actor's performances usually do, but it is too mild for its material. Hook, the periwigged pirate, is a baroque creation, even if he has doubts about his own authenticity. If a man is going to say, "Back, you pewling spawn! I'll show you all the road to dusty death," I don't think he should swallow the words, even if he is on the verge of being swallowed himself. Somehow this climactic shipboard scene is only a shadow of what one feels it should be. To be fair, it usually is; it was probably more richly imagined than written.

The production very tactfully spares our blushes by skating gently over the "do you believe in fairies" episode, but I question the wisdom of having Tinker Bell appear from time to time in the, so to speak, flesh: I found myself wondering for the first time why, instead of drinking Peter's poisoned medicine, she didn't simply spill it on the ground.

Overall, though, this production considerably surpasses the celebrated Royal Shakespeare Company production that was the first "serious" Peter. That felt like an essay on the play, or a journey round it; this is a realization of it, and a trip to its centre. It is the first straight Peter Pan that didn't make me secretly wish I were watching the musical.
