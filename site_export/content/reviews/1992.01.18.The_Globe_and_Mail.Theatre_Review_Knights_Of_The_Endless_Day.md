THEATRE REVIEW KNIGHTS OF THE ENDLESS DAY

Saturday, January 18, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY ROBERT PRIEST Directed by Jennifer Stein Starring Cliff Saunders At Young People's Theatre Rating: YPT's enlightened tale is a knightly quest for a plot High dudgeon and dragons BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto GOOD intentions are running amok at YPT where they ride out to slay the unenlightened dragons of medievalism under the sacred banners of childcare, health food, environmental concern and a classless society.

There actually are dragons on view in Knights of the Endless Day -a big one and its brood - and very impressive they look, curled and many-faceted and taking up every available space on the multi-crannied stage. It turns out, though, that they are more afraid of that predator Man than he is of them so the threat turns out to be a swindle.

And so it goes. All of it.

The five-year-old next to me was still asking at the end what the title meant, and I found it hard to enlighten him. Casting my mind back, I could recall an early scene in which a bright yellow disc hung a few feet above the stage and people sang about it. The music was fidgety and the words indistinct, but the general impression was that this was a sun that never set. The matter is never alluded to again. But we do learn that author Robert Priest is not the man to let mere theatricality stand between him and an easy pun.

All the early scenes (and songs) are choppily unhelpful, but eventually a situation emerges. There is a king who proclaims his intention to dub three new knights, humble birth no object. From an impenetrably staged series of tests three candidates emerge - two toffs and a commoner called Ogo - and, as in all folklore, the lowliest comes out on top. Untraditionally, he faces judgment in the middle of the sequence rather than at the end of it. Imagine Cinderella trying on the slipper with one Ugly Sister still to go, or the leaden casket opened before the silver one.

All three are allotted Quests, which occupy the second act and are non- events. The most uppity adventurer endures a humorous humiliation which might be fun to watch if it didn't happen entirely off stage. The second, who thought he was facing monsters, has a pain-free journey in search of garlic. Our hero, who must transport the royal infant across the kingdom to his mother, gets the giants and dragons, only it turns out they aren't really dangerous. Ogo doesn't draw his sword because that might endanger the baby, and, besides, his mother has counselled him in non-violence. Of course, the giant survives to eat more people, but nobody said pacifism was easy.

Not even this play says it. Ogo has an inner battle to fight, against his own warlike instincts. But he wins it every time in two seconds flat, so it's not much of a conflict. The YPT production is most charitably described as inconclusive, though the baby noises are stylishly done and Cliff Saunders' Ogo is the triumph of likeability over winsomeness. His carol on changing diapers has a perky tune but gives away its joke in the first stanza, and the rest of the score is mired in the innocuous banalities of folk-rock. The finale may be the most perfunctory on record. "Happy earthday - it's your birthday." The royal babe is one year old. Kids - of any age - deserve better. Knights of the Endless Day continues at Young People's Theatre until Feb. 9.
