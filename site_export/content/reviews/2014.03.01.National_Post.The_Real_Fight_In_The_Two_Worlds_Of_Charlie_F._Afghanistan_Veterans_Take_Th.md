The grandiose title The Two Worlds of Charlie F. conceals a fairly simple production. It's a dramadocumentary about British service personnel invalided out of Afghanistan, and it grew from a rehab-come-therapy project in the U.K. Listening to the testimonies of these men and women injured, traumatized, permanently crippled or all of the above, it occurred to the organizers that their experiences might make a show. And so it has. A few private performances led to public ones, under professional direction, then to a U.K. tour, and now to a second longer one, with a Toronto detour as its opening date.

The performers are either actual vets or actual actors, plus one who qualifies in both categories. This is Cassidy Little, whose pre-military career included separate stints as a dancer and a standup comic, and who also happens, by blessed coincidence, to have been born and partly brought up in Canada, "and don't you forget it." (He says those last five words himself. I couldn't help wonder if they'd always been in the script, or if they were added for local consumption. Either way, they got first-night cheers.) Little was a medic in Afghanistan, where most of his right leg was blown off. He plays Corporal Charlie Fowler - the Charlie F of the title - who was similarly maimed. His two worlds are the beforeand-after of this event, and they are also inhabited, to one degree or another, by most of the other characters. A minor but abiding puzzle of the piece is how closely the fictional Fowler is meant to resemble the actual Little, a nagging uncertainty that extends itself to the rest of the cast; even with the actors brought in for the occasion, one wonders whether they're playing real individuals or composites. The show has a credited writer, Owen Sheers, but it's fairly clear that most of the script derives from interviews.

The show begins with Littleas-Charlie seen in silhouette on his hospital bed, in mental and physical agony and possibly hallucinating. He then comes forward in a wheelchair, stump plainly visible, and addresses us directly; this prologue done, he fades out for a time, but he comes back full force later on. It does seem that, by however unfortunate a road, he's found his vocation; he's an easy, magnetic performer with a fluency and authority beyond anyone else in the cast, amateur or professional. In fact, having noncombatants in the cast at all may be a mistake. Little apart, none of the performers makes any great individual impact. The show's power derives from its authority as firsthand collective testimony. The direction, by Stephen Rayne, is efficient but unimaginative. There are songs, specially composed and apparently included because having them is some kind of documentary theatre rule. They range from innocuous to terrible; definitely in the latter category is a rehab number consisting of the names of drugs. (Choreography is even worse. What's it doing there?) Charlie's opening line, therefore the show's, is "your hearing is the last thing to go and the first to come back"; it's arresting information, and in the second act he relays it to a therapist, but nothing is made of the idea in between. The best sustained sequence, by far, is a lecture on all the horrific things that can happen to a soldier who is shot or who steps on a mine. The chilling central message is that though armaments and areas of engagement change with the ages, the real battlefield is always the human body. The listening soldiers are not, at this point, being instructed in self-preservation. They`re being told how to tend to the fallen guy next to them. This is a constant theme: The soldier's primary loyalty is not to a cause or a country but to his mates.

Background to the conflict is confined to a whistle stop listing of previous attempts, British or Russian, to subdue Afghanistan; why the U.S. and its allies should have been there at this particular time, or how the British and commonwealth troops might have felt about it, is never explored, indeed hardly mentioned. Everything is kept local and personal. We are given the contrast between the blandishments of the recruiting office and the first experience on the parade ground. We watch the soldiers bonding over letters from home, and over estranged wives or girlfriends (never boyfriends, though some of the troops are female) in both the before and after worlds. Charlie's fiancee leaves him, rendering him uncertain of just how he feels. (Little conveys this very well. It seems that in real life, he's happily married.) Most of these things, though, we've encountered in other plays, films, books, about this or other wars, and they seldom rise here above the generic. Comparisons keep suggesting themselves. Oh What a Lovely War gave a far more pungent grunt's eye view, and its songs were authentic (and authentically double-edged.) London Road gave us professional actors delivering the actual utterances of actual named people, and music was its raison d'etre, not an add-on. Afghanada and This Is War have already given us a Canadian, if determinedly apolitical, view of the same campaign. One oddity here: Charlie, in his initial ramblings, claims to have been taken prisoner by the Taliban and tortured for weeks. How did he escape. Or is it meant to be a delusion? It probably is, but - in a show so dependent on the authenticity of experience, it would be good to have it cleared up. The production can only be moving - which it is - to the extent that we believe it's real.
