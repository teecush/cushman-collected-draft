THE MILL ON THE FLOSS

Premier Dance Theatre

A celebrated critic once said that if a novel worked on stage, it was fairly safe to assume that it wasn't a first-rate novel to begin with. He said it at the end of the 1950s, and we have learned a few things since then: We've learned how to make dramatizations of novels into more than breathless enactments of the basic story- line. We have found ways of capturing a novel's narrative voice as well as its incidents, and we have learned to give the texts room to breathe; the Royal Shakespeare Company's eight-hour celebration of Dickens' Nicholas Nickleby remains the classic in both respects. At the very least we've discovered that, even when it doesn't entirely work, an adaptation of a great 19th-century novel (that's usually what we're talking about) can provide a richer experience than a simple bad play. It has a penumbra; it can at least hint at richness of character, of metaphor and of social background.

All this can be felt, somewhere, in The Mill on the Floss, Soulpepper's contribution to the World Stage season and a production that reunites this company with its first director, Robin Phillips. It gets, though, progressively harder to hold on to.

The main device of Helen Edmundson's version of the George Eliot book is to chop its heroine into thirds. Three actresses share the role of Maggie Tulliver, daughter of a rural English mill-owner and possessed -- like her author -- of more passion, intelligence and imagination than her society knows what to do with.

Maggie finds neither a personal nor professional outlet for her abilities. She has little luck with men. She is passionately attached to her father, and especially to her older brother Tom, but both in different ways let her down. Two men court her : Philip Wakem, the sensitive and musical hunchback son of a lawyer, and a drawing-room Lothario named Stephen Guest.

She is drawn to both, but social and personal pressures prevent a union with either. Her father goes bankrupt and loses the mill, though he's allowed, humiliatingly, to stay on as manager. Maggie and Tom are immersed in the struggle to regain their inheritance and -- to adopt the prevailing metaphor -- to keep their heads above water.

The three Maggies are not cast according to age. (We first meet the character as a nine-year old, but none of them is that young, nor tries to be.) Rather they are cast according to developing temperament. So, Torri Higginson is the headstrong Maggie, whom her family call "the gypsy"; Julia Arkos is the ascetic Maggie, who finds a creed in the teachings of Thomas a Kempis on self- abnegation; and Brenda Robins is the gauntly sexual Maggie, trying to come to terms with her own emotions.

All of these characteristics, of course, are at least latent throughout, so while each performer carries the burden of the action for a certain time, the other two are always on hand to argue, advise or observe. The device has its moments, and the first transformation is magic, but mostly it's just irritating. It prevents any of the actresses from having a real crack at the part, and it prevents the audience from bonding with the character. (They've got each other. Why do they need us?) But we need to have a stake in Maggie, if we are to follow her through a long, increasingly complicated evening.

For the first half, adaptor and director have the narrative pretty well in hand. Eliot's family comedy is of Shakespearean richness, and it travels well. Terence Kelly, if not the rugged patriarch I have always imagined, brings out the prickly pride that makes Mr. Tulliver buy his son a classical education (against his own natural doubts) and that eventually brings him to financial and physical ruin.

Roberta Maxwell is economically delightful, and a little frightening, as his wife, obsessed with the family linen. The same mature actress inexplicably doubles as Maggie's cousin Lucy, her rival for Stephen's love, and it is here that disaster sets in. The play, which hitherto has pretty much set its own tempo, begins to sink under the sheer pressure of the novel's events; while, with no sense of the girls being contemporaries, tension evaporates, and meaning, and even understanding.

Most of the men, too, are cast above their proper age, but they get away with it. (And if that sounds sexist, too bad.) Steven Sutcliffe is superbly sensitive as the long-suffering Philip; Tom McCamus is suitably Byronic as Stephen (and even better as a sarcastic school-teacher); and Stephen Ouimette, cast rewardingly against type, has the full measure of Tom, from self-centred but likeable cub to prematurely hardened young bear.

We do, though, lose track of the relationship between brother and sister, and this has devastating consequences for the finale. The novel's epigraph is "in their deaths, they were not divided," and the pair end up consumed by the river that flows beside their home.

Even in the book, this conclusion seems arbitrary; in the play it comes out of nowhere, though in theory it's been set up by Maggie's life-long obsession with the fate of medieval witches who were hanged if they were and drowned if they weren't. As a woman intellectually advanced but emotionally unsupported, Maggie's felt herself in a similar position. Up until now Phillips has staged the play with sober skill, on a rostrum with a couple of drop-in screens. His evocation of the flood, involving a full complement of actors waving umbrellas, is all right but hardly cataclysmic; and when it was over, the audience seemed surprised to find that the play was too. From any director, this concluding stumble in rhythm would be regrettable; from one of Phillips' stature it's dumbfounding.
