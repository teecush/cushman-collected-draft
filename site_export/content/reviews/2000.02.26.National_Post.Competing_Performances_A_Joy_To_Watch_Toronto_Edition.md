ENIGMA VARIATIONS

Royal Alexandra

A now deceased English character actor once remarked of an especially portentous play in which he had been inveigled into appearing that "it all took place in Scandinavia, and rightly." Enigma Variations is not portentous; though it has some weighty themes, it handles them with a very light touch. It was written by a Frenchman, Eric-Emmanuel Schmitt, but takes place in Scandinavia -- on a remote island, in fact, off the Norwegian coast -- and this seems to suit it.

It especially suits the more assertive of the two characters, a Nobel Prize-winning novelist whom the awards committee must have welcomed as one of their own. The role is played by Donald Sutherland who, returning to the stage after an absence of 20 years, looks as if he has never been away.

He also looks, as he often has in movies, impeccably Nordic: tall, gaunt, and disconcertingly genial. It's disconcerting because the geniality on this occasion is that of egomania. Sutherland's performance describes a great arc of self-absorption, one that begins in vociferous scorn for the idea of conventionally tender feelings and ends by hugging them to his own chest.

He is moving here, partly because he is playing someone who has denied the possibility of being moved.

The one quality that you don't necessarily expect of film stars, he possesses in abundance: he can shape and sustain long speeches. There are moments when he seems like credible casting for King Lear.

Sutherland's novelist, who bears the uncommon name of Abel Znorko, has written a long and successful series of chilly philosophical tales but has scored his greatest hit with a piece of outright romance: a book that consists of an exchange of letters between two lovers who choose to live apart rather than risk having their passion sullied by routine. Everyone wonders what has caused this change, whether the book is autobiographical, and if so who the woman might be.

Abel, whose imposing island retreat is both the reward of his fame and the self-chosen symbol of his aloofness, has refused all requests for interviews, but he does give in to one. It comes from Erik Larsen, who claims to feel a deep connection to Abel's work and wants to write him up for a small-town newspaper -- though it turns out that the place in question is not just any small town.

Erik is a physically small man, nervous but pertinacious and with his own agenda. John Rubinstein plays him almost to perfection; the flaws are a few self-indulgent moments when he lingers too calculatingly on some speeches about the pains, pleasures and rewards of everyday love.

Abel, apparently, can tell that Erik is married just by looking at him. Abel believes in love as sex, which doesn't last except in memory; Erik believes in love as friendship, which endures almost by definition. In real life, of course, the two conceptions are not that starkly opposed; most people get a bit of both. But this is a stripped-down kind of play, and the convention works perfectly well.

Abel scoffs at Erik's literal-minded interpretation of his novel, but it turns out that Erik is right and that the book is a transcript of Abel's own experience. (As, more than critical theory would like to admit, works of fiction very often are.) And that is about as much of the action as I feel comfortable disclosing, and maybe more than I should.

Part of the play's fascination is that each of the two participants is playing a cat-and-mouse game, though Abel, accustomed to domination, is the more relishingly upfront about it.

The story is told through a series of surprises; there are four major revelations, two of them coming so close together that they seem like one. The first, and the most crucial, can be seen coming a long way off and is the more satisfying and persuasive because of it; it feels inevitable. The last is thoroughly disorienting; it means rethinking everything that has happened so far, and at the time I resented it. Now I think it makes sense.

This isn't a play that one would necessarily want to see a second time (except perhaps for the pleasure of comparing performances), not so much because it proceeds by shock effects (including, come to think of it, Sutherland's shock of white hair) as because everything in it is very precisely laid out.

The characters know what they think and feel, and they tell us; and when they are forced to change their minds or their hearts, they are very lucid about that, too. So it's all on the surface, which is not the same as being, in any derogatory sense, superficial. It is, in the French tradition, very elegant and economical, and the structural pleasure it provides is acute. (Abel has a gun, and the author has taken to heart Chekhov's admonition that you don't bring one of those on stage unless you mean to use it; it's used here as recurring dramatic punctuation, and it means something different each time.)

The things it says about love -- and, to a lesser extent, creativity -- are not new, but they are intelligent and felt, and often wittily expressed; the translation, acknowledged in the program in surprisingly small print, is by Roeg Sutherland, the star's son.

The only thing I don't like about the play is its title. It refers to the Elgar composition (of which we hear occasional snatches) and is used as a fancy cultural metaphor for the many- faceted unknowability of a woman. This is a subject that the two men establish perfectly well when they talk about her.

Anthony Page's direction is both intense and relaxed, and the set, by the celebrated Ming Cho Lee, contributes a glorious sense of space and elevation. The production is bound for Broadway, where it should be an enormous success and -- what is not always the case -- a deserving one.
