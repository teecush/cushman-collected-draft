HAPPY

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

That Ronnie Burkett is a master puppeteer can by now be taken for granted.

The marionette work in Happy, his contribution to World Stage, is fully up to standard. Each of the dolls is a lovingly individualized creation, and they are worked -- sometimes as many as half-a-dozen of them at once -- with the usual fearsomely intricate skill. Burkett pulls all the strings, does all the voices; he also designed the set and costumes, and of course wrote the script. Hooray for him.

He also has dramatic ambitions far beyond those of your average Punch-and-Judy merchant. His previous show, Street of Blood, realized them triumphantly. This one doesn't, though it certainly tries hard.

Its title, which has big-time thematic significance, is also the name of its chief character and raisonneur. Happy, named without apparent irony, is a loquacious old man who lives in a small-town rooming house, most of whose other occupants are his contemporaries. The exceptions are a married couple, very young and very loving; their happiness is cut short when the husband suffers an aneurysm and abruptly expires, just as they are on the verge of making love.

The rest of the action is largely concerned with his widow's attempts to come to terms with her loss. The old people around her are mostly in similar states of mourning, many of them maintained for years. They all nourish fantasies, some of them concerning one another. One innocent old fellow, for example, has for years been infatuated with the now-housebound town vamp.

I have to quote from the press release, which says that the play explodes "the commonly held belief that life exists somewhere in the subtle shadings of grey."

I am at a loss here because this common belief is not one I have ever come across, at least not expressed in those terms, or anyway not outside the lyrics of pop songs.

The contrary belief, apparently, is that "life exists ... in a rainbow of colours and endless dimensions." And Happy, whose crackerbarrel philosophisings are interminable, actually tells us with a straight face that if you want the rainbow, you must have the rain.

To support this theory of colours, Burkett has supplied an oppressively unwieldy superstructure. At regular intervals the doll's-house set is trundled around to reveal a puppet stage upon the puppet stage. On it there cavorts a godlike and/or Satanic figure, who seems to be running some kind of baleful nightclub while luxuriating over what fools them mortals be. His exact power over them is difficult to gauge; maybe he's just a voyeur.

Anyway, he don't allow no colour in his kingdom; the performers on his bill sing songs that make Jacques Brel sound cheerful, and he gets very angry when his assistant actually appears with traces of red on his cheeks. He himself has nothing new to say, and keeps on saying it.

Life below is repetitive, too. Every so often the play says things, about love and loss, that I found both true and moving, but they never have any vital connection to the people saying them. To call the characters puppets may seem pointless in the circumstances, but the joy of Burkett's best work is that one takes the characters as real, more so than many whom we see portrayed in conventional plays by flesh-and-blood actors.

The bereaved young wife, though she has a nasty surprise to spring on us at the end, spends most of the time just moping, repeating a catch-phrase about jumping or being pushed that doggedly fails to resonate.

Happy himself has very few scenes with his less-fortunate compatriots, and this adds to the monotony. (It also seems to undercut the play's philosophy: If you believe in the clash of colours, why not bring them together?)

Near the end, we learn his optimism has not come easily, but at this point the revelations about the traumas in his past seem gratuitous. It's just putting on the agony, like the last-minute disclosure in Street of Blood that the housewife heroine had AIDS.

Burkett's acting skills, which I had previously thought inexhaustible, turn out to have limits. They do not extend to giving vocal life to a stageful of characters who are old or depressed or both; the overall tone is a drone -- a whimsical drone, at that.

There is, though, one notably succesful scene. In Street of Blood, Burkett's confessed obsession with the Holocaust seemed dragged-in, perhaps because there was quite enough going on in the play without it.

Here, though, with less competition, he makes good on it. Happy remembers one of his wartime buddies who took part in the liberation of a camp, and we see him desperately trying to comfort a survivor. It's a very simple scene, and a powerful one. I don't think it would work with actors.
