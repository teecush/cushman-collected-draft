ANTIGONE

The Great Hall, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Time has played some interesting tricks on Jean Anouilh's Antigone, which is fair enough since the play itself plays tricks with the Sophoclean drama on which it is based. Anouilh wrote his version during the Nazi occupation of France, and it must have been plain that his Antigone, defying Theban martial law by burying the body of her brother Polyneices after he has been declared an enemy of the state, stood for all the desperate heroes of the French Resistance. It could not be made too plain, however; and Anouilh was careful to give Creon, king of Thebes and Antigones uncle, arguments as good as his nieces.

This makes, of course, for excellent drama; the play, certainly in this taut new production by the Red Red Rose company, exerts a superb, unbroken grip. However, one vital element has gone irretrievably missing. I said we find Creon's arguments as good as Antigone's; actually, most of us will find them better. We no longer see Creon as a smooth apologist for collaboration; we take him at his own valuation as a conscientious workman, trying to restore order after a vicious civil war. When Antigone, having been caught at her corpse-covering, is brought before him, Creon -- her putative father-in-law as well as her uncle -- tells her not to be so silly and to go to her room. When Antigone makes it clear she will immediately leave it to re-scatter the earth that Creons guards have removed, he tries another tack. Does she, he asks, really believe all that superstitious nonsense about a mans shade being doomed to wander to eternity if it is not interred to the accompaniment of some pious words hurriedly intoned by bored and boring priests? Antigone admits that she doesn't.

This is where Anouilh departs most significantly from his source. Sophocles' Antigone does believe. His Creon does, too; he knows he is elevating laws of man above laws of the gods and, later on, the gods themselves tell him so. Sophocles play has been celebrated for centuries as a model of faultlessly balanced dialectic, but it actually leaves no doubt that Creon is a tyrant.

Anouilh, writing in an irreligious age, is far more ambiguous. His Antigone survives Creons assault by asserting that, superstition notwithstanding, the right thing is the right thing. She even withstands his assertions that the brother she mourns was actually a selfish thug. She finally breaks down when he reveals that he doesn't know if the body is even Polyneices'; it is just as likely to be that of his equally worthless brother Eteocles, who died fighting him and has been proclaimed a hero by virtue of having been in the right place at the right time. Creon then unwisely presses his advantage by telling Antigone about the happiness in store for her as the wife of his son Haemon, who loves her and whom she loves. Sensing that such mediocre pleasures will merely dull her idealism, Antigone jumps right back and opts for death. This is even less persuasive than the similar moment in Shaws Saint Joan, when the maid, having given up on her voices, goes to the stake only because it seems preferable to being locked up.

In the days of the Resistance, most of whose acts were suicide missions, it would have struck a chord. To us, it seems what Creon calls it: self-indulgence. Antigone, choosing death as an awfully big adventure preferable to growing up, appears to be auditioning for the role of Peter Pans ideal girlfriend.

The idea for which Sarah Phillips' production seems most pleased with itself is also its most questionable. It consists of eliminating Anouilh's impersonal one-actor chorus, and giving the lines to Antigone herself. The chorus function, apart from filling in holes in the story, is to expound a theory of tragedy; tragedy, we are told, differs from drama (melodrama might be a better word) in being calm, unhysterical, a matter of playing out pre-determined roles. A person has ideals, acts on them, and the machine is in motion. Letting Antigone herself tell us this may save an extra salary but it invites us, and the actress, to take her rather too much at her own valuation; she talks quite enough as things are, and now she is permitted not just to behave like a tragic heroine but to present herself as one.

This is not to attack the way Christine Brubaker speaks the choral lines; she does them impeccably. She is a little less successful when it comes to putting theory into practice, as Antigone herself. The characters salient quality is the one with which most actors have the most trouble: implacability. The temptation is always to work your way round it. When this Antigone berates her sister Ismene, whom she wants for an accomplice, she seems to be trying to persuade her: But Antigone would never stoop to persuasion. (Ismene says she sort of sees what Uncle Creon means; Antigone replies that she doesn't want to "sort of see" anything, which gives you the girl in a nutshell.) But she is very fine, the picture of a blackmailing waif, when she confronts Creon, and finer still, confused on the edge of her grave, in the last scene, shared only with a guard (Michael Healey, pricelessly embarrassed) whose intentions are good but whose emotional and intellectual resources are rigorously limited.

Richard Greenblatt plays Creon with a wry, weary reasonableness that I found almost totally convincing as argument and entirely so as characterization; only at the end does he seem insufficiently weighty and worn down; this, after all, is his tragedy as well -- he, too, has taken on a role he has to play -- and he loses as much as Antigone, with the extra twist that he has to live with it.

Haemon is bland and Ismene weak, but Elizabeth Brown as the nurse is excellent. She is played French -- or, presumably, French- Canadian -- and is constantly sliding between the original text (I assume it's the original) and the directors own new translation; Antigone replies in kind, and it gives their scenes an extraordinary intimacy. Anouilh, as you would expect, is masterly at supplying the kind of domestic detail the Greek tragedians disdained. This doesnt make his characters more vivid than Sophocles'; it makes them vivid in a different way. Probably it's a lesser way, and some of it seems excessively knowing. But it gets at least within striking distance of the ideal of tragedy that it propounds; and this production, beautifully set by Camellia Koo with a stark assortment of chairs and lamps, does it proud.
