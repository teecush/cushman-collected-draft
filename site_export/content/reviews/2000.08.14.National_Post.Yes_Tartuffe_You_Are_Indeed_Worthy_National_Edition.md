TARTUFFE

Festival Theatre, Stratford

STRATFORD - At the end of Friday's premiere of Tartuffe, Richard Monette, director of the Stratford Festival and of this production, paid tribute to Alec Guinness, the festivals first-ever leading man. Last week, fortuitously, was Ontario Moliere Week -- this Tartuffe following on the heels of Soulpeppers The School for Wives -- and the coincidence had me thinking what a waste it had been that Guinness, one of the premier comedians of the 20th century, never got to appear in anything by the most celebrated of comic playwrights. Think of what the master delineator of the beleaguered bourgeois might have made of the jealous Arnolphe in School; or of how our greatest actor of holy men, true and false, might have flowered as the arch-hypocrite Tartuffe.

None of which is to impugn the current performances by Joseph Ziegler and Brian Bedford. Bedford indeed, returning to a part that he played triumphantly at Stratford 17 years ago, renders Tartuffe as I like to think Guinness might have done: as a consummate religious poseur who may actually believe his own protestations. This makes him virtually impregnable. He is the lowest of confidence- tricksters, insinuating himself into the house of the wealthy Monsieur Orgon and proceeding to take over the whole family.

He stands by, volubly protesting his own unworthiness, while Orgon plans to marry his daughter to Tartuffe and to disinherit his only son in his favour. All the while he is plotting the seduction of his benefactor's wife. Tartuffe's first line, in Richard Wilburs translation, is a casual hang up my hair-shirt; Bedford -- sad- faced, scrubby-bearded and penitentially gowned -- never drops his guard for a moment. He takes a theological stance -- "heaven is not averse to compromise" -- even when proposing adultery. Since he is the first to confess that he is a sinful man, nobody can shame him by accusing him of it.

When his victim finally learns the truth, Tartuffe, enraged, takes the moral high ground. When definitively unmasked and consigned to prison, Bedford spits contemptuously at his persecutors and strolls impenitently off -- not an unmasked villain but a man unshakeably conscious of his own superiority. He will be out again soon and will find other dupes. Bedford plays with his customary dazzling economy; he is the only actor, in either of these two new productions, who consistently makes Wilburs rhyming couplets dance. He makes them sound like a very sensible, as well as a very attractive, way of carrying on a conversation.

For all that, something is missing now that was present the last time: sexual tension. I remember the seduction scene in John Hirsch's production as one of the most transfixing I have ever seen on the festival stage; Bedford and Domini Blythe gave it a degree of contact and intimacy that on that sweeping platform is very rare indeed. Each character seemed to be drawing out unsuspected depths in the other. In Monette's production, its high-grade situation comedy. Lucy Peacock, carrying her head as delectably high as that of her plumed namesake, plays the besieged Elmire with unruffled confidence; shes afraid that her husband, hidden beneath the table, may not emerge from his hiding place in time to rescue her, but she never has any doubts about herself.

The play's comic set pieces are done, on the whole, very well. Seana McKenna, in the best maid's part ever written (I read that somewhere and it seems to be true), has a great scene in which, bidden by an enraged Orgon to hold her tongue, she sits emitting great waves of infuriating silence; she gets more out of this than any actress I have seen.

Earlier she has had a famous duet with her master -- one in which he returns home and is told whats happened in the family in his absence. Informed that his wife has been seriously ill, Orgon responds with just two reiterated phrases. One is Ah! And Tartuffe?; the other, when he is reassured that his protege has been sitting up and wolfing down all available nourishment, is "poor fellow!"

James Blendick hits the perfect bass notes for this repeated tune -- hes something of a barrel-Orgon -- and in general does as much for the role as bemused stolidity can.

This, however, may not be enough. Orgon's part is bigger than Tartuffe's (Moliere played it himself, which should be a clue) and it is he who goes through the familiar Moliere hoops: He is the fool whose folly is anatomized and flayed, the monomaniac who refuses all good advice. (Most of the latter comes from his invariably sensible brother-in-law -- Keith Diniciol does an amusing job here of being both intelligent and insufferable) Blendick conscientiously huffs, but by the last act, when Orgon is undeceived and furious, he is all out of puff; the scene in which a bailiff arrives to throw him out of his own house is here very limp all round. Even when this Orgon is in the grip of his obsession, his dependency is strangely untroubling. The play doesnt require the insistent darkness with which Lazslo Marton has paradoxically illuminated The School for Wives -- Tartuffe is a stronger play to begin with -- but it could be more painful; and if it were, it would be funnier.

The costumes here seem a hundred years later than the play, leaving you wondering just which French king is being celebrated in the denouement, when he emerges as rex ex machina. (Molieres Louis XIV reigned long but not that long; and neither of his successors had his sunny reputation.) The set, on the other hand, is timeless; carried over from the earlier production, it's the work of Tanya Moiseiwitsch, doyenne of designers, creator of this stage, and present in person to receive another first-night tribute and a magnificently merited standing ovation.
