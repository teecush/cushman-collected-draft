ean Anouilhs Ring Round the Moon is a play built on extravagance and magic. You might deduce as much from its title, which admittedly was not Anouilh's own. He called his play, a wry twist on the Cinderella story, L'Invitation au Chateau, which would sound a lot more prosaic rendered literally into English than it does in French. The English title was the inspiration of the translator, Christopher Fry. In the immediate aftermath of the Second World War, Fry and Anouilh were regarded as complementary figures, young masters of verbal and theatrical legerdemain, restoring wit and fun and glamour to cultures starved by austerity and, in Frances case, scarred by occupation.

Immensely popular in the 40s and '50s, both men had fallen violently out of fashion by the committed '60s; when I was an undergraduate, my earnest friends could hardly pronounce either of their names without spitting. Of the two, Anouilh has since fared better; Frys own jingling verse plays have fallen almost completely (and probably unfairly) off the radar, but Ring Round the Moon, in prose, has survived. For reasons artistic or contractual or both, this is a rare example of a foreign play persisting in the repertoire for nearly 60 years without being re-translated. For all that, Im not sure that Fry's version does it full justice. Anouilh's characteristic tone is one of bitter-sweet farce, and though in this play the sweet predominates, there is an underlying toughness with which Fry, despite some lyrical moments and some very funny ones, seems uneasy.

It could still work, and it has done. But something is wrong with Joseph Zieglers Soulpepper production; maybe, its that as a director Ziegler is more logician than magician. (He's a magical actor, and if Soulpepper or any other Canadian company were to revive Anouilh's best play The Waltz of the Toreadors, hed be my first choice for the lead.) Individual scenes here make sense, but they dont fold into, or explode out of, one another. There isn't a rhythm.

The Cinderella is Isabelle, a penniless ballet-dancer who's invited to the chateau of Madame Desmortes who, naturally, is giving a ball. The invitation has actually been extended, though, by her nephew Hugo. Hugo has an identical twin brother, Frederic; theyre played, of course, by the same actor. Hugo is nasty, Frederic, nice, though this seems to boil down in practice to Hugo being worldly (he claims that the 10 minutes by which hes the elder have taught him all there is to know about women) and Frederic, shy. But Hugo is certainly manipulative, he wants Isabelle to attract Frederic, and thus arouse the jealousy of Frederic's unkind fiancee Diana Messerschmann, whose father is a former tailor from Kracow, now one of the richest men in the world and not enjoying it much. Hugo turns out not to be as dispassionate or uncaring as he'd like to think. Isabelles own feelings get involved, and hurt. Sub-plots and side-actions add to the overall mix of whimsy and satire.

Christina Poddubiuks delicate anteroom of a set promises enchantment that it never quite delivers. (Richer lighting might help; the first act in particular seems to unfold in shades of grey.) The ball itself is going on next door; it's well evoked by Mike Ross's musical score, less well by the dancing couples who periodically spill over onto the stage and whose efforts seem effortful. The plays most famous scene, tangential to the plot but vital to the mood, may be a tango-with-dialogue performed by Lady India, the millionaires self-consciously smouldering mistress, and his secretary Patrice, her reluctant other lover. It misfires here, with the words ill-matched to the moves and with Kristen Thomson radically miscast as a feather-witted femme fatale. (Her partner, Kevin Bundy, gets his petrified character perfectly.) Also off-key is Diana Donnelly's Diana, less spoiled than graceless.

Most of the performances are fine. Brenda Robins has never been better than she is as Isabelle's mother, a refugee from dowdiness intoxicated by glamour, and Brenda Bazinet, who plays her old friend and Madames tyrannized companion, never as good. Madame herself is Nancy Palk, giving the best of her grande-dame performances as a wheelchair-bound chatelaine who never does an unkind thing and never says a kind one; she unrolls her sentences with superb bite. (She looks as if shes about to break into Liaisons from A Little Night Music; not such a stretch as Sondheim and Prince considered this play and Jean Renoir's Rules of the Game as source-material before settling on Smiles of a Summer Night.) Somehow, though, she's never as much at the centre of the play as she should be. Nor, despite his charm and his constant presence as one brother or the other, is Jordan Pettle; Frederic gives him no trouble, and he has a very fair shot at Hugo, witty but without capturing the diabolism that presumably should drive the action. To be fair: For a self-proclaimed bastard, Hugo has a disconcerting habit of acting in everybodys best interests. Thankfully, the production has an

emotional core in Patricia Fagans Isabelle, rather drably gowned for the belle of the ball, but alight with moral and emotional fervour, and especially good at conveying varying shades of shame. She shares the best scene with Diego Matamoros, wearily ruthless as the melancholy Messerschmann; when the two of them tear up a selection of his bank-notes, her bitter cry of "let's not forget the poor" echoes in the mind. (These days Matamoros's authority on a stage almost constitutes unfair competition, but shes up to it.) The cast is completed by Richard Clarkin as Isabelles protector, who wants to add her to his butterfly collection, and John Jarvis, who seems a little spry and Jeevesian for the text's "crumbling butler" but is admirable nonetheless. This, though, is a play that should beguile you, then disturb you, then charm you all over again, all seamlessly. Here it's a bumpy ride that leaves you clutching at moments.
