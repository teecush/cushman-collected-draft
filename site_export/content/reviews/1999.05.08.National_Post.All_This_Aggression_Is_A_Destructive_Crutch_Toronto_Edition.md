BETTER LIVING

George F. Walker's Better Living is about a family, predominantly female, that has cast out a tyrannical and abusive father. Some years later he returns and, before you know it, he has taken over their lives again and even got them liking it, at least some of them, some of the time.

It could be an allegory for a society that, after living under a self-righteous and authoritarian regime, turns right around and elects it again. Indeed -- since this is not a new play but a revival of one first seen in 1986 -- you might go further and credit the author with prophetic gifts.

Or maybe he just knows the way the world goes.

The particular microcosm he holds up for us is a cluttered, chaotic, peeled-wallpaper house in what is obviously one of the less fashionable sections of Toronto.

Here lives Nora, with whichever of her three daughters happens to be passing through at the time. There is Elizabeth, who is now a lawyer but once supported the family through her earnings as a prostitute. There is Mary Ann, married and a mess, who has surrendered her own child to the custody of her husband. There is Gail, the youngest, enthusiastically making out with a blockhead boyfriend, with the firm approval of her mother who fancies having as many grandchildren as possible.

At the start of the play, Gail and her partner are surprised by Uncle Jack, a priest who seems to have lost his vocation but kept his job. Gail acknowledges apologetically, or as close to apologetically as anybody gets in this play, that Jack must disapprove "of sex outside the church." (It takes Jack a bemused moment to realize that this is Gail's idea of a metaphor.)

The memory of Tom, her ex-policeman husband, has so traumatized Nora that when he reappears she refuses to recognize him. She calls him Tim. (She does, after he has moved back in and made himself at home, acknowledge a resemblance.)

One may sympathize with Tom, having his identity denied, but that doesn't last. He wants the family to repair and restore the house -- something they have signally failed at hitherto -- but he also wants to turn it into a fortress against the incursions of people less fortunate than they are: a startling concept, but they agree that such people may exist. Nora is newly energized, and sad Mary Ann finds that licking envelopes gives her a new purpose in life.

Tom's dreams of a society built on selfishness have to be violently imposed; and here, it turns out, history is repeating itself. His earlier behaviour had been so horrifying that Nora and Jack had, on several occasions, tried to kill him. They had of course failed; which, as they nonchalantly remark, is not like them at all.

There are echoes of the American Sam Shepard (a Gothic skeleton in every closet), and the British Joe Orton (mayhem taken comically for granted), but there is no question that Walker has created his own dramatic world. It is characterized by a high level of violence, physical and verbal, but it is cartoon violence in which nobody seems to get hurt. That may not be the intention; even if Walker is not exactly in the compassion business, I think he wants us to feel for his characters. But though they have a strong collective impact, and though they wake some disturbing echoes of the cruel world outside, they have no dramatic life as individuals.

That at least is the impression in this slapdash production by the author himself. It is very fast and very loud. Speed is certainly preferable to sloth, but it has no intrinsic theatrical merit unless it is allied to precision. Here, everyone gabbles, so the play is garbled. It's worse because -- this being a play about a family -- everybody also shouts. (Walker isn't in the subtlety business either.) Information gets obscured, and feelings drowned.

For the actors, all this aggression serves as a crutch, but a destructive one. The first night was two-thirds over before Nancy Beatty had established any kind of hold on Nora.

I felt grateful when Fiona Highet's Elizabeth was quiet and lucid, or when Thea Gill's Mary Ann was quiet and distraught -- and let down when they joined everybody else on the rampage. The men are less pressured. David Fox as Tom, the really crazy one, is ironically required to stay in control, and he does so with an imperturbable presence surprisingly similar to that of the benign idiot savant he played in The Drawer Boy. Michael Healey, the author of that play, appears in this one as the mentally-defrocked Jack. He is not, on this showing, as interesting an actor as he is a writer, but he is the nearest thing around to a calming presence, and benefits from being -- literally and metaphorically -- the last person in the cast to yell.

Either he has the funniest lines, or he is the only one given the breathing space to time them.

Runs until June 6 at Factory Theatre. (416)-504-9971
