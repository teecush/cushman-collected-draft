- - -

The myth of Medea will never make a good feminist tract, unless you take a grimly apocalyptic view of feminism.

Medea, you will remember, was the sorceress who helped Jason, one of the more dubious Greek heroes, find and steal the Golden Fleece, cheerfully sacrificing members of her own family in the process. Euripides's tragedy finds the enterprising couple arrived in Crete, whose king's daughter Jason proposes to marry. Medea, not one to take rejection lightly, presents the bride with a poisoned robe, in whose embrace she dies an agonizing death. Her father, touching her body, shares her fate; this wasn't in the game plan but Medea gladly accepts it as a bonus. Then, so as to make Jason feel really bad, she kills the three children they have had together.

After that, in Euripides, she escapes in a magic chariot, presumably to live happily ever after, pausing only to cast a few well-chosen taunts in her faithless lover's direction.

Liz Lochhead's version, brought to World Stage by Scotland's Theatre Babel, changes this last development; Lochhead leaves the couple on Earth together, exchanging insults and recriminations.

Lochhead's text is described not as a translation from the Greek but as a new play "after Euripides." It includes a new scene between Medea and Jason's fiancee; Euripides left the latter young lady off- stage, but the encounter comes off as something he might well have written. It's like one of the catfights in The Trojan Women: The princess tells her rival, with patronizing sweetness, that Jason needs someone younger and that Medea should give in gracefully; Medea snarls back that older women know a lot more, which in her case is undoubtedly true.

All the Greek characters here speak in broad, and very salty, Scottish accents. This has an especially invigorating effect on the lower orders: on Medea's nurse (Carol Ann Crawford), loyal despite everything, and on the tutor (Lewis Howden), shiftier and more easily shocked, rising to a magnificent delivery of the play's lacerating messenger speech. Medea herself, the sinuous alien, has a European sound and stance that sometimes suggests Marlene Dietrich, seeing what the boys in the back room will have and serving it up with arsenic.

The chorus are also foreigners, but from closer to home. They are slave women, captured from neighbouring countries, and so speak with English, mainly Cockney, accents. They start by sympathizing with Medea, accommodating her views on Jason to their own on men in general; they are neutral, but intrigued, when she turns homicidal; and finally withdraw sympathy when the children are -- to use their mother's view of the situation -- sacrificed.

This is pretty much the trajectory they follow in the original, but it is far more immediate here than usual. The chorus, often an embarrassment, are among the triumphs of the script and of Graham McLaren's production. There are half-a-dozen of them, and they come alive not as a group of individuals but -- far more difficult -- as a group that is itself an individual.

Maureen Beattie's Medea is sulphurously, relentlessly uncompromising; a performance that proudly (and prudently) refrains from extorting pity, even her own. Jason, in Duncan Duff's performance, is not just a creep -- which seems inevitable -- but a wheedling chancer, going on about what a great sex-life he and Medea can continue to have when no one's looking. There is little here of the humanity that Seana McKenna and Scott Wentworth brought to the roles at Stratford two years ago. McLaren's direction is bleak, powerful, and brilliantly concentrated, with never a false or inflated moment.

- - -

The first line of Inertia [Phase Two] is, "Well, let's get on with it," which happens to be the last line of Jean-Paul Sartre's Huis Clos: the play (variously known in English as Vicious Circle, In Camera and No Exit) that corralled three sinners in a plush hotel room for an eternity of one another's company, and which itself ends with the famous discovery that "Hell is other people." (The play, whose celebrity is only equalled by its rarity, was scheduled but then cancelled for this year's inaugural season at the Stratford Studio. It would be good to see if there's more to it than its finale.) I thought I recognized one other Sartrean line --something about the lights never being turned off -- but otherwise this collage piece's main source is Chekov's Three Sisters, whose disconsolate heroines are here transformed into three nursing sisters who hate their work. The delightfully named OOmph! Group are the presenters, under the direction of Chad Dembski.

There is some fascination -- and even a few laughs -- in hearing the familiar lines juggled to fit a new environment, and there is some ingenuity in the way it has been done. But it becomes progressively clear that the Chekhov text is providing the show with its only interest and its only authority (the Sartre quotes only add a gloss of facile pessimism), and that the production is taking a free ride on a classic, illuminating neither its source nor anything else (as opposed to the Lochhead Medea, which is a genuine piece of work that does credit to Euripides, as well as sharing it with him).

Billed as "a multimedia collision," Inertia also features video screens and segments from the soap opera General Hospital, but these are just confusing. They also convey the feeling that the theatrical use of technology is a virtue in itself.

It does at least give us a nostalgic glimpse of the days when nurses, by virtue of wearing uniforms, were identifiably nurses. Sean MacMahon plays their one all-purpose patient who eventually and understandably discharges himself. The angels of mercy are Natasha Priest, the presently ubiquitous Michelle Polak, and Erika Hennebury, who rouses the house with a mockingly vamped rendition of a splendidly tacky pop song. Welcome to show business.
