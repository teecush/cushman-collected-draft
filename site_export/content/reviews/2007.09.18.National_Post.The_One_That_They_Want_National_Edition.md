I was fortunate when attending High School Musical to be briefed on the production's antecedents by a member of its target demographic, a well-spoken, well-dressed young lady of about six. "First," she explained, "it was a TV show. Then a movie. Then a book. Then shoes." She and her contemporaries were as polite and enthusiastic as they were knowledgeable. They hold out great hope for the future of theatre audiences. It's unfortunate that the object of their affections doesn't do the same for the future of theatre.

Its fans are obviously in no position to know this, but High School Musical is a show that sets new levels of unoriginality. And I'm not referring to the way in which this latest Disney product cannibalizes its own previous incarnations. I mean the way in which it recycles everything else. The characters may be highschoolers, but the show starts out like every old college musical there ever was, with students merrymaking and cheerleaders pom-pom-pomming on what we presume to be the steps of the library. Not that there's much evidence that anyone in this academy ever cracks a book. But then they never did in Good News or Best Foot Forward, either.

Sorry to get so archeological. What we more immediately have here is a mix of Grease and Hairspray, a product that could be the most alarming tonsorial development since Sweeney Todd. As the students at East High in Albuquerque, N.M., begin to sort themselves out, we learn that the population divides into Jocks and Brainiacs. Troy, a Jock (basketball) and Gabriella, a Brainiac (math) and new around here, met up over the summer and they reminisce about it, in what sounds like a weak, not to say virginal, imitation of that old Grease-y favourite, Summer Nights. The Hairspray analogies start oozing when we meet Sharpay, East Side's Queen Brat, who for years has ruled the school's third clique, the Thespians, with a whim of iron. The Thesps are mounting a home-grown musical entitled Juliet and Romeo, with a happy ending in which the lovers survive and move to Toronto. (Or wherever else High School Musical happens to be playing.) Sharpay takes it as read that she will play Juliet, and she gets all snarky and Machiavellian when Gabriella proposes to audition, since Gabriella can maybe sing and is certainly nicer. For some reason, the teenaged manipulators who oil the plots of these shows are never fun; they're just obnoxious. Somewhere along the line, High School also pays homage, to put it tactfully, to The Telephone Hour from Bye Bye Birdie, A Chorus Line passim, and even - - in the character of Troy's dad, who's also the school coach and who first jeers, then cheers, his son's inclination to try out for the musical himself -- Billy Elliott. And Ms. Darbus, the crabby drama teacher, who is kind of fun, is a close cousin of Miss Hannigan from Annie.

We have no reason, beyond the fact that she's the heroine, to think that Gabriella would make a better Juliet than Sharpay, though it's probable that Ryan, Sharpay's twin brother and performance partner, would be a less credible Romeo than Troy, who at least is interested in girls. But it's best not to go too deeply into plot and motivation. As if Sharpay's machinations weren't enough, the Jocks and Brainiacs, fearful of losing their respective star attractions, hatch impenetrable plots to stop them auditioning and then immediately repent and concoct even murkier schemes to repair the damage. Everything hinges on a last-minute alteration to the audition schedule, but how this comes about and why it couldn't immediately be put right is very unclear. The book is credited, if that's the word, to somebody with the magical name of David Simpatico. The songs for which the story periodically stops (they certainly don't move it forward) are the work of 13 composers, working in various combinations, and none of them sounds like the new Gershwin, or even the new Marc Shaiman. The score's nadir may be something called Stick It to the Status Quo, which is meant to be that ghastly thing called "empowering," but is actually just jerky.

The show may be stale but the production and performances are surprisingly fresh. Jeff Calhoun's direction is smooth, Lisa Stevens' choreography is energetic and Kenneth Foy's sets are excellent -- with their rows and rows of overhead lights, they are impressively spacious and make institutional architecture look like a good idea. John Jeffrey Martin and Arielle Jacobs are an appealing Troy and Gabriella, the only two people in nthe show who resemble real human beings. The rest of the cast don't get that chance, but they caricature with vigour. Chandra Lee Schwartz and Bobby List are as amusing a pair of terrible twins as anyone has a right to expect, given their material, and Ellen Harvey as Ms. Darbus does a very good Carol Burnett; even her passion for the drama is weirdly, indeed alarmingly, believable. Nice too is Olivia Ogama as the timid composer of the show-within-the-show which, for all we are allowed to see or hear of it, may be a masterpiece. (Though, on the basis of the one song we do hear, probably not.) Like everyone else, she gets to pair off at the end; in her case, it's with the school's resident newsreader, who spends the entire show making hysterically cheery announcements and mysteriously gets to the end of the evening without somebody strangling him. I notice that though the casting is proudly multicultural, the actual couplings are strictly intra- racial. And though Ryan, too, is allowed to find a partner, that encounter is kept very coy and upstage. School fiction, in any genre, is always aimed at an audience younger than the characters it depicts -- nobody finds their own contemporaries glamorous --but in this show, the gap yawns wider than ever before. - High School Musical runs until Sept. 23 at the Princess of Wales Theatre.
