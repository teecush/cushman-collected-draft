JACQUES BREL IS ALIVE AND WELL AND LIVING IN PARIS

National Arts Centre, Ottawa

Fate, as the late and chronically glum Jacques Brel would surely have agreed, is a cruel goddess; she may take her time about it, but in the end she will sashay up to you and slug you in the chops. For thirty years, I had avoided seeing Jacques Brel is Alive and Well and Living in Paris. Last week, at the National Arts Centre in Ottawa, I finally caught up with this perennially popular compilation of the Belgian bard's words and music; or perhaps I should say it caught up with me. Either way, I emerged with my spirits low, my ears enraged, and my prejudices bleakly confirmed.

In a former life, I developed a deep suspicion of actors who, with a whole world of song at their disposal, would choose a number from this show as an audition piece. Two of their selections stand out in my memory. One was Amsterdam, the landlocked sea shanty about a sailor who, at various unrelated times, "swallows the moon" and "zips up his fly." The other was Jacquie, an even more disjointed lyric in which a guy -- apparently some kind of troubadour -- breathlessly reels off three separate ways in which he might have sold out and been "cute, cute, cute, in a stupid-arse way." (A good description, I've always thought, of this show's title, even in the days when it was factually accurate.)

In this latter song, the author seems to be congratulating himself on his own integrity. That was also the impression I received from those long-ago auditionees (catch them being frivolous and ingratiating and singing Cole Porter) and, in spades, from this brand new production. (It re-opens today in Vancouver, and comes to Toronto next month.) It throbs with self-righteous emotion.

Jacques Brel is an American show, and it's possible that the French lyrics are not as wordy and incoherent as the translations (by Eric Blau and Mort Shuman) make them seem. Brel himself may not always have grabbed for the nearest lurid image as a way of getting to the end of the next line, and he may have written phrases in which the number of syllables bore some singable relation to the number of notes.

But the music, of course, is the same, and though this production comes with new and colourful orchestrations (by Steve Margoshes), they cannot disguise the poverty of the material.

No song boasts more than a single melodic idea, which it proceeds to pound into the dust. Sometimes the tempo steadily increases, and this can be effective; the celebrated if hardly profound Carousel number ("we're on a carousel . . . and then we go around") works this way, or might if the performance were not so intense as to be funny. Often, the repeated phrase is yanked up a key to signify passion. Always a song will end with somebody shouting.

This, I must admit, does move some people. There were cheers at the final curtain in Ottawa. But then, as an English critic once observed, an audience that's been yelled at for an evening is naturally going to want to yell back.

The show taps into a certain kind of Anglo-American snobbery; one that believes that the French chanson is, by definition, raw and uncommercial and deeply sexual and incredibly honest. But, without going outside this tradition, a writer like Charles Aznavour leaves Brel gasping in terms of musical invention and lyrical detail.

Even Brel's smartest satirical ideas (such as Middle Class Men, in which a pair of nonconformists become what they used to deride) run out of things to say or ways to say them, and fade into an overall surly smugness. And at that, Brel's misanthropy is preferable to his sentimental side. His ballads are as turgid as they come, culminating in If We Only Had Love, a strenuous and sanctimonious anthem that ranks with My Way and I Dreamed a Dream as among the worst songs ever written, even in Paris.

What is appalling is how influential all this has been: not so much the show itself as the sensibility it represents. You can trace it in the deafening pomposities of the European megamusical, in the quieter but equally formless breast-beating of all the North American singer-songwriters who don't happen to be Randy Newman, even in what remains of the New York cabaret tradition, which has taken to screaming the virtues of Love as if it were a brand of soap flakes. Craft has gone, wit has gone, irony is everywhere proclaimed but nowhere present, and bellowing is taken to be a guarantee of feeling.

Elly Stone, a veteran of the show's first cast, directs this revival in appropriately sepulchral fashion, on a set whose principal feature is a monolith with a doorway cut in it. There are some odd jokes: One song is mysteriously performed in Salvation Army costume, its heroine mocked for using the same outspoken language (e.g., "her new fuck-you stand") on which the show prides itself for the rest of the time. A number about bullfights has the ensemble laboriously acting out everything the soloist is describing.

The cast are four of the Canadian musical's top-rated voices, but voices is pretty much what they remain. The women, Louise Pitre and Susan Henley, are avenging angels, distinguishable only by one being fair-haired and the other dark. Michael Burgess flourishes a beautiful lyric tenor that doesn't always connect with the acrid material; he is stolid in a way common among actors who have done extensive time in Les Miserables. Jeff Hyslop is more malleable, with an impish gargoyle touch and nimble feet.

The men share a song, Madeleine, about waiting at a movie for a non-arriving date, that is the jauntiest thing in the show. It has humour, a tune, and a developing situation. It is, in fact, the only moment of joy in the evening.
