THEATRE CANDIDA

Saturday, July 10, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. -- BY BERNARD SHAW Directed by Christopher Newton Starring Seana McKenna, Jim Mezon and Duncan Ollerenshaw At the Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake Rating: The Candida conundrum In this naturalistic play, in which nothing really happens, there is little space for Shaw the intellectual comedian or Shaw the crazy clown ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. OF all Shaw's plays, Candida is the least like a public meeting. That sounds like a compliment but isnt. Generally the fun in Shaw comes from seeing ideas hurled through the air from one player to another, and returned at unexpected angles. Shaw the intellectual comedian is entertaining in himself; his doppelganger, the crazy clown, is even better value. The partners are inseparable, and they bring out the best in one another.

There is little space for either of them in Candida, a perfectly conventional (by Shavian standards, anyway), naturalistic piece in which nothing happens. I know that last clause is the standard complaint about Shaw, but it isnt generally true. His debating plays may be short on what is officially known as plot, but they are crowded with incident, much of it riotous. Candida has a plot: a married woman has to choose between her husband and a feverish young admirer. That is all. It is a shapely enough action, one that would keep many craftsman playwrights thoroughly occupied for an evening; and advocates of the well-made play, Edwardian style, used to seize on it with relief. Its having only one set and half-a-dozen characters didn't hurt its commercial chances either. To us it has to seem emaciated.

The one set is a drawing-room in unglamorous North East London, 1894. It belongs to the Rev. James Mavor Morell, Socialist parson, and it doubles as his office. Morell's doctrines, largely Shaws own as far as explicit politics goes, are sincerely espoused but they matter less for their own sake than for what he gets out of them: mountainous self- satisfaction at the sound of his own voice and the sensation of his own energy. Again, he seems much like his creator, but Shaw has put another part of himself into Eugene Marchbanks, an agonizingly shy and sincere young poet (post-pre-Raphaelite, it seems) whose revulsion at the sordid realities of life - i.e. poverty - is as heartfelt as Morells. What affronts him, though, is not the injustice but the ugliness. The focus for his esthetic transports is Mrs. Morell: Candida.

Not having seen the play in decades, I was struck by how little the title character is given to say, and how much of what she does say is bad- tempered. She certainly has her reasons; for years, she has made her husband's career possible by running his house and family, and she gets no thanks: only generalized worship. Now she has to put up with a teenage poet's unworldly rhapsodies as well.

Most actresses - mindful that they are the leading lady, dammit, and two men are supposed to be head-over-heels with them - cover the facts of the case with a generalized veil of charm. It may be to Seana McKennas credit that she refuses to do this, but the play suffers for her integrity. I make it a rule never to question the likelihood of anyone, in fact or in fiction, falling in love with anyone else, but here goes: Marchbanks is looking for a muse, and I cannot see what he sees in this one.

It is certainly a tingling moment when this Candida turns on the two men bidding for her affections and says, tightly, I am up for auction, it seems. The words leap off the stage. But then they leap back again, because they connect to nothing else in the text. Candida turns out to be an up-market version of Nancy ("as long as he needs me") in Oliver!; and a program-note denying that this is a feminist play (whatever that might be) seems perfectly justified.

A traditional problem with Candida is her father, a successful sweat- shop merchant, who is one of Shaws comic Cockneys: all vulgar bounce and fluent self-interest. Father and daughter usually seem miles apart. This production brings them closer together, Candida being aggressively self- made and Sandy Webster as her dad a touch wistful and crushable. Even their vowels have some points of contact. It ought to work but it drains the juice of both characters. Maybe Candida should have been staged in the main Festival house; it needs to be brought out of itself, not turned in on itself. I cannot decide whether Jim Mezon is giving a bad performance of Shaw's Morell or a good one of somebody else but the self-confidence he offers seems so ascetically hollow from the start that there is no point or poignancy in seeing it crumble.

Christopher Newton's production does, however, boast two excellent performances. It is easy to make us feel for Marchbanks; less easy, as Duncan Ollerenshaw does, to make us feel with him, even while laughing at him. Elizabeth Brown performs a similar balancing-act with Prossy, Morell's adoring typist: an economic and emotional victim but not defined by that. The scene in which they discuss the impossibility of expressing love - he intolerably extravagant in his sensibility but inarguably right, she goaded out of her prim professional shell into a desperate avowal - breaks the play's complacent surface as nothing else does.
