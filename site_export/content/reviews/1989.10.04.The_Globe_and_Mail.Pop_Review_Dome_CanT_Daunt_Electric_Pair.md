POP REVIEW Dome can't daunt electric pair

Wednesday, October 04, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail They were two tiny, strutting dots on a small platform, somewhere at the bottom of a huge, tiered canyon. You had to watch them on the big video that unfolded above their heads, and that is not what you go to a live concert for. The sound system was muggy, though mysteriously sharper for him than for her. All the same, Frank Sinatra and Liza Minnelli , performing at Torontos SkyDome Monday night, were magnificent.

In his solo set, Sinatra padded over the stage as if he owned it. Apologies for the staleness of that image, but he is a proprietorial performer. He owns the members of the orchestra; he more or less conducts them with his singing and surveys them during the instrumental breaks with confident paternal pride. (Appropriately: Frank Jr. is now his musical director.) He adopts the audience, with an ease that in any other singer would be insufferable, and of course he takes possession of the songs.

Some very good singers can persuade you that they have written every word they give voice to. Sinatras authority is different. The song is a pre-existent entity, and he displays and explores it. He can find beauty in the strangest places; someone should make a study of the way he sings the word "and, coming down very hard on the final consonant. The chat in his act is kept to a blessed minimum - he seems to be saving himself for the important business of singing - but he did at one point refer to our great library of American songs" and he seems, after some years of floundering with repertoire, to have found himself as its curator.

He meticulously credits composers. Before embarking on For Once in My Life, he announced it as the work of Orlando Murden and Ronald Miller "whoever the hell they are. (They are the guys who wrote For Once in My Life. They have no other identity.) But mostly the names are Rodgers, with both Hart and Hammerstein, Arlen and Mercer, Cole Porter. There are nostalgic lapses from this standard, but he lightly mocks Strangers in the Night (doesn't even say who wrote it) and tells us that when he first heard My Way he didn't like it. I always knew he had taste. The song is, and always has been, atrocious, but the way he sings it now, shorn of bombast, it is hard to resist.

The voice, variable in recent years, was excellent Monday, compensating for the missing high notes with gravelly low ones. He no longer sings the songs of old age in which he specialized when he was merely approaching it, and he takes extraordinary chances. He managed, for example, the entire Soliloquy from Carousel, with every mood in place and strength in reserve for the climax. And there were other songs that he has recorded superbly but which I never expected to hear him do live: Come Rain or Come Shine, and Cy Colemans wonderfully taut and carefree The Best Is Yet To Come. These performances find him at his most commanding and exuberant, riding and cresting exhilarating orchestrations by Don Costa and Quincy Jones. He credits arrangers as well as writers, but he establishes a different stage relationship with them. He seems to be calling the orchestral figures into existence as he goes. It is a truism that Sinatra has perfected the art of singing directly to and for the listener. But he does it less by direct address than by telepathy. He imagines the song inside his head, and it reaches you. How many thousands of times has he sung One for My Baby? It still comes alive.

If Sinatra has drawn back to jazz, Minnelli, the compulsive extrovert, has found herself on Broadway. She can act, and is at her best when doing so with uncomplicated panache (Some People, from Gypsy) or daffy wit (Sara Lee - yes, about cakes). She also offered a disco version of Losing My Mind that suited the songs nightmare quality surprisingly well, especially as she kept the lyrics crisp against the beat. She still can be tempted into the maudlin or the melodramatic - two stark Charles Aznavour laments sounded melodically identical, but then French songs do - but these tendencies are held far more likably in check than they used to be.

When, by way of overture, the band struck up a few bars of New York, New York, I fell to wondering whether Sinatra or Minnelli would sing it. In the end, they both did, as the climax of a strange closing set: one that was probably rather hastily stitched together after the hospitalization of co-star Sammy Davis Jr. A Guys and Dolls medley threatened to become a recital of the entire score in a rather peculiar order, and when they came to alternating their trademark songs, it was apparent that he had a lot more of them to draw on than she.

Sinatra is not one of nature's duetists, nor is he of an age to compete with Minnelli's compulsive trouping. As she cavorted, he uncomfortably stood, and for the first time in the evening he blew lyrics.

I always knew these were good for something," said Minnelli, winning all hearts by removing her eyelashes. "They're for catching bugs." Ground level at the Dome was an insects' paradise; this, plus the acoustics and the distance and the fact that the orchestra sounded like it was playing on an adjacent planet, drove some people to ask for refunds. This, to understate matters, was a shame. I want these performers to come back, some place where I can see them better. Or just where I can see them.
