THEATRE REVIEW

Julie Sits Waiting

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

Julie is the middle-aged wife of a cop named Rick. She is having an affair with an Anglican priest named Mick. We don't meet Rick; we do very much meet Mick. The rhyming names are presumably meant to tell us something, this being a new avant-garde opera rather than one of those vulgar musical comedies with their snappy lyrics and rhymes used for comic effect. Being funny is about the last thing on this show's mind.

Tom Walmsley wrote the libretto, and Louis Dufort the music. One of the traditional differences between operas and musicals has been that in musicals the tunes are usually written first and the lyrics fitted to them, while the opera composer generally fashions his notes to what the librettist has given him. Nevertheless, and paradoxically, it's taken for granted that in opera the music is the most important element, to the extent that operas are often performed in languages their audiences don't understand and at pitches that make most of the words incomprehensible anyway.

Surtitles, employed these days even when the text is in the audience's native language, may ease this problem, but they don't eliminate it. They may even aggravate it, since they entail a double focus, detaching us from the total musical dramatic experience opera is advertised as providing.

In recent decades the two genres have been drawing closer together. Many musicals have been through-sung; Julie Sits Waiting, officially an opera, contains spoken passages, which certainly aren't intended to relax the tension. Dufort's score is officially described as electro-acoustic, with the electric part coming from the instrumentation and the acoustic, sometimes "enhanced," from the singers. Both are, in the main, nervejanglingly effective, which is what Walmsley's text demands of them. Whether it deserves all the effort is another matter.

Like the relationship it depicts, it starts out all right. Julie and Mick come together (I'm afraid, yes, I do mean that) in a vacant house that Julie's husband is renovating. They have never clapped eyes on one another before, having arranged their assignation electronically (the libretto's best line may be, "Thank God for the Internet"), but are enchanted and excited when they do meet.

After a decent amount of vocal foreplay, they retire offstage and get on with it. While they are gone, the space is filled with fizzy light-show projections, designed (by Jeremy Mimnagh) to suggest what a good time the couple are having, and succeeding better at it than so cliched a device probably has any right to. We gather, when the characters return, that the sex has been wild and violent and that they've both enjoyed it. The sensible thing would seem to be to let them get on with it.

Which, it appears, they do for a while, but their author throws rocks in their way. Both develop qualms of conscience. Julie's, involving her loyalties less to her husband than to their teenaged daughter, seem the more prosaic and believable though not, given what we were initially shown, insurmountable. Mick's are higher-strung and farther fetched. He turns out to be one in that long line of theatrical clergy who have lost their faith but kept their guilt. This leads him certainly to self-hatred, probably to hatred of others and eventually to acts of unmotivated mayhem.

It might all seem less contrived if Mick had been given words that sing, musically or dramatically, but his remain ploddingly explanatory: prosaic without sounding natural. They revived my unworthy suspicions about whether audiences would be able to take opera quite as seriously if they could hear the words. Which in this case you can: all of them. To be fair, the words in most pop operas, sometimes known as rock musicals, are worse. Although of course those shows are less pretentious. (Scratch that; they aren't.)

The piece is presented by a company who combine the irresistible name of Good Hair Day Productions with a praiseworthy commitment to championing new forms of music theatre. It's directed, by Heidi Strauss and Alex Fallis, on a sustained note of visual and aural intensity; and it gets, from Fides Krucker and Richard Armstrong, the virtuoso performances it demands, rigorously unglamorous but searingly passionate. Armstrong is, if anything, too sweatily believable; Krucker is white-hot (as opposed to her partner's red), making her closing moments crazily credible, however dubious the steps it's taken to get her there.

I'm unsure why she gets the title billing, when the two characters are equally important. Nor am I clear as to what she's waiting for, unless it's to be punished for experiencing pleasure. For all its assaults on the senses, this is a very puritanical show.
