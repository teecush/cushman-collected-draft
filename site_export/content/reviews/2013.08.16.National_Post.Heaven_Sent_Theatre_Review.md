Angels in America

Performing Arts, Toronto

I first saw Angels in America, Tony Kushner's two-part sixhour gay fantasia on national themes at the National Theatre in London where it was staged, to rave reviews, ahead of its equally acclaimed New york debut in 1993. I had to write myself down a skeptic; I thought that a good little play about roy Cohn had been bloated to inordinate proportions by a lot of fancy stuff about angels. Too many angels, not enough America. I was wrong, as is proved by Soulpeppers new production, which lights up nearly every moment of the marathon while returning the company itself to the level of acting magnificence that distinguished its earliest days. Still Cohn, the one historical figure among the plays half-dozen principals, remains its most immediately compelling character, and he provides a good entry-point to the work as a whole.

Early in Part One Cohn, a hugely successful New york lawyer and former flunky and confidant of Joe McCarthy, is diagnosed with AIDS. He angrily denies that this means he's homosexual, his reason being that homosexuals are losers, while he himself is clout personified. No, he says, he isn't homosexual; he just likes to have sex with men. Nobody ever said that Kushner doesnt write great lines.

He also writes great scenes, and Cohns death and its aftermath are among them. Cohn enters hospital, under cover of having liver cancer, using his contacts, of whom he is inordinately proud, to procure a huge private stash of AZT. It doesn't save his life and when he dies - about halfway through Part Two - Belize, his gay male nurse, appropriates the drugs to distribute among his own stricken friends. Partly to cover this, and partly as a mark of grudging respect towards a man whose guts he reciprocally hated but with whom he had established a kind of rapport, Belize enlists Louis Ironson, the only other Jew he knows, to act as his drug-mule and to say kaddish over Cohn's body. Louis, definitely secular, is nervous about this, and initially gets the prayer confused with the blessing over wine, but he perseveres, prompted by the ghost of Ethel rosenberg, whom Cohn claims to have personally sent to the chair in the 50s and who has been balefully haunting his bedside, occasionally bemused by things like buttons on phones. The beneficiaries of Cohns unconscious bequest will include Prior Walter, Louis' partner whom he abandoned when Prior caught the plague. Prior's life will be not just saved, but transformed.

This scene buoyantly embodies the plays wild and apparently spontaneous blend of history and invention, realism and fantasy, laughter and pain. It also involves or invokes most of the main characters. Theres one notable absentee, Joe Pitt, legal clerk and protege of Cohn's, a self-denying gay man who eventually surrenders to a relationship with Louis, and who also happens to be a Mormon. All the main men in this play are gay; none of the women are. The most prominent females are Harper, Joe's unhappy wife, strung out on Valium and agoraphobia; and Hannah, Joes mother, who leaves Salt Lake City for New york after her son has come out to her over the phone.

Journeys, past or present, actual or imagined, are a key part of Kushners play; so is ancestry. The kickoff speech is delivered by a rabbi, at Louis' grandmother's funeral, recalling the trip from Lithuania to "the melting-pot where nothing melts." Prior, from an old WASP family, receives delirious sickbed visitations from two prior Priors. He lives further up to his name by wrapping himself, when he ventures back into the world, in what looks like a monastic cowl. Among the places he visits is a Mormon museum where both Pitt women have taken up residence and where the chief exhibit is a diorama of the Saints trek to utah, headed by a figure who looks very much like Joe and whom Harper addresses as if he were Joe. Whats more, he responds.

The staging of this tableau, poised between pathos and farce, is one of the triumphs of Albert Schultz's production. The method of the scene harks back to an earlier episode in which Prior, delirious with illness, finds his fantasy intersecting with that of Harper, whom he has never met and who imagines herself in Antarctica, saving the ozone layer. (It's 1985.) What, Prior asks Harper, "are you doing in my hallucination?" Thereby both getting a laugh and setting up a very handy method of making thematic connections while shortcutting exposition. And then there are those angels.

Or rather that angel. She drops down on wires through Priors ceiling, scattering plaster in her wake, to tell him that millennium approaches and that hes a prophet and has a mission. He demurs; but in Part Two, when he's feeling much better, he climbs the ladder she's brought with her and finds himself in what seems to be the American wing of heaven, a place apparently deserted by god in 1906 on what seems, by world cataclysmic standards, the rather flimsy pretext of the San Francisco earthquake. Prior declares that the celestial delegates should respond to the divine desertion by taking responsibility for their own affairs and returns to earth to put his principles into practice. Just how remains vague.

So I still have trouble with the plays mystical aspects; and indeed the heavenly conclave is one of the less satisfactory sequences of a generally swinging production. Schultzs direction thoroughly outclasses what I remember as the plodding British staging, not least in getting more exciting rather than more exhausting as it goes on. It unfolds fluently on a set (by Lorenzo Savoini) divided into three playing areas to accommodate the multifarious plotlines; occasionally an actor will invade somebody else's space without even a fantasy warrant, but they're mostly kept rigorously distinct.

Most impressive, though, is the productions undeviating sensitivity to the text, and the actors responsiveness to it and to one another. There's a great performance everywhere you look, each growing greater by feeding off its fellows, with Bonnie Beecher's refreshingly bright lighting throwing all of them into the sharpest relief. Hardly a laugh, a pang, an image or an argument goes missing.

Very Steven Spielberg says Prior when the angel first descends, striking, in damien Atkins superb performance, the note of the true movie maven, awestruck but detached. Even when Priors in extreme physical or emotional pain, Atkins never plays for self-pity. He focuses instead on Prior's bemused intellectual growth and above all on his reflexive wit; he sees and shares the quirky side of everything. Kushner's jokes are the highest proof of his talent, and Atkins as Prior has some of the best of them: in a secondary role - everyone here gets to play more than one - he has the very best.

Diego Matamoros Cohn begins as a virtuoso display of brute intelligence, deepens into a study of agonized, unforgiving (and mostly unforgiven) loneliness. Especially fabulous are his scenes with Troy Adams off-duty drag-queen Belize, a demon of deflation with people he likes, a conscientiously ministering devil with this man he loathes.

The consummate transformation artist is Nancy Palk, who starts out sublime and unrecognizable as the rabbi, and proceeds impeccably through four other roles, including a sedately implacable Mrs. rosenberg and, as her main assignment, Hannah the Mormon mama, who comes to town to save her son and finds less expected, more charitable, outlets for her innate practicality.

Which brings us to the ending. The russian word for restructuring is perestroika, which is the subtitle of Part Two. The last scene finds Louis and Belize in an obviously endless argument about the Middle East, Prior tolerantly shrugging, and Hannah playing den-mother; Joe is significantly absent. There's a shoutout to what was going on in russia under gorbachev, as a hopeful sign of the times in general. It might all seem rather facile; in fact it does. But time has been kind to the play by being cruel. Subsequent events, including those in russia and especially in the context of a "gay fantasia," have lent this epilogue an enriching layer of irony. Besides that, there's the impact of the event itself. Size and ambition have a habit of bringing out the best in actors. They certainly do here. They have a matching effect on audiences. They bond both sides of the house in a spirit of mutual generosity, of all-of-us-in-it-togetherness. At the last moment, Atkins steps modestly forward to, in effect, bless us.

On the first night - more exactly, the second first night - we responded in kind with a thunderous, heartfelt standing ovation. I cannot imagine subsequent audiences feeling any different.
