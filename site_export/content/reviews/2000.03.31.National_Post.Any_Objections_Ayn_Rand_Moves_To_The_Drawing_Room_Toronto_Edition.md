THE EMOTIONALISTS

The Music Gallery, Toronto

Sky Gilbert's excellent new play about Ayn Rand bears a surprising outward resemblance to the old urbane kind of drawing- room comedy.

As in a piece of vintage Noel Coward or Philip Barry, we watch a magnetic central figure in comfortable surroundings, reclining on a sofa and accepting homage. Being female, she has a solicitous husband on hand to greet callers and be generally understanding. Being a successful writer, she is obliged to grant audiences to worshipful fans; in this case, having hit the jackpot as a novelist and as an ideologue, she gets a double dose of adulation. And since she was born in Russia but now lives in New York, she reaps all the rewards of Americas fascination with European accents -- which, at least in women, are taken to be infinitely wise and infinitely sexy.

Gilbert himself tells us in a program note that he encountered Rands works in early adolescence and worshipped them. Today, he still loves the novels but hates the ideas. I can't comment on the former, since I haven't read them, and all I really know of the latter is what I have gleaned from newspaper articles, which made them seem loathsome.

Gilberts play has left my distaste intact, and was probably intended to, but he makes the woman herself enjoyable theatrical company: engagingly monstrous and, by the end, somewhat pitiable. Since pity seems to be the last thing she would have wanted (its certainly the last thing she was ever inclined to offer other people), there's a special kick in extending it to her.

We meet her in 1955, riding high on the success of her first novel, The Fountainhead. We gather she divides the human race into two categories. There are the Objectivists, those with the courage and intelligence to see life as it is, i.e. to agree with her about everything. And, in a vast and despised majority, there are the Emotionalists, weak-willed and weak-brained, who use filthy words like feeling and appear to believe they actually mean something.

Ayn refers to Frank, her husband of 26 years, as "my rock" and my highest value, the latter being not exactly a synonym for love but a pretext for it. Recently, though, she has discovered another, younger, highest value: a psychotherapist in training (and also apparently in practice) named Nathaniel Branden.

Ayn and Nat announce to their respective spouses that they are in love and would like to have time together, twice a week. Whether these meetings will involve sexual intimacy is a matter on which Ayn remains maddeningly ambiguous, perhaps because she doesn't really know herself. She preaches complete honesty but will lie, to herself and others, when it suits her.

She is, in fact, as much at the mercy of her emotions as any Emotionalist. This much is predictable at the start: indeed, its signalled in the plays title. Gilbert's achievement is to mine this essentially static situation for high ironic comedy, and to sustain the mood throughout.

We have already watched Ayn indulge another kind of emotion, the joy of bullying. She has quizzed Branden's wife, Barbara, on the merits of three pieces of music, and has mercilessly berated her for placing Beethoven and Shostakovich above Saint-Saens. Barbara suggests it may be a matter of personal taste, but taste, to Ayn, is a feeble-minded obscenity.

Barbara seems understandably ready to fall apart, and its suggested she let her husband treat her. (This would, of course, be a major breach of professional ethics, but the subject isnt mentioned and one appreciates that from an Objectivist viewpoint it's a trifle, beneath consideration.) Confronted with her husband's putative infidelity, Barbara reverts to conventional type, and seems the stronger for it.

The imperturbable Frank, meanwhile, finally gets perturbed -- not (he says) by jealousy, but by the possibility his wife is about to be exploited by the ambitious Branden.

From what we see of Branden this seems thoroughly likely. Hes an accomplished sexual and intellectual blackmailer who knows exactly how to push Ayns buttons.

There is a parallel plot. Marcel, a young devotee of Ayn's, comes to grovel and to seek advice. He says he was homosexually abused as a boy, and the experience has robbed him of the ability to connect with women. Ayn plainly regards him as beneath contempt, but she preaches courage to him, and off he goes determined to emulate the egomaniacal hero of The Fountainhead.

He invites his assailant out for the evening and they picnic on top of a hill. The older man brings cucumber sandwiches, which gets the conversation around to Oscar Wilde. Marcel, barely able to utter the word "homosexual," lets out his rage, in full confidence he is doing the right Randian thing.

This is the closest the play gets to a critique of Rand's social and political influence, and there is not a word about her virulent economic views (which, through her disciple Alan Greenspan, may have affected us all).

But what we have is plenty. Gilbert has written dialogue that is witty, easeful and revealing; he has shaped it to tell a couple of compelling stories; and he has directed his own play with delicacy and lucidity.

Ellen-Ray Hennessys Ayn is a fiercely disciplined portrait of an outrageous woman, who commits some of the worst outrages on herself. Its especially fascinating to see Hennessy, in the play's early stages, pause and draw herself up before inflicting another humiliation.

She is finely supported by Philip Shepherd, whose Frank is a study in pained, watchful quiescence; by Veronica Hurnik, a touching portrait of decent ordinariness trying conscientiously but vainly to be something else; and by Alex Poch-Goldin, whose Branden, the one character with whom the author cannot bring himself to sympathize, is at least a glistening creep. Gil Garratt is brilliant as Marcel, clinging to furniture in an effort to express inexpressible shame; Patrick Conner as his antagonist is good, and gets better the closer he comes to the edge.

The play is presented by The Cabaret Company, a new group Gilbert has founded for the production of his own plays. That is in fact, he says engagingly, its only purpose. He adds that "each different project will bring together artists that I have worked with before, artists that I have never worked with before, and artists that I have longed to work with."

That seems pretty thorough. I think it means he will cast each play as best he can -- like everybody else.
