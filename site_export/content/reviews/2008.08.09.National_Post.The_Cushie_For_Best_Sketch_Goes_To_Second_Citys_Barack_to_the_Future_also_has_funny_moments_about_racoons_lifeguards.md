Getting nominated for a Dora this year may have gone to Second City's heads. I don't mean that in a bad way, or not entirely. The best number in their new revue -- it comes at the end of the first half and begins the redemption of what has hitherto been a pretty dismal evening -- is a sketch about awards shows that nails absolutely everything. The prizes in this number are presented for documentary film, and they're called the Dockies.

It's all here. The hyperactive presenters trying to conceal their boredom and disgust with the entire proceedings. The caucus-race categories ("Best Photography. Best Final Credits. Best Photography of Final Credits.") The recidivist winners, clambering manically on stage to nab every trophy for their movie about lupus; there's a giggling couple who insist on taking the longest route to the platform every time, and then can never find their way off of it. The meaningless acceptance speeches, whether triumphalist or gee-shucks. The recidivist loser, crying foul; in this case she's a vindictive hausfrau (well, she looks like a hausfrau) whose nominated picture was Indo- Malaise: Chronic Fatigue Syndrome in the South Pacific. (Other nominated titles include C-C-Canada: A Stuttering Immigrant's Dream and One Hump or Two: Diversity in Camels). The presenters' assistant seems uncertain whether he's an usher or a bouncer, or if he's necessary at all. It's heaven.

The title of the show, which is less inspired, is Barack to the Future, though if this revue runs until November, some re-branding may be called for. To be fair, this is one Second City topical pun that actually justifies itself onstage, in the form of a number. It's the opener, in fact, showing President Obama pre-empting the Dockie winners by making his own acceptance speech, paying due tribute to his own "charismatic but humble Obama eyes." Once past this point however, and even with the help of an assassination attempt ("that's not funny, Hillary"), the sketch has no place to go. And at that it's ahead of most of the other sketches in the first half, which don't even have a place to start.

I will except a fairly funny item about a couple of pool guards unable to save a single client from drowning, except for one bratty little girl who rescues them. Otherwise, until the Dockies come along, it looks as if the Second Citizens, who didn't actually win that Dora, may be resting on their almost-laurels. Part 2, however, starts on another high, a number -- a mini-ballet really -- about two raccoons attacking one of the city's giant new garbage cans. (Whatever these things may or may not be doing for recycling, they're certainly proving a boon to urban comedy; first, An Inconvenient Musical, now this.) They come equipped with flashlight and picklock, as many of us have long suspected, and their grace and precision in overturning and exploring the receptacle is immaculate. Karen Parker and Leslie Seiler, in black fur and black masks, execute what a close acquaintance of mine has described as a paws de deux. It could be Toronto's answer to Cats.

Seiler, described in another number as "the femme fatale of funny," lives up to it throughout the evening. She is especially stunning as a Bay Street high roller with a busy schedule, doing speed-dating speedier than might be thought humanly, or humanely, possible. Parker, the only other lady present, is good to have around, especially when awful old ladies or awful little girls are required, but has fewer opportunities; the best thing that can be said about her turn as a Coronation Street woman avidly recalling her penurious youth is that she has an impressive command of the accent. (This ee-by-gum, when-I-were-a-lad satire has been thoroughly mined by everyone from Alan Bennett to Monty Python. Leave it to the natives.)

The excellent Darryl Hinds comes into his own (and has the best punchline of the evening, and indeed of many revues past) demonstrating what he claims to be a traditional Jamaican method of picking up girls. (Jamaicans in the audience showed hearty approval.) But he too is starved of material; he deserves better than to be the go-to guy for impersonating celebrities of colour, from Obama to -- an especially low point -- Omar Khadr. Marty Adams repeats his fat-man's specialty of miming, with relishing grossness, very small people; last time around it was a baby, this time it's an adult: a foot-and-a-half high, on a job interview. (Seiler is his interviewer, and it's another score for her.) In general, Adams makes a winning loser. There are two new faces, belonging to Kerry Griffin and Reid Janisse. Musical numbers are down to one, but it's good: a hymn to Canada ("you can-can do it," they sing, doing can-can kicks) that sounds genuinely cutting and genuinely patriotic. Bruce Pirrie directs, and on this occasion he's a better stager than editor. There's good wood here, but there's also too much that's dead.
