THE DRAWER BOY

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

Michael Healey is an actor whose past writing credits include a collaboration and his own one-man show. The Drawer Boy is his first, full-scale play for other performers, and it's a stunning debut.

The set for the play represents an Ontario farmhouse -- a bit of the inside, a bit of the out -- and the action starts with Miles, the youngest character, wandering in from the auditorium and inspecting it as if it were the set for a play. This is because Miles is an actor and he looks at everything that way.

The farm is owned and worked by two middle-aged men called Morgan and Angus. Angus is an apparent simpleton whose activities seem confined to baking bread, which he invariably overcooks, and doing the accounts, at which he is brilliant. He does the figures in his head, though he is unable to retain any other information for more than a second. Morgan explains his friends memory loss as the result of an injury sustained 30 years earlier during the London blitz, when the two were soldiers.

Miles comes to the farm for research purposes: He is working on a collective project bearing a strong resemblance to The Farm Show, which was an iconic Canadian production of the 1970s. One of the actors in that show was indeed called Miles -- Miles Potter -- and he is now the director of The Drawer Boy. So this is partly hall-of- mirrors entertainment.

Morgan permits Miles to stick around on condition that he help out with the chores. He then proceeds to razz the city-boy mercilessly. Miles asks what it feels like to rear animals and then slaughter them, to plant crops in order to pull them up again; Miles, says Morgan, looking him steadily in the eye, "its an emotional roller coaster." He also has the kid get up at 3 a.m. to rotate the crops.

Back at rehearsal, Miles' new agricultural expertise gets rebuffed; even his efforts to impersonate, what he calls, "the essence of cow" are spurned by his colleagues who, he claims, want the same old bourgeois cow.

The play may be meant, in part, as an affectionate homage to The Farm Show, but it does suggest that, these days, we're better off hearing about the old classic than watching it.

The only thing Miles can get into the show is something he overhears; the story, repeated by Morgan to Angus every night, of Angus accident, and of his youthful talent as an artist (hence the plays mysterious title), and of the two Englishwomen, magically described as "the tall girl" and the taller girl, whom they romanced and brought back to Canada, and of how that idyll ended.

Miles invites his two new friends to a run-through (his innocent insensitivity does take some swallowing) and is amazed at the effect it has on them: Angus starts remembering things and Morgan is not pleased.

It's surely no coincidence that in Act Two, Miles tells Angus the story of Hamlet, another reckless exponent of drama as therapy. I can hardly bear to type the words "reality and illusion," but, for once, that grindingly obvious old theme gets redeemed, lightly, intelligently, humanely, and with an unfolding awareness of all the ambiguities.

On a narrative level, the play resembles Pinter's The Caretaker, that fountainhead of contemporary drama; its another play about two symbiotic residents and an intruder, the difference being that Healeys characters all have the best of intentions. He has a wonderful ear and a great mastery of tone. You laugh and you grieve; it's an emotional roller coaster.

He also manages to combine in-house satire with an original story, or rather stories. Narrative trap-doors keep opening, and the pressure only slackens at the final set of revelations; they are perfectly believable but they seem, as is the way of final revelations, somewhat pat. This, significantly, is the only point at which the performances fall into predictable rhythms. Otherwise, the acting is lovely, with Jerry Franken's Morgan as witty, abrasive, and concerned, and Tom Barnett's Miles as gauche, earnest, and, beneath it all, strong. Angus, with his trust and his whimsy, is a role in which any actor might break your heart; David Fox, striking the keynote of the whole show, gains your respect as well.

Stephan Droege's design, principally the kitchen, is, as it should be, a self-effacing archetype.

I cannot recall a new Canadian play I have liked as much.
