CONSECRATED GROUND

Factory Theatre

- - -

Halfway through its action, Consecrated Ground reveals the reason for its title. George Boyds play is set in 1965 in Africville, a shantytown in Nova Scotia, maintained by the Halifax city authorities as a black ghetto. The houses have been left to rot, a garbage dump and an abattoir have been installed virtually on the premises, and the place is infested with rats. They attack a baby boy, whose mother is busy raising some of the evenings few laughs by taking a broom to other, presumably mechanical, rats who are scuttling over the floor of the next room. The child dies (if you detect a slight uncertainty of tone in this juxtaposition, you will not be mistaken). The mother, Clarice, whose family has lived in Africville for generations, demands that her son be buried, not in the nearby cemetery, as seems customary, but on the "consecrated ground" of the village itself.

First Question: Where have Clarice's ancestors, of whom she often speaks, been buried? She gives no indication that they are not in the cemetery along with everyone else. She has suffered a terrible tragedy, but does that give her the right to change the local burial regulations?

Second Question: Since Clarice was not alone when the rats appeared in her kitchen, why didn't she dispatch the friend who was with her to look after the baby? And why does she feel no apparent guilt about not having done so? Most mothers would be prostrated by the death of a child, however it happened. Clarice shows no emotion but anger, some of it directed at the white folks (who certainly bear a heavy load of responsibility for having let Africville degenerate into a slum) and some at her husband, Willum. Willum, who unlike his wife has no sentimental ties to the place, wants to accept a government offer to move into the city where, he points out, he can have a real job and they can have a real home. The author presumably wants us to feel that both sides have a case, but he tilts the play heavily in favour of Clarice, who is accorded all of the dramatic privileges of a tragic matriarch and none of the responsibilities. This may be self-defeating. I found myself inclined to favour Willum, since he at least listens to his wifes arguments while she shows not a shred of respect for his. (Hes also far better acted, but we'll get to that later.)

Neither of the above questions is posed in the play, let alone confronted. It might be a richer, more textured piece if they were. The play was first staged, fittingly, in Halifax in 1999 and was nominated for a Governor-General's Award the following year. Unity (1918) has actually won this award; like the Pulitzer down south, it seems to have more to do with Worthiness of Subject than with anything else. The buy-out/expropriation/ethnic cleansing of Africville actually happened, and the facts, and the authors indignation over them, do set off some sparks, especially when the process actually gets underway. But the action halts and hops from one scene to another, with issues picked up and quickly dropped.

The production, by Obsidian Theatre, does the play no favours. Obsidian made a bright beginning with Adventures of a Black Girl in Search of God, hardly a great text but a lively one (and with some resemblances to the current piece, the defence of Negro Creek being a later, lighter equivalent of the defence of Africville) and vibrantly staged and performed. They have been floundering since. David R. Collins direction here is limp, hardly able to build a scene, let alone a play. No lines of communication open up between actors; people barely look at those to whom they are supposed to be talking, and few lines are uttered as if the speaker actually expected a reply.

Kevin Hanchard as Willum is the only actor to play with consistent force and fluency; his performance here is not up to his brilliant work in Blue/Orange, but then neither are the circumstances. He shares the best sustained scene in the play with Tom Clancy (Norman C. Owen), the junior bureaucrat sent to enforce the city's will and not much liking it; like all well-meaning men in impossible situations (see the conscientious clergyman in The Crucible) this token white man emerges as protagonist by default; he's the only character permitted complexity. As the minister and de facto community leader, Nigel Shawn Williams comes through in fits and starts; he certainly has authority but, like anyone, he could use some help. Far more in need of it are the women in the cast: Shakura SAida as Clarice, Lili Francks as an elderly all-purpose babysitter, Abena Malika as the local tramp, named Groovey. None of them is bad exactly but they are all amateurish, indistinct and unable to hold or vary a feeling.

Third Question: Is the government guy Groovey is said to be involved with the same Tom Clancy who spends so much of the play bustling around the place? If he is, its odd they don't have any scenes together. If he isn't, its even odder that the matter is mentioned at all.

Fourth Question: How is it that Groovey has time to move to Montreal and then come back again, while the infant body still lies unburied?

Fifth Question: Whats Groovey's purpose in the play anyway?

The remaining male character is Doublespeak (Troy Adams), who accepts the government's offer and later regrets it: a good scene that should have been followed up. His family is moved out in a garbage truck. Clarice and others claim this is part of a systematic process of humiliation. Clancy says it was a one-off mistake.

Sixth Question: Which of these is true? Were never told, and it matters. Doublespeak justifies his name by talking with a stammer.

Seventh Question: Just what is this supposed to add? The play ends with a funeral. I suppose it would be naive to make my Eighth Question a simple Why? So I will ask instead: Shouldnt the staging make it clear that this is the end, rather than leaving a painful moment of uncertainty between the last notes of the burial hymn and the first claps of the curtain call?
