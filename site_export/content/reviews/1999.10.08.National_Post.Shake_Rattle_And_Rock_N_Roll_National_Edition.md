SHAKING THE FOUNDATIONS

Buddies in Bad Times, Toronto

I went in innocent and came out impressed. The heyday of Carole Pope and her band Rough Trade was in the 1970s, long before I came to Toronto, and I probably wouldn't have been very interested even if I had been here. The band is usually cited as a progenitor of Canadian punk rock, and my own experience of British punk was that it was essentially -- indeed aggressively -- anti-musical.

The first and most durable surprise of Shaking the Foundations -- a staged assortment of numbers written by Pope and her collaborator Kevan Staples -- is how very musical it is. The songs, as performed by five female singers and one female pianist, seem to come out of the best end of rock 'n' roll -- the rhythm and blues end -- and they lend themselves very easily to the richly chorded jazz treatment they're given here. The result is a show that's cool in look, sound and attitude.

Highschool Confidential, Rough Trade's biggest hit (or foremost of the Pope-Staples pop staples), emerges unsurprisingly as their most immediately effective. The music swings and the lyric is wittily specific. Blisteringly sung by Tanya Rich as the second-act opener, it's a song of lesbian lust for the most icily desirable girl in the class that could probably, in the most traditional pop fashion, work just as well as a song for a horny male. (Whether the world would have been ready then for a man singing a lyric about creaming his jeans, any more than they apparently were for a woman, is of course another matter.)

It is, though, one of the few numbers in the lineup that could ever be that ecumenical. Most of the show is strictly girl-on-girl, "strictly" being the operative adverb since there's a strong S&M component as well: sometimes satirized, sometimes exalted.

It does get monotonous, though this is more a matter of style than content. Despite the resourcefulness of Bryden Macdonald's direction, which distributes the performers all over the Buddies in Bad Times cabaret space, the songs, like all pop songs, are easier to make theatrical than to make dramatic.

They are all confessional monologues, even if they're fiercer and more self-consciously outspoken than most. Every one of them is a solo. Some of them are solos with accompanying vocal harmonies, some are solos in which the lines are split five ways, but there's never any interplay between characters. Indeed, there are never, for more than a song at a time, any definable characters. (There is plenty of personality.) Any overall shape, as often happens in staged songfests, is in the eye of the compiler. As usually happens, there's an outbreak of togetherness at the end of the show.

The lyrics can be agreeably conversational -- "but I digress," says one song, in a Sondheim moment -- but they can also be melodramatic and pretentious. "There's no escape from this modern Third Reich," complains somebody (have they any idea what the Third Reich was actually like?), compounding the felony by adding, "there's no escape from this endless night." I think that this, which adds ineptitude to overkill, is meant to be a rhyme. There's no law saying that lyrics have to rhyme. Why try if you can't do it?

There is also a lot of the protest writing, endemic ever since Dylan and the Beatles, in which you spray aggression without worrying about the relation between the people you're attacking and the things you're accusing them of, or, come to that, the relation between one line and the next.

Some things are good, though. A very funny song called Grade B Movie is the closest the show comes to acknowledging the existence of heterosexual relationships. (It takes a dim view of them.) It is put over, with hilariously lampooned ardour, by Paula Wolfson, a chunky and magnetic performer who startlingly resembles the young Annie Ross: in her wry, tart, pervasive good humour; in her rhythmic sense; in her range, which goes from pealing highs to rocking lows; in her colloquial phrasing; in her general sense of grown-upness; even -- in certain lights -- in her carrot-red hair.

She also looks great in black lingerie, as do the admirable rest of the troupe -- Jane Miller, Gwyneth Baillie, Astrid Van Wieren, Tanya Rich and, not least, pianist and musical director Holly Arsenault. She, apart from Wolfson, is the real star of the show; she is certainly its pulsating engine.
