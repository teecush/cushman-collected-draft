This year's Shaw Festival begins with one of the old boy's most infallible creations. Not that he was so old at the time; Arms and the Man, written in 1894, was only Bernard Shaw's fourth play. He called it, with his tongue only partly in his cheek, "a romantic comedy." It's really an anti-romantic, anti-heroic and anti- idealistic comedy that finishes by setting up more palatable ideals of both heroism and romance. It borrows its title from the first line of The Aeneid, and brings the assumptions of that bellicose epic down to Earth. It takes for its setting a real and recent war in the Balkans. Later conflicts in that region have made this one look positively cozy, and Shaw himself, whimsical as ever, can't help making it mock-picturesque. It was the first of his plays to be turned into a musical -- a downright comic opera in fact, The Chocolate Soldier. This incensed Shaw, but in a way he was asking for it. The Shaw Festival team gives it to him all over again.

Jackie Maxwell's production is as cute as a whole row of buttons. The approach has its charms, but it ends up selling the play short. It starts very well; at least once we've got past a prelude treating us to a series of dollhouse views of the

upper-crust Bulgarian manse in which the action takes place. When the stage buckles down to its prescribed business representing the heroine's bedroom, we get a fine duet between Raina Petkoff, the daughter of the house, and Captain Bluntschli, the Swiss mercenary in flight with the defeated Serbs, who climbs into her chamber, begins by threatening her and ends by throwing himself on her mercy, which is freely granted. Raina, whose father is a senior officer and whose fiance, Sergius, is

a cavalry-charging hero, has a

starry-eyed view of war, which Bluntschli, the professional soldier who has just spent three days under fire, systematically deflates.

He makes her angry, but he also makes an impression. Raina is

Diana Donnelly, who has gained enormously in charm, humour and authority since playing Major

Barbara last season. Patrick Galligan, torn between politeness and fatigue, is enchantingly deft as Bluntschli, whose native genius for logic and logistics is enhanced by his being the son of a hotel tycoon and whose holster is stuffed not with ammunition but with far more useful chocolate creams.

The first scene, then, is beautifully written and beautifully played. After that, things slide. The production becomes soft- centred, even by chocolate-cream standards. The men come home, Sergius acting passionate and petulant by turns. He is an inveterate poseur, but that doesn't mean he has to strike literal poses every moment. Mike Shara can hardly utter a line without resting his fist on his brow, his elbow on his knee, or his leg on a convenient rock: sometimes all three at once. In between gestures he gives a good, if lightweight, performance, but all this pantomime subverts part of the truth about Sergius, which is that he partly sees through his own pretensions. As Raina partly sees through hers, when it gets to the point that they see through one another's, their relationship is -- quite cheerfully -- doomed.

In a program note Maxwell describes the characters as "wonderfully eccentric." But they aren't; they're conventional people, vigorously drawn, which is where the fun lies. Peter Hutt catches the tone perfectly as Major Petkoff, who only wants to settle into his comfortable old life and his comfortable old coat. Peter Millard gets close to it as the servant Nicola, though for some reason he plays his crucial line about having the soul of a servant as if it were an expression of anger rather than self- satisfaction. (Characters in Shaw rarely get angry; those who do almost invariably look ridiculous.) But the maid Louka (Catherine McGregor) is played as a fiery feminist, rather than a clever and sexy girl with an eye to the main chance, while Nora McLellan's Petkoff mama, having been very helpful to begin with, progressively loses touch with reality. It may be that the overpowering turban she wears in company leaves her with little option.

William Schmuck's costumes and Sue LePage's sets increasingly dominate the evening, driving out the real humour. The Petkoffs are awfully proud of their library, "the only one in Bulgaria." Shaw's stage directions make clear that it has very few books but is "a most comfortable sitting-room," not terribly grand. Here it's a yawningly wide chamber, furnished with intimidating elegance and with no books whatsoever; they must all be shelved on the fourth wall. It's the kind of design that gets audience applause, especially in Niagara, but does nothing for the play. Jokes pass unlaughed-at; the recouplings of Sergius and Louka, Bluntschli and Raina, have no tension. Galligan remains delightful, but he lacks force; the energy that should leave the other characters gasping at the close is a shadow of what it should be. ("What a man!" says Sergius, flattened by this whirlwind of an anti-hero, and then adds, "Is he a man?" The curtain falls on the question.) Donnelly forfeits her poise and, worse yet, her timing. Raina's final admission of love for her chocolate-cream soldier should be the play's romantic peak; its anti-romantic peak as well. It's hard to forgive a production that lets it pass as a non-event.
