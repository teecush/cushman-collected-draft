THE CORONATION VOYAGE

The Playhouse, Vancouver

It is 1953, and an ocean liner is sailing from Montreal to England, carrying customers for the crowning of Elizabeth II. The passengers we meet, all first-class, represent a cross-section of the French-Canadian upper crust -- and most of them have much to be cross and crusty about.

There is the Chief, kingpin of the Montreal underworld, now in desperate need of a new life and a new identity; and there is the Diplomat, who is prepared to accommodate him, for a consideration. The Chief is travelling with his two sons, born to him by two different mistresses, one French and one Italian. The Diplomat's price for three fake passports is a night in bed with the younger of the two boys, the Italian one.

Then there is the Minister, a thin-blooded high-rise politician whose own three sons were sacrificed 10 years earlier on the beaches at Dieppe, and his wife, who will not forget, and their daughter.

Aboard as light relief -- they certainly carry less baggage, emotional and physical -- are three girls called Elisabeth, lottery winners who are there because they have the same name and birthday as the English queen though not quite -- note the subtly nationalist point -- the same spelling.

The opening scenes, in which the characters are introduced to us as they embark, are rather like the long first number of Titanic (the musical), minus the reassuring knowledge that most of them are going to drown. The piece as a whole belongs to a much longer tradition of plays about representative figures outward bound -- going for trips on symbolic ships.

The Coronation Voyage is a five-year-old work by prolific Quebec playwright Michel Marc Bouchard. It's getting around; it received its English-language premiere earlier this year in Calgary, while the current production is a joint venture by the Vancouver Playhouse and the National Arts Centre in Ottawa. The stiff, formal language of Linda Gaboriau's translation may well be an accurate reflection of the original, but the play probably sounds better in French -- not just because abstraction usually does, but because the author presents a specifically French-Canadian view of a historical moment and of its reverberations backward and forward. Stripped of its native accents and performed in other provinces, the flavour becomes diluted and the stance questionable.

It has a few excellent jokes and a sharp and powerful ending. It also, early on, has suspense; we want to know how the blackmailed Chief will cope with his dilemma. This story, though, hangs fire while the author explores other avenues and then runs them in parallel; the situation may seem like Tosca or Measure for Measure with a sex change, but the example the play quotes is that of Abraham and Isaac, with the intervening angel left out.

So we have the Minister's wife, lamenting her killed or crippled sons; and we have her daughter and the Chief's elder son, both of them talented pianists. The boy, indeed, was a prodigy until a couple of his dad's mobster rivals slammed the piano lid on his hands and ended his career. The wife is named Alice, the daughter Marguerite, and the youth Etienne; his young half-brother is called Sandro. On this voyage, in a radical extension of traditional shipwreck etiquette, only women and children have names.

There is one further masculine-type figure: the Biographer, who functions as a part-time narrator and whose presence seems -- even in a play as avowedly non-naturalistic as this -- wildly implausible. The excuse for his presence is that he's writing the Chief's highly sanitized memoirs; from this, we are supposed to extrapolate the distance between the clean history we read in books and the dirty history that gets lived. On a national level we gather that, even in the flag-waving year of '53, Canadians -- certainly French Canadians -- weren't as delirious about England and its monarchy as the official record would suggest. (I wouldn't know, but I can tell you one thing from the other side: When we English kids watched the coronation parade, the guys we really cheered for were the Mounties. We loved you, even if you despised us. We also booed Churchill; I can't remember why, but the news should please Bouchard, one of whose characters advances the interesting theory that Churchill fought the Second World War as a favour to Stalin. I always thought that Britain and the Commonwealth countries were fighting the Nazis long before Russia, and that this was something to be proud of, but maybe this, too, is just the kind of history you read in books.)

On a more immediate level, we get the characters' ignoble behaviour and the Biographer's glowing interpretations simultaneously. The juxtaposition is good for a few ironic chuckles, but it gets worked overtime. The Biographer doesn't seem to be much of a writer; he describes sacrifices made by the Chief "to the edification of his success." Maybe he means "edifice." The writing of this role is, in fact, unspeakable; certainly that's how it sounds here.

The kind of updated morality drama that rates characters by their functions -- what you might call the genre of the generic -- is not my favourite kind of show, but I accept that it has its place. It demands, though, stern and ruthless playing; it has -- or should have -- no room for sentiment. Marti Maraden's production is lucidly organized, on a setting by Pam Johnson that's as handsome as luxury liner sets invariably are.

Too often, though, it goes soggy. Elizabeth Shepherd as the Minister's wife delivers her account of the slaughter at Dieppe not with the anger it seems to call for but as some kind of tone poem. She is fine, however, in the character's other, comedic aspect: as a frustrated mondaine, somewhat resembling Angela Lansbury in regal mode. (We are told that, when she requested a seat at the captain's table, the captain gave her his.)

Leon Pownall, one of the best actors in Canada (Stratford needs him), is a powerfully impassive Chief, never asking for sympathy even when it seems within reach. With these two players, experience tells.

Its opposite can be quite nifty, as well. Eric Bojm, a 13-year- old, is wonderfully natural as the threatened Sandro; even when he sounds awkward, he sounds real -- real enough for his possible sacrifice to be truly disturbing.

Inexperience is less helpful when it gets old enough to be self- conscious; Kevin MacDonald, an actor fresh out of drama school, is overtaxed by the embittered Etienne, who unfortunately is the pivotal character -- or at least the only one who undergoes any kind of development.

The Diplomat (Allan Morgan), by contrast, is bisected as well as bisexual: half slimy seducer, half sensitive war veteran. There is no reason why these characteristics shouldn't co-exist, but the author does no more than pull an on-off switch.

Patti Allan is pretty funny as a protocol expert, who instructs the three tin Lizzies in the niceties of obeisance to royalty and is mightily peeved when a cargo of Indians refuse to appear in ceremonial headdresses that seem to have been hired from a touring company of Annie Get Your Gun.

All these pieces fit together; story and ideas are slickly conflated. Neither, though, is terribly interesting, because the people aren't. If plays are cakes, this one is well seasoned but half-baked.
