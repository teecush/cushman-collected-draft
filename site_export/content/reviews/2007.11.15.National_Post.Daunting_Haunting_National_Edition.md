BLITHE SPIRIT

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

In the vast corpus of Noel Coward's work, Blithe Spirit ranks as a medium play: Below Hay Fever, Private Lives and Present Laughter, but ahead of most of the others. It's also a medium play because there's a medium in it:Charles Condomine, a successful English novelist, calls -- for research purposes only -- on the services of Madame Arcati, the Ouija-board lady from the local village, and she inadvertently conjures up the ghost of Charles' late wife Elvira, much to the displeasure of his current wife, Ruth. Charles isn't too pleased either, suddenly finding himself caught between two brides, of whom only one is technically alive though both are kicking.

Morris Panych's Soulpepper production is an unevenly blended cocktail of truth and exaggeration. He's on his best behaviour with the scenes between Charles and Ruth, which he takes with the same playful seriousness that distinguished his Shaw Festival production of Coward's Design for Living. Joseph Ziegler and Fiona Reid depict, with maximum sharpness, a match in which supposedly comfortable badinage covers an emotional void. It helps, of course, that both actors are superb comic technicians. Reid, in a knockout series of evening frocks, manages, as usual, to locate the most flammable word in any sentence and then ignite it. Ziegler, forced to cater to a mischievous former beloved invisible to everyone but him, is very droll in his double takes; he also strikes to the spoiled childish heart of a man who's been both bullied and protected by all the women who have ever entered his life. Neither performance, though, can quite sustain the length of the evening. It's largely the fault of the writing: Ruth more or less fades out, while Charles sticks around to do the plot's heavy lifting without being granted much of a personality. (Coward himself knew this; he refused to play the role himself, except when trapped into it on tour.) By the end, Ziegler has been reduced to playing prim crossed with cute. The Christmas season chez Soulpepper has come to mean Joe Ziegler seeing ghosts, and I did get to feeling nostalgic for his matchless Scrooge. (It will be back next year.)

Brenda Robins, red of hair and suit, and unearthly-white of face, is an unusually abrasive Elvira but without the melting flirtatiousness that one assumes Coward intended would balance the equation. (He did have a weakness for throaty charmers.) As for Madame Arcati, the happy medium, there are historically two ways of playing her: The full-flower eccentricity of Margaret Rutherford, whose original performance is providentially preserved on film, or the cheerfully practical businesswoman epitomized by Jenifer Phipps in a Shaw production that made me think more highly of the play than I have before or since. Nancy Palk offers a bit of both: She makes a bracing first impression, but the production pushes her way over the top. Madame boasts of being able to bicycle up hill and down dale, but calisthenics in the drawing room are much too much. So too, and more, is the maid Edith. The script decrees that she performs all her duties at the double, but that doesn't mean she has to go everywhere with knees bent, crouched in the starting position. Melody A. Johnson, who plays her, seems to have been directed to be insanely funny, something of which she is more than capable but not when the production stands over her and makes her do it.

The one unquestionably triumphant thing about this show is its look. Ken MacDonald's set and Charlotte Dean's costumes transport us to a vanished world where doctors made house calls in evening dress, or indeed to a vanished world where doctors made house calls. Everything that isn't explicitly ghostly is done in shades of grey; the cups in the china cabinet match the upholstery on the sofa. In scale and proportion, this is a paragon among country-house settings, majestic without being overpowering. At the end, when the ghosts turn to poltergeists, the set even gets to do a turn of its own, with furnishings flying all over the place. It could even afford to go on longer and more violently; this is a rare example of Panych erring on the side of restraint. Overall, there's a whiff of superior summer theatre about this winter show, starting with the choice of play. It's Soulpepper punching below its weight. - Blithe Spirit runs until Dec. 15.
