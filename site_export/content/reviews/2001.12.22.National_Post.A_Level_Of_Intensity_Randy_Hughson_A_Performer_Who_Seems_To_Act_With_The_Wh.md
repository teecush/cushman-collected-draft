The Toronto acting world is a small one, a fact that can be both frustrating and comforting. Actors get used to working together, in constantly shifting combinations, and audiences enjoy matching them up. I have a porous memory for press releases, and these days I try not to look too closely at the program before the play starts. I like to be taken by surprise.

So this fall I found myself at Theatre Passe Muraille watching a play called You Are Here, by which I was less enraptured than some, when on to the stage, quite late in the first act and to my great pleasure and surprise, walked Randy Hughson.

The pleasure derived from the transforming energy and precision of his performance. The surprise derived partly from the fact that Hughson was playing a sophisticated and successful film director, though he is best known as an actor of colourful derelicts; he had started the year with a show-stealing performance of one of them in Zadie's Shoes and had gone on to another in Earshot, a show at the Tarragon that he had no occasion to steal since he was the only actor in it. (A set with a spectacular false perspective did give him some serious competition.)

Shortly after that it was back to Passe Muraille to play a bewildered husband in a phantasmagoric piece called This Hotel. Which is another reason that his being there in You Are Here was so unexpected. It was clear by now that Hughson was in demand, but surely, along about now, he was due for a rest.

Since then there has been Soldier's Heart, which has just closed at the Tarragon, and he is now in rehearsal for The Lonesome West, which opens at Canadian Stage in January. After that he does the commercial re-mount of Zadies Shoes.

I met him for lunch this week and asked him just how long this has been going on. He traces it back to the opening of Slavs!, at the Tarragon in the fall of 2000; since then he has done seven plays, plus the occasional TV and independent film, and has never had a day out of work.

I asked him what he thought was the quality that got him hired.

"I have, he said, a level of intensity. Its not difficult for me to access deeper souls. Also, I come from a working-class family and background and I use that part of myself. I have an Everyman quality. I'm very much in touch with my blue-collar roots."

He offered this self-analysis with the gentle assurance of a cherub. And, in fact, his performances are as notable not only for their sense of banked-up violence but for their remarkable sweetness, largely conveyed by offbeat but infallible comic timing.

The Lonesome West is one of the new wave of Irish plays -- written by Martin McDonough, author of the transfixing Beauty Queen of Leenane -- and is set in the same remote, poverty-stricken village. Hughson plays a 40-year-old man (his own age, exactly) whom he describes as "an adult male with a child's sense of justice and play and anger.

He is also one of two brothers, and opening rehearsals have been largely devoted to fights and accents. Hughson actually sounded Irish while we were talking; he admitted he was under orders to use it as much as possible. He also worried there might be an overlay from the Newfoundland voice he had just finished using in Soldiers Heart, then brightened up and said that he spoke with a Kingston brogue anyway.

Kingston is where he grew up. He studied English for a year at Queens University and then took the theatre course at Ryerson, from which he graduated in 1984. He spent six or seven years" acting in Saskatchewan and Alberta and came back to Toronto to find himself "not part of the stream of working actors; I definitely felt I was an outcast.

His turning point came in 1990 when Urjo Kareda, director of the Tarragon, cast him in a production of Judith Thompson's The Crackwalker.

Urjo," he says, "really stood behind me, then and afterwards. Gradually I started getting offers instead of having to audition. (He now generally knows what he will be doing a year in advance.)

I can make a living right now," he says, touching wood with a double layer of self-mockery.)

Another early triumph came in Whale Riding Weather, in which he struck me as the first stage performer I had ever seen who seemed to be acting with the whites of his eyes. In this he played another "very sinister fragile man-child fighting a sexual battle -- half- homo, half-hetero -- that I hadn't had to explore before."

It hasnt all been like that. "When I was younger, I was doing marginalized bad-assed emotionally disturbed young people. Ive got more comedic roles as I've got older.

Unusual among prominent Toronto-based actors, he has never played at either Stratford or Shaw, though not for want of being asked. I didn't feel the need to get settled there for a year."

He had played plenty of classics earlier, including Benvolio in Robert Lepages bilingual Romeo and Juliet, and he was Sir Toby Belch in Soulpeppers Twelfth Night. He also took on, and surmounted, the biggest classical challenge any Toronto actor has taken on in recent years, that of playing the title role in Daniel Brooks' extraordinary production of Goethe's Faust (Part One only, though hes still trying to talk Brooks into adding Part Two sometime).

"Thats the most work I've ever done in my life: full of hour- long philosophical monologues. He made them grippingly comprehensible, and his sympathetic quality can never have been more useful.

He performed a similar feat as the misanthropic recluse, tormented by over-sensitive hearing, in Morris Panych's Earshot. It was like memorizing a novel. I wrote lines all over the set; the audience never knew -- the set was so dark. Morris said he wrote it for me. He said it was me."

And was it? "Well, I dont suffer from advanced hearing. And that desperation isnt part of me. But certain aspects I could relate to. His loneliness, his isolation, his sense of removal from the rest of the planet. I reacted to the man's pettiness. And to his sense of humour about himself."

The last is plainly true. Quite apart from his talent, it's obvious why Hughson gets so much work. He must be a first-rate person to have in a company.
