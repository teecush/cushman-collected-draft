FALSTAFF

The Third Stage, Stratford, Ont.

The play the Stratford Festival is calling Falstaff begins with a prologue spoken by the symbolic character of Rumour. Since the play is really Henry IV Part Two, his main purpose is to bring us up to speed on what happened in Part One, and especially to remind us of the result of the Battle of Shrewsbury: King won, Rebels lost. By emphasizing the misleading reports that follow such an event (smooth comforts false, worse than true wrongs), he ushers us into this new plays sliding, rotten-ripe world. In this Stratford production, Rumours address is given an ideal delivery by Keith Dinicol, daintily sarcastic.

As good as the prologue is the epilogue, a speech so rarely done I had forgotten it existed. It is spoken here by Douglas Campbell, whom we have just seen as Falstaff, receiving his catastrophic comeuppance from his beloved Prince Hal, newly crowned as Henry V. Stepping nimbly away from his humiliation, he promises us a sequel to a sequel. His two identities blur; he is Falstaff, rejecting his own rejection, and he is the rubicund and orotund actor, part of the festival furniture, who has been playing comic leads here since the first season, 49 years ago. Either way, we are pleased to listen to him.

The space between these two elegant bookends is uneven: sometimes superb, sometimes rather dull. Scott Wentworth's mixed-period direction, as in Part One, is more notable for intelligence and lucidity than for imagination, which means it depends heavily on the skills and personalities of its actors. If the retitling has been done for commercial reasons (audiences don't want to feel theyve wandered into the middle of something, especially if they have), it also has a certain artistic logic; this, even more than Part One, is Falstaffs play.

Probably the crucial difference between the two productions is though Campbell has played the Part One Falstaff countless times before, this is his first attempt at Part Two. In Part One, he could greet the lines as old friends and concentrate on phrasing them, which he did with results that were often hilarious, sometimes surprising and never sentimental. In Part Two, he is far less secure. For an octogenarian actor to be essaying so lengthy a role for the first time is remarkable in itself, even if he does have a characterization all ready to fit it; and in fact much of what he does is excellent.

Dressed this time as a 19th-century country squire, he gives the role a firm outline, from his cynical beginning as a war profiteer, trading on a spurious reputation and some genuine diseases, through to the news of Hal's accession, at which his face lights radiantly up (I suspect the stage lights light up as well), and then down again to his final discomfiture, which is complete but economical.

There is nothing very probing, varied or original about any of this, but it is accurate and, when the vocal assurance is in place, very enjoyable. He can still build and relish the soliloquies; he can still toss off a perfect sardonic aside. The latter, in fact, is what he does best, but he can only do it when the words come out clean; and on the first night, though he didn't forget, he did fumble.

There is another problem. The most compelling aspect of Part One was Falstaffs relationship with Graham Abbeys prince, but in this play, with Hal increasingly alienated from his old life, the two hardly meet. When they do collide, on Hal's last trip to the Eastcheap tavern, a great scene comes to barbed if belated life.

Elsewhere, Abbey stints a bit on his character's world- weariness, and his delivery of the regulation speech about royal insomnia (it runs in the family) is very much by the book, but most of the time he is riveting. Even when king, he stays in his old black leather until the coronation. He gets more tension and control than I would have thought possible into the scene where he first threatens, then reassures, his brothers and the Lord Chief Justice.

Equally fine is his last-breath reconciliation with his father. Benedict Campbell (Mr. Campbell the Younger they would have billed him in the old days) plays the dying usurper as a man compulsively revisiting the past, both seeking and refusing vindication. He carries this habit, thrillingly, toward his deathbed, flinging open the chamber door to call rhetorically on other nations, putting a pillow on his head in mockery of the crown his son has prematurely taken from him, and incidentally recalling Falstaffs royal masquerade in the previous instalment.

The baronial scenes in this play are always hard to activate. This production gets them off to a compelling start with the conflicting accounts of Hotspurs death; his ghost wanders through the scene at one moment, and Jane Spence caps this with a powerful appearance as his widow. But the impetus is not maintained; the minor rebels are grey and there is a strange symbolic pseudo-battle in the middle of the peace conference. Bernard Hopkins' Archbishop has some good visionary moments, but I don't see why his every speech has to be a sermon.

Stratford this season has talent to burn, but it is surely a gratuitous conflagration that confines the great Richard McMillan to the role of the extremely functional Earl of Warwick, even if his revelation that Henrys death chamber is prophetically called Jerusalem gets probably its most quietly effective delivery in theatrical history.

Back in the part of the play with Sir John in it, Barry MacGregor completes his reliable Bardolph, equipped with a nose to challenge Cyranos but sadly deprived of his best scene. Diane D'Aquila's force and intelligence as the Hostess are as striking as they are unusual, but they dont seem to have much relation to the characters grasshopper mind. Kate Trotter's Doll Tearsheet is all right when she stops shouting, but the tavern is neither as funny nor as mellow as it should be, and not a patch on the equivalent scene in Part One.

The miraculous Gloucestershire episodes, which testify in loving detail to the persistence of rural and domestic life while princes and politicians kill and betray one another, are enlivened by a vivid bunch of recruits and by William Needles' aged Justice Silence, inexorably reminding his cousin that in his youth he was called "lusty Shallow" and not, as he now insists, "mad Shallow." Lewis Gordon plays the mad or lusty one, and though hardly one of the great or deep Shallows (like the Hostess, he isnt silly or voluble enough), he has a fine head-shaking moment at the end when he prophesies Falstaffs doom.

The confluence of so many Stratford veterans, besides being pleasant in itself, fits with the play itself, which is about age and change and the end of illusion. But, though the production has some neat overlaps and juxtapositions, the various parts never really come together and none of it matters as much as it should.
