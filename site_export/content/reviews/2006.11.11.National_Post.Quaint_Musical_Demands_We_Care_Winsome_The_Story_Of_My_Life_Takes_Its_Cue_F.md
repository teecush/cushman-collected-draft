There is a new book out whose sub-title is The Dream of a Canadian Musical Theatre. Its principal title is Broadway North, which strikes me as a very strange choice indeed. The author, Mel Atkey, has cast himself as both advocate for and chronicler of an independent and indigenous Canadian musical. So why has he chosen a label that, apart from being a runaway cliche in itself, explicitly looks to the United States for validation?

The Broadway (South) success of The Drowsy Chaperone allows the book an upbeat ending, though the vagaries of timing have meant that it has to be squeezed into a last-minute epilogue. But, of course, Chaperone has been given an American overhaul in staging and casting, and was a pastiche of American models in the first place.

Now at CanStage we have the world premiere of The Story of My Life, a musical that's practically a study in cross-pollination. Its authors are Canadians now based in New York. Its director is American. Of its two actors, one has spent most of his career in the United States, though he has also worked at Stratford; the other is a Canadian treasure who also enjoys star status on Broadway and whose name, not to keep you in too much suspense, is Brent Carver. The presentation offers very few grounds for complaint. The piece itself is close to unbearable.

It doesnt, as it happens, owe much to Broadway. It does, though, owe a lot to off-Broadway, and to a tradition of small or at least smaller musicals thats been flourishing in Greenwich Village and points north ever since New York discovered The Threepenny Opera in the mid-1950s. There's a useful history of the genre in the CanStage program, though one that's sometimes reckless in the scope of what it includes; it tries, for example, to rope in A Chorus Line. I saw A Chorus Line in its tryout at the Public Theatre and even then it was not, believe me, small.

Even off-Broadway musicals that are small dont have to be winsome (see Urinetown). But they very often are. And sometimes, as happens in The Story of My Life, that winsomeness is wedded to a throbbing emotionalism, a demand that we care about the people in front of us, however little reason we may be given to do so, because the authors care so much, dammit, and they have integrity, as is proved by the fact that theyre working on a modest scale. The word for this is sentimentality. Big musicals, of course, are hardly immune to it, but if I had to choose, I'd say that they do it less offensively if only because, with all that space at their disposal, they can spread the goo around a bit so that we can lap it up in a less concentrated form.

The Story of My Life is about two men, friends from boyhood, who grow up in a small town where it always seems to be winter. At any rate, Glenn Davidson has designed it as a snowscape dotted with piles of books. The prime reason is that much of the show takes place in and around the town bookstore. The town's name is Angel Falls and it might be either here or there, Canada or the United States; I dont remember ever being told which. I did get the feeling, though, when one of the characters moved to a successful literary life in an equally unspecified big city, that he was in New York rather than Toronto. The wider cultural references, too, seem to be American: Melville and Twain.

One of the cleverer things in the show is a recurring situation in which a character attends a funeral incognito, a la Tom Sawyer. Coming down a few pegs, there are constant references to Its a Wonderful Life. Alvin, who stays in Angel Falls to run his dad's bookstore (into the ground, eventually) is fixated on that picture, and especially on its angel Gabriel. So he sure picked the right place to live. Thomas, his buddy, is less fixated on both the town and the film; he refers to the latter as Frank Capra's feelgood Christmas movie." This is one of the more trenchant lines in Brian Hills book, which is both a lot shorter and a little more barbed than Neil Bartrams score, and Carver as Thomas fell on the tart words with every appearance of delight.

Alvin, like Jacob Marley, is dead to begin with, and Thomas has come back home after many years' absence to deliver his eulogy, in accordance with a long-ago promise (mutual). This, of course, leads to flashbacks, beginning with the friends' boyhood snowball fights. We see the school-age Thomas trying to protect Alvin from the consequences of his own neediness, not to mention his nerdiness, and we also see Thomas unexpectedly discovering a vocation as a writer and moving away to pursue it. We see various things, but theyre never more than incidents, and claustrophobically repetitive. Alvin eventually claims that Thomas has built his career on retelling stories that Alvin told him first and indeed had actually lived, but we have no way of judging the validity of this claim; even if its true, we don't know how much Thomas (who snarls "I never share credit") may have added in the way of style and imagination. Aren't all authors thieves? Throwing away his scripted funeral speech, Thomas decides to look in his heart and, in effect, improvise the show we have just seen that, he implies, will tell his hearers about Alvin. Only it wont; the show has told us little about Alvin and virtually nothing about Thomas. Which is why The Story of My Life is so deadly.

Well, because of that and its overall quaintness. In one respect, the show does progress a bit beyond its stylistic forebears from the 50s and after; it offers what I can only call explicit acknowledgement of implicit homosexuality. This, we infer, is what caused Thomas to call off his marriage and Alvin never even to come close. Thomas puts his fiancee on ice in one of the better songs, certainly one of the more slyly conversational. The score in general is competently crafted but pallid. It pleads for sympathy; even a bouncy song about an inspirational teacher has too much of the puppy- dog about it. Carver is in beautiful voice throughout, though I wish that on this occasion some of the harder edges of his acting had found their way into his singing. Jeffrey Kuhn is first-rate as Alvin, touchingly eager to please when young, powerfully embittered when older. The director is Michael Bush. This isn't the dream of a Canadian musical, and it's too mild even to be the nightmare of an American one.
