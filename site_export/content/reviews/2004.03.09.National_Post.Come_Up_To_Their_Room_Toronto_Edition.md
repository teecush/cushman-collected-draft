TAPE

Gladstone Hotel, Toronto

There is always an attraction in seeing a play outside the confines of an orthodox theatre, though the refreshment comes from the change itself, rather than from any specific virtue in the new locale. This is the case even when a play is produced in its "real" setting, since what is now known as site-specific theatre is no more real than any other form. To a purist, it may even be less so. A play set in a hotel room, and actually performed in one, undoubtedly offers a jolt, but how do we account for the presence, amid all this authenticity, of ourselves, the spectators, each of us keenly conscious of the others' presence, and all of us more clearly visible than we would be in any old conventional space? The further you go toward realism in the theatre, the more artificial you become. The paradoxes of theatrical representation are, as always, enough to make your head spin.

Tape is a one-act play set in a seedy hotel room in Lansing, Mich. It receives its Canadian premiere in a seedy hotel room in Toronto -- or, in charity to the managements feelings, in one of the few rooms at the now-trendy Gladstone on Queen West that the renovators have not yet reached. Eighteen of us voyeurs are squeezed in two packed rows along one wall, otherwise everything is as time and nature left it, though some fortuitous new holes in the peeling wallpaper make the atmosphere even more atmospheric. The show is the inaugural venture of Crate Productions, whose stated aim is "to engage non-traditional audiences in alternative venues." Im not sure of the definition of a non-traditional audience, but I'm fairly sure I don't; all the same, I was glad to be there.

The play, first seen in New York in 1999, is the work of a rising dramatist, Stephen Belber, who has a new play due on Broadway this season. His past credits include being one of the team responsible for The Laramie Project, which means that, going strictly by the numbers, he is currently the most-produced playwright in Toronto.

In conventional surroundings Tape would probably seem decent but ordinary. Here, the veracity of the setting and the closeness of the quarters give it more tension and impact than it might otherwise have. It starts with one of those one-upping contests between old buddies that I suspect of being more common in American drama than they are in American life. Jon and Vince were friends in high school. Jon is now an aspiring filmmaker on the edge of achievement; his first picture is about to be shown at the Lansing festival. Vince has come from California -- where he works as a firefighter and, more steadily, as a drug dealer -- to cheer his old pal on. That at least is how it seems at first.

We are in Vinces room. (Jon, we assume, has a much smarter one somewhere else.) It turns out that the two of them have issues, long- simmering ones, over a girl named Amy whom they both dated and on whom Jon may or may not have forced his attentions. The title refers to a piece of recorded evidence, and also, I fancy, to the sticky bonds that still envelop the three characters. For Amy, conveniently, is also in Lansing. She works there, in fact, as a lawyer and, from the look and sound of her, a high-flying one. She has her own story, and it trumps the egos of both the guys. This is a Rashomon in which we are fairly sure whom to believe.

There are no great shafts of wit or insight in Belbers individual lines, but he builds up a powerful cumulative picture of male insecurity. As Vince and Jon shift positions we come to understand -- sometimes immediately, sometimes in retrospect -- why each needs to believe the worst of the other. More subtly, we also understand why each needs to believe the worst of himself.

The play offers an ironic take on the old saw about nice guys finishing last, the teaser being the difficulty of working out which of these guys might, at least in comparative terms, be the nice one. The verdict, such as it is, is a surprise. For the actors, required to be aggressive in a confined space, the main problem seems to be finding the right level: not just of intensity but of volume. The urge to underplay leads John Gordon (Vince) and Chris Reynolds (Jon) into a pair of performances too restrained to be credible. It's only when Vince lights up a joint, and Jon partakes, that their mellowness begins to make sense -- becomes, in fact, a style. (Vince has come well supplied with dope, and also with drink. When the play starts, the floor is already littered with stove-in cans.)

Once properly poised, they stay satisfactorily on target; Gordon taking Vince's angry pain and hanging it out to dry, Reynolds Jon contriving to seem smug and open-faced at the same time. Kate Meehans Amy comes on like an ice goddess out on a date, as if the heroine of Neil LaBute's The Shape of Things had wandered into a David Mamet play and proceeded to show the boys what tough really was. Here too the play twists an old maxim: It may be that hell hath no fury like a woman scorned but here the fury is caused not, as the men would like to believe, by what they did in the past but by what they're doing now.

Chad Dembskis production might make more inventive use of the space (which is, after all, the selling point) but its intelligent about character. And the room gives an ineradicable performance of its own, one that's enhanced every time someone opens the bathroom door or lifts the window to give us a peek at Queen Street. Every overheard sound contributes, and every surly bulb of neon. Come to Toronto if you want to shoot a film about Lansing. You could show it at both festivals.
