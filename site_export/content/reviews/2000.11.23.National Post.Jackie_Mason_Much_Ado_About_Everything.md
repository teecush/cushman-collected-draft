JACKIE MASON: MUCH ADO ABOUT EVERYTHING

Toronto Centre for the Arts

Jackie Mason has two phrases that he employs as props, to get him and us through two and a half hours of solo performance. They arent exactly catchphrases; a comic usually only repeats a particular catchphrase once in an evening, for recognition purposes. Mason uses his repeatedly. They are the nearest thing he has to breathing spaces. In other respects, he's unstoppable.

One phrase, applied whenever this most aggressively Jewish of mainstream comedians wishes to express disapproval of someone, is "Nazi bastard!" When I saw him on Broadway a few years ago he was applying it to any member of his audience who seemed reluctant to laugh; now, though, he seems to have mellowed and only uses it for people, or groups of people, who have aroused his displeasure in the outside world.

The other phrase is I don't want to pick on anyone (or, occasionally, "I dont mean to give offence"), invariably uttered immediately before he proceeds to do just that. One of the nicest things about Mason is that he isnt nice. Even at the end, just when he seems about to embarrass us by telling us what a lovely audience we've been and what a lovely city Toronto is, he salvages the moment by thanking us for catering to his egomania.

He does what he does extremely well. His delivery mixes English, Yiddish, and the spoken equivalent of scat-singing at machine-gun speed and with atomic intensity. It's just as well that hes sensitively miked. (On Broadway, the sound-mix was very harsh; every word came out as a bark, and I left at intermission. It wasnt that I didn't like him; it was just that I felt I'd had enough.)

His body is almost as ceaselessly active as his voice. he prowls the stage in a glossily tailored suit, with a stiff gait that proves surprisingly flexible, especially in the matter of -- literally -- bending over backwards.

He can sing and do impersonations; he offers a stunning Bing Crosby, especially on the low notes, and a rotten Frank Sinatra, for which he uses the same voice but different hand gestures. In speech, he expertly mimics Richard Nixon, Henry Kissinger, Jean Chretien (for local consumption) and Ted Kennedy, though he does them not with words but with assortments of guttural gibberish; Masons Kennedy converses in a rhythmic riff that seems to have been borrowed from Danny Kaye.

Most of the time, though, hes talking about social trends, especially those that come under the heading of style-snobbery. He lays into Starbucks and Evian and people who drive what they insist on calling sports-utility vehicles when it fact they're just trucks. (It looks like a truck, it drives like a truck -- I don't think he actually says it quacks like a truck.) Old people, he tells us, have a terrible time trying to climb into these vehicles, just as they have a terrible time trying to locate the cream and the cutlery in pretentious coffee-shops. Mason doesnt actually call them "old people"; he talks about elderly Jews.

His world is rigidly divided into Jews and Gentiles. They are good at different things. Gentiles like to stand in line; Jews push to the head of it, and sell refreshments to the schmucks standing behind them. Gentiles are good at basketball and coal mining; Jews arent. ("Can you imagine a yarmulke with a light on the front?") Jews are good accountants. (Even Gentiles don't want Gentile accountants.) Jews are good businessmen but also good consumers, especially when the desserts are free, at which point they magically forget that they're on diets.

And can you imagine such a thing as a Jewish farmer? (Actually I can, very easily, in Israel.) Mason never mentions the Middle East; when he discusses Jew-Gentile relations hes operating, for all his vaunted outspokenness, in a very safe and limited zone.

"They accuse me," he says, of dealing in stereotypes, but all Im saying is that people are different, right? What he's really saying is that large groups of people are different from other large groups, but that within those constituencies they behave with numbing consistency. What he's doing, of course, is taking anti- Semitic stereotypes, and, rather than denying them, glorying in them. ("Yes, we are smarter than you are. And were hypochondriacs.")

I doubt if this will lead to any increase in anti-Semitic feeling, except perhaps among Jews themselves, who may not like being lumped together like this, especially by an ex-rabbi. (Mason famously got out of the religion business when he found that all people liked about his sermons was the jokes. I think this was a little irresponsible of him. We could use some funny rabbis, God knows.)

This, of course, is the time to say that, of course, I have no time for political correctness. But then neither does anybody else I meet, so the battle against it hardly seems a medal-earning business. Were all so busy protesting our dislike of political correctness that there's nobody left over to be politically correct. (Yes. PC does exist, and it has its own spheres of influence, and it is a pain. But it doesn't present much of a threat to you or me or Jackie Mason.)

Masons role-reversal shtick is a bracing exercise for a time, but it does get to seem mechanical. So its a relief when, in the second act, he breaks away a little from his own well-cultivated patch, and talks about politics. He's funny when he turns to current events in Florida; presumably he's had his Bush-Gore routine all ready to go for some time now, but hes awe-inspiringly fluent in bringing it up to date.

He throws in some Canadian jokes, also presumably got up for the occasion. He does some great, though presumably not new, stuff on Bill Clinton and Monica Lewinsky, in which connection he plays the race card very well. (A Jewish girl is not interested in oral sex ... an oral surgeon, maybe.) I especially liked his ruminations on the presidential decision ("Its his field, he should know") that oral sex isn't sex. (All over the country, men were calling up hookers and asking for their money back.)

Mason's gibes at current pop-music sound merely, Im afraid, like standard Borscht-Belt disgruntlement (people have been making these jokes since Presley) but he seems genuinely appalled by Titanic: The Musical. Not the movie -- he loved the movie -- but the solemn-to-a- fault stage show, which he mysteriously characterizes as an empty- headed romp in which people did merry dances while the ship went down. (Apparently its all right to have the drownings serve as a backdrop to an invented romance between Kate Winslet and Leonardo DiCaprio.)

I bring this up because show business is one thing he seems really serious about: fittingly, because he is, at bottom, a traditional Catskills comic. Where he differs from the prototype is in not creating a biography for himself. Alan King, say, would spend his whole act telling us about his marriage, and whether or not he was telling the truth, he was establishing a character. Mason tells us almost nothing about himself.

It's also where he differs most from his current competition in town, Dame Edna Everage. Both pick on their audience, but Mason chooses just a couple of victims while Edna is more generous with her favours -- and more imaginative in the way she torments the recipients.

Most importantly, though, Edna is a character, and in the end, the one who's being most mercilessly satirized. Mason presents himself merely as a cranky but neutral commentator; he asks us to trust him.

What he does have in common with the Dame is the ability to fly. The best parts of his show come at the end of each half. He announces the intermission or the final curtain and then delays it, spinning out new ideas, and for the only time in the evening you dont want him to stop.

And the very end is charming. He tells us that we can leave but he doesnt see why he should. "So if you want to stick around and watch a little Jewish fellow doing nothing for a couple of hours ... " He does go, of course, and returns for the regulation bows, and gets the regulation ovation. The last thing Jackie Mason would ever be caught doing in front of an audience is nothing.
