700 SUNDAYS

Canon Theatre

- - -

Billy Crystal's father died when Billy himself was 15; 700 Sundays is Crystal's calculation of the number of precious days father and son were able to spend together in and around the family house in Long Beach, N.Y. Crystal Senior loved his family and they loved him back, but he had to stint on quality time since he worked two jobs. One was as manager of the legendary Commodore Record Store in Manhattan, a pioneer in the marketing and even the production of jazz. The other, stemming from the first and a source of great pride and delight for his son, was as an emcee at a jazz club. What with one thing and the other, a lot of great musicians passed through the Crystal home. Crystal has a story about his grandmother solicitously asking Louis Armstrong -- at a Jewish Seder, no less -- why he didn't just cough it up. I hope it's true.

Embroidered or not, the mixture of jazz and Judaism obviously made for a rich childhood, and they make for a fine, fun show. An autobiographical monologue that's a cross between a play and an especially elaborate stand-up comedy act, 700 Sundays is performed with a winning mixture of shrewdness, charm and bounce. There's a set representing the outside of the house; Crystal strolls on, in front of it, and starts talking about his family, as it was in -- mainly -- the 1950s. He begins with the family car, accidentally totalled on its first day by the local Mafia boss, whose extravagant offers of reparation Mr. and Mrs. Crystal proudly refused. Then it's on to the grandparents, a ripe collection, at least as depicted here, who spoke Yiddish ("a mixture of German and phlegm").

We get to see these people in projected photos, with which Crystal rightly calculates we will identify. ("We all have the same five relatives; they just jump from album to album.") Some he acts out, in a series of percussive vocal cameos. At one point, he combines the two modes by impersonating a home movie of an aggressive uncle who fancied himself king of the barbecue; read his lips, if you dare. His most sustained impersonation is of his Aunt Sheila, "who always told the truth," and whom we see -- in a Great Chronological Leap Forward -- on the phone in Florida, ceaselessly mobile despite having a titanium hip, telling a friend about her daughter's lesbian wedding, while occasionally deigning to throw a remark at her husband ("Leonard, get the car!"). This sequence is the funniest thing I have seen on a stage in ages, and a bit touching as well.

In parallel with this, we have the black musicians, who called young Crystal "Face" and seem to have adopted him as a kind of mascot. His mother's brother, the founder of the Commodore firm, was Milt Gabler, who among other things wrote lyrics for Duke Ellington tunes and produced some of Billie Holiday's most notable records -- including Strange Fruit, on which her regular label wouldn't take a chance. Billie, we learn, took Billy to his first movie. Worlds combine when the family take their first and only holiday together, in the Catskills, and Billy falls in love with the borscht-belt comics. His future is decided -- by him anyway -- and he starts to amass a repertoire. Act One ends with him doing a terrific mime to Spike Jones' You Always Hurt the One You Love - a commentary on a parody.

Act Two goes more for the heartstrings than the funny-bone. Billy's father, already reduced by the demise of the store, dies of a heart attack: ironically, on the day on which his son, moping over a lost girlfriend, had shouted at him for the first and only time. (I trust that this also is true. It's not the kind of thing anybody should make up, and Crystal seems trustworthy). Billy's mother promises him that, broke though they are, he will go to college, like his two brothers before him, and she works heroically to make it happen. It's none of my business, I suppose, but I wondered why his Uncle Miltie, by now a big wheel in the music business, couldn't have helped out.

The story sags a bit as Billy stumbles into puberty, with his genitals developing not only a mind but, in his telling, a voice of their own. It's one joke, prolonged. There is also more than seems necessary about his career on the school basketball team. He speaks touchingly, but at too much length, about the void -- in his heart, in his mother's heart, in the house -- left by their bereavement. This can't be argued with, and of course we identify. But there remains a problem about getting up in front of an audience and telling them, without the mediation of a playwright or of other actors, about your own feelings, especially if the feelings are a credit to you. Elaine Stritch at Liberty, the other major Broadway confessional of recent years, got around this by concentrating on adulthood and dwelling on the dark side, while also throwing in more actual performance than Crystal does -- which, considering that we go to see these people because they're performers, was a good thing. Neil Simon, in Brighton Beach Memoirs, trod much the same social and psychological territory as Crystal. He made a full-scale play of it, which I think is more satisfying, if only because it puts paid to worries about the boundaries between fact and fiction.

Crystal also deals with his mother's (much later) death, which left him, as he says, "an orphan at 53." He is quick to acknowledge that this, or something like it, happens to everybody; but, charming though that is, it doesn't quite dispose of the matter. He is, after all, getting paid and applauded for telling us how much he loves his parents; and of course he wouldn't get to do that if he weren't already a star. Whether that disqualifies him from being a worthy audience surrogate, I'm honestly not sure. But he's appealing, and immensely talented, and projects decency without priggishness, which is an accomplishment. Des McAnuff's production, apart from the trifling matter of being 30 minutes too long -- Crystal does three hours, all on his own --puts him in a perfect light.
