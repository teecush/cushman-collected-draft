My favourite moment in The Sopranos -- many other peoples too, I hear -- comes during Episode 5. Tony Soprano, the middling gang boss from New Jersey, turns on his pre-collegiate daughter, who has just plucked up the courage to ask him if he is in the Mafia. He doesnt merely deny it -- he explodes, in an ecstasy of defensiveness that deliriously mixes straight street talk and self-righteous mediaspeak: "That's total crap ... That's a stereotype, and its offensive. Im in the waste management business ... There is no Mafia."

Well, the last sentence is a conscious lie , though there may be a tiny portion of his brain that can believe it. The bit about waste- management is true, to the extent that that's the front Tony uses for his extortion activities. The rest he, in a way, believes; he doesn't want to think of himself as a gangster. Certainly not of the kind that fills the public imagination. He doesnt feel part of the world that he saw depicted in The Godfather and Goodfellas; apart from anything else, he knows its too glamorous. Besides, what else is a conscientious father supposed to tell his daughter? His buddies and henchmen have the same problem with inquisitive offspring. It's tough, one of them laments, "raising a kid in the information age."

Writers can generally be judged by the quality of their jokes, and those in The Sopranos are about the best to be heard in any shows currently on television, including the avowedly comic ones. To an extent, the style of the humour goes back to Damon Runyon -- tough guys who talk fancy -- but it rings true here, especially in the grainy Italian-American accent. It's comedy of character, epitomized by Tonys mother, resentfully confined to a nursing home and exclaiming, when she hears that her son is seeing a psychiatrist, Hes gone to complain about his mother.

The Sopranos is both the best crime drama and the best family drama to come out of American television since, well, possibly ever, and the Mafia is of course the perfect site on which to combine the two genres.

On the one hand, we have Tony and his wife, coping with their sullen teenage daughter and unreadable schoolboy son.("Anthony Junior, just because you're suspended, it doesn't mean you can stay in bed all day.") Facing up to a critical child psychiatrist, Mr. and Mrs. Soprano seem oddly like a more intelligent Homer and a hipper Marge, anxiously inquiring about Bart; they have their differences, but their kids come first -- though they dont, of course, have the Simpsons financial problems.

On the other, we have the businessman who savagely beats his debtors and personally garrottes a suspected informer; the clan chieftain who fears he can never measure up to his dead father, can barely control his ambitious nephew and has handed over a dangerous amount of power to the uncle who played ball with him when he was a kid; the benefactor who thoughtfully burns down a restaurant so that his friend the owner won't be embarrassed by having a murder committed there. It's no wonder that the contradictions send Tony to a shrink.

The scenes one longs for in every episode are the consultations in which James Gandolfinis wondrous Tony sits hunched and fidgeting while Lorraine Braccos Dr. Melfi, her chin cupped in her hands, surveys him with enormous sympathy and fathomless stupidity. When we see her outside her office, she looks completely different (and oddly less glamorous, even when dressed up for a date) but in her own domain she is supreme, and it's no wonder that Tony falls for her.

I would have liked to see her get an Emmy -- as Edie Falco, who plays Tony's wife, Carmela, did last year. This time, however, The Sopranos was shut out even more comprehensively than before. Gandolfini got his due, but there was nothing for David Chase, the shows writer, creator, executive producer and occasional director, or for anyone else.

It was The West Wing that cleaned up, and it was notable that its producer began his acceptance speech for best drama by paying tribute to The Sopranos. Its conventional for the winner to pay tribute to the losers in bulk, but to single one of them out like that is unheard of; one detected the sound of fences being mended. (Though sentiment was not enough to secure a deserved award for the late Nancy Marchand, who played Tony's monster mother, a superb character who reminded me, among other things, of a line of Kaye Ballard's: You know the thing about Italian families -- theyre so Jewish. When even death cant sway the voters, then the ranks have really closed.) The West Wing's triumph may have owed something to its being the new show on the block, but then so, last year, was The Sopranos.

I suspect that a show about the White House -- with everybody behaving comparatively decently -- seems somehow more literate, more civilized, more in line with what "quality TV" is supposed to be. More, come to think of it, British, which remains a yardstick for American TV when it wants to rake in prestige as well as money. The New York Times has published a celebratory book on The Sopranos, including an introductory essay in which their critic Stephen Holden goes looking for exalted transatlantic comparisons. Unfortunately, he goes looking in the direction of Masterpiece Theatre. He claims The Sopranos to be superior in its richly textured comic realism, its "complexity and truthfulness" to Upstairs Downstairs, Brideshead Revisited and I Claudius. Of course it is; those genially escapist shows aren't even in the running.

If you want British exemplars for The Sopranos, go to the borderline crime series: to Cracker, whose protagonist is Tony Sopranos only serious rival for richness and breadth; or to Between the Lines (Inside the Line over here), that superbly exciting account of cops investigating cops; or Alan Bleasdales insanely ambitious masterpiece GBH. They are the shows with the real comic and dramatic edge, the ones that look at society from the underside, which is generally the most illuminating way of looking at it. The Sopranos surpasses them visually (American TV being a film culture and British TV a theatrical one) and it also goes them one better in eliminating the good guys, even the most compromised ones. Cops in The Sopranos are conspicuous by their absence. Tony and his various families have to work out their own damnation.

The West Wing also had the electoral advantage of being a major network production. The Sopranos was confined to HBO in its home country and to The Movie Network here; now we get to see it on CTV, without commercials and in versions edited by the creators themselves -- edited, we are assured, for time, not morality. (There is less nudity in the series than you may have heard, but the profanity and the violence are as advertised.)

Watch it from the start, because the pilot, directed by Chase himself, is a minor classic; with superb economy, it sets up all the characters, all the conflicts.

Also all the authentic locations: The Sopranos is one American series that got shot without disrupting Canadian traffic. (I've shot in Toronto, said Chase, "it isn't New Jersey."). After that, you should be hooked; like the British cop shows Ive cited, The Sopranos presents episodes each of which stand in their own right but whose main fascination is the way in which they contribute to an over-arching story.

The first instalment, by the way, gives us Tony squaring up to Dr. Melfi (I had a semester and a half in college -- I know Freud) and lamenting the loss of old-style values as exemplified by mobsters who squeal rather than go to the pen ("Whatever happened to the old respect for the penal experience?")

Writing like that, delivered with Gandolfinis particular brand of shamefaced bravura, you don't too often get.
