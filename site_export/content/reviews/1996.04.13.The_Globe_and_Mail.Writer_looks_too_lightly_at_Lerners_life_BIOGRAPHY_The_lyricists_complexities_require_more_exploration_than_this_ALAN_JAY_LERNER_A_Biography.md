Writer looks too lightly at Lerner's life BIOGRAPHY / The lyricist's complexities require more exploration than this ALAN JAY LERNER: A Biography

By Edward Jablonski Henry Holt, 345 pages, $49

Saturday, April 13, 1996

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Review by ROBERT CUSHMAN 'I COULDN'T write My Fair Lady now" said Alan Jay Lerner some time in the 1970s, "because I don't feel My Fair Lady now." Along with the rest of the world he had grown sadder, if not necessarily wiser. The trouble was that writing My Fair Lady (1956) was what he did best. He never equalled its success.

Neither, admittedly, did anybody else. Some musicals, in these inflationary times, have run longer and made more money and a few have enjoyed comparable critical esteem, but none - not even A Chorus Line - have done both. No subsequent musical has acquired the same cultural prestige or been so unarguably the show of its time, the one you simply had to see. It was the capstone of Broadway's great age of confidence - the era of the well-made narrative musical that was ushered in by Rodgers and Hammerstein's Oklahoma] in 1943.

Alan Lerner came in with that era. He was born into a comfortable if troubled household; his father was the masterful, philandering founder of a department store. He grew up in New York, seeing all the shows; went to Harvard; drifted into advertising and radio writing; joined the Lambs Club, a hangout for theatricals, where he literally bumped into Frederick Loewe, a composer looking for a lyricist. Their first show together, What's Up, opened the same year as Oklahoma] and was a thorough flop. Two years later they came up with The Day Before Spring, a success d'estime ("a success that runs out of steam") and two years after that with Brigadoon, a major hit and a charter member of the golden-age repertoire. After that there was Paint Your Wagon, which wasn't really a great success but has somehow maintained the aura of one, and then came My Fair Lady. That had a virtual, and triumphant, cinematic spinoff in Gigi, and Lerner and Loewe ended their stage collaboration with Camelot, a troubled show but an enduring one. It sounds like velvet.

It wasn't, of course. Lerner's private life was a romantic shambles. It's difficult to see how else you describe the existence of a man who married eight times. One of his seven divorces, from a predatory-sounding French lady, was quite spectacularly messy, gossip-page fodder for months. His biographer, Edward Jablonski, tries hard to be urbane about it but the cracks show. Jablonski is an experienced and knowledgable historian of musical theatre, who has written meticulously and respectfully on the Gershwins and Harold Arlen. He finds it hard to say a word against any of his subjects, so his books tend to be informative but bland. I am not suggesting that Lerner deserves a hatchet-job, but his complexities require more exploration than Jablonski can supply.

Actually, he has already had a hatchet-job. Gene Lees's Inventing Champagne, a joint biography of Lerner and Loewe published a few years ago, professed admiration for its subject but offered precious little of it. I never thought I would miss that book, but I have to admit that Lees is a better - or at least a more detailed - guide to Lerner's problems with debts, drugs, and women than the more circumspect Jablonski. Still, at least Jablonski, unlike Lees, does appear to have seen and studied all the shows. He doesn't go into them in much depth but he does tell us something, especially about the flops.

There were plenty of these. After Camelot, Loewe, an older and less driven man, took early retirement but Lerner persevered. He had already taken time out to work with Kurt Weill on Love Life (another success d'estime) and with the fine composer Burton Lane on the Fred Astaire movie Royal Wedding: a negligible film with a fine score. In 1965 he reunited with Lane for One a Clear Day You Can See Forever, which didn't really work; and that was as good as it got. Lolita My Love (1976), a foolhardy attempt to musicalize the Nabokov novel, closed out of town. Dance a Little Closer (1983) made it to New York but only for one night. Both had excellent lyrics.

Lerner, with the clout of his earlier shows, was the prime mover in most of his later ones. He was intellectually ambitious, highly intelligent, politically aware. (He was also, in my experience of him, shy, modest and courteous; and his last marriage, to the charming English actress Liz Robertson, looked happy.) He wanted to tackle new and uncomortable subjects, but his heart and the nature of his craftsmanship were with the older idioms; he was marooned between the worlds of Oscar Hammerstein and Stephen Sondheim. Like Hammerstein he was a librettist as well as a lyricist; like Sondheim he rarely had a pop hit.

Jablonski quotes a remark of Ira Gershwin's: "Alan, you don't understand trick rhymes." It's a shame he doesn't pursue this, because Lerner certainly tried for trick rhymes, rather in the whimsical word- inventing style of Gershwin's friend and contemporary E. Y. Harburg, another who mixed fantasy and social consciousness. The difference may just be a matter of generations: Wordplay came more easily to the writers of the thirties than to those of the fifties. Harburg took an almost messianic attitude towards romance; so did Lerner. In Brigadoon, love conquers literally everything. This is a sticky message to get across, and it works best in My Fair Lady, where the musical pits the cynicism of Shaw's original play against the rich operetta tones of Loewe's music. Shaw denied that Professor Higgins married Eliza Doolittle; audiences have always known better and My Fair Lady makes our fantasy flesh. I suspect that Lerner went on feeling My Fair Lady longer than he admitted. It was his good fortune, and ours, that in 1956 everybody else, on both sides of the proscenium, felt it too. NOTA BENE

From Alan Jay Lerner: At six-thirty the next morning, (Lerner) was in his chair again, pad before him and the aroma of coffee suffusing the room; again nothing. This continued for a week. By the second week he was panicky, and the third was no better. What especially alarmed him was the fact that he knew what he wanted to say but could not find the words to say it. By the fourth week he had lost eight pounds and 'become a basket case.' Distraught, almost frantic, he realized he needed help and sought out the analyst who had helped him through his first divorce, Dr. Bela Mittelmann. . . .

Enouched, Lerner tried to describe his anxieties, with scant enlightenment as to the cause of his mental inertia. The doctor observed, 'You write as if your life depended on every line.' 'It does.' "
