Ihad the same knee-jerk reaction as everyone else to the announcement that the Stratford Festival would be producing Youre a Good Man, Charlie Brown: Why was Stratford, in a season already heavily freighted with musicals, scheduling a trifling piece thats basically a children's show? That wasn't fair of me: As originally written, Charlie Brown is, at the very least, a better musical than some Stratford has selected. (Its better, for example, than 42nd Street.) And, like the Peanuts strip on which its based, it wasn't principally intended for children, though it obviously, like other superior entertainment, from Alice in Wonderland to The Simpsons, has crossover appeal.

The trouble is--one of the troubles is -- the show Stratford is presenting isn't the original, which was a small, charming piece with a small, charming score by Clark Gesner; that was adulterated (not perhaps the mot juste) some years ago with, presumably, the consent of Gesner himself who was alive then but isnt now. Andrew Lippa wrote some new songs, which arent up to the standard of the old ones and, worse yet, caused some of the old ones to be eliminated. I especially miss the breezily cynical Little Known Facts ( "I know now that you can't trust a daisy / They tell, every chance they get.") The book, also by Gesner, has been interfered with as well. I may be imagining that it used to include a sketch--a better term than scene since the show is more a revue than a narrative--about Lucy kicking Charlie Brown's football away; but Im positive that there used to be one about Linus and the Great Pumpkin. Why lose them?

Gesners original score gets slurpy on its closing number Happiness, but that's forgivable given such a delight as the ensemble Book Report, with its five viewpoints on Peter Rabbit, culminating in Charlie Brown's own shout out to procrastinators everywhere: I work best under pressure / And therell be lots of pressure / If I wait till tomorrow. That, of course, is a prime example of the shows fidelity to the spirit of Charles M. Schulz; it presents varieties of adult angst and projects them on to a group of six-year-olds (and a dog).

It's a dark show masquerading as bland. It's also undeniably cute, but that element needs to be kept in check. Stratford, though, at both the programming and production levels, has bought into the idea that its all for the kids, and it goes to exhausting lengths to ingratiate itself with them, or maybe just to make itself feel youthful. Donna Feore, directing and choreographing, injects a lot of hip hop. Michael Gianfrancescos set design mainly consists of garish screen images, incorporating speech bubbles, just to remind us of the show's cartoon origins. Snoopy is also heavily into video--he's a gaming addict. This may make 21st century youngsters feel at home; it may also make them feel condescended to.

The cast is mostly very good, especially Ken James Stewart, whos Charlie Brown to the hopeful heartbreaking life; his monologue both hoping and fearing to be noticed by the little red-haired girl is the best thing in the show (and, not coincidentally, the quietest). Kevin Yees Linus is charming, likewise Andrew Broderick's Schroeder, and Erica Peck is a powerhouse Lucy. Amy Wallis works unbecomingly hard to provide an equally powerhouse Sally, which the show doesn't need; blame the new authorized version that put her in and put Peppermint Patty out. As Snoopy, Stephen Patterson has razzmatazz to spare, and keeps sharing it with the humans, though hes meant to be laid-back and aloof, except when hungry. His big number Suppertime is supposed to be a surprise, and being so can work very simply; this production, and this performance, have already upped the ante so far that they have no option but to overkill us some more. Left to his own devices, which might not include descending an illuminated staircase and having the other actors butt in as chorus, this Snoopy would probably be very effective.

Musical massacre on a larger scale comes in The Pirates of Penzance, Stratfords return to Gilbert and Sullivan. Ethan MacSweeney's production isn't without its pleasures; in a staging that takes us into the sea as well as beside it, I greatly enjoyed the surprise appearance of one character in a frogmans suit. Im also a sucker for elaborate scenes that take us backstage at a Victorian theatre before reversing perspective for the show proper, though in this case I couldn't see the ultimate point. What sinks this particular pirate ship--most surprisingly from a director who has professed his delight at finding how funny the piece is--is how hard it is to make out most of the words. C. David Johnson's difficulties with rhythm and enunciation may have been peculiar to the first night; all the same, if you splutter your way through Major General Stanleys major general patter song, you arent meeting the job description. Insult gets added to injury in an interpolated reprise commemorating Stratford's 60th year; this kind of self-celebration is faintly embarrassing anyway, and the notion that "gang of four" rhymes with Bangalore amounts, in a show with W.S. Gilbert's name on its cover, to a diabolical liberty.

Elsewhere, over-production runs riot, especially in an overextended fight scene that effectively kills the scripted ending. On the way, even such a traditionally infallible number as the police sergeants lament about his lot not being a happy one gets ruined, so tricked out with Scottish-accented-coppers in kilts that lift to reveal Union Jack underpants, that even the great Steve Ross cant save it. Sean Arbuckle plays the Pirate King on the same lines of accident-prone flamboyance that made Kevin Kline a Broadway star some years back; by now, this role could use less Errol Flynn and Johnny Depp and more Robert Newton.

There are some compensations: Gabrielle Jones makes a good, clean, efficient job of Ruth, the requisite G&S heavy lady. Above all, and blessedly, there is Kyle Blair who sings like an angel, moves like a demon, and makes of Frederic, the pirate apprentice and subtitular "slave of duty," a delightfully dedicated and demented idealist. Full-bloodedly tongue in cheek, this is likely a definitive performance. Amy Wallis, more happily cast here than in Charlie Brown, meets him on his vocal heights as a self-parodying kind of coloratura. Not that I'm convinced there's any other kind. Sullivan's taste for operatic parody is well-known. In Pirates, it seems to account for about 80% of the score.
