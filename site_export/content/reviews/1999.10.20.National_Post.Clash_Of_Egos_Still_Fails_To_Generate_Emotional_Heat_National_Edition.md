IT'S ALL TRUE

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre

Jason Sherman's It's All True, back for its second Toronto run this year, remains a sizeable and very tasty slice of theatre. There has been some rewriting and re-casting, and both text and performance have sharpened and deepened. The dialogue has great stage presence -- it is weighty without being heavy -- and the construction is both graceful and ingenious. The show is more worth seeing than almost anything else in town. It is still, however, cold at the core.

This is a play set in a theatre and it uses the whole space. Richard Rose's production, in a spare and evocative set by Charlotte Dean, sits even better at Buddies in Bad Times than it did at the Tarragon; it makes especially effective use of a catwalk on one side of the auditorium, backed by a corrugated wall. This is where characters gather in twos and threes to confer and complain and romance and reminisce.

The time is 1937. We are in the throes of Roosevelt's Federal Theatre Project. The Cradle Will Rock, a "labour opera" written and composed by the unknown Marc Blitzstein and directed by the already famous 22-year-old Orson Welles, is about to open in New York when the government slaps a ban on it. Unexpectedly the actors' and musicians' unions, with their own territories to defend, play along with the authorities. "This show," protests Blitzstein, "is for unions." "But your union," says his producer silkily, "is not."

What happened next is theatrical legend. With their own theatre padlocked, the company marched downtown to another. Forbidden to appear on stage, the actors sang their parts from the house; denied musicians, Blitzstein played the whole score at an onstage piano. Welles' elaborate production, of course, went by the board, and the historical consensus has been good riddance. Whenever the show is revived (it's done with surprising frequency; I've seen it in New York, London and Toronto) it's staged as an approximation of that original impromptu spartan performance. I confess to an unregenerate wish to see it done with all of Welles' planned spectacular trappings and with a full orchestra.

Sherman's play shows us Welles and Blitzstein snapping continually over what one's showmanship is doing either to or for the other's pure political intentions. It is set on the crisis day, with flashbacks to earlier moments of planning, rehearsal and personal and political intrigue. One especially clever sequence juxtaposes the rehearsal of a key scene with instant fragments of the participants' private lives, called to mind by individual words and phrases, but it is clever rather than revealing. The play no longer presents its protagonists so insistently as men haunted by dead women (Welles' mother, Blitzstein's wife), but it still doesn't get very far with the relationship between them. There is Welles sure enough, the insomniac egomaniac, and there is Blitzstein, the nervous idealist, and they are both insecure (but then who isn't?) and they can both compromise despite their protestations. But the mere fact that they collaborated on a traumatic project doesn't make them illuminate one another.

What now stands out in the sharpest relief is the affair between two of Cradle's actors. There is Howard da Silva, who played the show's proletarian hero and is himself a glibly passionate social activist; and there is Olive Stanton, a desperate mother of three plucked from obscurity to play a highly symbolic prostitute, whom Howard seduces and drops when Welles and Blitzstein persuade him that it will be good for her performance and hence for the show. Martin Julien plays Howard with scrupulous attention to his mixed motivations, and Melody Johnson's Olive is a brilliant mixture of the downtrodden and the determined, pinched and scared but nobody's pushover.

There are two newcomers in the cast: Joseph Ziegler, in the most fluently detailed performance in the show, brings Blitzstein to life as a man in a perpetual state of discomfort with himself and everybody else; Vickie Papavs doubles as Welles' embittered wife and his stage manager, Jean Rosenthal, holding the show together as firmly and unobtrusively as the original must have done.

Victor Ertmanis' Welles still strikes me as implausibly charmless and sneering, but he is certainly forceful; and as John Houseman -- his colleague, producer and whipping boy -- Richard Binsley has grown considerably, from a spruce cartoon to a figure of bruised dignity. At one point, Welles tells him that he hopes he never takes to acting -- an elegant moment since that is just what Houseman, at the end of his life, successfully did.

Last time round I sniped at the absence of Blitzstein's actual music; unfairly, since the rights had been taken by Tim Robbins' imminent (and eagerly awaited) Cradle Will Rock movie. It's still a loss, though, with serious repercussions. If you want to show a historical instance of life imitating art, you break the rules if you manufacture the art yourself; and it's too tempting to lay on ready-made ironies. The play cheats; it pretends that Cradle is largely about the relationship between the Howard and Olive characters, whereas in fact they barely collide. Sherman has Blitzstein shouting, almost literally, "what about the workers?" when Welles supposedly cuts them from the last scene. But in the score as we have it they are only a rumbling offstage presence, and more powerful for it. Massed crowd scenes would have been more Welles' style than Blitzstein's anyway. The play seems willing to throw anything at Orson.

This may explain why the last scene, though atmospheric, is surprisingly unmoving. Blitzstein strikes up his one-man band; Olive, who has backed out because she cannot afford to be fired, nevertheless stands up and sings her opening lines. We aren't told why she changed her mind. Presumably she just felt that the show had to go on. The same feeling presumably unites all these rampaging egos. Sentimentalist that I am, I would go along with that. I just don't believe the play does.
