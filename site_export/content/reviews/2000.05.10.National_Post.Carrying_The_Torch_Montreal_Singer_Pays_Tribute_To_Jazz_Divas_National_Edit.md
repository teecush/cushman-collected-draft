DARK DIVAS

Ranee Lee (Justin Time, JUST 132/3-2)

I have trouble with the word "diva," or at least with its connotations. It even bothers me on its home ground of opera, where it suggests soprano-worshippers devoutly savouring the finer points of their favourites' delivery -- literally comparing notes -- while displaying minimal interest in the piece itself.

Now, of course, jazz, pop and musicals have their divas, too, though they paint in broader strokes. If your classical diva- fancier listens for arcane detail, his non-classical equivalent reserves his adoration for rampant histrionics.

In both worlds, diva-status is determined less by what the performers do than by what their audiences do. Attending a show becomes a matter of counting off the minutes before attempting a seemingly impossible physical feat, that of prostrating yourself in a standing ovation. Diva worship is fan worship raised to a higher masochistic (and narcissistic) power.

It also affects other vocalists, generally younger ones -- post- divas -- who naturally admire their predecessors. The urge to pay explicit homage -- and also perhaps the lure of alliteration -- has impelled Ranee Lee, a highly skilled and very versatile Montreal jazz chanteuse, to put together a double CD called Dark Divas on which she does songs associated with seven famous performers who happened to be both black and female: Josephine Baker, Billie Holiday, Lena Horne (the only one still living), Pearl Bailey, Dinah Washington, Sarah Vaughan and Ella Fitzgerald. (What, no Carmen McRae?)

It's a selection that defines its own territory: the mid-20th- century musical mainstream, where jazz interacted with pop, while receiving a few infusions from old vaudeville and current Broadway.

It stops short of the classic blues at one end (so, no Bessie Smith) and of soul and the avant-garde at the other (so, no Aretha Franklin or Betty Carter) and bypasses the more rarefied world of cabaret (so, no Mabel Mercer).

The real missing figure, though, is Ethel Waters, surely the mother of them all and the only one who could do jazz and theatre with equal authority.

Every subsequent singer, male or female, black or white, is in her debt. I would say she deserves a place here more than Josephine Baker, who was more of a dancer than a singer, and whose real importance is as a symbol: the beautiful black American who had to emigrate to Paris on order to get personal and artistic respect.

That seems to be why she's here. Lee's CD is the pilot for a stage show (a cast album before the fact) and the finished product will apparently include considerable biographical information about its heroines.

It certainly needs a context. On the disc, we get only a generalized snippet of description before each section. It's mostly hackneyed stuff, though it is refreshing to be told Billie Holiday was great "not because of the dreadful things that happened to her, but in spite of them."

Lee never presents these women as victims: a welcome change from the only comparable show I've seen -- A Star Is Torn, by the Australian singer Robyn Archer -- which presented them as nothing but.

There's always a problem when a good singer devotes a whole evening to pretending to be another singer -- what is she trying to prove? -- and it only multiplies when she presents a group of them. Is she trying to impersonate them? The usual answer is, no; the truthful answer would be, sometimes; or, where possible.

Tonally, Lee does a very accurate Dinah Washington and a fair Sarah Vaughan. (Considering Vaughan's scary range, that's an accomplishment.) Otherwise, she contents herself with reproducing her subjects' phrasing. I don't quite see the point of reproducing, note-for-note, Ella Fitzgerald's scat extravaganza on Lady Be Good, but Lee has certainly got it down pat.

There is also an issue with repertoire. A lot of the time, after all, these singers were singing the same songs, and Fitzgerald was the repertory singer par excellence. There seems no particular reason for Ill Wind to turn up under Holiday, or One for My Baby under Lena Horne. They never owned those songs, and Lee's versions don't sound much like either of them. (Baby, in fact, summons the immovable ghost of Sinatra, while her Mack the Knife is less like Ella's than like Bobby Darin's.)

What we get, in fact, a lot of the time, is just Ranee Lee singing standards. But isn't that what she'd be doing anyway?

Sometimes, the selections belie their introductions. She tells us Horne's club act reflected her independent spirit --which may have been true when she proclaimed The Lady Is a Tramp, but not surely when she torched Stormy Weather, her signature song. (Surprisingly, there is nothing here from her civil-rights repertoire, for instance The Eagle and Me.)

Lee herself is a performer of great authority and she rides every one of these songs with unshakable confidence, backed by an excellent small band that sometimes sounds big. Her recording career started with a live album that was showbiz jazz at its worst, but she has long outgrown it. Indeed, the vaudeville touches in her work are now positively enriching. So the best section here is the one devoted to Pearl Bailey, a somewhat neglected figure who I am delighted to see being canonized, and whose personal (and largely unfamiliar) repertoire allows Lee to swing and be funny at the same time, honouring the original but on her own terms.

Vaughan, though, and even more Fitzgerald, were never remotely dramatic or autobiographical singers and there's nothing Lee can tell us about them except they had lovely voices -- though I do honour her for including my favourite Vaughan song, If You Could See Me Now.

She ends with one of my unfavourite songs, Yesterday When I Was Young, sung -- or so I assume -- in her own person. This is a typically monotonous French tune with a typically melodramatic English lyric. It also doesn't make any sense. It's a song about self-absorption: the singer lacerates herself for being concerned with herself and "no one else at all."

But Lee has just spent an entire double album talking about other people, and if the lyric is meant to reflect on them then it's ungracious and, if on her own showing, untrue. The one characteristic that unites all the women she honours is emotional generosity, reflected in music.

I suspect she was influenced by one line about being "left alone on stage to end the play" and by the opportunity the thing gives her for passion-tearing. This she certainly takes: divadom descends with a vengeance.

The last song isn't worthy of her, and if any of her subjects ever sang it (none of them, mercifully, was a sobber) then they were slumming.

There's some very canned-sounding applause sprinkled throughout the discs, but she can probably count on the real thing when she does the material on stage.
