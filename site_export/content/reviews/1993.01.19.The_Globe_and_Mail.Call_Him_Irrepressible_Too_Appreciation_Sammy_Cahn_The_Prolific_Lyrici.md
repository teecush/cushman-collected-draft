Call him irrepressible, too APPRECIATION " Sammy Cahn, the prolific lyricist who died Friday was not only renowned as one of the fastest writers in the business but also the industry's pet parodist who happily burlesqued his own lyrics

Tuesday, January 19, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail IN his later years Sammy Cahn, the extraordinarily prolific lyricist who died on Friday, developed a second career as a performer. He would begin his stage act by singing one of his most successful songs, Call Me Irresponsible. He would then point out that the lyrics employed several five-syllable words, and that this was remarkable as he came from a two- syllable neighbourhood.

He belonged to the tradition of New York Jewish songwriters who made (and often changed) their names, doing triumphant battle with the English language. Unlike such predecessors as Ira Gershwin or Yip Harburg, Cahn kept his sophistication under wraps. He didn't flaunt his rhymes, though he was as fanatical as any of his peers about getting them exact and about matching speech rhythms to musical rhythms. His words always fell naturally, which is how they seem to have come to him. He was renowned as one of the fastest writers in the business.

Rhythm Is Our Business was actually the title of his first successful song, and it became the theme tune of the Jimmie Lunceford band. That was in 1935; Cahn arrived just in time for the swing era, and he remained a pop writer, thinking in terms of songs rather than scores; in a 50-year career he wrote only five Broadway shows, one of them (the 1947 High Button Shoes) a success. Jule Styne, his collaborator on that and on much else, thought Cahn's facility undid him as a theatre writer; he didn't have the patience or the inclination to dig into character. He was a pragmatist, knocking out his lyrics on a typewriter. Which came first, the words or the music? First came the phone-call.

He did create two characters. One was his own; his TV and theatre retrospectives of his own material evoked the glory days of Hollywood and Tin Pan Alley. ("Shows you how long ago that was", he would say every time some once-famous name of the 30s, 40s or 50s surfaced in his reminiscence.) He was a natural clown, and if he couldn't sing he could certainly phrase. He punched out syllables with fastidious urgency, as if determined that none of his words should escape our notice. He sang as he talked, with a quiet voice and a loud personality. The show was the natural climax of his career as a hustler of his own wares. Frank Sinatra, in a tribute one likes to think was unghosted, wrote affectionately of being "subjected to his song demonstrations, complete with thin top notes and physical gyrations . . . Bashful, he's not". He was happy, though, to give colleagues their due, and immensely proud of his position as President of the Songwriters Hall of Fame. My own longest meeting with him came when I was en route to the stage version of 42nd Street. "I envy you the fun you're going to have" he said with scratch-voiced fervour, and repeated the line, as he often did to make a point. Maybe that was his lyricist's instinct.

His other creation was Sinatra; at least he helped. From the 40s on - first with Styne as composer, then with Jimmy Van Heusen - he wrote most of Sinatra's film songs and special material, defining him first as the devoted bashful romantic (Time After Time, I Fall in Love Too Easily), then as the high-loving playboy (Come Fly with Me, The Tender Trap) subject to moods of benign or lacerating introspection (Only the Lonely, All My Tomorrows, September of My Years.) Cahn's lyrics could be euphoric or gentle, rarely sanctimonious or sentimental. He could strike a nerve. In a world full of songs about first love, The Second Time Around was an inspiration. Verses - the introductions that set up a song's main strain - are an increasingly neglected form, but he handled them with great finesse. He was also the industry's pet parodist, happily burlesquing his own lyrics ("This is my first affair, so what goes where?") or other people's ("You made me love you, you woke me up to do it.") Call him irrepressible.

Robert Cushman hosts Songbook on CBC Stereo and teaches Aesthetics and Criticism at York University.
