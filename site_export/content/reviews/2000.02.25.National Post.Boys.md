BOYS

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

Theatre Direct, the young people's theatre company who last year gave us the excellent Waiting for Lewis, are now presenting Buncha' Young Artists Having Their Say: a rather self-conscious label for a short, rotating festival more soberly announced as "four plays by young writers speaking to young adults." If the first one is any guide, they will be speaking to others as well.

Boys is a one-person play for three voices, or a three-character play for one actor. Its author and performer is Paul Dunn, who was seen last season at Stratford as the youngest of the four faces of Glenn Gould. One of his aims here seems to be to explore the pressures exerted on males of his own generation as they try to discover what it is to be a man. Another is to provide himself with a whacking good acting vehicle. He succeeds on both counts.

There are three boys in Boys: Tambourine Boy, Ham and Cheese Boy and Army Slut Boy. Tambourine Boy, whose friends, if he had any, would call him Jeff, is a busker with a very individual shtick; he hops around while banging out rhythms on a tambourine, in time to a soundtrack of tunes playing in his own head. The tunes are all by Abba and he describes them, with approval, as the work of Satan: his bulwark against a universe that otherwise seems to have rejected him. He's even convinced that one member of the group is his mother. (We in the audience do get to hear snatches of the music. They confirmed me in my long-held detestation of Abba and all their works, though I think I would stop short of calling them Satanic. Further thoughts on this subject had better await the coming of Mamma Mia.)

Ham and Cheese Boy, whose given name is Mike, works on an assembly line slapping together the sandwiches that his title suggests. His girlfriend resents the dead-endedness of his job and ditches him; he occupies himself piecing together a large jigsaw puzzle and deeply resents it if any of his friends butt in. He would rather get it wrong without their help than right with it. Finally there is Army Slut Boy, a.k.a Andy, a student whose campus psychiatrist wants him first to acknowledge his homosexuality, and second to go out and get laid. He finds love at a school dance, an experience he memorably recalls as being "finally in on what the rest of the world has been doing": a description that crosses all sexual boundaries. The object of his affection is about to enlist, and while Andy maintains that it's the guy himself to whom he is attracted, his shrink is pretty sure that it's the dog-tags; Andy, she insists, has a military fetish. It turns out to be a bit of both.

The play is composed of alternating monologues, the author-actor stepping nimbly, under Rosemary Rowe's firm direction, from one character to another with a change of sweater, apron or spectacles. For Andy's consultations with his lady-doctor (a sort of prose rerun of Gee Officer Krupke) he effects instant changes of identity by sliding his glasses up and down his nose. The boys themselves collide occasionally. Ham and Cheese, irritated by Tambourine's routine, insults and is mildly assaulted by him. Army Slut, feeling euphoric, gives Tambourine a large tip, which he spends on one of Ham and Cheese's ex-sandwiches. (Mike has just been fired, and there's no filling.) Ham and Cheese and Army Slut both turn up, though for different reasons, at a military recruitment centre, this being the only point at which the parallel impersonations become momentarily confusing.

There is a dummy soldier overpeering the action, and we heard a recorded announcement recommending the army as the career-solution for the modern young man ("and," it says as a hasty afterthought, "woman"). This connects with what Mike and Jeff are going through but it doesn't seem to have much to do with Andy. Indeed the three characters don't cross-refer as illuminatingly as the author obviously hopes. Jeff, with his musical and maternal obsessions, is entertainingly established at the start, but can't do much more than repeat them afterwards. Mike and Andy have stronger stories to tell, though Andy's is developed in far more detail. In addition, the play seems unsure how and when to end. There's no great statement here about modern youth, but the script is bursting with sympathetic humour, with just enough mordant implications to keep us feeling intellectually respectable. Dunn acts it marvellously; he is unpatronizingly witty, vulnerable without being anybody's puppy dog, and all-round appealing. His technique and concentration are awesome; each of the myriad opportunities he has set up for himself is also a potential trap, but he doesn't put a foot or an inflection wrong. This is the best performance -- or conceivably three of the best performances -- currently on offer in Toronto. It is also that great rarity: a solo show that keeps its audience awake.
