A couple of years ago, I reported enthusiastically on The James Plays, as presented by the National Theatre of Scotland at the other National Theatre in London. They performed three plays (the Jameses in question are the first three Scottish kings of that name from the fifteenth century) in the platform-stage Olivier Theatre, which they transformed into a theatre-in-the-round.

Those who wished could take in all three plays in a single day, as I did. It was exciting, as theatrical marathons often are, but as I wrote at the time, I couldn't imagine any Canadian company putting on anything so ambitious, and so historically and geographically remote. I added, though, that World Stage or Luminato could and should import them."

Sometimes you get what you wish for.

The James Plays arrive at the Luminato Festival this week, somewhat revised and recast, but basically as their original productions. The director is Laurie Sansom, and I had coffee with him this week at his Toronto hotel.

Sansom is the outgoing artistic director of the National Theatre of Scotland. Hes 44, and hes been in the job for three years. The company itself is ten years old, and has no fixed abode; it tours the country, all of it. Sansom says that he was "intrigued by a national theatre that didn't have to be an iconic building and was in fact the first of its roving kind. The model has since been copied by the National Theatre of Wales.

According to Sansom, just last Saturday, we closed a show at a barn in Perthshire, about men, deserters, shot for treason in the First World War. We draw on pop art, performance art. In that sense, the James Plays are quite an anomaly for us."

They're the work of Rona Munro, a Scottish writer and historian. Sansom found the first draft of the plays on his desk when he took over from his predecessor, Vicki Featherstone.

"Vicki had asked Rona what her dream project was. Rona told her, and said, Youll never do that.' Vicki said, 'Do it. So she did. When I read them, I was struck by their use of contemporary language, by their storytelling, wit and invention. They were exploring how Scotland should be governed.

The three Jameses, though widely different personalities, had several things in common. They all married foreign princesses, they all faced virulent opposition from the local lords, and they all met violent ends. James I spent his early years as a prisoner: a hostage at the English court of Henry V, who appears in the first play in a less chivalrous light than that depicted in Shakespeare. When Henry died," says Sansom, "James returned to Scotland to take the crown, and to impose a tax-based regime on the English model. He wanted to bring an established government to a country that hadnt had one for 40 years.

James II spent the early years of his reign as a hunted, haunted child. His play starts with a nightmare sequence showing him hiding in a series of boxes of increasing size. It's a brilliant image which was superbly realized in Sansom's original production. The boxes were sent whizzing about the stage while their grown-up former occupant

watched his younger boxedup selves being impersonated by a succession of puppets. I was grieved to learn that the puppets, as part of a general clarifying operation, have now been eliminated. But I take Sansoms point that the great bonus is that the actor can now live through the traumatic events himself " rather than handing them over to a bunch of dolls.

When we get to James III, were introduced to an item that Sansom justly calls a "great dramatic device. His Danish wife imports a mirror from Venice which, unlike all previous looking-glasses, reveals people as they actually look. This queen Margaret is altogether a fascinating character. As Sansom says, She ruled Scotland for her last two years." She was certainly far more capable than her husband.

Where James I and II were forced by circumstances to become increasingly ruthless, James III was one of your artist-kings, asking only to be left alone with his own personal choristers. In Sansom's words, "Margaret takes over the play, as she took over the country.

Margaret has been neglected by historians. But so has the whole period. This, according to Sansom, is a period of Scottish history forgotten in Scotland. There have been only three books written about it: one for each king.

"The plays ask: How do you govern a country. Do you fight for it?" They were first staged at the time of the Scottish referendum, whose result seems to have been a bitter disappointment for most of the actors.

There's a large majority of Scottish artists who are in favour of independence. In The James Plays, there are twenty actors of whom all but two are Scottish. The exceptions are an English actress who plays James Is English wife (who would rather live in France where they have orchards and better weather) and a Swedish lady as Danish Margaret.

"At our best," says Sansom, "at the National Theatre of Scotland, weve taken a rough Scots tradition and brought a new aesthetic to it. We're both populist and rule-breaking; there's not much respect for rules in the Scottish theatre, generally."

High praise coming from Sansom, who himself is English.
