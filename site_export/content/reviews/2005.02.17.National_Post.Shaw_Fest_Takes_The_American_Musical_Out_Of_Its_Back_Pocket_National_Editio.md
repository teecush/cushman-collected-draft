Even enthusiasts of American musicals can have their doubts about the extent to which they have taken over the classical theatres in Canada, just as they already had in the U.K. (Not, ironically, in the United States, which doesn't have a classical theatre.) So the appearance of a musical on the mainstage of the Shaw Festival (1- 800-511-7429) -- which has previously kept them pocket-sized -- may be regarded as a two-edged sword. Still, the show itself is one of the best: Gypsy, lyrics by Stephen Sondheim and book by Arthur Laurents. Nora McLellan, who has given good Ethel Merman in the past, takes on the Merm's greatest role, the archetypal battleaxe stage mother, in a production by Jackie Maxwell. There's a smaller show as well, the return, from two seasons ago, of the Bertolt Brecht-Kurt Weill Happy End, German of course but set in an imaginary U.S., with some of the duo's best songs, if hardly their best script. Stratford (1-800-567-1600), meanwhile, offers another Sondheim, the festival's first attempt at a show for which he wrote both music and lyrics, the late masterpiece Into the Woods. More populistically, if there is such a word, there's Hello Dolly, which Noel Coward memorably described as a show he didn't like but hugely enjoyed. It's a common reaction. Dolly herself is usually cast with a personality whose mere appearance at the top of that staircase gets the crowd cheering. But the show is based on a distinguished play (The Matchmaker), and Stratford has cast a real actress-who- can-sing, Lucy Peacock. This may be a first. It should certainly be an event.
