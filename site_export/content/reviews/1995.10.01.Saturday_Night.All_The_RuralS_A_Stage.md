To see first-rate classical theatre in Canada you have to leave town. Perhaps only a pampered and insular metropolitan would put it quite like that; still, it's remarkable that Toronto, which preens itself on being the third(or is it the I second?) theatrical centre in North America, has an I overpowering commercial sector, a seething if permanently impoverished underground, and virtually nothing in between. In other cities the picture is similar, if smaller.

In the theatre, as everywhere else, to be cut off from the past is to be diminished. It limits everybody: actors, directors, audiences. (It's especially bad for critics; judgments made in a vacuum are apt to be vacuous.) It's an open if embarrassing secret that old plays are often more refreshing than new ones. Partly it's because they have to be fairly good to survive; but it's also because they allow us the pleasure of double vision. They cater to our conviction that human nature never changes (this is affirmed every time we laugh at an old joke) and to our more skeptical belief that everything else does. Past and present illuminate one another. Mercifully, the slack gets taken up each summer: at least for those prepared to travel. You may go to Stratford or to Niagara-on-the-Lake or, as of this year, to Wolfville, Nova Scotia, where a new classical festival has got off to a fine, invigorating start.

Wolfville, a charming town even by the standards of the Annapolis Valley, is the home of Acadia University and, now, of the Atlantic Theatre Festival. The theatre is housed in what was once the university hockey rink. Michael Bawtree, head of the university drama department, is the artistic director; he is also a former associate director at Stratford. His own associate at Wolfville is Michael Langham, who ran Stratford during its formative years of the 1950s and 1960s; at seventy-six he must rank as the elder statesman of Shakespeare production.

Wolfville has a thrust stage, strikingly similar to Stratford's, though more modest. At Stratford the player-spectator relationship is confrontational; the stage makes a public statement, almost irrespective of what you put on it, and the wraparound auditorium responds by clenching the play and the actors in its fist. At Wolfville the audience is more gently fanned out. A play needs to have a certain size, physical or emotional, to fill that space but it does not have to be epic. The three plays of Wolfville's opening season were The Tempest, Shakespeare's most reclusive work; Chekhov's The Cherry Orchard implicitly concerning the whole of pre-revolutionary Russian gentry fourteen years before the revolution but technically continued to a single house and grounds; and Feydeau's A Flea in Her Ear, the paradigm of a French bedroom farce. All three worked;

Langham's production of The Tempest was a model of lucidity. It shone a harsh light on the inner struggle of the exiled Prospero, torn between revenge and forgiveness and here supported by an unusually grim and recalcitrant collection of spirits. It also painted an exciting set of pictures. The opening storm took full and realistic possession of a stage in full sail. (So it should in the Maritimes. Actually, open stages invariably make very good ships.) The , shipwrecked nobles beat invisible bushes with their swords as they roamed distracted around Prospero's island. Phantom banquets appeared and vanished with bewitched and bewitching speed. ("Cool," breathed the nine-year-old next to me, who had been having trouble with the blank verse.) The wedding masque of goddesses was a stylized show -- half Greek, half kabuki that seemed for once to have some relevance to the betrothed couple watching it.

Langham co-directed Cherry Orchard with his actress-wife, Helen Bums, and this too was a good story. Anyone seeing this play for the first time should be agog to know whether the orchard will be sold and, if so, to whom. Interwoven with this central thread are half a dozen others that the author may seem to let drop but that it's the business of the production to keep in sight. The pivotal role of Madame Ranevskaya came over as shallow (whether intentionally or not was hard to say), but Taham's mastery of grouping and an overall quality of elegiac tartness -- we knew they were doomed, they didn't -- kept the piece flowing. Especially striking was the interlocking drama of the servants, each fighting for some kind of survival; I've never before seen it suggested that Dunyasha, the maid, not only gets seduced in the course of the action but also gets pregnant.

The twin enclosures of drawing room and bordello are not obvious candidates for the open staging demanded by a thrust stage, and characters in Michael Bawtree's production of a Flea in Her Ear sometimes took longer to get from point A to point B than is customary in farce. But this enforced deliberation paid off. It lent an air of solidity, even of reflection, that was rather helpful. For Feydeau's flirts, roues, and cuckolds are real people who occasionally stop to think, if seldom very edifyingly. This piece of Paris from the 1830s is not just social history but dispassionate social comment. It is also the most fun you can have in a theatre while still remaining cultural.

Two pieces from the dead centre of the established repertoire and one from the frivolous fringe may seem a safe selection. Actually, the better known a play, the more daunting the comparisons, and the greater the danger of slipping into routine. The Atlantic Festival is in the same position that Stratford faced at its inception: trying to build an acting tradition from local talent, fortified by some expert outsiders.

Three outstanding players have centred this year's company: a returning star, a stalwart from the Canadian classical establishment, and a local boy making surpassingly good. The star is Peter Donat, born just down the road from Wolfville and an old boy of Acadia, but long settled in California. He is now an edgy, quirky actor, slightly to the left of your conventional leading man. His Prospero is hoarse but compelling, a little overawed by his own magic. I preferred him, though, as Gayev, the fussy, helpless aristocrat of The Cherry Orchard, who goes through the play eating chocolates, radiating helpless goodwill, and reciting billiard gambits. Donat, uniquely in my experience, relates this last tic to Gayev's feelings and situation at each given moment, and the gain to the coherence of both play and character is immense.

Leon Pownall, a pillar of Stratford for many years, is vibrant as well as reliable here. Funny and warm in the Chekhov, flamboyantly hilarious as a homicidally jealous Spaniard in Feydeau, he is best of all as the faithful Gonzalo in The Tempest, usually an amiable bore but here a forceful and even radiant spokesman for good Pownall is the kind of actor no company should be without. Finally -- and apparently a revelation not only to me but to his local fans there is Bill Carr, well known in the Maritimes as a comedian and after-dinner speaker. A Flea in Her Ear is really his show. He plays twin roles: a solid-citizen husband and a downtrodden hotel porter whom he endows with a comic strength, alternately sweet and rough, that suggests music hall raised to a higher power. The same qualities, ground still finer, inform his Lopakhin, the peasant turned millionaire of The Cherry Orchard able to buy and sell any of the gentry but unable to shake off his emotional dependence on them. This is one of the great roles of the world, a historical moment made flesh; it's also one of the trickiest. I have never believed so completely in Lopakhin's compulsive need to work

I had expected good things, especially from Langham, but my expectations were surpassed. A winning choice of actors and plays was enhanced by the appeal of the town itself and by the pioneering excitement, shared by participants and spectators, of launching a new venture. There is a palpable local pride about, which I hope will weather what are bound to be difficult years ahead. Actors wander off, theatres grow into institutions, and towns turn into tourist-traps.

Stratford has been trapping tourists for a long time now, while Niagara-on-the-Lake a town that always seems to me to have been carved out of fudge -- was a holiday resort long before the Shaw Festival set up shop there. Both Ontario festivals do some pandering to undiscriminating audiences. The Shaw Festival, under artistic director Christopher Newton, deals with the mass-appeal problem by hiving it off. They do thoroughly respectable productions of vintage murder-mysteries (this year it was Ladies in Retirement, with the admirable Jennifer Phipps), taking them quite seriously on their own terms, and everybody is happy.

The Shaw's peculiar mandate -- plays written in the lifetime of its patron saint takes in Ibsen, Strindberg, Chekhov, Pirandello, Brecht, O'Neill: all of whom it has virtually ignored. But it also takes in quantities of minor plays that can still speak to us, largely because they have never stopped Every play written since the 1920s is familiar to us on film; if we have not seen it, we have seen one just like it, and so have the actors. This year John van Druten's The Voice of the Turtle charmingly reminded us of the amazing amount of booze that innocent young women used to get through in the 1940s. Robert Sherwood's The Petrified Forest is a windy play but its large cast and Depression dust bowl setting offer, immense opportunities for atmosphere, magnificently taken (and expanded) in Neil Munro's production. Jim Moon gives a superbly growly performance as a fatalistic gangster, kept largely in shadow to avoid damaging comparisons with Humphrey Bogart.

More obviously spectacular was Noel Coward's Cavalcade, directed by Newton and Duncan McIntosh with a cast of dozens. It was logistically flawless, visually stunning, and humanly empty, since this chronicle of the century's first thirty years has space for only one character (well done by Fiona Reid with wit and nerves plus an accelerating bitterness). It also has a great song, "Twentieth Century Blues," summing up a historical action that has been one bloody disaster after another. For all that, : the play has an upbeat aura that the production, savagely though it tried, could never quite dispel. The only emotion possible was awe at the spectacle.

Shaw himself was well served this year. Newron's production of You Never Can Tell, a seaside fantasia that blossoms into a harlequinade, is a pleasure to watch. It also has a perfect performance from Jack Medley as the omniscient old waiter. The Philanderer, a revealing piece of self-satire -- the artist as heartless womanizer -- was fitted out at some performances with an additional act. This begins with an anatomy of a dead marriage that for cold horror is unmatched in its authors work.'S wonderful. No wonder he suppressed it. Both these comedies are sex-war pieces, and when lines turn up that are critical or satirical of women, the actors tie themselves in fascinating knots to demonstrate their disapproval of what they are saying.

Not that the Shaw audience itself is beyond reproach. It seems to have terrible taste in acting. Exit applause at Niagara means that something horrendous has just happened: Nora McLellan steamrollering the epigrams of Oscar Wilde's An Ideal Husband or Simon Bradbury doing everything but turning cartwheels in The Philanderer. But the festival, guided by Newton's blend of scholarship and showmanship, still looks serious, and its uniquely specialized repertoire gives it an identity that's invaluable.

Stratford, in theory, can do anything. In practice, this is less enviable than it sounds. Stratford, for all its prestige and its three stages, is a summer festival. It is not a fully furnished, fully financed national theatre: the kind of year-round place whose absence I lamented at the beginning of this article. It can do only a few plays a year: in this financially oppressed year, fewer than usual. With the whole of world drama to choose from, where do you start? Something has to centre the repertoire, and there are practical as well as pious reasons for assigning this responsibility to Shakespeare. But if a season's Shakespeares fail to catch fire, as happened this year, then the whole bill starts to look arbitrary. This was Richard Monette's second season as artistic director. His first was a bravura success. This year he had to cut down to nine shows, two of them revivals.

No-one could begrudge a second showing to Diana Leblanc's world-class production of Long Day's Journey into Night; and time has welded these actors into an unchangeable family. William Hutt gets the peasant roots, peasant simplicity, and peasant meanness of old Tyrone, and he breaks your heart (which is more than Olivier ever did) when the old actor sobs over the ruin of his talent. Peter Donaldson drills with bloated accuracy into the depths of son Jamie's alcoholic self-hatred, and Tom McCamus, presumably Stratford's next Hamlet, makes Edmund, O'Neill's self-indulgent self-portrait, a thriving neurotic in his own right. It's the play's greatest irony that these squabbling men finally attain a dram (the right word, since it's whisky-induced) of mutual understanding while the wife and mother they all love slips away from them into a drug-addict's stupor. Some of the steps on Martha Henry's descent are uncomfortably actressy, but her best moments, especially the last, are uncanny.

Also outstanding was Werley's The Country Wife. Douglas Campbell's production went a little easy on the characters' I misogyny and cruelty; nevertheless this is the best staging of this great Restoration comedy that I have seen, and incomparably the funniest. Tom McCamus, Stephen Ouimette, and Scott Wentworth as lecher, fop, and cuckold seized on their roles and situations with deadly accuracy, and offered them up with infectious relish.

When actors can command the stage like that, the Festival Theatre becomes the most exhilarating space there is. It happened in this year's Macbeth: not, unfortunately, in the protagonist's soul searchings but in Bernard Hopkins's brief interlude as the Porter. Otherwise the play slid, as it so often does, inexorably into mediocrity. The only other Shakespeares were the most lightweight in the canon. I missed The Comedy of Errors. Monette's production of The Merry Wives of Winsdor (co-directed by Antoni Cimolino) had some hilarious moments, but it wasn't enough.

And then there were the musicals, done all too obviously to pay the bills. The Boy Friend was energetic but heartless. The Gondoliers had scenic and choreographic delights, nullified by a contemptuous attitude to the script. The show's basic comic point was scupperd along with some perfectly visible character jokes, in favour of new lyrics about contemporary Canada. The audience lapped them up, but they made no sense in the context of the original, which otherwise trundled along undisturbed A visiting American was heard marveling what a great show they had made out of a nothing source. You don't have to be a Gilbert and Sullivan fanatic to feel that something is wrong. I doubt that G & S would be treated so cavalierly if they were done at Niagara -- into whose time-frame they would of course fit perfectly. The only way of making an audience believe in a play is to believe in it yourself.

So standards at Shaw, and especially at Stratford, are uneven; and the consistency of Wolfville was achieved in the special circumstances of a first season which, in the nature of things, was likely to be either a triumph or a disaster. But all theatres are uneven, and the best work done at these three will stand comparison with any in the English-speaking world. Wolfville hopes to build an ongoing ensemble. Stratford and Shaw have more continuity in casting than the British classical companies have managed in years. Experience tells; and you can now hear Shakespeare and Shaw spoken at least as well -- as intelligently and dynamically in Canada as in Britain. Probably the festival set-up helps; actors will commit themselves for a summer more easily, and more repeatedly, than for a full year. I shall continue to regret our lack of urban repertory companies, but it's more important that we hold on to the classical theatres we do have and hold them to their best selves.
