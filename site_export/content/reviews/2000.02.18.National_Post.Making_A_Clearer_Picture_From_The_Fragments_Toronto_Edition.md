SUNDAY IN THE PARK WITH GEORGE

Equity Showcase Theatre, Toronto

I have sometimes speculated that if Andrew Lloyd Webber had written Sunday in the Park with George he would have called it Dots.

The Stephen Sondheim-James Lapine musical, a succes d'estime on Broadway 15 years ago, is very loosely based on the reclusive life and short career of Georges Seurat, the Parisian pointillist painter who worked in tiny specks of colour which, seen from a sufficient distance, coalesced into dazzling and elaborate canvasses.

We watch him circa 1885, both in his studio and on location, assembling his urban-pastoral masterwork Sunday Afternoon on the Island of the Grande Jatte. He interacts, fitfully and reluctantly, with the locals and tourists who pass through this pleasure-spot in the middle of the Seine and who are to enjoy an unsuspecting immortality as elements of his composition.

He has a slightly more elaborate relationship with his mistress and model, an unschooled but vivacious young lady who occasionally fantasizes about being in the Folies but more often dreams of really, truly, attracting her lovers attention. And her name -- emblematic of all the atoms that make up the finished painting -- is Dot.

Tiring of the relationship (Thats right, George, hide behind your painting -- "I'm not hiding behind my painting, I'm hiding in it.") Dot, at the end of Act One, ups and emigrates to America. She has married a local baker but is carrying Georges child.

Act Two moves us on to contemporary New York; the child is now a 95-year-old woman, and her grandson, also called George, is himself an artist, presenting mechanized light-shows called chromolumes. He himself, enjoying a precarious success on the cocktail-chatter circuit, might be called a chromoluminary; and he doesnt like it much.

His grandmother sings from her wheelchair a wry, meditative, gently passionate song called Children and Art, whose burden is that those two things are all any of us can hope to leave behind us. This debatable thesis (which seems unnecessarily hard on childless non- artists) ties together, by main force, the show's principal preoccupations; fortunately it's a very good song.

Its balanced by another, Lesson #8, in which George hesitantly seeks and finds meaning in the simple questions posed in his great- grandmothers teach-yourself-grammar book. He makes a pilgrimage to his namesake's old Parisian painting-ground and there encounters the ghost of Dot.

She sings at him an anthem called Move On that alternates Rodgers- and-Hammerstein uplift with shrewd observation. (Stop worrying if your vision/Is new/Let others make that decision/They usually do.)

The show, plainly, is more about art than is, say, the play Art. When it deals with the Plight of the Artist -- as in George the Second's sparring with the Manhattan culture-vultures -- it becomes brittle and self-pitying. When it concentrates on the process of creating art -- as in George the Firsts driven, resigned song about staying home and finishing the hat -- it is illuminating and exciting.

But its real theme, admittedly closely linked, is time, or its redemption: George Is mother laments the passing of the park she used to know even while her son is painting it, and by the time George II arrives it's almost gone. The painting is all we have to remind us, and that of course is one man's manipulative interpretation.

The first-act finale, echoed at the end, is a soaring chorale during which the artist shifts the other characters around to fit his vision. Its moving and fulfilling and also somewhat unsettling.

I have always found this one of the least approachable Sondheim shows. It contains some of his loveliest and (as befits the subject) most shimmering music, and some of his most eloquent lyrics.

Lapines book has some suggestive scenes and some sharp lines. (I especially relish the comment of George's technical assistant who is leaving the art-world to go back to NASA because "there's too much pressure in this line of work.") But the action is fragmentary and the characters, apart from George and Dot and their later reincarnations, are lay-figures; and though this may be inherent in the conception its still frustrating.

Seeing it, though, for the first time since the original production, I found it unexpectedly coherent. The Toronto production combines professionals from Equity Showcase and students from George Brown Theatre School, and the lead actors alternate; I saw a student George and a professional Dot.

There are obvious limitations; I missed the sumptuous, tantalizing greenery of the original staging. (It got a Tony for design though Seurat himself, a vital collaborator, wasnt cited.) There are also gains. Bernadette Peters' Broadway Dot will never be equalled, but maybe she unbalanced the show; one missed her cruelly when she was off and resented that nobody else was as lively as she was.

Mi-Anne Dionisio (who was Toronto's Miss Saigon and Maria in Stratfords West Side Story) doesnt have much sparkle but she is intelligent and sings beautifully. Ken McClure as George is far more sympathetic than Mandy Patinkin's prototype, though his singing- voice is very similar; he is heavy-set and believably insecure without being a nervous wreck, and he centres the show.

The supporting professionals -- Shawn Wright as a rival artist, Jayne Lewis as his wife, Mary Pitt as the mother -- all do well; and the student talents seem ideally suited to the cut-out roles they're required to play. They seize with especial glee on the museum parasites who are skewered (unfortunately without having first been created) in a song too-cleverly titled The State of the Art.

Sue Miner's production is excellent, especially in its intricate movement. The musicians, under John Tute, play tautly and splendidly though they sometimes -- in an unamplified and acoustically unfriendly house -- drown the singers. But the dots get connected.
