THEATRE REVIEW

The Game of Love and Chance

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Pierre Marivaux's The Game of Love and Chance (1730) is situation comedy raised to its highest power. The situation is classic: A young woman, Silvia, whose father wants her to marry a young man whom she has never met, stipulates that she be allowed to check him out from a safe distance by changing places with her maid. The twist here is that her suitor, Dorante, has had the equivalent idea and comes a-calling disguised as his own valet. Think She Stoops to Conquer and then double it. Think, too, that Marivaux got there first, by 40 years.

Needless to say, the disguised young master and mistress fall for one another, as do the two disguised servants. Each member of the quartet believes that he or she has snagged a partner from the other side of the class barrier, and reacts with the appropriate amount of delight or dismay. The audience has to keep reminding itself which character is which and how much each knows about the person with whom he or she is interacting. It's a demanding and stimulating exercise in what the textbooks call "discrepancy of awareness" and it gives the play much of its kick. That kick is intensified when one of the lovers reveals his or her real identity to his or her partner (pardon my pronouns but it should be a surprise) and the favour is not reciprocated. The awareness becomes even more discrepant. Add in that Silvia's father (we never meet Dorante's) knows what's going on, as does her brother, and that both are having too much fun with the situation to allow anyone to call a halt.

Silvia's maid is named Lisette; Dorante's valet seems to have two names. He's referred to in the dialogue as Bourguignon, but in the program as Arlequino. That is, he's descended from the Harlequin figure of the Commedia dell'Arte, a phrase calculated to strike terror into the heart of anybody who has seen its effect on modern directors.

In Matthew Jocelyn's Canadian Stage production it signifies an hysterical, hyperkinetic style in which, especially among the lower orders, voices are high-pitched and bodies are twisted into pretzels. Jocelyn took a similar acrobatic approach in his production of Fernando Krapp and it worked there because the antics were kept under control and were keyed to what the people performing them might actually have been feeling. Krapp, paradoxically, was a modern play with no apparent Commedia connections. Marivaux does pay explicit homage to the old tradition, though I suspect that in his case it was more a matter of lip service and that he would have ditched the whole idea if it hadn't been in his contract. This production only works when it does the same: when, that is, it allows its characters to behave like human beings.

For some unaccountably snobbish reason, only the bosses are permitted this privilege. The servants act like wind-up dolls, mechanical in every sense, bombarding us with external and relentlessly unfunny effects. (For an instructive contrast, see the hilarious automaton scene in the Canadian Opera Company's excellent production of The Tales of Hoffman.) Apart from not being very entertaining in itself, this undercuts what's most interesting about the play.

The Game of Love and Chance, like Beaumarchais' The Marriage of Figaro before they turned it into a musical, is one of those pre-revolutionary French comedies that suggested servants might have emotional lives as interesting as those of their masters. The original audience, missing the point, laughed all the way to the guillotine, but we don't have to join them. We know that, by all social and theatrical rules, the play will end with the young people pairing off according to rank. When the valet dreams of marrying the heiress, it's funny, but it's also ironic and even poignant; or it could be, if he were allowed to be a real person. Marivaux's ending is more realistic than any wish-fulfilling democratic finale might be, but for that very reason it should carry a charge.

The production hoists its double standard in the very first scene: Mistress and maid conduct an expository conversation in a long corridor with a door at each end, to the accompaniment of much slamming and screaming. But while Trish Lindstrom as Silvia goes through these motions with a wit, an intelligence, and a command of shifting tones that gets even keener as the evening proceeds - this is her best performance to date - Gemma James-Smith's Lisette looks and sounds as if she were in a Carry On movie, all twitter and flutter and underlining of innuendo. This may be a blow for equality; usually in the classics, male comics have the monopoly on being lewd and crude.

When we get to see the full stage, it's a room full of mirrors: a thematically apposite design by Anick La Bissonniere. Linda Brunelle's costumes are modern, which doesn't really suit the social and sexual context, but they're stylish and unobtrusive enough for it not to matter. Nicolas Billon's translation is also modernish, as far as it's anything.

Harry Judge's Dorante oscillates between the production's two poles; mostly, sharing his scenes with Lindstrom, he's at the positive end, and he does hint at more complexity of feeling than anyone else. Arlequino is theoretically the star role; certainly Gil Garratt plays it that way: I suppose he deserves marks for intrepidity, but he's exhausting to watch and tormenting to hear.

As the lightly heavy father William Webster, with experience on his side, keeps his head while most around him are frenziedly clutching theirs; that especially goes for his son whom Zach Fraser, presumably obeying orders, plays as a smirking candymunching booby. He seems an unnecessary character, which may well be Marivaux's fault. Nothing else here is; this is a great rare play and a missed opportunity.

The Game of Love and Chance runs until May 12. For tickets, call 416-368-3110 or visit canadianstage.com.
