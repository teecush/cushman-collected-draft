A Whistle in the Dark plays until Feb. 5 (416-368-3110); The Bear until ; The Glass Menagerie until Feb. 26 (416-368-3110).; robertcushman@lycos.com

A new group calling itself The Company Theatre gets off to a magnificent start with a show that has everything. A Whistle in the Dark (Berkeley Street Upstairs) is a relentlessly compelling play, 44 years old but new to Canada, given a flawless ensemble production with a great performance at its centre.

Tom Murphy's play, written when he was 24 and at the start of a long career, shows us the Carneys, a patriarchal Irish family, most of whose members are living in England. Their code is one of violence, verbal and physical.

Michael, the eldest son, is trying, like his namesake in The Godfather, to turn his back on it. However, his Coventry home has been invaded by three of his brothers: the hulking Iggy (short for Ignatius), the taciturn Hubert and the flash sardonic Harry, who is borderline psychopathic and, unhappily, a natural leader. They are now joined by two visitors from the old country: the boys dad, an ex-cop who claims, probably rightly, to have made them what they are, and Des, the youngest brother, baby-faced and raring to prove himself.

Looming ahead is a battle with a rival clan of expatriates. Everyone looks forward to it except Michael and his wife Betty, reviled by her in-laws for the double crime of being a woman and English.

On my way out of the theatre, I heard three separate people joking that their own families were just like that. In fact, there are chords here that will resonate with almost anybody. Take away the fists, the knuckledusters and the broken bottles, and the tensions at work here -- the jibes, recriminations, occasional bursts of affection and the incessant jockeying for position -- are universal. There are strands that have to do with the condition of the working-class Irish male c. 1960, but the play goes beyond that. Michael is the emblematic figure of the man struggling to escape his heritage. Nothing that he tries, reasoned protest or accusing silence, seems to work; it merely exacerbates his siblings angry sense that he sets himself above them. As was famously noted by another Irish writer, each man ends up killing the thing he loves, and there are many ways of doing it.

The orchestration of all this, with the narrative excitement inexorably rising, is a prodigious achievement. The language, as one expects of an Irish play, is rich and witty; it is also, as one doesn't always expect, forceful and economic, looking forward to Martin McDonough, rather than back to O'Casey and Synge. (There is no profanity, but the dialogue hits just as hard for all that.) The characters may be mostly thugs, but they are inexhaustibly varied, and one of them is a major creation.

This is the boys DaDa, a timorous windbag posing as the hardest of the hard, and given by Joseph Ziegler what may well be the performance of his career. He is at once immense and delicate, courtly when he first pays his respects to the lady of the house," very funny when he deplores the unhealthy effects of trees ("all that carbon dioxide"), piteous when in his cups and revealing the depths of his own desperation, scarifying when he climbs on a chair to play the sentimental tyrant. Most terrible of all, and most brilliantly revealed by the actor, is his ability to shut out any fact or argument he doesnt want to hear, right down, in a moment of blackest comedy, to the play's climax.

The producers have imported an Irish director, Jason Byrne, whose work is a revelation. This is the tightest yet richest production imaginable, with not a move or word wasted, and every performance crammed to the limit. Michael is Jonathan Goad, who finds the right mind and stance for the role and has the perfect name. No character has ever been more pitilessly goaded, by himself and by others. Sarah Dodd plays his wife with a fine blend of pity and anger and a perfect sense of time and place. David Jansen is creepy and amusing as a Carney hanger-on, Oliver Becker brings weight and an occasional disconcerting gentleness to Iggy and Aaron Poole endows Hubert with a smile that's the visual equivalent of a snarl.

The remaining brothers are played by Company Theatres two artistic directors; Philip Riccio is the baby-faced Des and Allan Hawco the magnetically dangerous Harry, loaded with discontent. These are the two most unpredictable characters in the play, because one of them has the glimmerings of a conscience and the other hasnt. One doesn't know, either, what else this company has in store, but it has made the most stunning debut since Soulpepper's Don Carlos. It may even, judging by its aesthetic and by much of its casting, be Son of Soulpepper.

Also highly recommended: The Bear, Chekhovs one-act farce about the boorish landowner who comes to collect a debt from a widow and ends up collecting her instead. (Its an old story; the Romans had a word for it, if not the Greeks.) This is given in the Grange Room, a beautiful if defiantly un-Russian chamber at the AGO. Christopher Morris' production is boisterous, inventive and adventurous; Madame's first appearance is a scream. It is also surprisingly delicate; this is Chekhov, after all, and David Ferry is touching as well as hilarious when, against all his wishes and in defiance of his own best interests, he finds his misogyny melting. His lady (Nicole St. Martin) is less accomplished, but still tears some delicious strips off the memory of her late unlamented.

Coming down some steep steps: The Glass Menagerie (Berkeley Street Downstairs) has most of the musical and atmospheric trappings that Tennessee Williams specified, but in Chris Abrahams production they come off more doomy than delicate. There are also audibility problems, due to a yawning set and some exaggerated accents. Rosemary Dunsmores maternal belle is the hardest to hear and also the hardest to take, making the woman so terrible a nag that the collapse of her dreams has no emotional impact, which means that the play doesn't, either. As the withdrawn daughter Laura, Michelle Monteith similarly overdoes the wan and winsome, but she is touching in the scene with her Gentleman Caller, whom Seann Gallagher makes the most likeable character in the play. Williams may have intended us to deplore his breezy philistinism but, in this hot-house atmosphere, it comes as a relief. Ironically, too, this caller is a gentleman; he smashes fragile glass unicorns and more fragile feelings, but he's genuinely sorry about it.
