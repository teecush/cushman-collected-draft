PUPPETRY OF THE PENIS

New Yorker Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Nearly everything you have heard about Puppetry of the Penis is true. It does indeed consist of two men up on stage twisting their genitals into weird and, I guess, wonderful shapes for our delectation. It's also true that this is entertainment from down under, by which of I course mean the performing pair hail from Australia.

What has been falsely reported is that they perform in a state of full frontal nudity. True, they come on stage wearing classical cloaks that they duly and speedily cast aside, and they have no other garments beneath. But they do, throughout the proceedings, keep their shoes and socks on, as in some revue sketch about Englishmen making love. And one of them wears a funny hat; I think it's a pith helmet.

The two guys are named Simon Morley and David Friend. The former refers to the latter as Friendy. Friendy is the one in the helmet; he also wears a goofy grin and, by my unscientific calculation, demonstrates more than half of the couple's inventions or, as they call them, installations. For his facial wear, Morley favours the dry smile, and he also does most of the talking; in traditional comedy-duo terms, he is the straight man. As comics, they are very ingratiating. They swap roles a bit. But the rule is that one talks while the other does. Be assured that the evening contains no organ duets.

Their show's title, as they are keen to point out, undersells them. They don't just play with their penises; they throw in, liberally, their testicles and scrota. There will probably be sequels, with similarly alliterative titles. Stop trying to invent them and pay attention. The scrotum stretching, in particular, is taken to spectacular lengths. If the show on the first night seemed to be more -- or at least more vocally -- appreciated by the ladies in the audience, that may be because the gentlemen could never quite bring themselves to believe the actors' assurances that it didn't hurt. We felt their pain, even if they didn't.

Probably the most ingenious impersonation comes early in the act, when Friendy makes all his bits disappear from view and hops around the stage like one of his compatriot kangaroos. He calls this impersonation the Woman. Trailing this item closely in cleverness, and surpassing it in outrageousness, is the Hamburger, which I shall not attempt to describe, except to say that it involves a lot of folding and wrapping. They also do animals.

The performers have an assistant, who actually precedes them on stage, and announces himself as Priapus, ancient god of the phallus. He then retreats into the auditorium and films the show as it happens. (He seems to be better with a camera than he is at speaking lines.) The film appears on a large screen behind the actors so that we may get a good view of details we might otherwise miss. It's like watching a game at SkyDome. You don't know where to look.

Now, what has this production to tell us about sexual liberation? That is a complex question, with a simple answer: It tells us absolutely nothing about sexual liberation or sexual anything. (Except, of course, that there has to be a permissive climate for the show to get on in the first place.) The show is advertised as un- sexual; I would be inclined to call it pre-sexual. That puts it at the opposite extreme from the previous attraction at this theatre, the very verbal Vagina Monologues -- or, as I have come to think of it, Bridge of Thighs -- which might be called post-sexual. One show doesn't talk about it, the other only talks about it. On a strictly anatomical level, the difference between them is the difference between show and tell. Both productions, a little unfairly, seem to be aimed principally at women.

Both the differences and the similarities are reinforced by the presence in the current show of a warm-up act from the female comedy circuit: a British lady, now living here, called Wendy Vousden, who - - not unnaturally in the circumstances -- told jokes about the male body. Very blunt and aggressive she was, though maybe she needed somebody to warm her up, since her turn was nearly over before she said anything imaginative enough to be funny.

Having her there makes sense, though, because a) the audience, however broad-minded, is likely to arrive feeling queasy and is grateful for an icebreaker; and b) Morley and Friend, though sweet, have a limited repertoire and wisely ration their stage time to less than an hour. Even at that, they can get monotonous. At their best, though, they are hilarious. There is also audience participation; a lady is induced to go up on stage and help out. Friday's volunteer said it was her birthday and seemed to enjoy her surprise present.

I am uneasily aware that some readers may have tuned in hoping for a column of suggestive wordplay, and that I have short-changed them. It's late, but there is a line in Macbeth that goes "by the pricking of my thumbs." Perhaps we could try turning that around. I would just like to add that the two penis puppeteers struck me as being very -- um -- Australian. I don't think the show would work in any other accent.
