Julie Andrews asked me, on the phone from Los Angeles, if I remembered Educating Archie. As one whose childhood occurred in England in the 1950s, I certainly did. It was a BBC radio comedy show, very popular with the younger set since it revolved around a ventriloquist's dummy, a perpetual schoolboy named Archie Andrews. It ran for years, with many changes of cast, but the 15-year-old Julie was one of the originals.

Half a century on, she mused for a moment on the bizarre phenomenon of a sound-only ventriloquist act. Mind you, in those halcyon days, people even accepted radio conjours.

"You played Archie's girl-friend," I said.

She corrected me with the crisp friendliness that has defined her from those days to this. "Not exactly. I was the girl next door." That she was, a well-behaved young lady in contrast to the more mischievous young females with whom Archie spent most of the show getting into scrapes. Actually, young Julie's verbal exchanges with Archie were the merest prelude to the real reason for her presence on the show -- her weekly song.

She was already a stage and radio veteran, part of an act known as Ted and Barbara Andrews and Julie. Barbara was her mother, Ted her stepfather. They toured the country's music halls, and were also mainstays of an even more venerable radio variety series, a lunch- hour relic of the war called Workers' Playtime. Gradually, Julie became the mainstay and focus of the act.

"I had a genuinely freakish voice, with a huge range and an adult larynx. I could sing operatic arias." She made her West End solo debut, doing just that at the London Hippodrome. She was 12. She became a solo attraction.

Her subsequent Broadway and Hollywood career is fabled: My Fair Lady, Camelot, Mary Poppins (P. L. Travers, the fearsome author of the original stories told her "you're too pretty, but you've got the nose for it"), The Sound of Music, Victor/Victoria, plus non- singing roles in satirical comedy (The Americanization of Emily) and even a Hitchcock thriller (Torn Curtain). But it all started, theatrically, with The Boy Friend. That was the show that brought her to the United States in 1954, and it's now the show with which she has made her debut as a director. Her production, first seen at the Goodspeed musical-revivals house in Connecticut, has been touring the U.S. and it opens tonight at the Royal Alex in Toronto.

The Boy Friend was already a British phenomenon when first it played New York: A loving period re-creation of the tunefully absurd musicals of three decades earlier ("a new musical of the 1920s" said the posters) that set London long-run records. The London producers wouldn't release the West End cast, so an entirely new British team was recruited for Broadway. Julie was cast as the heroine.

The original U.S. opening of The Boy Friend is famous for having had trouble on the road. At least, that's what I'd thought; "We didn't go on the road," said Julie. "We had all our troubles in previews in New York." In full view, so to speak. "Cy Feuer [of the producing team, Feuer and Martin] thought the show needed to reach an extra level; that what had been a sweet piece of lace in London wasn't viable on Broadway, where the ante automatically goes up. There were ... disagreements." Sandy Wilson -- the show's author, lyricist and composer -- was barred from the theatre, as was the British director Vida Hope. Feuer himself took over and the show was a hit -- though less of one than in London.

So who was right? "They were probably both right -- and I don't say that just to be tactful. The producers were right in their own terms, though maybe they weren't wildly tactful about it at the time." (Wilson, to judge from his autobiography, certainly thought they weren't. He decided in the end to take the money and gracefully walk.)

Julie, during all these battles, was keeping her head down, a nervous 20-year-old new to Broadway, "learning on my feet, very fast. All I knew when I came to the States was how to belt out a song and," she says diplomatically, recalling those valuable early days on the halls, "how to deal with certain audiences. Thank God for variety."

There's always been steel behind that English propriety. One remembers Alan Jay Lerner, one of her idols and mentors, recalling her rock-like solidity in the even more fraught pre-opening circumstances of Camelot: "She makes you wonder how the British ever lost their Empire." And the current, very entertaining, British stage version of Mary Poppins, in which the title character becomes almost a supporting role, makes you reflect that being Julie Andrews can't be as easy as she makes it look.

Her own production of The Boy Friend falls, by her account, between the poles of the delicate London and brash New York originals. The show, she says, has a "sweet inanity -- it's endearing. You have to steer a course between being camp and arch, and 'aren't we being funny?' " The show is set in Wilson's idea of the '20s musical idea of the French Lido; the designer is Julie's first husband, Tony Walton. "Visually, we've borrowed from Raoul Dufy, who was painting the South of France at just that time; he has a radiance that transports us to that period. Of course, people now don't know what the musicals of the '20s were like, but we've tried to make it like bubbles in a glass of champagne." On previous legs of the tour the production has been greeted, she says, "either with condescension or outright delight. I hope that audiences will come out smiling and toe tapping." In her first venture as a director, Julie seems to have been having a feel-good time. "I'd love to do more directing. It's been nervous-making, but I'm fond of my company, I feel they're my family. And they tell me the Royal Alex is a beautiful little theatre, just right for the show." Well, up here we think it's quite big, but after the barns they've been playing in, we know what they mean.
