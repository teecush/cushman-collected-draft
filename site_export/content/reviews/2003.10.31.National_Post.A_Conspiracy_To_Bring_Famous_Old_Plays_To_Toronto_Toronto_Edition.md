Instead of comedies, John Neville's acting company starts with Ibsen's masterpieces

The three last plays of Henrik Ibsen are among the mountain- peaks of European drama: bleak, bracing, glistening and inaccessible. More prosaically, all of them -- Little Eyolf, John Gabriel Borkman, When We Dead Awaken -- are set within easy walking distance of the nearest icy heights, and all have protagonists who dream of scaling them. The hero of the last -- Ibsen's "dramatic epilogue" -- even looks as if he might make it.

The plays are rarely produced; but last weekend, all three were given rehearsed readings at Hart House under the aegis of the veteran (for once, the word seems justified) actor and director, John Neville.

The event was partly a fundraiser for the threatened Hart House Theatre itself, and partly a shop window and possible launching pad for Neville's own dream of a new acting company in Toronto, one he has roguishly christened the Classical Theatre Conspiracy. It's unclear whom its members would be conspiring against, but the CTC's stated aim of bringing the city famous old plays it would never otherwise see has an obvious similarity to that of a company that started operations here six years ago.

In an interview with this paper, Neville hinted he is less than impressed with Soulpepper (for whom he has acted and who gave his memorable performance in Krapp's Last Tape an extended life after its initial showing at World Stage.) If Neville's Conspiracy does get off the ground, it will be inevitably perceived to bear the same adversarial relation to Soulpepper that Soulpepper, despite vehement denials from both sides, is thought to hold with the Stratford Festival.

And Neville does seem, in this impromptu trial run, to have exposed a chink in his rival's armour. With a few notable exceptions, like Don Carlos and Phedre, Soulpepper's choice of repertoire has tended to the lightweight, or the comparatively recent, or both. This of course is a characteristic it shares with Stratford (when it isn't doing Shakespeare, and sometimes when it is); with the Shaw Festival, and with practically every other classically-based company in the English-speaking world. They all spend most of their time doing comedies. There are sound reasons for this, both commercial (people like to laugh), and artistic (most of the world's best plays are comedies); but it can lead to some of the sterner masterpieces -- those late Ibsens for example -- being, to use an appropriate phrase, frozen out.

My hopes for the weekend were not high; the plays are difficult, the company (though not its individual members) was untried, and readings seemed a half-hearted substitute for actual productions. I could not have been more wrong. The plays were fascinating, and one of the evenings -- the middle one, not to keep you in suspense -- was transcendent.

There were, it turned out, several ways of doing rehearsed readings. Little Eyolf, directed by Neville himself, gave us the most conventional; actors moved around, just as they might in a proper staging except they happened to be holding books in their hands. It was also the weakest; since there had been no time to make he movement meaningful.

More than almost any other Ibsen, this play risks turning into self-parody, a problem that begins, at least to unsympathetic Anglophone ears, with the title. Eyolf is a 10-year-old who fell off a table while his parents were off making love, and was crippled. By the end of the first act he is dead, drowned, leaving his parents with a burden of guilt even heavier than the one they started out with.

All these plays are about the mortal conflict between love and vocation. All have a hero surveying his past achievements and wondering if they are worth the suffering caused to the women in his life. The women, angrily and volubly, wonder the same thing. In Eyolf, the hero is a would-be writer, repulsed by his wife's sexuality; anyway, he has neglected her, first to pursue a dubious muse, and more recently to go on mountain hikes with his son. With the boy gone, he clings with suspicious closeness to his half sister, whom he calls his "big Eyolf."

The play ends on a note of acrid ambiguity, with the central couple promising to devote their lives to the children of the neighbouring poor. The actors, led by Robin Ward and Brenda Bazinet, tried fatally to downplay and naturalize everything. Only Eyolf himself (Andrew Chalmers), too young to be self-conscious, seemed to be acting his lines rather than apologizing for them. It was clear the play, if done well, could be shattering, but doing it well is hideously hard. The frankness of its sexual content is amazing; the imagery in which it's couched risks seeming coy and evasive.

John Gabriel Borkman, a magnificent play and the best-known of the three, was more formally staged, with the actors at lecterns. There were always, however, more lecterns than speakers; a character could move from one to another with terrific effect. The allowances one had instinctively made for Little Eyolf turned out to have been misguided; this was the way to do it. Graham Cozzubbo, clearly a director to note, gave a production that, however economical, was a production, with superb lighting and sound.

Superb acting too. Borkman is a financier whose dreams of glory (power for himself, betterment for all mankind) were ended when he was caught with his hand in the till: a scenario with plenty of parallels then and since.

Released from prison, he has prowled in an upstairs room for eight years, while his embittered wife waits below, trusting in their son to clear the family name. The family have all been supported for years by the wife's twin sister, whose love Borkman sacrificed in order to placate an influential rival. Elizabeth Shepherd's splendidly uncompromising Mrs. Borkman might have allowed herself a touch more light, and Dixie Seatle, her pliant but equally determined sibling, a touch more shade, but their duelling duet was spellbinding.

Gary Reineke, erect and high-domed like the legendary Broadway showman George Abbott, found in the title role an unexpected note of sardonic whimsy, and fell short only in the final scene when Borkman, a miner's son, ventures out into the upper air and is killed by it. Borkman is a tragic figure, in a foreshortened way; he is also a monster of egotism, as are the two women. The comic aspects of this are underlined by Borkman's faithful clerk (a lovely performance by Keith Dinicol) who cheerfully points out that the function of friends is to minister to each other's illusions. Indeed we had been laughing already, at the characters' sheer brazenness, but there was nothing defensive about the laughter; it never undercut the play. This production deserves an extended life. It could even have one as a reading; Al Pacino ran Salome that way on Broadway.

When We Dead Awaken, which Cozzubbo and Neville co-directed, offered a third, intermediate way of staging; the actors sat round a long table, slanting away from us. This worked both formally and realistically; the play is mostly talk. This is the "last play" to end all last plays -- its authors' obsessions, whittled down to their purest form. Most of his plays proceed by recapitulation, but they generally progress forward at the same time; this one has little action, and that little is frail; the mountain air still stimulates but it has grown very thin. This time the guilty hero is a sculptor of, for once, genuine accomplishment; the wronged woman is his former muse and model, whom he used for his masterpiece, and then abandoned. (What really seems to bug her -- shades of the marriage in Little Eyolf -- is that when she posed nude, he never made a move. I don't think I'm vulgarizing.)

Meeting again, they lure one another up into the snow, and die happily ever after. The roles call for actors of transfiguring power: David Gardner's old artist was crinkly and sensible but hardly charismatic, better at disillusion than at dreaming. Dixie Seatle, the only performer to appear in more than one play, fixed here on a single effective note of mordant seductiveness, effective but limited. All the same, the power and intelligence of this actress, who is well-known but seldom seen, were a revelation. I hope that her reinstatement on the Toronto stage is one of the things for which the Conspiracy is conspiring.
