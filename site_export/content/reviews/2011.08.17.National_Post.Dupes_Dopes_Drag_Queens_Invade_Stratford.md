THEATRE REVIEW

The Misanthrope

Festival Theatre, Stratford

Hosanna

Studio Theatre, Stratford

Moliere wrote comedies about monomaniacs: misers, hypochondriacs, religious charlatans and their dupes, grotesquely possessive husbands. In all of these the audience is on the outside, laughing in; as is the author himself.

The Misanthrope, by general consent his greatest though possibly least funny play, is also about a monomaniac: Alceste, who believes in telling people to their faces exactly what he thinks of them and is willing to take the consequences. So far, so admirable; but Alceste has, as he himself admits, an Achilles heel. He's in love: with Celimene, a compulsive flirt who also believes in saying what she thinks about people, but only behind their backs and in sycophantic company.

He has another problem, too; he's inclined to glory in his own rudeness. The question of where honesty ends and vanity begins is a nice one, and the play makes no pretence of resolving it. That's true even on the level of plot; Alceste regularly threatens to depart to some desert place where he can be his uncompromising self, and at the end of this one he does indeed storm off with that apparent intention. But his friends - yes, he has friends - go after him. As in Twelfth Night, currently available on the same stage, they pursue him and entreat him to a peace, and maybe they'll have better luck than the Illyrians had with Malvolio. This seems like a situation that could go on for ever.

Plainly, Moliere had more invested in Alceste than in any of his other protagonists. It's not just that he played the role himself (as actor-manager, he always starred in his own shows); it's that his much younger wife played Celimene. Jealousy in this play goes deeper than in any of Moliere's others. It's as much intellectual as physical; Alceste fears that Celimene is mocking not just his devotion but his ideals.

The Stratford production, directed by David Grindley, has two great things going for it. One is its elegant simplicity. The old Festival Theatre stage, with its steps and balcony, has been restored, and as ever it provides a wonderful focus, especially for this formal comedy. Everyone seems to be in the right and meaningful place at the right time, and there's especially witty use of the upper level. The designer, John Lee Beatty, has draped the old Tanya Moiseiwitsch structure in gilt and gold, and Robin Fraser Paye has decked the actors out in handsome Louis XIV costumes.

The second treat is that, more than ever before in my experience, the play becomes an evenly matched love story for two people.

Ben Carlson's Alceste, as expected, wraps his tongue pointedly and wittily around the rhyming couplets of Richard Wilbur's translation. He's delightful in the moments in which Alceste makes an agonized try at being tactful. When the dam bursts and he lets fly he might, if anything, be more explosive than he is.

What really strikes home is the desperation with which he tries, in their last and remarkably moving scenes together, to prove to himself, and to make her prove, that Celimene is worthy of him. And what strikes equally hard is the refusal of Sara Topham's Celimene to grant the necessity of any such thing. She has her own corner to fight, and her own identity to protect. She may be a coquette but she's nobody's fool; her shallowness does not, so to speak, go very deep. There's a lovely moment at which she spreads the folds of her voluminous dress over a pouffe in front of her, and controls the scene from there. It's plain that these two love one another, and that they both lose.

As Celimene's prudish rival Arsinoe, too, often played as a mere frump, Kelli Fox, gives a delicious, velvet-clawed performance. Her command of the text rivals Carlson's, and their scene together is a joy. Peter Hutt, with threatening eyebrows, makes the courtier-poet Oronte a man whose wrath Alceste might have some reason to fear, which proves him a heroic critic indeed. As Philinte, the voice of moderation or hypocrisy depending on your point of view, Juan Chioran stops the show, or did on the first night, with his impersonation of Alceste before the courts, scaling heights of anger that actually exceed any displayed by the hero himself. As Eliante, his female counterpart, Martha Farrell is uneven; sometimes defeated by the script's technical demands, at others sailing right through them, but always believable as another prize worth winning.

The production is refreshingly uncynical about these two. This has been a vintage misanthropic year; Stratford's production isn't better than the one at Tarragon, which was really a different play, but it's as good.

Stratford has another play translated from the French, or at least the French-Canadian: Michel Tremblay's Hosanna, the saga of a disillusioned drag queen. Gareth Potter, shoulders heaving at moments of stress, gives a very good performance of the passionate Liz Taylor impersonator, though he doesn't muster the full venom for the role.

Oliver Becker is excellent as his biker boyfriend, and Weyni Mengesha's production is very well-paced, much the best of the four I've seen. So if I still felt, halfway through the second act, that I now knew as much about these characters as the author could tell me, I guess it must be the play's fault. (I've never understood the theories about the play being a metaphor for a Quebec with an identity crisis, but then I wasn't around at the time.)

Toward the end it gets very sentimental, falling back on having the couple say "I love you" as if it were a revelation. Meanwhile the idea that Hosanna can only become a real man by crossing crossdressing off his list now seems dubious; this is a play that Sky Gilbert might consider updating. Anyhow the production completes a good Stratford season, with several peaks and no clunkers.
