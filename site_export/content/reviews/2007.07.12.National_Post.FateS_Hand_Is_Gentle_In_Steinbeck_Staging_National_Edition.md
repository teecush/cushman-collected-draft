The Stratford production of John Stein-beck's Of Mice and Men begins beautifully as a bucolic fable, with vagrant workers George and Lennie caught as in a snapshot, washing themselves by a riverbank in practically the last peaceful moment either of them is to know. The end of the play will find them back in the same spot, with Lennie, the simple, mouse-crushing giant, about to be shot by the friend and protector who feels compelled to kill him rather than stand by and see him lynched.

George and Lennie have reached the end of a long, ineluctable line. Martha Henry's production captures the sense of fate that traps a stageful of men, and one woman, victims alike of their own natures and of the Great Depression. It does so, though, in excessively gentle fashion, and certainly with less power than was unleashed in last year's CanStage production at the Bluma Appel Theatre, though it may well be the case that Of Mice and Men is simply not a play that one should see twice in quick succession; its insights, psychological and sociological, aren't that transfixing. And though its narrative is beautifully, inexorably structured, you may need time to forget it before succumbing to it again.

The rolling, golden cornfield sets of the CanStage production are missed, though they would hardly be practicable on the Tom Patterson stage. John Pennoyer, much aided by Bonnie Beecher's dappled lights, does a more modest but still evocative job on the great outdoors and a solid one on the bunkhouse and barn that house most of the action. It's more than unfortunate, though, that the play's most crucial scene--an accidental catastrophe that begets more catastrophe -- should be given a staging that's triply remote: on top of a platform, at one end of the stage and behind a rail.

The performances are rather remote, as well: accurate, but faint. Graham Greene's hulking Lennie, the man who kills the things he hugs, is powerful and somewhat affecting, but he seems to be existing on an island of his own. There is little sense of the binding comradeship he has with George, or of their touching dependence on one another. In George's case, the dependence is liberally laced with exasperation, but the impatience shouldn't blot out everything else. In Nicolas Van Burek's performance, surly and explanatory, it does. The bunkhouse crew have flavour, but it's damped down. Jerry Franken is endearingly gnarled as Candy, the crippled oldster who buys into George and Lennie's dream of buying their own farm somewhere, but the scene in which his buddies bully him into letting them kill his mangy old dog is surprisingly lacking in tension.

The presence of Franken, one of the original actors in The Drawer Boy, does, though, make one wonder how much Michael Healey's play owes to Of Mice and Men. Both plays have as their central device a clever man repeatedly telling a life-preserving story to a simple man who has heard it a hundred times before and wants to hear it a hundred times more.

Stephen Russell, though rather mature for the role, is a sympathetic authority figure as Slim, the foreman, and Brian Hamman a magnetically unsympathetic one as Curly, who's the boss's son and doesn't care who knows it. The role of Curly's wife is the one point at which the Stratford production decisively beats last year's Toronto one. The CanStage actress played her as if through the eyes of the men in the play, as a piece of arrant jailbait. Jennifer Mawhinney, though no less sexual, grants her a trapped, vulnerable identity of her own. - In repertory through Sept. 22. Call 1-800- 567-1600 for tickets.
