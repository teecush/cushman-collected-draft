All politicians are egomaniacs, most are corrupt, many are idiots and more than a handful, regardless of party, are indictable crooks. Or would be if there were any justice. Which there isn't.

I have no trouble believing any of this. Even if I wanted to be skeptical, my ongoing exposure to VideoCabaret's comic-strip history of Canada would have worn down my resistance long ago. There are other side effects, though, of being told exactly the same thing about every participant in every chapter of our continental story. You may not lose belief but you do lose interest.

"The only thing one knows for sure is that the new boss will be the same as the old boss." That, taking the words out of my mouth, is Michael Hollingsworth, author and director of the cycle, in his program note. He adds: "What is going to happen has already happened." It happens all over again in Canadian Pacific Scandal, which is Part 6, coming around -- like the whole enterprise -- for the second time. The new bosses, alternating as the show proceeds, are John A. Macdonald, boondoggling over the contracts for the new Canadian Pacific Railway, and Alexander Mackenzie, whose thing is temperance. (It's the Caledonian Age of Canadian politics.) Forced to choose between the colourful carpetbagger and the prohibitionist prig, the voters naturally end up plumping for the latter. Others present include Wilfrid Laurier, who is pompous, and -- the closest thing to a sympathetic figure -- Louis Riel, who goes insane.

I speculated when reviewing an earlier instalment that one reason for VideoCab's habit of working in short, sharp scenes is that they dare not linger on any episode or character for fear of stumbling over something complex, someone who had done something admirable or at least defensible. No chance of that happening here. The problem is that as one surveys this troupe of venal marionettes, one rapidly -- by the end of the second scene, in my case -- ceases to care who is screwing whom. Somewhere off stage, of course, are the ordinary Canadians who are really being screwed, but the play is such a closed system that they barely register. The chief pleasure is the homely one of seeing famous figures from the history books getting their pants pulled down. It's a revenge on school.

The designs (from a team headed by Andy Moro, set and lighting, and Astrid Janson, costumes) are still dazzlingly brazen, the quickness of the changes nearly as unbelievable. As for the actors: The Campbells are coming, the two arch Scots being played by Richard Alan Campbell (MacK) and Greg Campbell (MacD), dour and braw respectively. Jim Jones is, in accordance with VidCab tradition, a truly terrifying bigoted bishop, and Kerry Ann Doherty stands out, in every sense, as a pudding-bellied Alexander Galt.

- - -

VideoCabaret have been at it since 1985. The state of highly skilled monotony that it has taken them 20 years to reach is attained by the two-girl team known as Independent Auntie in 50 minutes flat. Their show Clean Irene and Dirty Maxine, resurrected from the SummerWorks festival of three years ago, is billed as "A Deadly Comedy"; well, they said it. This show, too, harks back to schooldays. It's a rhymed cautionary alphabet. Anna Chatterton and Evalyn Parry, writers and performers, tell us about a gaggle of contemporary urban females, all of whom come to bad ends, from Arty Mary, felled by her own pretensions, to Yoga Lola, caught for keeps in a compromising position. (They do admit that there are certain letters they bypass.)

Sometimes, though not often, one thing leads to another. Arty Mary is confronted at her own exhibition by Busy Lizzie, a critic who -- braver than most in the trade -- tells her what she thinks to her face, before taking off on her own doomed trajectory, too driven to look where others are driving. Chatterton and Parry use their voices percussively and their bodies pneumatically. They're especially good at bending their bodies into complementary shapes. Their physical wit, however, is not matched by their verbal, which stops short at relentless brightness. Their point seems to be that to want anything, or to be anyone, is to invite disaster, but they don't prove it. Even my favourite of their creations, Quaker Fanny, meets an arbitrary end, unless you're prepared to take her soubriquet very literally. You've heard of an idea looking for a show. This is the reverse.

- - -

The bulk of plays from the past 300 years were written for curtained proscenium arch theatres. They can work on open or platform stages, but there's much to be said for seeing and performing them in their natural habitat, especially if you're a student trying to find out how their parts fit together. Most new theatres, especially academic ones, go for the platform look, but York University has just opened a new house, the Sandra Faire and Ivan Fecan Theatre, in traditional proscenium formation -- handsomely done up, what's more, in traditional plush red.

It's part of York's new Accolade Project, which officially opened last Monday. The project is an arts centre, spread over two buildings, that people have been hoping and planning for through most of the 30 years that York's fine-arts faculty has been in existence. It also contains performance, rehearsal and exhibition spaces for music, dance, film and visual arts.

Phillip Silver, York's dean of fine arts and a distinguished stage designer in his own right, has done much to bring the Accolade Project to fruition. For him, the single most important thing about it is that it will give students in all disciplines the opportunity to work in facilities of the highest professional standard so that they'll be prepared for similar environments in the real world. If they're lucky, that is.
