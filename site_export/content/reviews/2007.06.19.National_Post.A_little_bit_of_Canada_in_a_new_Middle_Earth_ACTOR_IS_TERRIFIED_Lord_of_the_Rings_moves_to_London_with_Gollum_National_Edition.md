Tonight, for the first official time, Michael Therriault will reveal himself to West End audiences by slithering 50 vertical feet down the front cloth of the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane.

Therriault is repeating in London the award-winning performance he gave last year in Toronto as Gollum in the musical of The Lord of the Rings. That performance was contortionist enough, but it didn't incorporate this particular acrobatic feat. True, he has the support of an invisible wire, and he has had six weeks of previews to get used to it. Still, when I had tea with him at a London hotel just before those previews were due to start, he confessed to being "terrified. But," he added, "I'm proud that I'm actually doing it."

And that's just the start. Gollum, this time around, goes out as hair-raisingly as he goes in. One criticism made of the Toronto show was that its climax, Gollum plunging to his death in the fiery craters of Mount Doom, was insufficiently clear. Its restaging involves Therriault falling backward from a height of three metres "onto a giant bean-bag." And in between these extremes, he acts and sings -- very well, if his Toronto performance is any guide.

When I talked to Therriault, he had been in rehearsal for almost two-and-a-half months, while accepting that "most of my rehearsing will come in previews." In the midst of all the show's spectacle, Gollum's is an intimate role, most of whose scenes are either on his own or with the two principal hobbits, Frodo and Sam.

A couple of days before our meeting, I had dropped in on a technical rehearsal and found it to be very technical indeed, with acting about the last thing on anybody's mind. The stalls were a solid bank of computers. The director, Matthew Warchus, looked commendably unfazed by the task of helming one of the most complex and expensive shows in theatrical history.

Warchus is co-adaptor of The Lord of the Rings as well as director, so one of his jobs has been to cut it. According to producer Kevin Wallace, the show will be brought down to three hours by combining the kingdoms of Gondor and Rohan. Personally, I would have liked to see the same amalgamation imposed on the show's two races of Elves, but they remain separate. However, the song Lothlorien, which even I found a bore in Toronto, has been turned from a static solo by Galadriel into a full-scale Elvish anthem. The show, Wallace says, is now "more of a musical" with a clearer storyline.

Its Canadian content is now minimal. Three of the four major hobbits remain from Toronto, but they were British to begin with. The new Aragorn is a French performer, Jerome Pradon, but Toronto audiences may remember him as the title character in the ill-fated musical Napoleon.

And then, of course, there is Therriault, the self-described "small-town kind of guy" from Acadia who still seems slightly bemused at finding himself near the centre of a West End megamusical. This despite a Broadway stint in Fiddler on the Roof and a TV triumph as Tommy Douglas. He's endearingly self- deprecating about his position among Canadian actors. He won his spurs at the Stratford Festival, where he was one of the great young discoveries of the Richard Monette regime and one of the first graduates of the conservatory.

When it came time to leave Stratford to play the star role of Leo Bloom in the Toronto company of The Producers, he was not merely scared, but "depressed." His gloom should soon have lifted. His performance as the timorous accountant was a triumph and established him as a genuine acting-singing-dancing triple threat.

And it might well have paved the way for Gollum. Both characters share a certain manic obsequiousness. Both spend time crawling on the ground. And Leo's attachment to his blanket has a definite kinship with Gollum's passion for his Precious.
