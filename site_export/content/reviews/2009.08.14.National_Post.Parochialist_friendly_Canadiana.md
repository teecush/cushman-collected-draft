BILLY BISHOP GOES TO WAR

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

---

Here is one Canadian classic that deserves the title. Make that two Canadian classics: the play itself, written 30 years ago by John Gray in collaboration with Eric Peterson, and Peterson himself, reprising its title role. Since Billy Bishop Goes to War tells the story of Canada's First World War flying ace in his own words, which include a lot of reported conversations, he gets to play a lot of other people as well. As before he shares the stage with Gray, who delivers the odd spoken word but mostly confines himself to sitting at the piano playing and singing his own musical score, with Peterson joining in on the vocals. My impression is that Gray now does more of the singing, and Peterson less, than used to be the case but I wouldn't swear to it. Since that Soulpepper revival has been directed, with exemplary tact and imagination, by Ted Dykstra, it's tempting to think of it as One Piano, Four Hands.

I've only seen the piece once before, when it visited London in 1981 and when I didn't like it much: Blame it on my youth and, I guess, my parochialism. I didn't know Canada and, though I think I appreciated his energy and virtuosity, I didn't know Peterson; I now have an investment in both. Here, he's in robe and pajamas, as the older Billy remembering his youth, which lends a piquant edge to one of its climactic sequences in which he goes on a mission, also in pajamas under his flying-gear. Billy, as presented here, is a dead-end kid who joins up without quite knowing why. As the play's recurring theme song puts it "We were off to fight the Hun / We would shoot him with a gun ..." I used to resent the redundancy of that lyric, but its naivete, and the simplicity of the tune that accompanies it, now seemss appropriate. Billy, at this point, is naive, like so many young men who went to that war, both British and Canadian. "We were off to fight the Hun / And it looked like lots of fun." For him, it does turn out to be fun, though it takes time. Having, rather to his surprise, landed a cavalry command, he finds his hopes and prospects sinking in the mud of the trenches. Then he sees a plane landing. He talks himself into a position as a cockpit observer, and from there it's a quick progression to being a pilot.

"Somehow it didn't seem like war at all ..." It soon does, especially if you think of war

as a matter of survival. It's kill or be killed, and Billy proves incredibly adept at blowing enemy pilots out of the sky; in fact he racks up a record score, for which he receives multiple medals. He does this without ever seeming especially bloodthirsty, though he does develop an intense competitive streak. We never really know how he feels about it all; he writes letters home but they don't go into broader issues. We hear the British higher-ups making jokes about "colonials," and we see him being praised and patronised by the upper crust, but Billy's own opinions about being a North American embroiled in a European conflict are not explored. He isn't introspective.

This limits the play in some regards -- you leave with the odd feeling that you never really got to know the hero

-- but it also frees it from any temptation to preach. The ironies are there, built into the story, and we can make of them what we may. The closest the show comes to driving them home is in a fierce cabaret song, with an angular melody, about those who don't make it, performed in the style of a French chanteuse. Peterson does this with ferocious delicacy, and a bizarre physical daintiness. He moves beautifully throughout; his whole performance is a dance.

Among his other impersonations, I especially enjoyed his air ministry recruiting wallah, who has the voice, the praying-mantis eyes and the gleefully rubbing hands of an English Montgomery Burns. Gray also does his share of expert mimicry, both as singer and composer; there's one song in which he sounds, whether by accident or design, like a dead vocal ringer for Randy Newman. Camellia Koo has designed a simple objet-trouve kind of set; projections are held in reserve until the last moment and are the more effective for it. Peterson himself remains a sharp-eyed scamp underneath it all, a jewel in a cock-eyed Canadian crown. He adds, in himself, a layer to the evening, something and someone to cheer for. - Billy Bishop Goes to War runs until Aug. 29. Call 416-866-8666 or visit soulpepper.cafor tickets and information.
