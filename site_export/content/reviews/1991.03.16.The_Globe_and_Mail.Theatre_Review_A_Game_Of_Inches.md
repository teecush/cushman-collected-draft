THEATRE REVIEW A GAME OF INCHES

Saturday, March 16, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

WRITTEN BY AND STARRING LINDA GRIFFITHS Directed by Sandra Balcovske At Theatre Passe Muraille Mainspace, in Toronto In her latest solo turn, actress and writer Linda Griffiths presents a concoction thats too elaborate to be a comedy and too sketchy to cut it as a play A Game of Inches comes up short BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail IHAVE only seen one previous Linda Griffiths show, the embarrassingly amateurish Maggie and Pierre. I know it's considered a landmark in Canadian theatre, but so much the worse for Canadian theatre. I recall a smug endless monologue that was tickled pink by its own daring at lampooning the First Couple, and laid itself in the aisles at the audacity of having both of them done by the same actor. Since that actor's sole idea of characterization was to portray everyone as Barbra Streisand joke-Jewish, jabbing nasally at anything that resembled a comic point, the notion palled rather quickly.

I am pleased to report that, since then, the Griffiths timing has improved no end. The character she assumes in her new solo turn, A Game of Inches, is loaded with pent-up angst, but Griffithss delivery verges on the laid-back. The effect, as of someone teetering high-heeled on the edge of desperation, is rather piquant.

She no longer honks. She does squawk. Indeed I wondered for a time whether she had any lower register at all. It turned out she does - it comes out when her character briefly impersonates other characters, mostly male - but she sure is economical with it. Mostly, whether by accident or design, her range is cramped, like certain kinds of Oriental music. She plays on a single note of haughty hysteria. But there is something appropriately stylish about that; Pamela, the lady she plays, has dallied with some Eastern philosophy, not happily.

She hasnt dallied with men at all; not till recently. Alone in her 40s, surrounded by books, she decides with many misgivings that it's about time and invites into her spare room a man named John. One day he utters the fateful words, "Mind if I watch the game?" and from then on they are both glued to the television. Baseball invades her dreams; the Blue Jays become her fantasy-life. But on an earthly plane nothing happens and eventually John moves out, leaving Pamela to lament an ice- box stuffed and chances missed. It's very sad and very funny, no?

No. Its quite sad and a little funny. Pamela confronts the world with a mixture of rage and resignation. (Well, thats what they do, isn't it, she keeps saying of the men about whose filthy habits she has heard and imagined so much.) Griffiths brings her to a shadow of eccentric life, but in the end Pamela is just an excuse for a performance: one that gets only skeletal support from its script. The writing gestures hopefully toward black humour; Pamela leads off by telling us that she is suffering from "terror and horror and loss and panic . . . and terror and horror and loss and panic." (The audience laughs dutifully at the repetition; it can take a hint.) Sometimes she settles for getting laughs by dropping familiar Toronto names; sometimes this blossoms into a full routine. Even I, who could not care less about baseball in general or the Blue Jays in particular, could appreciate that her instant impressions of particular players were sharply observed and executed. They turn Pamela into a stand-up comedienne, working out of her own desperation - a role in which she seems more credible than at any other time. But A Game of Inches is too elaborate for a comedy act, too sketchy to be a play.

It is the third hour-length piece I have seen this week. I begin to yearn for a really elaborate play, one full of themes and characters, maybe with an intermission or two. I shall regret having said that.
