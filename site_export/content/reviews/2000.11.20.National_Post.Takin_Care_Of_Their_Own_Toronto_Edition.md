THUNDERSTRUCK

Factory Theatre, Toronto

The thunder in Thunderstruck is especially good, as is the lightning. Together they blow up a real McCoy of a storm, fearsomely created in lighting and sound. It's how the story starts. Lightning bolts invade the home of the Lasting clan, killing the father and mother of the house. This leaves their children -- three boys and a girl, all adopted -- to fend for one another, with the grudging consent of the local authorities. The three brothers are called, in chronological order, Rock, William and Fred-James. The sister, younger than any of them, is called Naomi, and she has a beautiful voice. When she is 20 she leaves home to tour as a country singer. A couple of years later she returns, brain-damaged. The brothers devote themselves to taking care of her, a situation that brings out both the best and the worst in them, laying bare all that divides them and, most essentially, all that unites them. The ending is drastic.

Thunderstruck is a play by Quebec writer Daniel Danis, premiered in Montreal nearly three years ago. It comes to us in an English translation by the ubiquitous Linda Gaboriau and in a production by the One Yellow Rabbit company of Calgary that has already triumphed at the Edinburgh Festival. (Much interesting Canadian work these days only reaches Toronto by way of Scotland.) So a play that must originally have been set in a rural area of Quebec now arrives carrying a strong whiff of the Prairies; theatrical Ontario is being set upon from both sides at once. The production taps into two deep- seated Toronto prejudices: a) that outside the metropolis, everything is wilderness; b) that theatre in other provinces is more audacious and imaginative than anything brewed here.

Thunderstruck is a remarkable example of text-based theatre, physically expressed. The four actors playing the siblings are the only ones who appear, and most of the time they stick to their own roles. But they are storytellers as well as impersonators, and sometimes they speak of themselves in the past tense and in the third person; they also, briefly, take on the voices of other characters. They begin the play as a chorus, recounting the history of the fatal storm, referring to their dead parents as "the father and mother, our own." As that suggests, they talk funny. "Unrile!" one of the brothers instructs another, this being apparently the family patois for "cool it." That probably sounds awful (and it does have its moments) but it works, with equal credit to the author, the translator, and the current interpreters who make it the material of a myth.

This is a folk tale but a modern one: The Lastings are devoted to their television and VCR, and deeply if skeptically concerned with the state of the health-care system. It's also a sort of psychological study, and the language has a function here, too. The last injunction given to the children by the parents, their own, was to "stay welded," and they do, chafing at the bonds but accepting them, often exulting in them. Like the children of multiple births - - who often communicate so well between themselves that they have less need than others to explain themselves to the outside world - Rock, William, Fred-James and Naomi were slow to learn speech. To encourage them their mother devised a contraption that she called "the Say-Sayer" (you put a new word in it and you get a reward) and this becomes the sacred symbol of their lives. In this production, designed by Terry Middleton, it's an intricate metal holder that exactly mirrors the structure of the house. It's false economy, though, to make it double as the family TV set.

That's my only cavil with the presentation. The production is directed by Blake Brooker and staged (which presumably means choreographed) by Denise Clarke; and the collaboration has produced strong, gentle work. Clarke also appears as Fred-James, the youngest and most delicate in feeling of the brothers, and she is especially fine. One of the play's most telling shifts of perspective is a quarrel between the older two that is relayed to us entirely throigh the voice of Fred-James, who speaks the words of both. Michael Green plays Rock, the oldest and most capable who cooks for the others and has the firmest connection to the outside world but is unthinkingly dictatorial in his own domain. Andy Curtis is William who has both admired and resented him for years ("when I was 10, I wrote on the red cardboard of the dictionary, 'my brother Rock is crazy'") and eventually takes a bloody revenge. There is a lovely moment when the trio stand side by side like Christmas figures, each of their faces illumined by a different-coloured lamp -- an effect that depends of course on both actors and technicians hitting their marks with absolute precision and thus typical of the show as a whole.

Elizabeth Stepkowksi as Naomi never gets to speak a coherent word, but sings hauntingly and has great physical presence. This she needs, since she spends much of the play in a harness, attached to a frame in the backyard, which is her brother's preferred site -- in warm weather, anyway -- for looking after her. (Who'd be an actor, I thought, as they winched her up.) The play may be saying something - - though nothing, I confess, very novel or profound -- about men's attitude to women. The brothers nagged and pampered her when she was small; adored her when she was well and could sing; treat her as a doll, though certainly a cherished one, when she isn't and can't. They still -- when they aren't prodding her, dosing her, dressing or making her up -- worship her. It seems that the lightning that killed their parents also entered her, leaving her with a tendency to toward phosphorescence; i.e. from time to time she lights up. For relief, once a month they go into town to party with select local girls and "untease that whoop-de-doo itch"; there were times when I was irreverently reminded of Seven Brides for Seven Brothers. They also live in a state of permanent mutual mistrust with what they call "the muncipalities" and it is the irruption of this outside world that precipitates the climax; a typical tale of a benevolent crowd of busy-bodies turning into a lynch mob. It's good to see a western again, even if it started out as an Eastern. This is my first encounter with the much-touted One Yellow Rabbit, and -- as an Anglocentric literary type -- I approached them, or it, all ready to be unimpressed. I was hooked.
