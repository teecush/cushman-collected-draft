Five ladies, all clad in fashionably inescapable black, sit in a row onstage and read statements written by other ladies about the emotional crises in their lives and the articles of clothing with which they associate them. The fact that none of the actresses has to learn any lines cuts down on rehearsal time, and makes the gig attractive to well-known performers who are between other engagements. Love, Loss, and What I Wore ranks in this proliferating semi-theatrical genre somewhere between Love Letters and -- given its gynocentricity -- a more inhibited version of The Vagina Monologues.

The show has its origin in an autobiographical book by American writer Ilene Beckerman. Two other American writers, Nora and Delia Ephron, have buffed it up by soliciting testimonials from acquaintances, and intercutting them. There cant be any plot, but the evening is subdivided by categories, each announced by one of the performers. Someone will say "the training bra"; and everyone onstage -- not to mention a sizeable proportion of the audience -- snaps to nostalgic attention. Its an evening of anecdote, lasting nearly two hours without intermission, and dread attends the mention of each new garment, as it means that the proceedings are about to be dragged out even further. They do, to be fair, make some amusing capital out of this at the final curtain.

For a boring show, it can be quite entertaining. The performers are all good and some of them are famous. Louise Pitre deals with the original Beckerman material, and I'd call her the standout if she didn't spend the entire show sitting down. She is stationed a little apart from the others, and more or less conducts them. She turns over drawings of dresses on an easel, and even gets to draw one herself. Textually, she delivers with exemplary tact and wit, never missing a wry comic point but never underlining one, either. Pitre is best known of course as a musical performer, but Im not trying to be back-handed when I say that Ive liked her best in shows -- this one and The Last Days of Judas Iscariot -- in which she's only talked. (Well, that's a bit hasty; I loved her in Annie Get Your Gun.) There are two opposed traps inherent in the rehearsed-reading format -- dry recitation and frenetic vocal colouring -- and Pitre, alone in this company, evades them both.

Shes helped by being cast, if not as a consistent character, then at least as a consistent voice. All the others have to do turns, and they all at some point overdo or underdo. This applies even to Andrea Martin, the most experienced stand-up comedienne of them all; she takes time to find a comfort level, though once she does shes razor-sharp, especially in a diatribe about purses or in a vengeful obituary for a husband who is, unpardonably, still alive. The latter is one of several occasions in which the script seems to want to break free from its own professed subject, or at least its own professed metaphor. Sharron Matthews has some fun with the frustrations of shopping. Paula Brancati has some nice wry moments. Mary Walsh has a couple of set-pieces; one, about choosing a bra after a mastectomy, is obviously meant to be the evening's gesture towards depth, and it does contain the script's most startlingly funny line, but shes really more memorable in grumpier mode, describing how to dress for accessibility on a prison visit while still getting past the guards. Occasionally, the players get to interact; some early exchanges about sartorially rebellious teenagers get the laughter of recognition: from women, obviously, but also from any man whos had a daughter, a sister or, come to that, a mother. But mostly, the show seems to be congratulating itself on its own superficiality; it takes for granted that all women are obsessed with their wardrobes, and never even asks why. Whenever it seems to be reaching out to something wider, it crawls back into its closet. The real costume it wears is a straitjacket.

It's closed now, but I'd like to record that in a recent show at, but not by, Second City, an actor playing Stephen Harper got up and said "shows with The Musical in the title are always terrible," suggesting that, whatever his future as a prime minister, he might make a hell of a theatre critic. Unhappily, the show in which he made this declaration was called Stephen Harper: The Musical, and it demonstrated that you can't escape a curse simply by acknowledging it. It showed the PM being persuaded to boost his popularity ratings by becoming the subject of a Broadway show, also called Stephen Harper: The Musical, a double structure that brought off the remarkable feat of being thin and confusing at the same time. Its closing number was called Nothing Rhymes with Canada; that may be true, but it still doesn't excuse setting the word so that the accent falls on the last syllable. There was a good joke about the Tories being hoisted on their own arts-cutting petard, and the performers were mostly all right. Mr. Harper himself emerged as a put-upon patsy which may, at least on a Toronto stage, be a first.
