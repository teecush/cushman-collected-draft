THEATRE REVIEW

Winners and Losers

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

Marcus Youssef and James Long, fortyish independent theatre luminaries from Vancouver, sit at opposite ends of a table and play a game whose name is the same as the title of their show: Winners and Losers. The game goes like this: One player will say a word, drop a name, and the other will say whether the person or issue cited could best be described as a winner or as a loser. Then one of them rings a bell. (That is not a metaphor.) Some examples prove hard to categorize. Sylvia Plath, for example, is ranked as a winner for writing The Bell Jar and as a loser for killing herself. Then there's Stephen Hawking: Which, they ask, would he prefer to have, his legacy or his legs? That doesnt play as crass as it probably reads.

For all that, the idea of assigning everything and everyone to one team or the other gets to seem both arbitrary and superficial. Maybe thats the point, questioning a way of thinking and being that turns society into a contest. But it's hard to feel that they're criticizing a game that theyre so intent on playing. Also, when they do bring up an interesting topic - the suggestion that South African apartheid was modelled on Canadas treatment of its First Nations, or the likelihood that modern history would have been completely different if the Mensheviks had been the winners in the Russian Revolution instead of the Bolsheviks - the quickfire rules of their game prevent them for pursuing it. They're both very fast talkers, Youssef especially.

One of their glancing blows is really searching: The accusation that in our society the sick or just the old count as losers. This comes quite late in the show, and the proceedings do get more interesting around the halfway mark, starting when Long temporarily leaves the stage and Youssef slows down and gets confidential with us. The evening takes several of these sharp turns, marked off by charged pauses, reminding us that a director (Chris Abraham) has been at work and that the performers aren't making it up as they go along.

Indeed they confess that most of their exchanges are transcribed from months and months of conversation. Theyre performing a script with maybe a few topical adlibs thrown in. Impromptu or rehearsed, they do seem to enjoy wrong-footing one another. And it gets personal. When Long returns, he accuses Youssef, whose father is a high-up banker, of being a privileged dilettante, dabbling in left-wing theatre and left-wing politics while waiting to inherit a million. The actual word Long uses is "tourist," and he instances Youssefs long delay in visiting Egypt, where his family came from, and his spending his time there being photographed at the Pyramids.

Long's own upbringing was far from sheltered; Youssef expresses sympathy but remarks that Long, if not rich, is not currently doing too badly and spends more on a pair of jeans than he himself would ever dream of doing. Long cops to both charges. Everything they say about one another is, I'm sure, factual. Blood is drawn. (That is a metaphor.) A friendship shatters before our eyes. Except it doesnt really. Theatre is never as real as it might like to be and these actors, even using their own names and their own lives, are acting: acting well, too, with Long dominating the first half and Youssef the second. In that sense theyre both winners. Or both losers. Whichever: They take a curtain-call together, and you know they'll be colleagues, doing the show again, tomorrow night. We've been taken for a fairly enjoyable ride.
