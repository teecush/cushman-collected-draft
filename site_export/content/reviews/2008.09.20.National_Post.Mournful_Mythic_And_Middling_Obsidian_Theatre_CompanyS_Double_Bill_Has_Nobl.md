The Obsidian Theatre Company's new double bill is a conscious attempt to break out of what might be called the ghetto conception of black theatre. One play is a domestic drama in which black actors play contemporary urbanites who might be of any race. The other is an updating of a Greek myth that has been given a specifically black context but one whose geographical location is very far from here. This is a praiseworthy initiative, or pair of initiatives, but neither play is much fun to sit through.

Late, by Marcia Johnson, is about a recently bereaved woman and the new inhabitant of her basement, who's there on a sublet. This rather tenuous relationship needs considerable setting-up, involving flashbacks and a third, intermediary, character who is otherwise superfluous. Donna has been happily married to a jazz pianist whose abrupt departure from her life leads her into a frenzy of reminiscence, mainly concerned with her early-morning routine. Carol, the new arrival, recently abandoned by her own boyfriend, makes it her mission to rescue Donna from her grief, though she seems rather more in need of rescue herself. Anyway, the two of them turn out to be good for one another, though scenes that show it are both cloudy and sketchy.

Meanwhile there's Locksley, Donna's original tenant, who is also a musician and who goes on the road, thereby making room for Carol, until circumstances and an anxious mother conspire to persuade him that " '70s rock can get on without me." He gets some other good lines, plus a hopeless lust for Donna, but he doesn't get or contribute much else, and it's a shock to realize that we're meant to care about him as well as about the two underwritten women. All three characters have to, in some sense, move on, to come to terms with the conflicts between their dreams and their realities, but that's not much to take home.

Johnson is an experienced radio playwright, and her play unfolds in what is now the expected succession of short scenes. This means that we spend much time watching the actors moving furniture in the half-light, to effect changes that don't look all that startling when we get there. The 19th century had heavy scenic realism; the Elizabethans had non-scenic fluidity. We seem to have stuck ourselves with the worst of both worlds and -- more to the point -- the best of neither. We get plays without shape leading, inexorably, to performances with the same deficiency. Marjorie Chan's direction is pretty lax in terms of what it lets its actors get away with. Sabryn Rock has an attractively world-weary presence as Donna, but there is little sense that she and Edwige Jean-Pierre, a querulous Carol, are conducting a conversation, let alone a relationship. Each strikes an attitude and then waits for the other to strike hers. The general effect, in all departments, is of something well-meaning but shallow.

The mythic Medea was a woman who killed her children to spite her husband. Euripides, the first playwright to tell this story, was able to mask its lack of endearingness by not allowing us too close a look at the kids. Wesley Enoch's Black Medea boldly begins by letting us not only see, but hear the child (only one in this version), a recognizably modern boy who, in a clever touch, is playing with a model of a boat, an ancient Greek trireme. We brace ourselves to feel the horrors of the tale as we have never felt them before. But by the time, the actual infanticide rolls around, we don't care, having long lost touch with recognizable reality.

Or at least with any that we could recognize without more help than we're given. Black Medea is an Australian play, rooted in Australian Aboriginal culture. This Medea is a woman from a remote settlement who defies local tradition to marry a Jason who lives and thrives in the outside world. Eventually he proves unfaithful: not in the specific, shabby-but-motivated fashion of his classical prototype but by dallying with unspecified "sluts." The social gradations, the different worlds from which Medea and Jason have sprung and in which they both have to live, are never filled in. In fact, after a tight couple of scenes in which past and present do seem to be illuminating one another, the play, as directed by Philip Akin, just about abandons drama and falls back on incantation. A two-woman chorus comes on, to lure Medea back to the magical world from which she has descended and she -- well, she either regresses or she finds her true spiritual self, depending on how you want to look at it. The upshot seems to be that it's all right to murder your son, providing you paint your face before doing it.

The chorus rather lost me, but I think that at one point I heard them talking about a man "raping his mother," which sounds like the wrong myth. Any countervailing argument is muffled by a text that presents Jason as an opportunist wife-beater, and a performance (by Lindsay Owen Pierre) that carries no conviction even on that level. Medea, denied the clever plotting of the original, isn't even allowed the dignity of her own murderousness; she's more victim than avenger, and Audrey Dwyer's initially powerful performance eventually --or not so eventually --becomes monotonous. Black Medea is a more impressive piece than its companion (though I'm tempted to put "impressive" in quotes) but it's also more boring You may know the feeling.
