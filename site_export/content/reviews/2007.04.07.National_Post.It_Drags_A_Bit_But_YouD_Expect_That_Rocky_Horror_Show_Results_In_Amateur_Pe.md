Commercially, the best thing that ever happened to The Rocky Horror Show was The Rocky Horror Picture Show. Artistically, if the word means anything in this context, it was probably the worst.

Many people, I know, don't believe that there was a stage Rocky Horror Show before the movie. But there was; I saw it. It debuted in the upstairs space at Londons Royal Court Theatre in 1973 and then moved up the street into a converted cinema in the Kings Road, Chelsea -- just the right locale for a fashion-conscious cross- dressing rock musical that was also a spoof on low-budget Frankenstein-monster pictures. It ran there for years, a neat and stylish little entertainment that never took itself too seriously. Recently, it was voted the most memorable production ever at the Royal Court, a theatre that has generally taken itself very seriously indeed. You may regard this either as an intolerable insult to the high-minded house of John Osborne, Edward Bond, Caryl Churchill and Sarah Kane, or as serving them right.

Anyroad, hit though it was in London, Rocky Horror flopped ignominiously in New York, which normally would have skewered its chances in the rest of the world. But then there came the movie, cheap and grungy (so I'm told, I've never seen it). This, too, tanked until audiences discovered the bliss of shouting responses back at the screen. The movie became a cult, and subsequent stage productions, of which there have been many, have had to imitate it.

The new CanStage production, directed by Ted Dykstra, seems to proceed on the principle of joining what it cant beat. It gives us a warmup prologue in which Eddie Glen, in cockney cheeky-chappie mode, welcomes us and invites our participation. He tells us pretty much what to shout (slut," mainly) and when we can shout it. The first-night audience certainly took him at his word, and then some. But there is a difference between shouting at actors on a screen and actors on a stage. The latter is boorish. Even at a movie screening, though, I would guess that the sound of people in the auditorium tirelessly trying to show how much smarter they are than the entertainment theyre watching would be "smugly. (That's an adjective I've just coined meaning smug and ugly. But it fooled spell-check, which thinks its an adverb.) And now that the whole thing has become a ritual, with preprogrammed responses, its pointless. There's no spontaneity here. We have amateur performers competing with professionals. They fail, of course. But they do succeed, much assisted by a zealously insensitive amplification system, at drowning out the original's words.

Ill admit that this hardly counts as desecrating a masterpiece. Richard OBrien, who wrote the book, music and lyrics, is a minor talent (his one subsequent musical, T. Zee, was a disaster that hasn't even had a schlock movie to rescue it) who, once in his life, got lucky by getting clever. His lyrics are insolent rather than witty (damn it, Janet" is about as funny as they get, though "there's a light shining over at the Frankenstein place is my favourite line), but his tunes heat the place up. However, like most rock, pastiche or otherwise, they are static rather than dramatic.

Stasis turns out to be the shows main problem or, perhaps more appositely, its main drag. Dykstras direction has more zing and energy than anything -- certainly anything musical -- seen on the Bluma stage in some years but, staking so much on keeping things moving, it's defeated by a second act that jumps up and down instead of progressing. You probably know the deal. Brad and Janet, all- American teenagers in love, seek refuge from a storm in the castle of Dr. Frank 'n Furter, a mad scientist who also happens to be omnisexual. He seduces both Brad and Janet, using identical lines on both (this, staged on an upright bed, is the productions best scene) and, it's safe to say, changes their lives forever. He test- tube incubates a hunk called Rocky, not apparently to take over the world but to have a new boy toy to play with. And, boy, is Frank mad when Rocky starts playing with others. This doesn't get us very far, so the show starts remembering its sci-firoots and drags in a Dr. Strangelove clone to thwart Franks rather vague plans. The upshot is that two of Franks minions take off in a rocket for the transsexual planet of Transylvania (and you thought it was a country), while Frank, for some reason, immolates himself.

The cast perform at fever pitch; they stay on it, too, which is impressive. Adam Brazier's Frank doesn't have the glitter or the surprising pathos of the roles creator, Tim Curry, but he has a commanding grin and an even more commanding voice. There is comparably strong acting and singing from Ron Pederson and Mairi Babb as the kids. Eddie Glenn reappears in a couple of cameos, in one of which he gets to lead the most invigorating number, Hot Patootie." Jody Ripplingers choreography is rote but reasonable; Erika Connor's costumes are, in all senses, sensational. The show's narration is delivered by the revered John Neville, on grainy inserts of black-and-white film. He does it with great style (and has a lovely dismissive parting line, probably of his own invention), but the device does deprive us of the originals most delightful moment, in which an onstage narrator, a staid BBC type, suddenly got caught up in the big Time Warp number. Perhaps because he was on film, Neville got shouted down by the audience more than anyone. This made it even harder to follow the story. It also, since he himself was in the first night audience, seemed very rude.
