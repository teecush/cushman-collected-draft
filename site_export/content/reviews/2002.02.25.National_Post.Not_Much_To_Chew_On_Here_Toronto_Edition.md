THE EDIBLE WOMAN

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - The protagonist of Margaret Atwood's 1969 novel The Edible Woman spends much of the book engaged to be married. There is a mid-story chapter that begins, "Marian was walking slowly down the aisle, keeping pace with the gentle music." We think for a moment that we have been fast-forwarded to Marian's wedding. It turns out we are accompanying her on a visit to the supermarket. Try bringing that effect off in the theatre. Or even in a movie.

Dave Carley's dramatization, which has arrived in Toronto from the Vancouver Playhouse, doesn't try. This is probably wise, but such wisdom multiplied comes out as folly. Like many such adaptations, this one keeps the narrative, most of the characters and even the dialogue, but loses the overall tone. In other words, it sacrifices what made the original worth reading in the first place -- in this case its wit -- and has nothing of its own to offer in exchange.

Marian, in her twenties, works for a consumer-research company; it's taken for granted she will have to leave when she marries. Her fiance, Peter, is a sleek young lawyer with the marks of success already upon him. On one of her questionnairing quests Marian meets Duncan, a preternaturally self-absorbed graduate student; he becomes Peter's rival, more in her head than in her bed.

Meanwhile, her roommate, Ainsley, has decided she wants a baby but not a husband and sets about seducing Marian's old college friend Len into performing the necessary biological operation; he, when he finds out what he has got into, is horrified. (He took Ainsley for a virgin -- a tribute to her acting skills. He prefers virgins. He shudders at the whole messy idea of birth.)

Under all these professional and emotional pressures Marian comes, horrified, to see herself as something to be consumed. She begins to identify herself with her food and -- in sympathy, as it were -- virtually stops eating.

The novel is in three parts. The first is narrated by Marian herself; the second is still seen entirely from her point of view, but told in the third person, as if she were now conducting a survey on herself; at the end, some resolution achieved, she's back on first. It's a puzzling device while you're reading, but it finally falls into place.

The play tries to reproduce it: Marian still narrates, and, in theory, the switch in pronouns should have a powerfully estranging theatrical effect. (Shall we forget the word "alienation"? Oh, I'm sorry, you already have.) In practice, it just glides by. This may have something to do with the central performance.

Marian is repeatedly said to be capable and intelligent, and the story loses all interest if she's played as some kind of idiot ingenue. Jillian Fargey goes through the first half of the evening on a simper; by the time she suggests some strength and intelligence, it's too late. Marian recalls that in her student days she scuppered an incipient relationship by making an ironic remark. The woman we see hardly seems capable of an ironic thought.

Some of the book's other characters have been conflated -- more, I imagine, in the cause of financial than of artistic economy. Ainsley now not only lives with Marian but works with her. Her other colleagues, the three "office virgins," are reduced to one who also doubles, improbably, as her supervisor. Duncan's two male room- mates, whom he humorously describes as his parents, are now also one.

If Duncan is half-orphaned, then so is the play; it feels drastically under-populated. This goes especially for a party scene in which everybody is meant to collide and pair off; designed as farce with underpinnings, it now just seems arbitrary. The introduction of a line of cardboard cut-outs to represent the actual guests is a real act of desperation.

There is a lot that seems desperate in Timothy Bond's production; it doesn't so much plan its effects as settle for them. We start with a tableau: Peter in the bath, fully clothed and humping (there really is no other word) an absent Marian. She's absent because she's at the side of the stage, telling us about it. But we don't concentrate on her commentary or on her detachment from her own body. We only register how crass Peter looks; and maybe we laugh and maybe we don't. It sets a tone all right, but it's the wrong one; it's the show that seems crass. (The incident is taken from the book, but it appears there at a later point and to more elegantly glancing effect.)

Later we get elaborate, and totally unnecessary, commuter scenes, to frame -- and obscure -- purely functional exchanges of information. They do nothing to create a sense of place or of time. The use of '60s songs (Atwood's text seems totally impervious to pop culture) does nothing to help us adjust to a world at once so like our own and so different from it, and Charlotte Dean's minimalist sets are garishly unhelpful.

The props, in a similar vein, are all mimed -- with one important, and obviously purposeful, exception. At the end Marian, in a gesture of self-exorcism, bakes a cake in the shape of a woman, and this we are allowed to see, though not very well. It is, after all, hardly a stage-size image. Even on the page it seems effortful, but there it doesn't matter as much. Drama may not be a higher form than the novel, but it is certainly a harder one; to use the appropriate imagery, one swallows a novelist's suggestions but one's stomach rebels when they take physical form on a stage. There, you have to believe.

Belief in this case is not much assisted by the acting, which is seriously substandard, certainly for this address. Tara Samuel is just about all right as Ainsley, and Daren Keay rather good as Duncan, though he is more your standard bohemian youth than the seriously creepy figure the author seems to have envisaged.

Alec McClure is compelled to play Len with a caricature of an English accent on the flimsy grounds that he has recently returned from working in the UK; given this, it would surely make sense for him to lose it when he, so to speak, loses it -- i.e. when the contemplation of Ainsley's fecundity reduces him to hysteria.

Lynne Cormack is doubly atrocious as the vestigial virgin (grimly cartooned) and as the heroines' censorious landlady. The latter character has been stripped of her original, more flavourful appellation of "the lady down below," which would counterpoint her with Marian's unseen bosses, "the men upstairs." This is hardly the most damaging decision of the evening, but it is one of the less accountable.
