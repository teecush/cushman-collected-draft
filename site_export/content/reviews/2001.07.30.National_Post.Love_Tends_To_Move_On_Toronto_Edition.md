LA RONDE

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - The Soulpepper company return brilliantly to form with La Ronde, that century-old exploration of the sexual daisy-chain, recast in the form of a circle. It is remarkable in having a rigorous, even rigid, directorial concept that yet allows for great individual creativity from the actors. That is a rare combination.

It is not flawless. There is a kind of chocolate called a Viennese whirl and that is pretty much what Arthur Schnitzler, Austrian playwright and physician, gives us here; it is episodic by definition, and a synopsis challenges the resources of the English language in coming up with uncensorable synonyms for intercourse. In Jason Shermans new, Canadianized adaptation, a Hooker services an Athlete who screws a Maid who is laid by a Student who has an affair with a Wife who has sex with her Husband who debauches a Girl who is picked up by a Writer who fornicates with an Actress who seduces a Politician who finds himself in bed with the Hooker. Everybody, literally and figuratively, fucks everybody else.

Sherman has updated the action as well as transplanting it; his Politician -- actually a Senator -- was a Count when Schnitzler last set eyes on him; and there seems no particular reason for making an Athlete (a notably un-conscientious one -- he smokes all the time, despite yapping about his trainer and being back in time for curfew) out of the original's Soldier.

Sherman, whether on his own initiative or obeying orders, has repeated the gaffe made by David Hare in the more radical reworking that he called The Blue Room. We are not dealing here with timeless Shakespearean myth. Schnitzler was a great social, as well as sexual, chronicler, and when his play is cut loose from its historical moorings, some important things cease to make sense. The Student (Schnitzler called him a Young Gentleman) comes from a middle-class family that apparently can afford a full-time Maid and cook. He has a very Teutonic relationship with his tutor, and it cannot be naturalized merely by having them agree to meet at a pub rather than a cafe.

The Husband is patronizingly confident of the innocence of his Wife (a mature woman in this production); sexism and the double standard may not have gone away in the last century but they have changed their spots. Of course, much of human behaviour remains unchanged, but that would strike us more forcibly if we saw it revealed in an alien setting. It is, when you think about it, very silly to imagine that you can make a play "relevant" simply by changing its clothes.

Enough of that. In its own terms, Sherman's text is racy and occasionally witty. Herbert Olschok, a German director, builds a world around it. Astrid Jansons set is a forbidding and appropriately circular wall that revolves to uncover each new rendezvous. The actors trundle it around, each joining the scene- shifting parade after having played his or her first scene. Some of these sequences function as extensions of their relationships; some as commentary.

At the end, nine players are standing in line to present the prostitute who functions as both an emblem and a victim for all of them; she is the plays action reduced to its essence. Holly Lewis, playing this role, conjures us into the play from her first soliciting line -- half plaintive, half steely.

Something of the same note is struck by the other two young women in the cast: Stephanie Baptist as the Maid, who transmits innumerable shades of detail with a superbly light touch, and Patricia Fagan, who finds a native equivalent for the Schnitzlerian concept of the available Sweet Girl. They are all three cynical victims.

The production is, among other things, the best advertisement yet for the Soulpepper Young Company: Both Lewis and Fagan are members of it, as are Kyle Hawton, who plays the Athlete, and Allan Hawco, who was good as the punk valet in Present Laughter and is especially fine here as the Student, alternately callow and callous.

We see all the characters before, during and after sex, though the production somehow manages to avoid nudity. The avoidance is more intriguing than the reality would be. The early couplings are brutal and suggest that intimacy in this society always occurs standing up; later encounters are more urbanely comic. (They are also cumulatively less effective, but that has always been true of this play.)

They occur to wailing saxophone accompaniment -- Colleen Allen is the onstage wandering minstrel -- and have been brilliantly choreographed; Mark Christmann is credited as movement coach, as he was on the High Park Tempest, and the improvement in his work is sensational.

The first older character to appear is the Wife, who arrives for her assignation with the Student nervously disguised in layers of furs and scarves; you would know her for an adulterous woman a mile off. Her unnervingly flat delivery takes some of the juice out of the scene, but she develops a teasing Cheshire-cat quality in the scene with the Husband, played with delightful neurotic pomposity by Tony Nardi. He compulsively dusts off his pattering feet each time he hops into bed; indeed, their scene is a symphony of feet, their love making distinguished by the patterns their toes make. This is the most deeply ironic of all the scenes: The lawful couple have the most to conceal.

Oliver Becker is magnetically narcissistic as the Writer. Some things don't change, and his professional vanity, governing his sexuality and everything else, rings very familiar. Nancy Palk, though a little heavy on the prima donna and light on the vulnerable, partners him effectively as the Actress. At one point, straddling epochs and cultures, the two arouse one another by singing the Tango Ballad from The Threepenny Opera, the duet of a whore and her pimp. Dean Gilmour's Politician, excessively fussy in his first scene, finds the bittersweetness, clinging at romantic illusion and robbed of it, in his second, which is also his last.

It is not that these people are passionless but that their passions are brief and have the rest of their natures, plus the whole world, to contend with. This is the play's great insight, and it abides. Olschok's production may have inflated and solemnized the original but it has not, I think, distorted it. It runs three hours, but there is hardly a moment I would sacrifice. There is no flab on the performances. The lines of force between each pair of characters are drawn tight, and they can afford to take their time.
