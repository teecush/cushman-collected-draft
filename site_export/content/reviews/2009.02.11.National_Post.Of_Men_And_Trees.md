As of now, Shakuntala is the oldest play running in Toronto -- no great wonder, since it was written somewhere around the year AD 400. Up until last Sunday, though, it had to yield first place in the antiquity stakes to the Canon production of Medea, its senior by a handsome eight centuries. And yet the older play, a Greek tragedy, is far more accessible to us than the younger, a classic from India that I can best describe as comic-epic-mystic-romantic.

Some of Shakuntala's strangeness is formal, deriving from its frequent dependence on song and stylized dance. But the hardest thing for us to accept is the characters' lack of culpability. As much as people talk about Fate, the characters in a Greek play are comparatively free agents, with some responsibility for what the gods do to them and more for how they respond. But in Shakuntala, written by a court poet named Kalidasa and based on a story from The Mahabharata, everything is down to magic. The title character is a forest girl who lives a secluded life among hermits, though not so secluded that she doesn't have a couple of (in this production, anyway) giggly confidantes. King Dushyanta encounters her while disguised and on a hunting trip, woos her, weds her and gets her pregnant. He then returns home, promising to send for her. But Shakuntala's local hermit gets angry with her and pronounces a curse: She will vanish from the memory of the one who loves her best. And so it proves. When his pregnant bride presents herself at the palace, a puzzled king denies all knowledge. (Thereby, of course, proving that, despite having an abundance -- or, it seems, an embarrassment -- of other wives, he does love her. As tests go, it's arcane but effective.) Torn between anger and heartbreak, she is providentially snatched up in a cloud, to heaven but not to death. Eventually, with a few magical aids, the king finds her and the child there, and there is a happy ending.

If the play resembles anything in our tradition, it's the final romances ofShakespeare, especially Pericles, another story of long-suffering innocence finally rewarded. There are times in Charles Roy's production (for Pleiades Theatre, whose mission is exploration) when we can feel a similar kind of greatness. It takes a long time to arrive: until the beginning of the second half, in fact. The early courtship sequence is long, drawn out and coy, especially the scenes involving Shakuntala's friends, who treat us to a continuous display of twirling and squealing. The crucial scene of the hermit's curse is rushed and non-specific.

Roy's adaptation veers between neat versifying and prosaic bathos. As a director, he confronts a fearsome task with limited resources: The choreography (by Hari Krishnan) is more dutiful than beguiling, though Itai Erdal's lighting can be magical, especially when summoning a slow dawn. The cast is mixed, in both background and accomplishment. In the two leading roles, Anita Majumdar and Sanjay Talwar are, ethnicity notwithstanding, miscast. Talwar, a fine actor of gawks and geeks, makes the king's puzzled conscientiousness endearing sympathetic; but he is not a natural authority figure in a dramatic world that calls out for it. Majumdar, by contrast, is too worldly and controlled for Sakuntala's initial innocence. They both give us the outlines of their predicaments, but not the texture. The other players do a lot of doubling, some of it confusing. Indian audiences may know the story by heart; the rest of us need to have it told as well as decorated. It feels like a masterpiece, but we have to take a lot on trust. - Shakuntala runs until Feb. 15. Call 416-973-4000 for more information.
