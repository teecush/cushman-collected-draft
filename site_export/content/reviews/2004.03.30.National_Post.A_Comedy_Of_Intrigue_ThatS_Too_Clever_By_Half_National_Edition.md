THE TERRIBLE FALSE DECEPTION, SMOKE

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

Rafe Macpherson describes The Terrible False Deception, of which he is the author, as "a four act play in 40 minutes, or 42 with laughs." He also tells us that there is no intermission between the acts, which might be surprising in other circumstances but is only to be expected in these. He must know, because he obviously planned it that way, that his play has a title tautological enough to worry Al Franken. This befits the piece, a merry exercise in which the same four actors repeat the same actions four times over, while speaking completely different lines each time around.

They also appear throughout in the same costumes and on the same set; the designs suggest they are appearing in some elegant period comedy, perhaps something by Marivaux. (He wrote Les Fausses Confidences, which there is nothing to stop somebody translating as The Terrible False Deceptive Confidences.) At the beginning of Act One, a middle-aged woman, perhaps a servant or a confidante, enters, takes a letter from a desk, and places it in her bosom; and if that's not shorthand for comedy of intrigue, I should like to know what is. A young man in a uniform, a hussar or dragoon, follows her on, and is followed in his turn by a youngish lady, posing and flirtatious in a white dress; these two must be the romantic interest, married or otherwise. Last on is an older man; he is clearly the older man.

These actors are not, though, speaking period dialogue, or indeed any kind of dialogue having anything to do with plot. Instead they are telling us what, as actors, they are thinking. They are thinking dissatisfied thoughts about the script, the production, and the talents of their fellow performers i.e. of one another. In Act Two they go through the same motions, but this time speaking the lines that were set down for them. Apparently the play they're in isn't French at all, it's Russian. It can't be Chekhov; rather it suggests those formidable Russian names one encounters in textbooks on the history of drama, names like Ostrovsky and Griboyedov. These masters have seldom been well served by their translators, and the quality of what we hear on this occasion richly confirms what we already know to be the opinion of the actors. They are forced to say things like "I cannot drink, and I trust never will I."

I'm a sucker for this kind of in-jokery, and I loved it when in Act One, Angela Fusco, as the actress playing the housekeeper, or whatever she is, picked up a hat and remarked that there was no earthly reason for her to do such a thing, but that she had been given absolutely NO direction. I liked it even more when Patricia Yeatman, as the actress playing the countess, or whatever she is, outlined the way in which she would deliver a big speech, starting quietly and ending very loud and throwing in arbitrary pauses of the kind guaranteed to impress audiences -- all it of course preparing for the Act Two pay-off in which, at the identical moment, she delivers just such a speech in just such a fashion. Macpherson (who has a day job as a dresser at Mamma Mia!) has provided only basic satire of the conventions of classic comedy, but he's stiletto-mean when dealing with conventions of actors.

Act One is billed as Theatre of the Self-Aware and Act Two as Theatre of the Unaware. Act Three is Theatre of the Underwear, which may make you think that at this point the author began to get a bit desperate. This time the actors are talking about their sexual fantasies; in this act they probably aren't actors at all but real people at a costume party. Act Four (Untitled) gives us another twist, the performers lamenting just how foolish they were to get involved in such a pretentious pseudo-experimental piece as the play we've been watching, and indeed are still watching. It's no wonder that one of them starts speculating on the possibility that they are all dead and in hell. Also, there's been hanky-panky over their contracts. But a play can't escape the charge of being too clever by half just by owning up to it -- and thereby augmenting the offence. "Too clever by half" is indeed a mathematically accurate estimate; the play stops being entertaining smack dab in its middle, or when it aims to be something more ambitious than a poor man's Noises Off. However, the laughs accumulated before then do allow the play to chalk up 41-and-a-half of the 42 minutes it's aiming for. It's well performed, with Kyle McDonald (who seems excessively callow) and Wally Michaels playing the men, though it might work better as an excuse for more established classical actors to send themselves up rotten.

Vinetta Stromberg directs the play, which she first mounted at the Toronto Fringe in 2002. The afterpiece, Smoke by Michael Maclean, was done by the same director at the Fringe of 1997. It's a morose monologue about a man with a craving for SOMETHING, whether it's tobacco or something more spiritually nourishing. The titular metaphor also curls around the character's description of his father's death and funeral, which seem to be the central disorienting events in his own life. It renewed my suspicion of all one-person plays that don't involve laughs, songs or Kristen Thomson. Otherwise, you might just as well read them as see them. Actually, it's the kind of piece that people always say, wrongly, would work better on radio. In fact radio drama depends even more than the theatre on action and on the contrast of personalities; the stage, when deprived of these things, still can't help giving you something to look at. Here it's Hume Baugh, with a face and voice that are open but ravaged. He does a good job, but not good enough to hold my attention, which had wandered within five minutes of the start.
