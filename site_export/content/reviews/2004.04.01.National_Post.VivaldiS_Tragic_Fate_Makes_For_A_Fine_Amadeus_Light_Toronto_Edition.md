THE RED PRIEST

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

Antonio Vivaldi became accustomed to going to parties, some of them given by nobles or even by kings, at which the house band would play The Four Seasons. This was before the days of royalties, even from royalty, but he knew it was doing him good in the world, and it was flattering besides. All the same, he began to wish people would realize that he had written other things as well, and he found himself listening to his greatest hit with gritted teeth. So much I have learned from Mieko Ouchis The Red Priest, in which Vivaldi is one of the two characters. Once a feted and fashionable composer, he is slipping from favour and is, as he prophetically tells us, only a short time away from a paupers grave. If you adopt the Peter Shaffer view of musical history, he is Mozart and Salieri combined, the doomed genius and the jealous cynic in one.

This might also be called the play in which Amadeus meets Arcadia. The other character, known only as the Woman, is the wife of a French aristocrat. She feels imprisoned in her marriage, which to her is symbolized by her husband's precise patterned gardens. Vivaldi is hired to teach her the violin; a wager has been made that, in a mere six weeks she will perform before the current king, who only happens to be Louis XIV. She professes little interest in learning -- and less aptitude. Vivaldi is a practised instructor; he spent years teaching convent girls, back home in Italy, and is theoretically in orders himself -- hence the play's title.

There is much initial mistrust between the two, even stretching to mutual contempt. They are both unhappily trapped in their social roles and take their feelings out on one another, with the difference that she can vent some of her feelings out loud while he is constrained to keep his under wraps. But the ice melts, and music brings them together; they even fall, chastely, in love. I wouldnt say this was predictable -- I didnt feel involved enough to wonder what would happen next -- but it's no great surprise, either. Much of the text is made up of the two characters' embittered soliloquies; some of these are interesting, especially those that feed us information about the composers past life, but they stand still, luxuriating in their own images instead of surging forward.

The duologues are as carefully laid out as the grounds of Monsieurs chateau, but they are hardly more exciting. We understand that The Woman's marital predicament is humiliating and may well be about to become even more so, but we never feel it. Conceivably she would be more interesting if she had a name, but I can appreciate the tact of not giving her one. Vivaldi is a historical personage, she is an imaginary one; so making her his equal by calling her Madame Something would ring subtly false. But then, why has a real composer been dragged in in the first place? It helps atmospherically, of course, to be able to have his music playing in the background, more or less continuously, but it feels like cultural ballast.

The play, especially in its earlier scenes, reads better than it acts, or at least better than it's acted here -- this despite the fact that the same cast and director have already done it in Calgary and Edmonton and that the playwright is also one of the performers. The worldwide commercial success of Two Pianos, Four Hands is said to have been limited by the difficulty of finding actors who can play the piano. It must be even harder to find an actress who can play the violin.

Ouchi fulfills this part of her own assignment admirably; she succeeds especially well in the presumably daunting task of charting the pupils progress from unbearable to really rather good. (Its hard to play bad on purpose.) Her non-instrumental playing, though, is pinched and suburban; lines that were presumably meant by their author to glitter -- and that look, on paper, as if they had a chance of doing so -- are delivered, also by their author, so that they droop. The Woman may be miserable in her position, but she should look and sound capable of filling it. Ouchi can be poignant when crushed, but in her haughtier moments, which are most of them, she presents no challenge.

Ashley Wright's Vivaldi is more fleshed-out, with a good line in acid self-torment, but no sparks fly between the two of them.

Ron Jenkins' production looks elegant enough on David Boechlers formal landscape of a set, but its pulse is slow, as if presuming on a dynamic that hasnt actually happened.

The play has a subtitle, Eight Ways to Say Goodbye. This derives from a game the Woman plays with herself, reeling off the different excuses she might give for leaving her husband. When she plays it for the last time, at the very end of the play, there's a twist. Vivaldi, shortly before this, gives his own version of the same routine, prophesying the steps by which he will approach his own demise. Terse and pungent, it's the best speech in the play, maybe because it summarizes actual events rather than filling out an author's frail fancy. He then adds a ninth step: Be forgotten for 200 years." So he was, and the words reverberate.

But the reverberations have nothing to do with what has gone before, where Vivaldi, apart from the tunes and the odd biographical snippet, could be anybody. Amadeus, for all its faults, had some ideas about what Mozart and his music may have meant to his contemporaries. It also had a vulgar energy of which no one could accuse The Red Priest.
