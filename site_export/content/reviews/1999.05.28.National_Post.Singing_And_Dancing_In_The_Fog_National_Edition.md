A FOGGY DAY

Royal George Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake

One of my favourite lovesongs, by the Gershwin brothers or anyone else, is Nice Work If You Can Get It, a marvellous piece of upbeat bitter-sweet. George's jauntily pulsating music -- one of the last melodic outposts of the Jazz Age -- sets off a piercingly wry lyric by Ira: Love is the nice work, "and if you get it . . . wont you tell me how?"

The song was written for, and thrown away in, the 1937 Fred Astaire movie A Damsel in Distress, where it was done as a comic glee-club number. That film was based on a play, an English country- house farce on the P. G. Wodehouse model -- hardly surprising, since Wodehouse wrote it, in collaboration with Ian Hay.

A Foggy Day -- the Shaw Festivals hit miniature musical, revived from last season -- weds the script of the play to the score of the film, filling out the latter with Gershwin songs from other sources. In this version, Nice Work gets a bit more respect. It's the opening number and is performed by the population of London, or by that percentage of it that spends its time dancing up and down flights of steps that are conveniently located on all the city's streets (well, such streets as have a registry office on one side and a stage-door on the other).

Its not clear why theyre singing this particular song, but it's certainly interesting to hear Canadian actors putting on strenuously English accents to deliver an extremely American tune. The arrangement is more authentically Gershwinian -- more syncopated, more pianistic -- than anything in Crazy for You, Broadway's latest and far more lavish plundering of the family vault.

There is much to enjoy in A Foggy Day -- its music, its good humour, its intimacy -- but it is dead at the centre. The story concerns an American songwriter in pursuit of an English heiress, who initially loves another -- I mean another American -- and has to be brought to realize her mistake. We have to want the central couple to get together, and this production gives us no reason to. (Crazy for You had the same problem, but could offer us more to take our minds off it.)

This is partly a matter of writing, and partly of performance. Glynis Ranney makes a sweet and clear heroine, but Larry Herbert as the hero is murderously charmless. Wodehouse called him George, in homage to Gershwin; hes been renamed Steve, though the actor does have the Gershwin nose. The amount of dancing he does, it must have been tempting to call him Fred, and he gets through long and complicated tap routines without putting a foot wrong.

But there is no heart or spirit in his dancing, no bite or wit in his singing, and not much personality in his acting. In a tight corner at the end of the first act, he says, or sings, that hes dancing and he can't be bothered now; he then goes into an elaborate number that ends up infecting the entire cast. If you're Astaire, this might be a reasonable way of reacting to trouble. If youre merely mortal, it looks like a forced way of bringing down the curtain.

John Mueller, who collaborated on the script with Norm Foster, wrote maybe the best book on Astaire, and he must know that Freds dancing -- especially in the Fred-and-Ginger movies -- was never unmotivated, always had a context. He must also know that the way to establish an attraction between characters in a musical is to have them sing and dance together. This show is nearly over before its principals share a number.

In the meantime, our attention and our affection have been usurped by two subsidiary couples. Richard Farrell, an endearing old buffer in many plays at Shaw, is now an even more endearing, more- or-less-singing-and-dancing buffer; he plays the distressed damsel's dad who is gobbled up by a Canadian show-business broad, a role in which Nora McLellan turns in a first-rate Ethel Merman impersonation. They share two duets and two-thirds of a trio, all of them delightful.

Then there is Todd Waite as an endlessly lanky dimwit, tormented by shyness, and his bespectacled beloved (Patty Jamieson), who is similarly afflicted. They also have a charming duet, and he leads a galloping ensemble number -- the very Bertie Woosterish Stiff Upper Lip -- which would be wonderful if it were cut by a third; overkill sets in when they lower the Union Jack and start spouting couplets whose attempts at rhyme would make P. G. Wodehouse turn in Ira Gershwin's grave.

Also very active is Karen Wood as a scheming Cockney skivvy, largely responsible for steering the plot. This is a mistaken- identity thing that takes some time to get going but is fun when it does.

Some of the interpolated songs work in their new setting, some dont. The ones that do tend to be funny; the ones that dont tend to be famous, and they also tend to be songs that were written to be sung to or with someone, and are here delivered into empty air. This brings me back to the opening song, not an interpolation but still somewhat arbitrarily treated; it's nice work but they don't quite get it.
