They look as if theyre about to do a number, though it would be difficult to guess what it might be: three jaunty old gentlemen gazing determinedly into the future. Knowing, because of the instructions attached to the photographing, that only the future would see them. Together they represent a generation of great British acting: ended now and -- but we knew that a long time in advance -- irreplaceable. And such good taste in hats. (But where's Sir John Gielgud?)

Sir Ralph looks a trifle grim, and he could be so at times: not so much forbidding as unknowable. His off-stage life was the stuff of legend: he rode a motorbike around London (it could be seen parked around the National Theatre), had a fearsome capacity for alcohol, never bothered with an unlisted phone number. This dovetailed with his stage personality, which was a potent mixture of innocence and strength, with a speech-rhythm that was all his own: deliberate but fanciful. His voice and manner were so individual that it became possible to apply them in one's mind to roles that he had never actually played -- King Lear, for example -- and be moved by the result. Few actors have been so loved; watching him you often wanted to hug him, but knew that he would never let you close enough to try.

Lord Olivier, in the middle, is stoically smiling, and its possible that he was in pain; he often was in the last 20 years of his life, and the others may well be supporting him. The most active actor of his group -- a physical and mental swashbuckler -- he was eventually betrayed by his body, though never by his voice; and its possible that he gained a vulnerability that made him moving in a way he'd never been before. He, more than any of the others, is history; there will always be great eccentrics like Richardson and great chameleons like Guinness, but the conditions are no longer there to nourish heroes like Olivier: a man who honed his ringing, outsize skills in the theatre, successfully exploited them in the movies and emerged as the undoubted leader of a profession that now has no use for figureheads. Asked about his qualifications to be first director of Britain's National Theatre, he said in effect that there was no-one else; and he was right. He was not able to lead his company into its permanent home on Londons South Bank, but at least they named one of its three stages after him. (Its rumoured he hated it.)

Sir Alec looks the spryest: the least assuming, the most dapper. (He was also considerably the youngest: an up-and-comer when the others were already stars.) His prime qualities were spirituality and mischief; adjusting the balance between the two he was able to encompass everything from Hamlet to Star Wars; and, despite that latter brush with the supernatural, he always seemed more of the world, more of our world -- than his peers.

You felt that you might discuss politics with him, or the weather. All three prodigies won their spurs in the same theatre and decade, the Old Vic of the 1930s; in 1946 they were even members of the same company. But they never appeared together, all three of them, on the same stage or screen; so this image may be unique.
