THEATRE REVIEW SHEILAS DAY

Saturday, June 09, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Conceived and written by Duma Ndlovu and Mbongeni Ngema Directed by Mbongeni Ngema Produced by Crossroads Theatre Co. at Harbourfronts Premiere Dance Theatre Sheila's Day depicts black struggle in a beautifully sculpted production BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THERE have been plays about apartheid in South Africa and there have been plays, though fewer of them, about the civil-rights struggle in the United States. Sheila's Day takes on both: an original idea and a brilliant one, and - like all the best notions - perfectly obvious, once somebody else has thought of it.

The show is in fact an African-American collaboration: one that started life at the Crossroads Theatre in New Brunswick, N.J. It opened here as part of the Harbourfront World Stage season on Thursday.

Sheilas Day is the day off given to black female domestics in South Africa by white employers who, confused by their names, called them all Sheila. It is also a day for strongly musicalized prayer meetings: for celebrating, mourning and remembering.

An opening sequence of irresistibly rhythmic chanting and hand-clapping puts us unshakably in the shows corner. The leaps from one story, one continent, one time frame to another can be shockingly abrupt but they are technically seamless. The director, Mbongeni Ngema, first came to international notice with Woza Albert, a two-hander about the black South African experience that I found worthy but interminable. But then it didn't have music. Ngema has since staged Sarafina, a musical that was a surprise success on Broadway and is due later this year at the Royal Alex.

Music oils the wheels of Sheila's Day: music exultant and magisterial, performed a capella by the 11 women who make up the cast. (The unaccompanied music matches the unadorned stage; everything is focussed on the performers.) "The same folks who were partying all Saturday were the first ones to arrive in church Sunday morning. Those words, spoken in the show by an Alabama waitress, obviously point up the unity of sacred and profane, blues and gospel. They also suggest a global unity in black culture: one that the show enacts in form and content.

The evening builds toward two murders: a black African thrown in front of a moving train by two white policemen and his American equivalent beaten to death by a segregationist mob. Both men died trying to protect women; and the shows protest and its affirmation are as much sexual as racial. In each case the murder is described to us by a female witness whose individual story we have previously been following. One has struggled painfully, sometimes illegally, toward a position as a teacher in Johannesburg. The other is the Alabama waitress, whose name is Ruby.

One performer, Ebony Jo-Ann, gets a special credit for creating, writing and acting this character (the others are listed with no identification) and she has also been issued with an individual dramatic licence. Ruby is on a bus when a black woman is arrested for refusing to give up her seat to a white; she is serving at a lunch counter that becomes the scene of a sit-in; she is back on the bus when freedom riders get on board. Being present at these historical flashpoints gives her no pleasure. She is a little annoyed and a lot frightened, and she transmits these feelings to us with an accumulation of why-me-ness that derives from a whole tradition of black comediennes but that she still makes her own.

As a theatrical device, this is both resourceful and appealing. We can sense Ruby getting reluctantly radicalized, but our blushes are spared; there is no scene of flaming conversion as in Brechts atrocious The Mother. The last words we hear are Ruby's account of the killing of her friend Jimmie; and for the first time she isn't funny. Then we may think back to the beginning of the show, and to her first words at the meeting: I cannot forget Jimmie Lee Jackson."

Qedusizi, her Zulu counterpart, also falls foul of history, caught up in an anti-passbook demonstration, and imprisoned in consequence. There is comedy in her story too, though it resides less with her than with the Afrikaner woman in whose house she works for seven years. The few white characters are all bracingly played - trenchantly outlined but not cartooned - and none is more devastatingly hit off than this young chatelaine, benevolent without kindness, cruel without malice. (Ruby too has a spell of domestic service; admonished by a remarkably similar employer to use plenty of elbow grease she spends her first morning looking for it.) The show has been criticized by some U.S. reviewers as being fragmentary. So it is; but that is the point. A full-scale epic, paradoxically, would have to concentrate on one conflict or the other. We are being offered glimpses of a world-wide whole: one that can be extrapolated beyond the black experience. The show is buoyed up by the knowledge that, however painful the cost, the struggles it depicts are going to be won. Imagine a similarly-structured drama that paralleled Nazi Germany and Khmer Rouge Cambodia. It could be done. You wouldn't want to sit through it.

In a sense Sheila's Day has it easy. It drives straight at our consciences and our sympathies, and it does so through joy. But this is a show that depicts oppression without indulging in it, and that takes a religious ground without sermonizing. It is a beautifully sculpted production. It has earned its keep.
