THEATRE REVIEW

THEM AND US

Theatre Passe Muraille

---

Tracy Dawson, who is both author of them and us and one-quarter of its cast, is a former writer-performer with Second City and her play shows it. It's a collection of sketches. Most of them are well-written and some of them are funny, though seldom to a laugh-out-loud degree. Some of the other attractions of revue are missing as well, such as music, drinks, an intermission and a variety of authorial styles and viewpoints. We have no choice but to regard the proceedings as a play, and seen that way it gets frustrating and a little wearisome. Beyond the actors' skills, which are considerable, we have nothing to hold on to.

There is a unity of theme, but even that turns out to be counter-productive. All the episodes are about relationships, a subject that offers the advantage of being of potential interest to nearly everybody and the disadvantage of being the most obvious topic under the sun. You can always say something about it but, unless you do some serious digging, it's unlikely to be anything novel or arresting. These couples -- the vast majority of the scenes are dialogues -- all arrive at the conclusion that interacting with another person is difficult and sometimes painful, but better than the alternative. Anyone disagree?

I lied a bit when I said there was no music. More accurately, there's no original music, and nothing performed live. Quite a bit gets piped in, and to good effect. The show starts with the Sinatra-Basie record of Fly Me to the Moon, the four actors on stage self-consciously grooving to it. It's one of the wittiest things in the evening, especially with Michael Healey shuffling somewhere on the edges of hip, and Sarah Dodd looking pained but determined. A later scene finds a Nina Simone record used as an emotional touchstone for four people at a dinner party. Dawson herself and Gray Powell, the two younger members of the team, are the hosts; Dodd and Healey are, respectively, a woman reeling from a break-up and a guy who's somehow managed to become a single father without ever having dated. They know they've been set up to be set up, and it's sweet -- if something of a credibility-stretch -- to watch them wrestle with their pre-ordained roles. One wishes them well.

There's another festive gathering later on, a sour balance to the first. Dawson is the host, Powell is her ex and Dodd is a woman whose romantic prospects for the evening are ruined by a mix of jealousy, intoxicating substances, and -- this, I think, is the real crusher --a fatal mismatching of tastes in movies. It's tempting to say that all the best scenes here

are the group ones, but that doesn't hold for a protracted item in which the guys on one side of the stage and the gals on the other demonstrate the different ways in which signals may be interpreted and stories told by the two genders. The Summer Days, Summer Nights song in Grease did it more economically and entertainingly. Indeed, if you want a set of vignettes about feelings solitary or shared, then song, with its power to concentrate a moment, may be the best way to go.

The spoken duets here include two in which an older man (Healey) fences with a younger woman (Dawson): much younger in one case since he says, flustered-like, that she's his partner's daughter. (Would that be life-partner or business-partner? It does make a difference.) Their first encounter, a sort of prenup contract, has him saying that he doesn't believe in romantic love, which probably means that he does: Coming early in the evening, it's plainly meant to state a theme, but it isn't one that gets developed, just repeated. The most "serious" encounter, and the most dispensable, finds a doctor giving a patient a diagnosis of cancer, followed by an embrace; this scene is unprofessional, on a number of levels. The most obviously revue-ish items are an exchange of false comfort between two women in a coffee shop, and a monologue by Dawson about having flatulence on a date; nothing very wrong with either of them, but they don't seem to belong with the author's higher ambitions.

I have few complaints, though, about the performance. Ruth Madoc-Jones' direction is supple and fluid, the myriad transitions excellently handled with much credit to Kimberly Purtell's bare set and bold, sensitive lighting. Of the players, Dawson herself is better at timing her lines than exploring what's beneath them; it's unfortunate, too, that she has to make much of the running in the first quarter of the evening. (I doubt if it was meant to be The Tracy Dawson Show, but it does come on that way.) Powell does unobtrusively and resourcefully well, especially with the night's first monologue. Healey explores, endearingly, many shades of rumpled; at one point he had me convinced that he'd changed the colour of his hair, though all he'd really done was muss it up a little. Dodd shows off the greatest range, from rage to loss to (occasional) glee, and hits each unfussily on the nose; I've never thought of her as a revue performer, and I doubt if she has either, but she has the economy and versatility for it. Some of the scenes -- I almost said numbers -- do echo or complete others, but this doesn't add up to structure. It's as if a Second City running gag were being asked, single-handedly, to repeal the law of diminishing returns - Them And Us Runs until Jan. 31. Call 416-504-7529 for more information.
