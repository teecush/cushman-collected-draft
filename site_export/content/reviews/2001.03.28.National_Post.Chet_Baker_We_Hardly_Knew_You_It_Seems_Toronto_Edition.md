TIME AFTER TIME

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

Subtitled The Chet Baker Project, Time after Time is a Citizen Kane that makes the bad mistake of assuming that the character in whom we are really interested is the reporter.

The play is less about Chet Baker, the late jazz trumpeter and crooner, than about What Chet Baker Means to James OReilly, author and principal performer. Strictly speaking, it's about what Baker means to an anonymous but loquacious narrator, but since O'Reilly plays this role himself its hard not to make the identification.

He begins by telling us how, 10 years ago in a restaurant, on a date with the woman he later married, he heard a Baker record for the first time. He was transfixed by the sound of this haunted, tender voice from the 1950s singing I Fall in Love Too Easily, an outstandingly tender tune in the first place. Another actor actually sits at the table and attempts to score with the young lady, but he is upstaged by OReilly, hovering and commenting and grabbing all the best lines.

The show's first 10 anecdotal minutes are self-centred stand-up comedy and a pointer to how the rest of it is going to go. O'Reilly sets out to discover the truth about Baker, mainly by talking to people who knew him, and turning the results into the play were watching. (Or is he writing a book, or making a movie? Its very amorphous.) Occasionally the character of Chet himself appears, singing, playing and even -- as dictated by the others' memories -- talking.

The narrator is fascinated by the apparent discrepancy between the man and the music. How, he asks, could someone who sings and plays with such apparent sensitivity have been so appalling in his private life, abusing and deserting a succession of wives, destroying himself and his talent through drugs, and finally falling to a farcically ignominious death from an Amsterdam hotel window? The answer, of course, is very easily. A more interesting question might be: What kind of idiot expects there to be a one-on-one correlation between a performer and his performance in the first place?

O'Reillys surrrogate asks how Baker, the beautiful boy of American music, only 23 and personally and professionally on top of his world, could have sung and played with such awareness of loss and loneliness. Well, 23 years are quite enough for most people to gather some first-hand information on those subjects; and even if you have avoided the feelings yourself, you will have read and heard enough about them -- from popular songs, mainly -- to know how they are supposed to feel. Your audience will know, too, and between your assumptions and theirs the trick will be done.

The material helps, of course. A singer is a kind of actor: He will, by and large, sound sad on sad songs and happy on happy ones. Baker, as it happens, sang very few happy songs; wistfully romantic was about as positive as he got. There is a sentimental confusion in many peoples minds between a performer's emotions, which can be faked (the word is not pejorative), and his personality, which does tend to be the same on and off stage. Nothing in Baker's musical personality suggests he was a nice guy. Charming, yes.

The first time I heard Chet Bakers singing, I couldnt take it seriously; the voice was pallid, and the lyrical interpretation almost non-existent. Later I found, like many others, that the blankness had its own appeal, especially in its similarity to the piercing withered-leaf simplicity of his trumpet playing.

Oddly enough, though the play makes a big deal about Baker's unknowability (so that philosophically it has to be about O'Reilly, for what can any of us know about anyone but ourselves, and all that jazz), it presents a rather comprehensible, unpuzzling picture of him: He had too much too soon and couldnt deal with it, so he destroyed himself, taking a few others down en route. It is not a unique story.

There are two scenes in which a man and a woman -- probably the narrators younger self and his lady friend, at any rate played by the same actors -- argue about Baker's recording of his signature tune, My Funny Valentine, each of them taking a diametrically different position on each occasion. They debate whether the rhetorical questions the song poses ("Is your figure less than Greek?/Is your mouth a little weak?") are tender things to say to a woman or just insulting.

We seem to be talking less about Baker's performance than about Lorenz Harts lyrics, so here are a few facts: The song was written for a show, to be sung by a woman about a character actually called Valentine. Its sung in his absence, and that is how most singers of any sense and of either gender continue to sing it -- as an affectionate mock ode. The beloved cannot be insulted, because he or she never hears it. It's possible that Hart, who felt unattractive, meant it as a love song to himself, and it's possible that Chet, who had a highly developed sense of self-pity, also felt it that way. Another of his key tunes was Everything Happens to Me. That might be interesting, but the play doesnt get into it. Its more ambitious, and I suppose more intelligent, than a big showbiz bio like Jolson or Buddy, but it is no more illuminating.

Director Jim Millan gives it a smoky, moody production, and Martin Julien deals efficiently with all the male characters who aren't Baker or O'Reilly. But there are really only three reasons for seeing the show. One is Danny DePoe, who reproduces Chets singing and playing, not to mention his movie-star looks, with uncanny accuracy (though his native trumpet style, as exhibited in the intermission, seems far more muscular). He isnt an actor, but he is a presence, and his affectless line readings are just right; he is the right kind of hole in the play.

A second is the music itself, as dispensed by DePoe and an onstage trio. The third, the clincher, is the white-heat work of Philippa Domville, who plays the fallen idol's female survivors, each more damaged and vitriolic than the last. The actress, after a string of ladylike roles at the Shaw Festival, here reinvents herself and keeps reinventing. It's in the narrator's encounter with this parade of exes -- one of whom is herself a pathetic performer - - that the echoes of Kane are most deafening. Rosebud here turns out to be a plangent chorus of Summertime. The song itself was accorded a jokey mention back in the first scene, so presumably some kind of wheel is coming some kind of circle.
