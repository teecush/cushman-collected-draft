SHOPPING AND FUCKING

du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

The play begins with a character vomiting, apparently from a mixture of junk food and heroin withdrawal. One of its last scenes centres on a brutal scene of homosexual rape, with the added twist that the act is enthusiastically welcomed by its victim. (Try getting away with that in a heterosexual play.) The victim, by the way, is 14 years old. My, he looks tall for his age; though, from the standpoint of the audience's sensibilities, this slight implausibility is maybe a blessing.

What happens in between is not especially graphic; even the language is surprisingly decorous by the standards of most contemporary drama, let alone contemporary life. Mark Ravenhills Shopping and Fucking comes to us with glowing credentials from London and New York; it isnt as original as its fans have been claiming, and its Toronto production is muffled to the point of incoherence, but it's a funny, bracing, and talented work.

It's also a morality, as you can tell from the title. It might, I suppose, have been more discreetly phrased: Sex and Supermarkets, perhaps -- or how about Laying Em in the Aisles? But they wouldnt have had the same aura about them: the flavour of brimstone preaching for an age of cool.

There are five characters in Ravenhill's play, and it's notable that though they all have names in the program, those names hardly ever crop up in the dialogue. This may not be meant to have a distancing or dehumanizing effect, but it does. It also means that I may be about to get the plot wrong.

Here goes: There are Lulu, Robbie, and Mark who live together in London until Mark moves out to kick his heroin habit. Lulu seeks employment from Brian, the one prosperous character in the piece. He is in commerce, to put it sweetly, and he sends Lulu out on a test drive: peddling Ecstasy in a club. She enlists Robbies help, but he ends up giving it away, which puts them in Brians debt and thus in his power. Mark, meanwhile, is out and cured, though he now believes himself capable only of depersonalized sex. He thinks -- wrongly, as it turns out -- that he has found it with young Gary, who was systematically abused by his stepfather, and now seems to be making a career of it. Gary has even made some money; and when Mark brings him home, the two stories come, elegantly and bloodily, together.

"Look," says Lulu, opening a box of multiethnic fast food. An empire under cellophane. It would be nice to avoid the phrase "consumer society," but it's plainly what Ravenhill is on about; he's describing a world in which everything, notably sex, has a credit rating, and in which people are increasingly shameless about it. This is true, but it isnt news. British drama in particular has been harping on it for the past 30 years, and its amusing that the armchair apocalyptics of the '70s should now be turning on the new generation of what have been dubbed the in-your-face playwrights, and accusing them of heartlessness.

The tone and the sawn-off techniques are very similar. What is different is that the previous lot, university graduates at a time when that meant something, were writing from a position of chilly superiority; Ravenhill's bunch seem personally involved. They may even have earned the right to act tough.

That doesnt mean that everything rings true. In the rape scene, the play seems to be simultaneously knitting its brow and licking its lips. (The audience, too. Theres a complicity: They act tougher than they are. We react cooler than we feel.) Brian, the boss-man, is a rampaging cliche: the sensitive gangster who talks tenderly and proudly of a son who's a prodigy violinist, before punching his listener in the stomach. He also informs us that "get the money" is the first law of life, and invokes Biblical authority. His real sacred text, though, seems to be The Lion King. He quotes the circle-of-life stuff repeatedly and with reverence; in a week where the arrival in Toronto of a Disney roadshow has been trumpeted as if it were the second coming, that scene comes pungently into its own.

The play is built on the dangerous London wit that was patented by Harold Pinter and has been common dramatic currency ever since. So it's vital that the actors do it right; they have to get the laughs and connect the feelings behind them. Jim Millans production succeeds occasionally with the first -- usually when its a set- piece that's too obvious to miss -- and fails absolutely with the second. Sometimes it's slow, sometimes its fast, but it never has pace.

The actors lunge at the rhythms as if dimly remembering the last British cop show they saw. (For all I know, some of them are British; they still sound like imitations.) They all play moments rather than scenes. Damien Atkins, whose overgrown child Gary is after all the most isolated character, has the best of them, with Matthew MacFadzean highlighting the resentful and the happy-go- lucky in Robbie without ever fusing them. Alex Poch-Goldins Mark has the shell of a disturbing character but never fills it out; ditto for Julian Richings, whose Brian is whiny when he should be scary.

Natalie Radford as Lulu is new to the theatre and frankly acts like it; her performance barely travels into the auditorium. This is especially damaging since she has speeches, about witnessing the robbery of a convenience store and her own confused reaction to it, that are potentially the most powerful in the play. Lulu, ironically, is a trained actress, and she quotes Chekov and Shakespeare, using Juliet to arouse a client during phone sex. For the most part, these characters are not hardened; they have compassion, but they don't know what to do with it -- which, the play powerfully suggests, is everybody's problem.
