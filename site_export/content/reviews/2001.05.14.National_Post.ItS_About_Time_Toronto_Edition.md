HISTORY PLAY

The Great Hall, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Last year, Jennifer Tarver handily staged an unstageable Gertrude Stein play and called it Not Faust. This year, with equal theatrical authority, she has devised and directed a dramatic collage, a collection of excerpts from pre-existing texts arranged around a central theme.

The theme is Time -- that philosophical hold-all that has exercised the human mind since ... I dont think I can finish that sentence without seeming to enter the debate. Since the beginning of time? For as long as anyone can remember?

Abstract thought can be difficult to follow in the theatre, or maybe its just me, but it does seem every time I have been asked to attend to a meditation on the nature of Time, the true subject has turned out to be the workings of memory. The argument always seems to go something like this: There are these literalist mugs who cling to the idea of time being a linear progression, but in fact it's nothing of the kind, as can be seen from the way in which our minds jump about between past, present and future.

To me, this appears to be a false antithesis, between two totally different spheres of experience. To shift the battlefield from time to space: I have never been to Moscow; that does not mean I don't believe Moscow exists.

Tarver mounts her symposium on a long, narrow platform that divides the hall. It is designed by Jan Komarek and inhabited by three actors, all of them in full and impressive command of what theyre saying. The principal spokesmen, to judge from the list of sources in the program, are Aristotle and the 20th-century theorist J. W. Dunne.

Aristotle, as delivered by Peter van Gestel, is young, confident, and enthusiastic. He can afford to be since, to be linear about it, he got on to the scene early. Dunne, embodied by David Jansen as a pleasantly skeptical campus sage of European origin, describes the process of free association: Our minds wander from one subject to another, and within half-a-dozen mental moves we find ourselves in a completely different area from the one at which we started.

This is a universal experience, but he gives it a name thatId certainly never heard before: the memory train." We go from one station to the next, he explains, and, if the trip is not too long, we can take the train back, again stop by stop. Above his head and ours, small objects whiz along a pair of parallel lines resembling those in old-fashioned drapery stores, and presumably illustrating Dunne's theory.

In the 1930s, Dunne influenced the "time plays of J. B. Priestley, including Time and the Conways, seen at last year's Shaw Festival. Priestley used them as a pretext for playing Act III before Act II, thus ensuring an unending supply of easy irony of the if only theyd known" variety. It also depended absolutely on the audience experiencing time in linear fashion, even if the characters didnt. We had to know more than they did.

Priestley's are absent from History Play's core curriculum, but several other playwrights are present. We hear Cassandra, from The Oresteia, prophesying upcoming murders. There are repeated scenes from Thornton Wilders Our Town and Edward Albees Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf? (Both involve characters called George, but they're played by different actors and I think the correspondence is just a coincidence -- if there is such a thing.)

Albees historian spars with the young scientist who was Aristotle a moment earlier and whom he accuses of undermining him and the whole tradition of secular humanism. Later, Martha, the historians formidable wife, recalls a (literal) boxing match she once had with him -- a memory that seems to define their whole relationship.

In Our Town, we watch the drugstore betrothal of the play's young lovers, as heart-rending when detached from the play as it is within it. Wilder makes us aware of all the possibilities they are passing up in order to seize on this one, and of course once you start thinking about the passage of time, there is nothing more poignant than young love, especially perhaps if it leads to something.

To drive the point home, we have heard Wilder's professor situating Grovers Corners, N.H., not only in historical but in archeological time, which makes us feel very small indeed. (Harold Pinters plays also appear in the credits, as do T. S. Eliot's Four Quartets, and they would certainly have been relevant, but if they actually put in an appearance, then I missed them. Proust, I guess, would have been just too obvious.)

None of this, either in isolation or in juxtaposition, adds up to more than a restatement of the obvious, but it is certainly well done. Van Gestel, as Wilder's fresh-faced young hero courting his true love and disillusion simultaneously, is almost unbearably real (the scene, like the play, makes us nostalgic for a probably mythical American innocence). And Jansen, whose roles include those of Wilders avuncular stage manager, is both soothing and probing, like a reassuring dentist.

Brenda Robins is accomplished in her various roles, but harder to believe. Her archeology lecturer is a comic turn; her young Emily in Our Town has an unshakeable edge of maturity, reinforced with a Glenda Jackson rasp; and when she recounts the Old Ladys tale of disaster from Voltaire's Candide (this is the lassy who in the Leonard Bernstein musical was easily assimilated but missing half of her backside), she loses the tone of bland, accepting irony that makes the horrors truly horrific. Besides, what is the speech doing here? It's remembering something that happened in the past? By that test, half of literature would qualify.

Robins does, however, have a great dismissive moment as Albee's Martha, dispatching the opposition with superb whiplash timing. Somebody, probably the designing Komarek, has furnished a fine sound score: The memory train seems to be one hell of a locomotive.

And the dramatic collage, whatever my qualms on this occasion, is a genre worthy of further exploration. It could, for example, tell us a lot about a specific time -- as opposed to a generalized Time.
