THEATRE REVIEW

Women Fully Clothed: Older and Hotter

Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

The four writing, performing and producing ladies who constitute Women Fully Clothed begin the evening (fully clothed) playing a team of stressed executives, working to horrendous deadline on some product whose specifications are difficult to grasp, since they all talk so very, very fast. But then between them they have a lot of their things on their minds: children's dental appointments, hot flashes, divorces, shared recipes, office gossip. This opening number sets a tone, a subject and a standard for the show. It's about the pressured modern woman, mainly middle-aged and middle-class; it's somewhat satirical, in an accepting kind of way; and it treads familiar comic ground with flair.

It's also, barring the very occasional solo song, a collection of group scenes. Not having seen WFC's first show I approached their second, Older and Hotter, under some misconceptions. I had expected, and rather dreaded, an evening of stand-up. What I found was an evening of sketch comedy: a single-sex Second City show minus the cabaret atmosphere, the tables and the nibbles, but with a higherthan-average standard of material. Robin Duke, Jayne Eastwood, Kathryn Greenwood and Teresa Pavlinek make up the quartette, and there are seldom less than three of them onstage at a time. Pavlinek, tall and dark, specializes in being lost and loopy; Greenwood is the youngest-looking and plays the characters who seem the most together, except when they aren't; Eastwood has an acerbic line in the blond and judgmental; and Duke, though in a recognizable tradition of obsessed and diminutive North American female clowns, has a fine madness all her own.

She is, for example, wonderfully mean and moody if not perhaps munificent, when sent to a school principal's office on a charge of bullying. It's a scene that, in a show that makes something of a fetish of hand-held technology, allows her to pull off the funniest cellphone gag I have ever seen. It is in fact my favourite moment in the entire show, and part of what is perhaps my favourite number; certainly it's the one that develops in the least predictable ways. It allows Pavlinek to play an aggrieved victim, at which she's very good, and Greenwood to act the unreasonably reasonable mediator, at which she's even better. Duke also convulses as a daytime TV anchor with a show about how to turn your divorce into trash (literally), with a sidelight on how to find a new husband to take it out. Nicely offbeat, too, is the saga of three Girl Guide leaders who lose their entire troupe, possibly because they've devoted too little time to teaching them woodcraft and too much to rehearsing them in songs from Annie, in hopes, as far as I could make out, of getting them a spot on Glee. Greenwood, a Girl Guide cookie in herself, is a memorably fraught Brown Owl, and the sight of Eastwood trying vainly to start a fire by rubbing things together, even while lighting up a cigarette, will probably stay with me.

All these scenes have satisfying shapes; the school one even manages to resurrect that almost-vanished species, the effective punchline. Other things fade out. Duke and Greenwood are a riot as a couple of competitively cranky seniors on a shopping trip to Target in Buffalo (Duke driving, while anticipating cataract surgery), but when they get halted at U.S. customs, the sketch, like the ladies themselves, has no place to go; first it twists in the wind, then it grinds to a stop. Another number that has the four clucking in counterpoint over the state of the planet while continuing to spend, spend, spend; it shows the team at their most Second City-fied, taking an obvious target (only this time it's Walmart, at least it starts that way) and driving it to an equally obvious conclusion.

This is the comedy of shared guilt, and even men in the audience can partake. Women Fully Clothed struck me at first as Women Over Miked, but by the end either the technicians had adjusted or I had. Most of what the show says hits a nerve, with the laughs ranging from rueful to uproarious. The stray musical interludes supply some of them, though they can't escape the curse of the comic song; they let off their key jokes early and then have to pad. As theatre vocalists, none of the four are likely to pose a threat to Louise Pitre, but they do well. And they come together very well when the show, right at its end, addresses the subject of children and what their post-Boomer parents have done for and to them, in a song that seems to be called Give Them Less: "We told them they could have it all, that couldn't hurt them could it? Now they have it all, but there's no place to put it." The rhyme isn't quite perfect; but then, as the show spends much of its time implicitly inquiring, what is? ? Runs until June 19.
