Well, hello Ruby Tuesday. This is the night of the week that E! has reserved for the story of Ruby Gettinger, a seriously overweight woman from Savannah, Ga., whose family, friends and physicians all advise her that if she doesn't reduce her weight she will die, sooner rather than later. We are witnesses to her struggle.

The show is less exploitative than that probably makes it sound. It is, though, very repetitive, a characteristic it shares with all reality shows. Thats the category in which its being promoted, and it does raise the question of what the dividing line is between "reality (the quotes are mine, and I'd add italics to them if I could) and documentary. Or maybe the former is just the fancy name that TV channels now give to the latter. I think, though, that there is there is a dividing line, and that it's crossed about halfway through this shows appropriately super-sized opening episode.

For the first half were in the house that Ruby, who's in her thirties, shares with Jeff, her friend since childhood, and Jim, her teenage nephew who's taken refuge with her after leaving his unsympathetic parents. Frequent visitors are Georgia, another old friend, and her son Ben. Its just us and them and the camera crew. Georgia, whos almost too appropriately named, cooks a lot of meals for Ruby: I love to cook Southern," she says -- which seems to be a large part of the problem. Ruby weighs 500 pounds, and even that is a reduction of what she once was; she shows us a picture of when she weighed 700. How she lost that 200 is not revealed, though in view of what we're told later, it's a reasonable question. She has tried various diets over the years ("the grapefruit diet -- weve tried everything), but never for longer than five days. She has a very pretty face and a sunny personality, I love my life," she says, "but the physical parts awful. And we can see for ourselves the difficulty she has simply in finding a chair she can fit into and get out of, and the embarrassments that can cause. I've lived in this shell," she says, "since I was eight.

So there's Ruby, and theres her ready made support system that, up till now, has not always been supportive in the best ways. (Weve been enablers," they admit.) A trip to her family doctor -- which must have been restaged for our benefit, unless you really believe in coincidence-- convinces her that she is "a metabolic time bomb waiting to go off and that she must lose it or, well, lose it. And it's here that the show changes course, and becomes reality," in the TV sense that defines reality as a contest.

Now if this were a hard core cruel show, Ruby would be just one of a group of overweight people (we're well into the second half before anyone uses the word "obese) and there would be judges keeping track of how well they stick to their new regimes and expelling, with censorious comments, those who fail to measure up. Viewers might even be asked to vote on who was likeliest to fall off the wagon. Ruby, needless to say, doesnt go that route. Rubys contest is with herself, which is certainly a subtler and more humane way to go, but does leave all the participants with a very limited number of things to say, and the show with the obligation to have them keep saying it. Ruby's West Coast best friend" Brittany (a name that seems to crop up in every reality show, though with variant spellings) comes for the weekend; this is

a case of opposites attracting, since Brittany is a health nut. (She apparently travels with an exercise bike.) She introduces Ruby to health food stores, signs her up at a gym and buys Georgia a new cookbook.

Ruby gets a personal trainer and enlists in water aerobics. There being "no bathing suits in my size, she wears instead a dress with safety pins." Everyone wishes her well, and intends to do their best by her; it's genuinely refreshing to encounter a non-fiction show full of genuinely nice people. But we do have to spend a lot of time listening to Ruby telling us, either to camera or in voice-over, how she knows that this time she really has to make a change; and to her helpers telling us the same. This is morally better than the customary reality-show narrator telling us over and over again how tense everybody is (this shows only commentary comes from Ruby herself, which is a good thing), but its no less monotonous.

Obviously Ruby's problems go beyond what she eats. So, as well as her own doctor and an "obesity doctor, she goes, with all the normal trepidation, to a psychiatrist: If you'll forgive me putting it his way, she goes to a shrink to help her get shrunk. And he gets her to admit that there are passages in her childhood whose memory she has just blotted out. I would have thought filming such a consultation was a breach of psychiatric etiquette, but what would I know? Future episodes will take us further along this route. If we have been wondering about Rubys romantic life -- and we have -- she tells us that she had a boyfriend who said he would marry her if she lost weight and who left when she didnt. That was six years ago. We're promised that next week he'll return. Another coincidence, or just the magic of television and its industrious researchers? A happy ending is, I think, practically guaranteed. After all, even in the most savage of competitive shows, the humiliations of the losers are drowned out by the triumphs of the winners. And when there's only one person who can either win or lose, you can guess which way it's going to go. And morally it has to, for the sake of all the other Rubys who may be buoyed up by her example. This is one reality show" that could do some real good.

- Ruby premieres tonight at 10 p. m. ET on E!
