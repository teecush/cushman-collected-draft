Asweet-faced, mild-mannered comedian is a rarity, especially these days, soDemetriMartin stands out. After the customary apprenticeship in stand-up comedy and a writer-performer gig on The Daily Show with Jon Stewart, hes now driving his own vehicle, Important Things with Demetri Martin. Every week he will turn his gaze, filtered through monologues and sketches, on some imposing abstract topic. He starts tonight with timing.

Well, timing is widely known to be the vital ingredient of successful comedy, so I suppose you could say that anything Martin does would be relevant. And on that strictly technical level he succeeds: He gives all his jokes their best shot. In soliloquy, he's an oddball observationist on the order of Steven Wright or the late Mitch Hedberg, but without their wild air of grievance and discovery. Like them, he jumps from one topic to another, but he doesn't push each to its limits as they would. Martin can be agreeably whimsical, as when he wonders whether there were any Goths in Gothic times and how well they would fit in if there were. But thats hardly in the same league as Hedbergs meditations on king-and queen-size beds. (How come all you kings are the same size? Hey, I got one for your lady as well.")Martin also talks about how hard it is to make conversation with a baby. This is very true, but it's not an idea that he pursues to any great effect.

Nor does it have much to do with timing, except in the sense that everything does, especially since "timing as investigated here seems to be interchangeable with the grander and even more inclusive subject of time." One of his sketches --it's really more a succession of micro-vignettes -- has him taking off in a time machine (thinly disguised as a pop-dispenser) and capitalizing on his historical knowledge: Encouraged by an initial encounter with Mary Magdalene, he makes bets with a 1929 flapper on the likelihood of a crash and appears, laden with potatoes, to an Irish peasant girl at the height of the famine. In another, even more desultory item, hes a rookie cop shooting up the wrong apartment. Both these subjects would probably be funnier if he just riffed on them to the camera.

In his most sustained attempt at working with other performers, hes an actor playing a break-up scene with his girlfriend, unable to get angry with her but getting very angry with the director, who keeps telling him to get angry. ("Use it!" says the director, but all Martin can do is lose it.) I suppose that's about timing. Anyway it's reasonably ingenious. And it makes good use of Martins incurable good-natured-ness. Hes almost a throwback to the Zen comics of the '60s; irrespective of what he's actually saying, he seems to be signalling peace and love, brother.

You cant help liking Martin, if only for being one of the few comedians who doesnt keep complaining about his terrible luck with women. But likeable isn't the same as funny. He features a running gag that I liked, a recurring commercial about buying rings of escalating value for every occasion up to and including bankruptcy. And, in a final display of multidimensional timing, he plays a variety of musical instruments while turning the pages of a chart with jokes written on it. (Flip charts were his principal shtick on the Stewart show.)

Not every Daily Show alumnus proves a major comic when out on his own, or even while still there (think of Lewis Black and despair), and I wouldn't at the moment give Martin more than a passing grade. He seems bright enough. Maybe he should trust it more. Selfdeprecation is all very well, but if you're not careful, you may find your audience agreeing with you.

- Very Important Things With Demetri Martin airs tonight at 10:30 p. m. on The Comedy Network.
