Three dollars for the best writing and acting on television strikes me as a steal. Let me explain. Im hooked on Horace and Pete, a series that can be downloaded from American comedian Louis C.K.s website, and is not available legally in any other way. So far there have been 10 episodes, released at weekly intervals, offering great bingewatching for the foreseeable future. C.K. (I hate referring to him that way, but it's his choice of surname) writes and directs, and is also one of its stars. He charges a premium $5 for the first show, a loss-leading $2 for the second and $3 for all subsequent episodes. (Well, all right, they're U.S. dollars. But still.)

This method of bypassing the traditional distributors - network, cable, even Netflix - isnt completely unprecedented (this type of thing is becoming the norm in the music business), but I cant think of any drama or comedy - Horace and Pete is both - undertaken in this way and on this scale, which has been released exclusively on the web. And compared with the $50 The Screening Room proposes to charge for the privilege of watching movies at home on the day they come out, C.K.'s product is a bargain. It also, in contrast to schemes inveigling us into watching big films on small screens, has the esthetic advantage of being beamed to the medium for which it was intended.

C.K.'s high profile enabled his show to keep a low one. The first anyone knew that Horace and Pete existed was when the first episode, or the invitation to buy it, cropped up on the site. He had enough followers for word to spread, and it has.

Its cheering to see a new way of delivering content, without the middleman, taking off. C.K. financed the show himself. Im not sure if that makes him the Donald Trump of independent production, but it would be pleasingly ironic if it did, as C.K., on that same website, has posted one of the most passionately persuasive anti-Trump pieces I have ever read. (It takes a comedian to know a comedian?) The most heartening thing of all is that Horace and Pete justifies its independence. It's its own animal, unlike anything else on TV (I guess it counts as TV). Its tone is both too leisurely and too outrageous for the networks; its visual style is too basic for cable.

It's set in a Brooklyn bar called Horace and Petes, founded exactly 100 years ago by a couple of brothers called Horace and Pete. That Horace had a son called Horace, that Pete had a son called Pete, and so it has gone, through the generations, both the names and the ownership of the bar. Theres a tradition in American drama of presenting the barroom as a reflector of the national mood: hopeful or, as in this case, fractured.

C.K. himself plays the current Horace, Steve Buscemi is the current Pete and Alan Alda, in a show-stealing performance, is their uncle, wonderfully identified as "the previous Pete," a foul-mouthed and cantankerous old bigot who occasionally says something disconcertingly sensible. Edie Falco, America's greatest actress, plays Sylvia, the proprietors' sister, and Jessica Lange is Marsha, their late fathers final girlfriend, or, as Sylvia graphically puts it, his last pleasure-chest. That, anyway, is the family set-up at the start; some surprise skeletons have been rattled since, but I wouldnt want to spoil them for you.

C.K.'s control over both content and distribution is something that Orson Welles would have killed for and that even Woody Allen, as an actor-writerdirector still dependent on studios and outside finance, might envy. C.K. isn't bound by time slots or the conventional television structure. His first two episodes each run for over an hour; most subsequent ones are about half that. In some - but not all - theres a break in the action and a caption appears saying, "Intermission." Episode five concluded with the message: End of Act One. C.K., it seems, goes where he pleases.

What this suggests is that, where most TV aspires to the condition of film, Horace and Pete sees itself as a play. And this is how its written, staged and performed: in extended dialogue sequences that may be duets, trios or ensembles. In television terms, this is so oldfashioned as to be revolutionary - a throwback to how sitcoms used to be shot, minus the audience and the laugh-track. Often the camera will pull back and let us see the actors, as in a theatre, in equal opportunity long-shot; it sometimes looks clunky, but it gives a sense, unusual in TV, of the spatial relationships between characters, which is especially rewarding when they're as vividly portrayed as these are. And it lets us see the whole bar.

Mostly it's the bar in the afternoon. The customers are what C.K., when interviewed by Jimmy Kimmel (the networks do have their uses), has described as "day drinkers:" the hard core. Two other comedians play regulars. Steven Wright, taciturn and morose, brings his own coaster, which is a statement in itself. Kurt Metzger is a contrastingly voluble patron whose profane gloss on the biblical story of Sodom and Gomorrah is the shows most sustained comic cadenza. Hes also, in his own way, political. He has persuaded a conservative and a liberal that, if they actually listened to one another's principles, they would find very little to be hostile about. In a more mordant variation on the same theme, he has suggested that the ideal U.S. election outcome would be for Trump and Bernie Sanders to be co-presidents: Bernie will keep taking money from the rich people; Trump will give it back. This "will reveal the whole nature of the system." Eventually, nobody will be worth anything and they'll tear it all down.

Up-to-the minute references, invariably disenchanted, are a feature of the show, an entertaining byproduct of its fast turnover. They raise the question: does C.K. actually write a whole new show every week? If he does, its a phenomenal achievement, considering everything else hes doing. My guess, though, is that he stockpiled the bulk of the scripts, sprinkling in the topical bits at the last moment. They're nice, they're funny, but they feel like add-ons.

Most of the show isnt that funny (though when the laughs come theyre big, genuine ones). It is, however, comic.

It's comic like Chekhov is comic: the comedy of desperate people - sometimes wellmeaning, sometimes not - each trapped in an individual bubble of self-regard, itself part of a larger collective bubble. And, just as the family in Chekhov's most famous play were trying to hold on to their cherry orchard, so the family in Horace and Pete are trying to hold on to Horace and Petes. At least, some of them are.

Pete, heart-rendingly played by Buscemi, seems to be the nicest, and most of the time the gentlest, person around; he spent 10 years in a mental hospital, still has psychotic episodes when hes off his meds and has nowhere else to go. Sylvia, whose rage and pain in Falco's performance burn up the screen, wants to sell; she has cancer and desperately needs the money. Horace wavers, but he feels an overwhelming obligation to tradition - a tradition that includes selling no mixed drinks and a very limited range of beers. Pete calls him "passive aggressive" and C.K., who doesn't have his colleagues acting chops, stresses the passive (though his mode as a stand-up comic has been aggressive). His Horace goes along to get along, listening and responding, generally acting kind but sometimes cruel.

This country, says Sylvia, apparently out of nowhere but her own despair, "is going to s-t in a handcart" (her own less elegant variation of the proverbial phrase about hell and handbaskets). The grandest, darkest example of the barroom as American metaphor is Eugene ONeill's The Iceman Cometh, and Horace and Pete sometimes suggests the tight family drama of O'Neills other masterpiece, Long Days Journey into Night, superimposed on the sprawling canvas of his last-chance saloon. Except that O'Neill didn't do jokes.

Whats truly remarkable about Horace and Pete is its riveting succession of long confessional monologues, all of them delivered by the ladies of the cast - by Falco, Lange or by one of the guest females who appear on a weekly basis (Horaces exwife, for example, who turns up to tell him about her new liaison with a much older man, and to seek and disregard his advice). These speeches are so completely inhabited by the performers that one suspects them of being improvised, though it seems logistically unlikely. There are cat-and-mouse duologues, first dates or morning-after fencing matches that are almost as good. There's a cunning, and increasingly gripping, dramatic shape. Horace and Pete proves C.K. to be a great enabler of actors and a major American playwright. And he's done it in his own format and on his own dime. Not to mention his own mailing-list.
