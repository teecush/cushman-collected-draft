INDIAN INK

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

My favourite Tom Stoppard story concerns a screenwriting offer he turned down because of a prior commitment to the BBC. You mean, said Hollywood, aghast, "you'd pass up a movie for a TV play?" Actually, said Stoppard (bashfully, one presumes) "its for a radio play."

Thats loyalty. Stoppard started in radio and went on to conquer theatre, television and film -- in that order and with an almost infallible sense of what belongs to each. Most of his successes, in fact, would be untransferrable from one medium to another. The exception is Indian Ink, a 1995 stage play that began life four years earlier as a radio piece called In the Native State. It works both ways. At CanStage, in a shimmering production, it brings the Toronto season to a sorely needed new high.

The play revolves around Flora Crewe, a fictitious English poet of questing intelligence and great personal attractiveness, who visits India in 1930, a year that ironically -- since she is supposed to be travelling for her health -- proves to be her last.

In the native state of Jummapur she encounters, and enchants, the Rajah who ostensibly governs the place, a young representative of the British government that supposedly controls the Rajah and -- most importantly -- an artist named Nirad Das, who offers to paint her portrait. While sitting, she composes poems -- poems that will eventually be published (posthumously, of course) under the title Indian Ink and will indeed prove to be, in a modest way, indelible. (The play's stage title makes a more reverberant pun than its radio equivalent.)

Crewe sends letters about her experiences to her younger sister Eleanor, back in London. Fifty years later, this correspondence becomes an important part of The Collected Letters of Flora Crewe, time and feminism having turned the once-neglected lady poet into a cult figure.

It makes me so cross, says her now-elderly sister, "that she missed it all ... Nobody gave tuppence about her while she was alive except to get her knickers off."

Eleanor now finds herself besieged by archeologists of Flora's past. These include the American academic who edited (i.e., heavily footnoted) the letters and is now surreptitiously researching a biography, and Anish Das, son of Nirad and also a painter.

The action criss-crosses between India and England, the 30s and the 80s. Partly it's about heredity and influence, and the unexpected ways in which these can work themselves out. With Indian independence a clear inevitability, Das the elder is a political nationalist and a cultural Anglophile, passionately devoted to Shakespeare, Dickens and -- above all -- Agatha Christie. (Civilizations, the play suggests, flatten at a distance; we are all cultural tourists, whether we stay home or visit.)

He never gets to the London he adores, but his son -- far more radical, artistically and politically -- ends up living there while deploring the continued British influence on his homeland.

Eleanor went out to India to visit her sister's grave, met another young English official and married him. Formerly a Communist, she became a sardonically observant lady of the Raj. Politics tend, in a Stoppard play, to be a function of whom you sleep with. Now Eleanor serves tea and homemade cake to visitors -- and laments that the tea, at least, never tastes as good as it did in India.

Stoppard is the best practising playwright in the English language, but his plays have always invited two kinds of suspicion: The simple, noticing that they contain ideas, assume they must be cold and difficult; and the solemn, noticing they contain jokes, assume they must be superficial. (One faction thinks them pretentious, the other not pretentious enough.)

Actually, the plays dont just contain jokes, they are made of them, in structure as well as dialogue -- serious jokes that blend and dovetail perfectly, and wrap up both the meaning and the actions. The only fault to be found with them is that the fizzy allusiveness sometimes leads to important plot points being sketched in rather than blocked out.

As for coldness: Stoppard must be the only dramatist who now writes intelligently about love, and the fact he does it with wit only makes his characters more endearing and -- often at unexpected moments -- more moving.

The heart of this play is in the encounters between Flora and Nirad Das, the Englishwoman and the Indian, trying to break through their ingrained notions not only of one another but of themselves. They proceed on at least three levels of contact and conflict -- sexual, political, artistic -- which of course overlap; when Das throws stones at the official English Daimler, whos to say whether it's an act of protest or of jealousy at a possible rival?

In Richard Cottrell's production these scenes come over even better than they did in the original London staging.

Fiona Reids Flora is, I think, unimprovable: perfect in sense of time and place, unimprovable in its style, pace and vulnerability. The wit is both verbal (You never mentioned Virginia Woolf, she is told after giving a literary talk. "I seldom do," she replies sweetly) and physical; Flora says she likes to wriggle her toes when shes working, and Reid shows her thoughts working excitedly through her feet. What she conveys most intensely is a proud, amused respect for her own integrity and everyone else's.

Sanjay Talwar as Das matches Reid in his sensitive alternation of eagerness and reserve. Their scenes include, by the way, the best- written nude scene ever -- a fortunate legacy of the play's radio origins. Das ends up painting Flora twice -- once with clothes, once without -- and the difference in the two pictures crystallizes all the plays themes.

All the minor Indian characters are excellently played. The Westerners, apart from Hazel Desbarats Eleanor -- and even she could vary her indomitability more than she does -- are less satisfactory. The footnoting U.S. scholar is clumsily done (his role is much enlarged from the radio version, and it seems a questionable decision) and Flora's pukka sahib admirer is dull, though the scene in which he takes her riding is pretty irresistible, staged as it is on a pair of pommel horses.

The staging throughout is lucid and graceful, and Susan Benson's designs, in which a translucent screen beautifully backs the whole width of the Bluma Appel stage, are the most exquisite the town has seen in an age.
