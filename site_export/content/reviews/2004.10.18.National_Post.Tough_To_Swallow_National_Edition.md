OMNIUM GATHERUM

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Dinner parties are among the few things in the world harder to arrange than plays, since for parties you don't usually get to rehearse. So hats should be off to the director and cast of Omnium Gatherum, who have had to cope with both challenges at once.

Here the party actually is the play; what's more, the normal hazards of serving and eating are accentuated by the fact that, for most of the performance, the dinner table is slowly revolving. There is an obvious technical reason for this; it means all seven actors (there is an eighth, but he's a late arrival) get equal time for acting with their backs to us. But we may also be reminded of the restaurants with spectacular views that you find near the tops of tall buildings: places like Windows on the World at the World Trade Center. Maybe I'm imagining the allusion but, since Omnium Gatherum is concerned with matters arising from Sept. 11, it seems apposite.

And there I just about run out of compliments. The play is a collaboration between two American writers, Theresa Rebeck and Alexandra Gersten-Vassilaros, who in the wake of the attacks found themselves incensed by the unmediated screaming of partisan opinions that passed for debate on TV and in print. Their reaction is understandable. Unfortunately, all they've done in their play is to reproduce the phenomenon they're deploring. Their characters are mouthpieces shouting past one another.

The party-thrower is Suzie, who runs a billion-dollar gracious- living business and is thus reminiscent of Martha Stewart, though much sillier. Her guests include an English intellectual journalist, neo-conservative but anti-Israeli and a very big drinker, who is bound to stir thoughts of Christopher Hitchens; a courtly Arab scholar who suggests a somewhat idealized Edward Said; and a bellicose novelist who everybody tells me is Tom Clancy.

There are others less easily tagged: a black woman who is apparently a minister, and a feminist whom the novelist thinks, wrongly, must be a lesbian. The gourmet meal, each of whose courses gets lovingly described, is wasted on her, since she's both a vegan and allergic to walnuts. They all, even Suzie, are writers of some kind; the only common ground they have is their hatred of the book reviews in The New York Times. Otherwise, they spend the night at one another's throats, disagreeing about literally everything. As the last topic clicks into place (the environment, I think), Suzie gives an approving gurgle. This is the kind of stimulating conversation she was hoping for.

I doubt that any sane hostess would knowingly assemble so rudely ill-assorted a bunch, and I also doubt that they would proceed in such an ordered fashion from one subject to the next. Real-life conversations go off at tangents. But then this, we learn, is no real-life party. We only learn it, though, very late in the show, and the revelation involves the authors in breaking what had seemed to be their own rules. David Storch's production has tried earlier to suggest that all is not what it seems, with flashing lights and loud noises and gusts of smoke, but it isn't enough to dispel the pervading air of ham-handed naturalism.

The seventh diner is at first conspicuously silent. He turns out to be a firefighter who was one of the rescuers at the twin towers. Suzie, roguishly donning a burqa, ushers in a surprise guest whom she announces as a real-live Arab terrorist and who plays that role to the hilt. Some physical violence gets mixed in with the verbal.

But if this is meant to be a party at the end of the world, or even if it isn't, then much of what we've heard is bewilderingly trivial. What is the point of the vegan lady's disquisition on her love life? Why does the black lady sing? Why does the Hitchens chap proclaim not just that humanity has been at war for all of its history (indisputable), but that peace is undesirable -- a position I cannot imagine even the contrarian Hitch espousing? These characters are made of straw. And is all the gourmandizing meant to make us feel guilty? The gap between rich and poor is a huge issue but, given that it exists, it's of very minor interest what specific luxuries the lucky ones choose to indulge in.

To be fair, the constant emphasis on food gives the play some metaphorical, if not intellectual, coherence. And to be even fairer, it is never actually boring; the state of the world takes care of that.

There are two admirable speeches for the Arabist. Both are finely delivered by Nigel Shawn Williams, an actor who is moving into maturity at full gallop. Fiona Reid, playing Suzie, never once slips outside her character; she turns crassness into poetry. It seems, all the same, a waste of her talents. Brenda Robins as the feminist acts well but with an unsuitable brittleness. Sam Kalilieh is vivid as the terrorist, Andrea Scott largely unnoticeable as the minister, and Ashley Wright powerfully quiet as the fireman -- though when he finally gets a big speech, one obviously intended to stop us in our tracks, it turns out to be a routine recap of familiar horrors. The crudest figures are the Englishman and the novelist; at least the performances (by James Macdonald and Hardee T. Lineham) make them seem. The tableware glistens becomingly.

Toward the end Suzie, proudly surveying her convocation, invokes the play's own awful title; but the irony, if that's what it is, rebounds on the authors. They have no apparent reason for bringing these people together other than as a means of paying lip service to a popular fallacy, i.e. that it is their bounden duty to write about their country's trauma. Unless they have something individual to say about it, it isn't. Their conclusion here, as unexceptionable as uncontroversial, is the one reached years ago, more wittily and succinctly, in the song of The Reluctant Cannibal: Eating people is wrong.
