AN ACRE OF TIME

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

The best thing about An Acre of Time is its stage. The Tarragon mainspace has been re-ordered so that the audience sits on three sides of a long rectangular platform; in such a small theatre, the feeling of the spectators surrounding the action can be very powerful. There seem to be two reasons for the arrangement. One is that it makes a nice change. The other is that it can give us the sensation of peering down into the inner workings of a society, this being what Jason Sherman's play is meant to be about.

At least that is half of what it is about. The remaining half concerns one woman and her personal problems. And though the author keeps trying to persuade us that each is a reflection of the other, the attempt is not convincing. We have two plays tied together with a fraying piece of string -- and they keep tripping one another up.

An Acre of Time is "inspired by" Phil Jenkins' book, which I have not read but which I gather is an impressionistic survey of the history of LeBreton Flats, a dilapidated piece of land by the Ottawa River. It was inhabited by the First Nations, appropriated by the French and English, run down and finally abandoned by everyone. According to its admirers, Jenkins' book is written in "poetic prose," but even with that liability it seems possible that the material it presents might have been fashioned into a workable semi- documentary theatre piece. Similar things have happened.

Sherman's play introduces a fictitious central character -- Julia Wright, a surveyor sent out by the National Capital Commission to help assess the Flats' potential for redevelopment. She has a personal connection to the region: Her young daughter drowned in the Ottawa River while on a camping trip with her father. (The couple have now, inevitably, split up.) This being the case, it seems unlikely that Julia would have accepted the assignment, but she seems to want to exorcise -- or maybe just exercise -- her memories.

She turns in what her superiors feel to be an unsatisfactory report, and the play shows her justifying it in flashback to a review board made up of the other members of the cast. The actors, an alert-enough bunch in the other roles they play during the evening, seem uncomfortable with this device. And so, apparently, does the author, since he has dropped it by the end of the show. Since this is hardly fourth-wall realism, he surely could have had Julia just talk to us; it might conceivably have given her a firmer position at the play's centre.

Julia spends some of her time fending off the advances of her boss, who has suffered his own bereavement, and of a boorish city planner, while succumbing to those of a young native filmmaker. (Like her, he is scouting locations.) She also has visions -- visions of figures associated with the area's past. She meets Samuel de Champlain and an 18th-century German practitioner of her own trade, as well as the entrepreneurial LeBreton for whom the flats were named. The tone of these encounters grows increasingly farcical. LeBreton, genial and accident-prone, sounds Cockney, but his six "nieces" (there seems some doubt about their true status) are done in as many different varieties of the English middle class, which at least gives the actress playing them a chance to show off.

We then get a play-within-the-play-within-the play, as one niece leads Julia inside a theatre where revue-like vignettes from the region's history are presented. The style resembles that of VideoCabaret's comic-strip chronicles, only not as expert and not as instructive. The cross-talking Jewish traders swapping gags about pogroms are probably unfunny on purpose, but that isn't much consolation while you're watching them. Brian Quirt's production is physically well manoeuvred, but if there is a comic tone here, he hasn't found it.

The play also keeps re-enacting Julia's last fateful conversation with her husband and child, without ever telling us any more than it did the first time -- as if reiteration in itself would move us. She also begins marking out an acre of ground with an old native measuring pole, to the bemusement of her Indian lover, who expected something more high-tech. ("Cutbacks?" he inquires. There are some good lines, and most of them are his.)

What she is doing, it transpires, is marking out an area for her own memory. That is a really forced metaphor, and it leads to an equally forced confrontation. Julia turns on the planner, who apparently is contemptible by definition, and tells him that whatever monstrosities he erects, he should leave an acre free to honour the past. She seems to be equating her own past with that of the territory, maybe of the country, and the equation does not hold.

Nor indeed do either of its constituent parts. We have learned very little about LeBreton Flats (nothing at all about its ordinary inhabitants) and not much about Julia. She keeps talking about her loss, but that doesn't mean that it has been dramatized. It is perhaps unfortunate for An Acre of Time that it arrives in Toronto after we have seen two productions of The Weir, which narrated the death of a child to far more devastating effect and managed to depict a society as well.

Pierre Brault, Timothy Hill and Kristen van Ginhoven do best among the supports. As Julia, Susan Coyne goes through the play looking and sounding glum, only flaring into life when she gets her tirade at the end. Even this, though, has only a momentary impact. It's as if the playwright were flogging himself into making some kind of statement. The play seems the theatrical equivalent of CanLit: aggressively good for you and very dispiriting.
