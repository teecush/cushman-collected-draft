In one of those glamorous continental opera boxes, sumptuously equipped for everything except watching opera, a Hungarian Hussar pays court to a married woman who happens to be an actress. Her husband, naturally, is an actor, supposedly out of town somewhere playing Cyrano. Nandor, the actor, is madly jealous of Ilona, the actress, who has a reputation for growing bored and unfaithful after six months, which is the precise time they've been married. So he decides to test her by making love to her in disguise; he, huzza, is the hussar. She, though professing herself flattered, at first spurns his suit (he's wearing a gold-barred uniform straight out of Mittel-European musical comedy); later, though, she shows distinct signs of yielding. This leaves him in a perfect frenzy of mixed emotions. Her, too.

The play is The Guardsman, written in 1910 by Ferenc Molnar and now directed for Soulpepper by Laszlo Marton, the company's own semi-resident Hungarian. That's the same combination that 10 years ago gave us a signature production of The Play's the Thing, another piece in which professional role-players find their lives forced into imitating their art.

The Guardsman is almost equally scintillating but cuts deeper, certainly in this version, which is the best I've seen (and that includes one at Stratford with Maggie Smith and Brian Bedford). Of course, on the surface it's incredible that a wife, especially one who's professionally accustomed to seeing her spouse in fancy dress, shouldn't see right through his charade. In fact, though, there's a good chance that she does; certainly she, when all is revealed, would like him to think so. But neither he nor we can ever be sure. And if she does know, is she playing along to spare his feelings or to teach him a lesson? He, meanwhile, is impaled on the horns of what I suppose has to be called a trilemma. As her husband, he wants her to stay virtuous; as her would-be seducer, he can't help wanting her to succumb; and as an actor, he has a vested emotional interest in carrying off his impersonation, whatever the outcome. The great achievement of Marton's production is that it keeps all these possibilities constantly, and teasingly, in play, and manages to reconcile several different styles in doing so.

The performance slips smoothly from agony to ecstasy; from high comedy to broad farce, mental and physical, and back again; which is what the play does, and what real life does. At one point, wife and lover ponder the possibility of the husband returning unexpectedly, knowing full well that in farce, as has often been pointed out, nobody ever returns expectedly. Except that in this case, at least one party knows that he will. The play's climax, though far from its resolution, is Nandor's slow deliberate re-assumption of his disguise: a sort of striptease in reverse. Underlying everything is a quicksand feeling of erotic uncertainty that looks forward both to the self-cancelling husband-lover of Irma la Douce and the marital fantasy of Pinter's The Lover.

The play starts with Nandor and Ilona at home, he very snappy, she seemingly and infuriatingly unperturbed: "If you knew," she says, "how little I was suffering, you wouldn't be so happy" -- one of several impeccably delivered lines that reveal a wit in Frank Marcus' translation that I would never have suspected. She sits at the piano, dreamily playing Chopin, which he takes as a very bad sign; he seethes, suffers and schemes. Nandor off duty is, in Albert Schultz's performance, a grumpy, vulnerable matinee idol, unused to domesticity and unsettled by it. In his guardsman gear, he's the complete opposite: gallant in get-up but shy, soft-spoken, socially out of his depth, painfully -- and, in the circumstances ironically-- sincere. Which is a surprise but, on consideration, logical: Nandor's masquerade allows him to express a gentleness that he otherwise keeps bottled up; it's fantasy on more than one level.

One thing that unites his personae is that both have the air of a small boy, apt to sulk in a corner; Schultz, apart from being expertly witty, keeps all the elements fizzing. Kristen Thomson's natural mode, casual and contemporary, doesn't sort too well with the brittle Ilona of the drawing-room, and verbal slips on the first night signalled discomfort. But in the opera-box-as-boudoir she's on the way to being enchanting, a coquette with a serious core, playing a game that only she--perhaps --fully understands. An actor, she emphatically informs her uniformed admirer, can be intelligent but not very intelligent. Whether the same goes for actresses, she isn't saying.

There's a third leading character who's said to be a critic by trade but spends his time hanging out with the stars and receiving their confidences; the job has certainly gone downhill in the last century or so. Diego Matamoros' role is similar to the one he had in The Play's the Thing, but this one's been removed from the controlling centre to the suffering sidelines. Anyway, in an evening where metatheatrics are already running riot, it's a pleasure to watch another instalment in the long-running, and constantly shifting, Schultz-Matamoros double act; no two actors play better together. A good supporting cast includes Dawn Greenhalgh as Ilona's dresser turned surrogate-mother (and Nandor's newly acquired and fearsome mother-in-law); Michael Simpson as an obsequious debt-collector who can be bought off with complimentary tickets; and Jennifer Phipps as an usherette who conveys, mostly in silence, that she knows exactly what she's worth and won't take a pfennig less. Camellia Koo's sets and Judith Bowden's costumes redeem the reputation of chocolate-box design: apt for a play with a sweet surface and a hard centre. - The Guardsman is in repertory at the Young Centre through Oct. 24.
