Eurydice, the beloved of the musician Orpheus, died of a snake bite on the very day of their wedding. Orpheus, distraught, followed her into Hades and was granted permission to bring her back to the land of the living, on condition that he walk in front of her on the journey. If he turned around to look at or speak to her, she would be lost to him forever. But he did, and she was.

If this Greek myth was ever dramatized by one or other of the great Greek tragedians, the results have not come down to us. The gap has been more than filled however by later generations of artists: theatre, literature, cinema, music, painting are crowded with treatments of the Orpheus-Eurydice story. The latest to take it on is the American playwright Sarah Ruhl. Or maybe I should say one of the latest; Ruhl's Eurydice was first staged at Yale in 2006, then the following year in New York, and who knows how many attempts there may have been to resurrect Eurydice since then? Ruhl's version reaches us now in a luminous production from Soulpepper.

Her play transposes the legend to something like the present day and overlays it with another myth, that of Persephone, who was abducted to the land of the dead because its king wanted her for his queen. (She ended up getting six months parole every year.) That, roughly, is what happens here to Eurydice: the King of the Underworld engineers her demise, and eventually makes her an offer she is in no position to refuse. There is an even more important layer to Ruhl's retelling, one for which there are no mythological precedents. Her play is less about husbands and wives than about fathers and daughters. It's founded on the idea, explicitly stated at one point, that a woman is married to her father (just spiritually of course) until she finds a mate of her own. It's a questionable theory, but the writing, and the acting in Alan Dilworth's production, make it moving. Eurydice's father is dead, but that doesn't stop him from sending his daughter affectionate letters, congratulating her on her engagement. ("I hear he's a musician".

Turns out dad once had musical aspirations himself.) Oliver Dennis, who has given uncountable fine performances for Soulpepper, may here be giving the finest of all. This father isn't a hundred per cent dead; the dead aren't allowed to remember, but it seems that, when in transit, he neglected to immerse himself fully in the fabled waters of forgetfulness. So he can write to Eurydice that he's still working, even posthumously; and Dennis, chippy and dapper in a business suit that he wears like a second skin, makes the claim seem credible. It doesn't erase the undercurrent of loss and melancholy. A later scene provides as poignant an image as you're likely to encounter: Orpheus and Eurydice dance happily at their wedding to the Andrews Sisters' "Don't Sit Under the Apple Tree"; on another stage level father, to the same music, dances alone. When Eurydice herself arrives below (in a red elevator in which it seems to be raining) she doesn't recognize him; she takes him for a porter, and asks for a room. Like a good father, he provides her one; in a long, silent and spellbinding scene, he constructs it for her himself, out of string.

Eurydice's impatience, her insistence on applying her own logical rules to a new and mystifying situation, may remind us of Alice in Wonderland, and Michelle Monteith does indeed look like the Alice of the Tenniel drawings. The role perfectly suits her personal blend of the ethereal and the scarily practical. Father does succeed in jogging her memory, and as they draw closer together we may start thinking of the great Shakespearean father-daughter reconciliations, in King Lear and Pericles. Turns out the play is ahead of us; Dennis opens a book that happens to be lying around and begins reading Lear's "we two will sing like birds i' th' cage". The book is important; Eurydice's husband never cared much for books, prizing only music. And when, after her second descent, she steps on the volume, asking what this "thing" might be, it's one of the two most wrenching moments in the show, rivalled only by the sight of Dennis marching proudly with his daughter on his arm, as if he were leading her down the aisle.

This all results in Orpheus himself being rather sidelined; his responsibility for his wife's loss unclear. As Gregory Prest plays him, probably accurately, he's self-absorbed, both in love and in loss; his best moments are his most impatient, as when he tries to send a telegram to his dead wife, via an understandably baffled operator. He has better luck when using worms as his messengers. But here we hit a credibility gap. The Greeks believed in the legends they re-told and re-interpreted; not perhaps in all their details but in their framework and in its religious significance. Later writers don't, and their adaptations - picking and choosing what to add and what to leave out - invariably have an air of preciousness. The French playwrights of the mid-20th century did a lot of this; Jean Cocteau wrote an Orpheus and Jean Anouilh, and there's a French flavour about Ruhl's play, especially when Stuart Hughes turns up in a raincoat as a messenger of death, later revealed - in a frightening flash of light - to be the underworld king himself, goat-headed. The cast is completed by a trio of infernal guardians, known as the Stones: Alex McCooeye is Big Stone, Courtney Ch'ng Lancaster Little Stone and Oyin Olajedo Loud Stone, and they live up to their respective billings. They seem at first to show the neo-Greek style at its whimsical worst, but they grew on me. Their function is to condemn any deviation from the hellish rules of universal amnesia, any backsliding into memory; they especially dislike music, and tell Eurydice and her dad that anyway, they're no good at it and they ain't got rhythm. Here they're wrong; the couple have got through a very creditable wordless chorus of the Gershwin song. It's one of the nicest things in the play.

Dilworth's production is beautiful, sensitive to every modulation in the text. It's magically designed by Lorenzo Savoini, whose motifs of earth and water are reinforced by sound (Debais Sinha) and especially lighting (Kimberly Purtell). The theme of the dead forgetting the living recalls the last act of Thornton Wilder's Our Town, which handled it more movingly. There are in fact many echoes but Ruhl's play still manages to have its own voice.

Eurydice is in repertory through June 18 at the Young Centre.
