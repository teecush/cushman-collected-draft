THEATRE REVIEW

Father Comes Home From the Wars (Parts I , II , III )

Young Centre , Toronto A black man, named Hero, is in the woods, somewhere in the South during the American Civil War. He wears a Confederate uniform, though he isn't altogether a soldier, more of a valet, attending on the colonel whose plantation slave he was before the war and whose military slave he is now. The colonel is there too, and so is a Union soldier, a captain apparently, whom they captured on the battlefield where he had been left with a wounded leg. The colonel, wanting to impress, puts a treasured white plume on his hat, says he used to wear it all the time: "Now I just mostly carry it around in a box." He adds, off-handedly, "Hero made the box."

This is called keeping your eye on the object: the writer's eye and the audience's too. Thought and feeling made flesh, words turned into action, action into words. A complex situation made achingly, wittily concrete. It's what drama is about. It happens time and time again in Father Comes Home From the Wars (Parts I, II and III) by the American playwright Suzan-Lori Parks, first seen in New York two years ago, and now given its Canadian premiere in Weyni Mengesha's soul-stirring Soulpepper production.

Six further parts, bringing the narrative up to the present, have yet to be seen, perhaps because they have yet to be written. It's best to regard what we have in the meantime as a single play in three acts. The act from which I've quoted is the middle one: the most naturalistic of the three, the most concentrated, the most gripping and suspenseful. The surrounding two are as powerful and as real, but more stylized, partly written in verse (though you hardly notice). It incorporates the equivalent of a Greek chorus.

It's all Greek, at least in outline. The template is Homer's Odyssey with Hero himself as Ulysses. He even adopts that name, having heard stories about northern general Ulysses S. Grant that lay especial emphasis on his craftiness. Hero would like to be crafty, but he comes off more as opportunist, and not always sure of which opportunity to grab.

Act One finds him on the Texas plantation, with his fellow-slaves making bets on whether or not he'll go to the war. The master has promised him his freedom if he goes, but he's made that promise before and broken it. Then there's the complication that if Hero does go and fight he'll be fighting against his freedom. His adoptive father and Penny, his almost-wife, give him conflicting advice; they even contradict themselves.

This is to be a recurring situation; in each part of the play, in varying circumstances, somebody will be faced with the agonizing choice of moving on or staying put: "Two sides of the same coin. And the coin ain't even in your pocket." Even before the play started, Hero, we learn, had made another kind of choice. Another slave had tried to escape, and Hero had betrayed him. He had then, on master's orders and tempted by master's promises, cut off the fugitive's foot. The victim is still very much around. His name is Homer.

So we have a Hero who isn't heroic and a Homer who is lame rather than blind. Parks is playful with her sources. Like his Odyssean prototype, Hero has a faithful dog, humorously named Odd-See; and when, in Act Three, he returns from the war, he too has to contend with his Penelope's rival suitors, or in this case with one. In other respects he appears in his homecoming less like the classical Ulysses than like Ulysses's supremely tactless comrade Agamemnon, as depicted in the Greek tragedy that bears his name. The play also references Shakespeare - especially Henry V - and, perhaps more surprisingly, Rodgers and Hammerstein.

Mengesha's direction is spare, clear and driving, on an impressively raw set by Lorenzo Savoini, lit with pitiless exactness by Kevin Lamotte. Dion Johnstone's Hero is remarkable in the sympathy he arouses for a character who hardly does an admirable thing. He's trapped, partly by his own choices, partly by history, and is aware enough to know it.

When Smith, the Yankee prisoner (a fine performance by Gregory Prest of an apparently open-hearted man with secrets to keep) tempts him with visions of freedom, he sees clear through to the present: "When a Patroller comes up to me, when I say, I'm my own and I own my ownself, you think they'll leave me be ?" The interplay between the three actors in this central section is beyond praiseworthy.

Oliver Dennis, who has never been better, is cunningly casting for the colonel; first seen singing and strumming a banjo, he seems a poorer, slighter man than the "Boss-Massa-Boss " whom we've imagined as the terror of the plantation. But that's the point. "I am grateful every day ," he says, "that God made me white;" it's all he has to cling to, that and owning other people. He asks the shocked Smith to guess Hero's monetary value. He enjoys baiting both of them. But his playing with Hero's freedom is only partly sadistic; it's a war between his own best and worst impulses. He is, unexpectedly, the most complex character in the play.

Runner-up would be Homer whom Daren A. Herbert, previously known for musicals, endows with an explosive mix of biting intelligence and frustrated devotion. All the actors meet the high emotional bars their author sets. Lisa Berry's Penny, a little stiff in the first act, rises in the third to thrilling levels of sustained angry passion. Walter Borden is a wily Oldest Old Man, Hero's fatherfigure. Peter Fernandes bounds and yelps delightfully as a messenger whose identity it would be unkind to reveal. Divine Brown supplies gorgeously sung commentary. The spoken chorus (Akosua Amo-Adem, Roy Lewis, Marcel Stewart) appear in the first act as Hero's fellow workers and in the last as fugitives from other plantations; they are individually compelling, though occasionally awkward in the semi-colloquial way that Greek choruses, real or imitation, tend to be.

The writing is hugely versatile, in tone, form, flavour and even period; "mark it " is a signature phrase at the beginning, "true dat " at the end. It's hardly a spoiler to say that the play concludes with Emancipation proclaimed, so it seems the runaways didn't have to run away. A greater irony, implicit but poised to erupt in future instalments, is that emancipation was only the first step on a still unfinished journey.

This is the most exhilaratingly ambitious American play to reach us since Stephen Adly Guirgis'The Last Days of Judas Iscariot. It has the feel of greatness.
