OTA

Artword Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - When it played at Harbourfront a few years ago, Robert Lepages The Seven Streams of the River Ota lasted about as many hours as there were streams, and at that it was still a work in progress. The show called Ota, presented by a group of recent graduates from the York University theatre program, plays for all of 90 minutes, and has the number of dramatic streams down to two, of which one now seems an insignificant tributary. It was given a long preview period at the Artword, followed by a tantalizingly brief official run. It must be brought back; it works extraordinarily well.

This is not a matter of cutting off surplus fat. Nobody who saw the original is likely to have wished it shorter; its length was not only part of its power but part of its charm. Like all Lepage's best work, it combined grandeur of theme and physical presentation with an engaging casualness in writing and performance. A delightful Feydeau-esque sequence about adultery is one of the things that has now gone missing. Like everything else it led, lightly but inexorably, to Hiroshima, from whose river the play takes its title.

Stephanie Saunders' adaptation makes no attempt to compare with the full version in richness, but it competes very well in imagination, intensity, wit and humanity. It takes one vital narrative thread and presents it in two segments.

In the first, set just after the war, an American military photographer, instructed to document the carnage, visits the stricken city, and falls in love with one of the living victims. Off to the side -- both of the stage and the story --sits a Czech- Jewish artist turned Buddhist monk discoursing on the peace to be found after surviving two holocausts. The role is played with sly strength by Rae Ellen Bodie, and survival is the plays theme, but it fits awkwardly in the new slim structure.

The love story, however, is beautifully done, told with very few words and staged with a deliberation that is measured but not portentous. Here, as elsewhere in Saunders production, the actors play with the kind of wise, shared rhythm that a director can inspire but not impose. Sherri McFarlane (who has her back to us throughout) and Adam Kelly make their union seem not only possible but inevitable.

If this sequence is the aftermath of the bomb, the remainder, set 20 years later, is the aftermath of the aftermath. Two young men named Jeffrey -- one American and tending a father sick with leukemia, the other American-Japanese -- encounter one another while lodging in the same funky (i.e., cheap) New York boarding house. Names apart, they seem to have little in common; they are, in fact, suspicious of one another. They turn out to have everything in common, and the revelation, which crowns a growing sense of shared kindliness plus an inspired recap of Abbott and Costello's "Who's on first?" routine, is very moving. Now, of course, more than ever.

The two Jeffreys are excellently played by Conor Green and Mike Realba. The latter -- the Japanese one -- has a delicate comic sense, especially marked in his scenes with a loud beat poet whose percussive hymn to the Hudson both intrigues him and keeps him awake. Could he not, this Jeffrey politely inquires, confine himself at night to working on the lyrics? This bard does his practising in the shared bathroom, and the very same bath also serves to develop photographs, which returns us to where we started.

Drew Nelsons set, which features sliding screens and slats that fall to the floor and start the play with a resounding crash, serves evocatively for the first sequence and cleverly for the second. A few performances are amateurish -- meaning they punch hard, but cant follow through -- but none are actually harmful.

What this short work and its long progenitor have that so many lack is mystery, which is not the same thing as obscurity. They make sense, but they also make more than sense. Anyone who still remembers alternately wincing and yawning through the pretentions of Hiroshima Mon Amour should find this show an antidote. More immediately, its advent -- and, if there is justice, its impending resurrection -- means that I have to withdraw my last week's assertion that Skylight offers the best dramatic writing in Toronto.
