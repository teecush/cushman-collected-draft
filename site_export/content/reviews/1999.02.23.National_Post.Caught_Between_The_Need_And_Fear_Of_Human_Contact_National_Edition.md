THE STONE ANGEL

Bluma Appel Theatre

St. Lawrence Centre

Hagar, a 90-year-old woman, lives with her son and daughter-in- law and resents it. She seems to have spent most of her life resenting things. The Stone Angel, James W. Nichol's dramatization of the Margaret Laurence novel, takes us backward and forward through that life: a long, packed, and rewarding ride.

"I always swore I'd never be a burden" says Hagar with self- indulgent relish. The paradox -- a common one -- is that her independence can only be asserted at enormous cost to everybody else. The saving grace is that her greatest victim is always herself. She is consistently infuriating: When her family is falling spectacularly apart, her greatest concern seems to be what the neighbours may think. She probably doesn't mean that, but it's significant she even says it.

All the same, she has her attractions. One is that though she may rage and complain, she never whines. Another is that, underneath it all, she doesn't have a heart of gold, although one lives in constant fear that her authors may decide to give her one. In her last days, in hospital, she does reach out to a couple of patients, but this is partly mischievous rebellion against the authorities. Besides, the play has consistently shown her to be as eager for human contact as she is frightened by it.

We see her as a girl, growing up in rural Manitoba at the end of the last century, alternately petted and bullied by her father. When she marries, he virtually disowns her. (He was planning to go on exploiting her in his old age.) Her husband turns out to be a drunk, though it's a nice question how much further she pushes him into it. At first repelled by his lovemaking, she grows to enjoy it, though she can never bring herself to tell him so. Of her two sons, she spoils one, and he, too, turns drunkard on her; she neglects the other, and when she finally apologizes to him, he hardly knows how to take it. We are not asked to judge how many of her misfortunes are her fault: We take her whole and acknowledge that, while her survival is itself a victory, the tide will roll over her as remorselessly as over everybody else.

Nora McLellan plays Hagar with complete command, slipping effortlessly into every age group that's required of her, anchoring all of them in the consciousness of the old and increasingly desperate woman. The play has been criticized for presuming to dramatize an interior monologue, but that is its strength. It makes an instructive contrast with Nichol's very free adaptation of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde, which did a calamitous number on its original. This time, he keeps a fine balance between the narrative voice and the exterior world.

Hagar may think the supporting actors are only things in her dream, but they still live. Brett Porter is too soft-grained for her mud-caked farmer of a spouse (he looks about to burst into something rousing from Seven Brides for Seven Brothers), but Robert Persichini plays the long-suffering son with admirable shambling bewilderment, and there are excellent multiple performances by Michael Spencer- Davis and Elizabeth Brown. Janet Wright's direction is scenically resourceful and, most importantly, never yields an inch to sentiment.
