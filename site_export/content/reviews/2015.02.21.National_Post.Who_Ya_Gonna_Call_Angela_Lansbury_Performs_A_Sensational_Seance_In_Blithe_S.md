Twice in Blithe Spirit, Angela Lansbury as Madame Arcati goes into a trance. The process involves traversing the stage while executing an Egyptian sand dance, minus the sand. As the madame herself says, its an exhausting process but a stimulating one, and besides, its her job. She's a medium, a happy one, and there are times when a trance may be the only way of raising a spirit or, as is later required, banishing one. Or two. She certainly raises our spirits. Lansbury, whose Mama Rose in Gypsy was the greatest performance I have ever seen in a musical and one of the greatest performances of any kind, doesn't have to explore as extensive a technical or emotional terrain in this role, but she brings to it the same commitment, the same acceptance of an extravagant character on the characters own terms. Not that Madame Arcati, as Lansbury plays her, would ever think of herself as extravagant; shes a disciplined professional who relishes her work, just as she relishes a long bike ride or a dry martini, but who approaches it with the utmost seriousness. Sadly accustomed to skeptics, she faces them down, politely but witheringly, with a sharp word or dismissive glance. She rules the room.

Lansbury is 89; it seems obligatory to mention it. This production of Noel Coward's spectral comedy is in fact a double triumph for octogenarians, since Michael Blakemore has staged it with the care and flair that have characterized his work since he began directing in the 1960s. A distinguishing feature of his productions is that they never confuse pace with rush; a former actor himself, he gives his performers room to breathe, to establish themselves and to build relationships. It's evident here from the opening moments, which find Susan Louise OConnor as Edith the maid stopped in her tracks through the living room by the sight of the billowing window curtains, disturbed by a breeze from who knows where: long-range preparation for her otherwise unaccountable metamorphosis into psychic sidekick. She also has, when her employer tips her at the end, a delightful moment of silent double entendre that Ive never encountered before.

Her boss is Charles Condomine, the researching novelist whose late first wife, Elvira, is summoned by seance, to the discomfiture of her successor, Ruth. Charles is the play's pacemaker, the one whose reactions steer the action. Charles Edwards finds an enormous amount of wit in a role that Coward himself thought too boring to play; he also finds a remarkable amount of feeling. Charlotte Parry is an unusually complex Ruth, and the two of them paint a persuasive picture of a cool, companionable marriage more troubled than either will admit. Conversely, there's a far greater sexual charge than usual to Charles scenes with the revenante Elvira, even though Jemima Rooper doesnt go much beyond the seductive throatiness that shows why the lady is a vamp. This is the first time I have ever believed in the existence of the past lovers whose names the characters keep hurling at one another.

The later parts of the play remain undisguisably drawn-out, and I doubt if Madame Arcati herself could explain the concluding comings and goings to my satisfaction, but all around this is the blithest Blithe Spirit I have seen.

In one of the numberless vignettes that make up The Dining Room, a young WASP student informs his old WASP aunt, whom he's been quizzing about tableware, that she is the object of his anthropological class project. She throws him out of the house, with a saltiness of language surprising in one of her breeding. A.R. Gurney's 1982 play is itself an anthropological study: an investigation of how the welloff lived in the northeastern United States; the earliest identifiable date is the 1920s, the latest is what would, when the play was new, have been now. Once families gathered around the dining table to eat and talk; now teenagers deface the old rituals or new owners devise ways of carving the room up.

The set is unchanging, but the room is generic; or at least, if its specific the house must have changed hands at a dizzying rate - which, to judge from some of the elders insistence on continuity, it can't have done.

The chronology leapfrogs; scenes occasionally overlap. The dining room outwears its usefulness as a symbol; some of the scenes, including an affecting one about a mother's dementia, could happen anywhere.

At times, with its abrupt series of crises, it seems like a succession of soap-opera clips without the vital ingredient of to be continued. Nor is it as critical its own world, either positively or negatively, as it purports to be. But its well-written, and Joseph Zieglers Soulpepper production is beautifully tuned.

Six actors play between them more than 50 roles; there's no gender-bending but there's plenty of age-swapping; I never thought to see Sarah Wilson playing Brenda Robins mother. Derek Boyes is appropriately boyish, even (or especially) when playing an adulterous husband; Jeff Lillico excellently plays a series of squirming adolescents, though hes less happy as a paterfamilias, in what is admittedly a cartooned scene out of step with everything else.

Courtney Ch'ng Lancaster drives deep as a divorced daughter, seeking succour in the family nest. Diego Matamoros has fun in his small-boy roles, and more than fun in his grown-up ones, including an affectionate family tyrant (well, one assumes he's affectionate) inveighing against the New Deal before it's happened. All the actors are company regulars, and knowing them so well adds to the attraction of the quick changes.

In a world where, in the words of the song, "Everybody Ought to Have a Maid," each of the actresses gets to play one; and their roles, like their sister's in Blithe Spirit, say a lot, even or especially when silent.

Blithe Spirit runs through March 15 at the Princess of Wales Theatre; The Dining Room runs through March 7 at the Young Centre.
