AS YOU LIKE IT

Festival Theatre, Stratford

---

Des McAnuff's production of As You Like It is the best thing he has done at Stratford, by miles. It's visually glowing, musical in manners both riotous and subtle, mines nearly all the humour in the text plus some from outside, and is finally very moving. The play is a clear-eyed celebration of love and marriage, and the test of a production's success is whether it can inspire both happiness and awe when the four couples are united at the close. This one brings it off triumphantly.

It also highlights the play's headlong mix of conventions and, in so doing, reconciles them. This is a production set in, though mercifully not "about" the 1920s. Debra Hanson's desings for the Forest of Arden draw on a surrealist palette to depict the script's all-purpose Arcadia in which storybook lions may not lie down with lambs but do co-exist with realistically tended sheep. Motifs out of Dali or Magritte drop from the flies, though the green beneath them, dazzlingly lit by Michael Walton, is surprisingly uncluttered; there are no trees to get in the way of the wood. Before we get there, we're in a court ruled by a usurping Duke who's an apprentice Hitler. There are some excesses here: Duke Frederick may be a neurotic tyrant, but it's a cliche to have him kill or beat up everybody who comes before him (I'm still trying to work out the identity of one of his victims); while the initial grappling of Orlando and his big bad brother is so prolonged that the official wrestling match in the next scene is an anti-climax.

A play that's mostly mood rather than narrative is hurt by having two intermissions. And I strongly object to having old Adam, just before the first break, drop dead from faithful exhaustion, while those around heartlessly get on with singing, "Blow, blow, thou winter wind." It may be a cynical song, but it doesn't work as a requiem. Most of the song settings, though, are delightfully jazzy (they're by Justin Ellington, himself related to a Duke), culminating in an irresistible treatment of that prototypical pop number "It was a lover and his lass." Other music is by Michael Roth; as in his Romeo and Juliet, McAnuff underscores the moments at which people fall in love, but this time he does it with delicacy.

Most of all, this Arden is a forest of good performances. Andrea Runge's Rosalind is a Bloomsbury painter (the unfortunate Touchstone has to lug her easel on his back, along with sundry suitcases), and her interpretation is as yet more of a sketch than a finished portrait, stronger on wit, which could still be sharper, than on ardour.

Thegreatlove-lessontoOrlando is still a shadow of what it might be. But she's astringent, responsive to moods, lovely when she declares the fathom-depth of her love, and--the acid test of a Rosalind -- she earns the right to speak the Epilogue. Like nearly everyone in this show, she makes the words leap off the stage. Of her two foils, Cara Ricketts is an aptly deflationary Celia, alternately tickled and shocked by her cousin's gender-bending excesses, while Paul Nolan's Orlando, his first non-musical role, is quite the revelation.

Brent Carver, in a Magrittean bowler hat, is not the most amusingJaquesIhaveseen, and his diction can carry casualness to the point of sloppiness, but he may be the most painful and authentic loner; his self-exile at the end is inevitable and makes you feel both with and for him. Ben Carlson's Touchstone suffers in his first scene from there being no 20th-century equivalent of a uniformed court jester, but once in the forest he blossoms as a sensual, sardonic commentator on everything including himself, not to mention an enthusiastic impromptu bassist. He too has a couple of fine foils: Lucy Peacock's enchantingly moonstruck Audrey and Randy Hughson's imperturbable herdsman Corin.

The philosophical debate of shepherd and clown is prefaced by offstage baa-ing, which is funny and apt, and interrupted by much more of it, which isn't. The younger shepherd Silvius is exceptionally well played, for both comedy and pathos, by Ian Lake, who is in turn well partnered by Dalal Badr's Phebe, vain but with a brain. Mike Shara's Oliver is better at redemption than at rage; good at both is Tom Rooney, who doubles the Dukes, showing the odd spark of frustrated affection as Frederick, while playing his banished brother for warmth rather than preachiness. Brian Tree's Adam, while alive, is a smashing serio-comic retainer, lifting very large stones and carrying them very short distances, while Dan Chameroy's rustic William is bewitchingly bothered and bewildered. In fact, this company has never functioned better; the most anonymous roles are likely to be lit up by established names (Sean Arbuckle) or unknown ones (Jonathan Purdon).

I come back to that ending. When Rosalind reappears in her woman's clothes, she's ushered in by Hymen, the god of marriage. In some productions he's a forester dressed up; here he's played (by Roy Lewis) as the real deal. The rustic pastor (Victor Ertmannis) who'd thought to perform the rites recognizes he's been out-ranked, and falls to his knees. It's a small moment but transcendent. A little later the Duke calls for a dance to close proceedings. It's nice, and you expect to go on for ever, but it's cut short. Someone has discovered tact, and it's a blessing.
