Four years ago, I reviewed a play called Goodness, whose subject was genocide. Actually, I was re-reviewing it. It had first been staged in Toronto in 2005, and that production was subsequently taken across Canada, and to the Edinburgh Festival and to New York, receiving raves at every stop. The 2009 Toronto remount was the prelude to the most testing of the production's touring dates: The cast and crew had been invited to present it in Rwanda.

After seeing the play in Canada, I'd expressed admiration, tinged with doubt. It seemed to be asking what made mass atrocities possible; but, I wrote, "the heart of darkness remains stubbornly, and perhaps inevitably, unreachable." Still I added, of their imminent African trip, "there, if anywhere, will be an audience qualified to judge."

A similar thought seems to have occurred to two of the actors in the show, Gord rand and Tara Hughes. They made an hour-long film of their trip, rand directing and Hughes producing. It's being shown today, as part of the reel world Film Festival at Canada Square in Toronto, though its future must surely be on television. The film's ramifying title is Goodness in Rwanda.

The play Goodness isn't explicitly about Rwanda and its 1994 genocide. Its author, Michael Redhill, is also its (one assumes, somewhat fictionalized) central character, played in this production by rand himself. A great-grandchild of the Holocaust, he's confronted by the story of a more recent genocide, one of whose architects is avoiding trial by feigning - maybe even having - Alzheimer's.

The play, abetted by its staging, casting, and choice of music - is cagey about where its events take place; it could be Europe, it could be Africa. But of course it's impossible to watch it without thinking about Rwanda. Kiki Gatire Katesi, director of a Rwandan arts festival, saw the production in Winnipeg, and said, "This must come to Rwanda", and set about making it happen.

In the publicity for the film, rand says "I've been lucky enough to be a Canadian actor for 15 years. But in 2009, I found myself asking, does it matter?" I had several reactions to that. One was that rand, whom I've seen many times on many stages, deserves all the luck he's had. Another was that he was being self-indulgent: Theatre, like any other art form, is either worth doing in itself, or it isn't. If you feel that entertaining people is an unworthy pursuit, then you should stop doing it. Taking on "important" subjects may make practitioners feel better about themselves, but it's no guarantee of producing important work. Also, I've seen too much self-styled political or community theatre in which actors telling audiences things that those audiences already, and often painfully, knew. It seemed to me that taking a play about genocide to Rwanda might be an extreme example of this.

Meeting rand for coffee, I discovered that he shared some of my doubts. He's "allergic to the idea that theatre is implicitly important," but thinks "what is important is that at the end of anything, the audience should feel changed in some way." He still believes, or wants to believe, that "if you tell good stories, you can change lives." up to a point, I agree, the point being that the change achieved -the sensation, which can be very acute, of being moved to laughter or tears - can also be short-lived.

What's fascinating about the film is that it admits all the doubts the Goodness company, being honest and intelligent people, had about their journey, that it shows these doubts being deepened rather than dispelled as it progressed, but still leaves the viewer feeling that the project was worthwhile.

It begins with the barest statistics about the slaughter of the Tutsis by the Hutus: "800,000 killed in 100 days," the equivalent of "one murder every 20 seconds - for three months." We hear rand himself saying that he wants to "show the world that what I do has some meaning," and we hear the question the play itself wants to pose, relayed to us by the director, Ross Manson, as "why do good people rush to do evil?"

That's amore active version of the familiar riddle about why bad things happen to good people, and it's equally resistant to solution, except perhaps the glib, though frankly persuasive, one of "peer pressure." The play itself doesn't give an answer, and neither does the film. The earnest visiting actors don't know; the visited, enforcedly pragmatic, don't seem to want to know. They have other concerns.

The film's turning point comes when the actors are acted upon. They visit the Genocide Centre, a building where they see, and we do too, the bloodstained walls, the familiar images - familiar from other sites if not from this one - of piled-up skulls and bones, including those of children. We hear rand, contemplating his mission to the Rwandans, say, "We haven't affected them, we haven't changed them, we haven't done anything. I feel useless, just a tourist."

What follows is equally, but differently, shattering. It's a reconciliation ceremony. One of the killers, released from prison, recounts his deeds in public, acknowledges his guilt. He doesn't tell us why either. But we're faced with another question, perhaps more necessary, and apparently permitting of an answer: "How do you forgive?"

Apparently, you do it by making bricks together, perpetrators working alongside survivors. It's a long process, and there's no suggestion that it's an easy or a sentimental one. What we do learn is that it's necessary. These people have to co-exist, even when, as one survivor acknowledges, he cannot look at a neighbour without wondering what he may have done.

And it occurred to me that this situation might be unique. The reconciliation process in South Africa isn't comparable; apartheid was horrific, and lasted far longer, but it wasn't genocide. There could be nothing similar in post-war Germany, because so few Jews survived or returned to be reconciled. And it seems to be working; as rand reminded me when we talked, Rwanda is currently one of Africa's success stories.

Back at base, before the last performance, we see one of the actors, Lili Francks, her knees shaking as she recalls what she'd seen that day; we hear another actor, Layne Coleman, uttering what may be the most profound words said by any of them: "These people need a comedy, more than they need this play." Still, the show seems to go well. An audience member, bearing witness to the universality of at least some cultural artifacts, tells one of the actors, "you were so intense, I was going to call you Jack Bauer."

Manson says that "the actors are putting their trip into their work - it's made it better."

"We're taking so much back," rand says at the end of the film "but what we've given -I don't know."

I don't know either, but I did believe Gloria Magambo, the festival administrator, when she told the company that the important thing was that they came - a tribute to the importance of what rand calls "cultural diplomacy," currently much undervalued.

Of course, the actors did the play partly to feel important, to feel good about themselves; but that's why anybody does anything. I doubt that the play did much to explain Rwanda to itself; I think the film does a surprising amount to show Rwanda to us. They can't have known when they set out that the healing would prove more important than the horror, but it's what they discovered, and it's believable. The film's title may not be so ironic after all.
