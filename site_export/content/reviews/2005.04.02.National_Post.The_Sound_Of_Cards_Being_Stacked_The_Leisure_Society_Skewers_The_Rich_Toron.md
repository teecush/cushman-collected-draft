robertcushman@lycos.com; The Leisure Society runs through April 24 at Factory Theatre Mainspace, 125 Bathurst St., 416-504-9971, factorytheatre.ca.; Blackbird runs through April 9 at 403 Yonge St.; call T.O. Tix, 416-831-0543, www.totix.ca. Only 30 seats.

Peter and Mary have been married for seven years, without an itch. They tell us so, in one of those expository conversations that is plainly being conducted for the audience's benefit rather than their own. Indeed, they are standing side by side and looking out front, so that there may be no doubt about the matter. They say theyre happy, so we know they arent.

We gather that they have a child, and are thinking of adopting another, from China, which apparently is the fashion these days. Not that they couldn't produce a second baby on their own, but Mary just doesn't have the time for all that pregnancy, not to mention its exhausting aftermath. In fact, we speedily learn that she is pregnant, but intends to have an abortion early next week, a course of action of which Peter thoroughly approves. He does worry a bit about her having to take the morning off work to have the procedure, but he reasons that her bosses will be delighted when they learn the reason. No more of that troublesome maternity leave.

The play Peter and Mary inhabit is called The Leisure Society, and they are fully paid-up members of it. We can tell as much by looking at their furniture, Marian Whaks set being so sleek that I thought for a moment I had wandered into the wrong theatre. (The Factory isnt known for visual elegance, or for plays about those who live in it.) They are also, as you may have gathered, rotten, and their author, Francois Archambault, spares no pains to tell us so.

For example, though they often mention their baby boy, and have a monitor in their living room so they can hear him if he cries, they never once mention him by name -- he is always just "the baby, an appurtenance. This is certainly repellent. It is also unbelievable. Here, as elsewhere in the evening, one hears the disagreeable sound of cards being stacked.

On the evening we meet them, Peter and Mary are having an old chum over, a recently divorced man named Mark, whom they feel they have, in some unspecified way, outgrown, and whom they have invited so that they may announce the end of the friendship.

This also strains belief; adult friendships often wither away but they aren't usually brought to such formal conclusions. Anyway, this purpose crosses tracks with another: Peter and Mary have promised one another a threesome, and it dawns on them -- or, to be more exact, it dawns on Mary -- that Mark would make the perfect third wheel. However, Mark, who is noisily enjoying his newly minted bachelorhood, has brought along his casual sex-partner (not his girlfriend), Paula, and Peter thinks for a time that he would like to have her along instead. (Peter, Paula and Mary; where have I almost heard that before? The answer, I guess, is blowing in the wind.) But the best-lay plans prove as fallible as the best-laid ones, and things work out differently.

It all seems fairly harmless, except perhaps for the one who gets left out, but the author seems convinced these shenanigans constitute a critique of our acquisitive civilization. His tone, which is icy, suggests it, and his program note, which is positively frozen, confirms it. It's the rich what gets the pleasure, yet again. The corollary must be that sexual experimentation is the privilege of the privileged, which sounds pretty snobbish to me.

It also sounds frivolous -- in a world full of war, starvation and oppression, an earnest playwright spends an evening tut-tutting about sex? This seems to be where the theatrical Left meets the Christian Right. There are offhand references to some of the characters less forgivable foibles -- hitting one anothers children, for example -- and these are credible, but they are mentioned only in passing.

That's not to say the play, first seen in Montreal, is a write- off. In its own hermetic way it works. It's economical, proceeding in a series of short, speedy, sawed-off scenes that are sometimes cruelly funny. It efficiently parades its characters heartlessness and exposes their loneliness. Bobby Theodores translation is so good that it doesn't sound like one. Ken Gass's direction is high- gloss, with especially searching performances from the two men: Geoffrey Pounsett is Mark, boyish and hollow, to the life; Richard Zeppieri does a masterly job on Peter, whose bravado is rather less than skin deep and who ends in an emotional pool filled about equally with sulks and shame. Carolyne Maraghis Paula is visually compelling, but aurally monotonous. The same goes, in more complex ways, for Irene Pooles Mary; she is sharp, throaty and intense, but she does not have the lightest of touches, and this weakens the impact, not just of her comic moments but of her sad and furious ones. Still, she leaves an impression, one -- and I mean this as a compliment -- of authentic misery.

I don't deny that the play gets down to being a persuasive anatomy of a particular marriage. What bugs me is the attempt to blow it up beyond that. That and the sense, implicit from the title onward, that the author is prejudiced against his characters from the start, which in turn prejudices me against him.

There could hardly be a greater contrast than Blackbird, both in ambience and in effect. Adam Rapp's play is the second offering from Crate Productions, who do site-specific shows, and who seem specifically specific about finding the grubbiest sites available and putting on newish, shortish American plays inside them. Their first one, Tape, took place in a room of the old Gladstone Hotel that the renovators hadnt yet got around to. Their new setting makes that one look palatial -- its a squatters' loft at Yonge and Gerrard, up several flights of alarmingly rickety stairs (though next door to a good Vietnamese restaurant). It stands in for a poverty-row room in New York, inhabited by two young people, a man and a woman whose bodies are prematurely decaying. It looks the part.

The couple are lovers, sort of. Baylis is incontinent, an affliction he owes not to his service in the Gulf War but to a civilian accident suffered when moving furniture into the home of people in Manhattan's own leisure society. Froggie is an ex- stripper (they met at her place of work) now largely confined to bed with hepatitis.

Shes a druggie, naturally; they both are, but shes more serious about it. One of her favourite phrases is get me some heroin": Baylis thinks she'd be better off with vitamin pills, and actually persuades her to take some. She has run away from a middle-class home, not, it transpires, without reason. His disability pay is hardly enough to cover their medical bills, let alone everything else. The floor, naturally, is littered with their detritus. Kimberly Purtell's production spares us little.

The story begins with the characters shouting abuse at one another, so that the play risks being another exercise in seeing how much squalor a well-brought-up audience will take. (Theres no limit.) It ends in a kind of tenderness, so it also risks sentimentality.

It comes closer to the latter, especially in its title, which refers to a presumably symbolic bird pecking away outside. Its also set on Christmas Eve, but it manages to keep that irony serviceable and, eventually, touching. The action largely hinges on Baylis's attempts, once he realizes the desperateness of Froggie's condition, to haul her out of it, and we find ourselves pulling for both of them. That is not, perhaps, the highest function of drama, but its a good one. It means we believe.

The performance is compelling without being ideal.

The two actors are also the producers, and both seem to be viewing the proceedings somewhat from the outside. There is very little variety in Chris Reynoldss Baylis; there is more in Kate Meehan's Froggie, but she's all shell -- her toughness, meant to be only flesh-deep, never cracks.

But they play well together, and it's a compelling evening, in this environment, a riveting one.

Site-specificity may be a gimmick, but here it's a necessary one: It makes you feel trapped.
