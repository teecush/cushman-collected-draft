SATURDAY NIGHT FEVER

Canon Theatre, Toronto

The Canon is the name that has been slapped on what was once the Pantages, the beauifully restored theatre at Yonge and Dundas where The Phantom of the Opera hung out for years.

It owes its new label to the involvement of Canon Canada Inc. and, according to the program, "exemplifies [the company's] commitment to the performing arts in Canada." Apparently, this commitment does not extend to preserving the name of a historic theatre chain, but there you increasingly go.

Whats happening on stage is a comparable act of cultural appropriation: a pitiful attempt to turn Saturday Night Fever into a musical. The 1977 movie, of course, was no such thing. It was a narrative picture that happened to involve a lot of dancing.

Its hero, Tony Manero, growing up poor in Brooklyn, lived for his nights at the disco and the picture let us get caught up in his excitement. But the dances were not the point of the thing, and they werent really presented as set numbers or as pieces of entertainment in their own right. Here, of course, they have to be: The stage production is desperate to give us something to applaud.

The stage show hews pretty closely to the narrative line of the film. Tony still lives at home, works in a paint store and fights with his unemployed father and devout Catholic mother. He still hangs out with a quartet of buddies and gets into fights. He is still the pride of the disco floor, and he still abandons one girl as a dancing and possible sexual partner and pursues another: a local young lady who works over in Manhattan and fancies herself as a social climber.

The implications of all this are bleak, but the show does its best to soft-pedal them. The film's dialogue remains (and sounds as flat as screen language usually does when spoken on stage), but it has been largely purged of profanity. The producers make no secret of this. In fact, their publicity employs the phrase "political correctness" not only without irony but with positive pride.

However, the efforts to turn Saturday Night Fever into family entertainment are constantly embarrassed by the elements of plot that keep turning up. This is now a show that presents a suicide and a gang-rape without providing any emotional context for them, and then hurries on as if they had never existed.

There is also a bewilderingly inconclusive and unnecessary sub- plot about Tony's brother, a self-defrocked priest, and a street fight that seems blissfully unaware of the prior existence of West Side Story.

When you talk about emotional context in a musical, of course, you are largely talking about the songs and how they fit in with the book.

Now, I always had a fondness, or thought I had, for the songs of the Bee Gees, if only because of the way in which they were anathematised by the strident prophets of rock. The two big tunes from the movie -- Staying Alive and How Deep is Your Love -- remain excellent pop songs. They bookend this show, the first coming right at the beginning, where it means nothing, and the other right at the end, where it is asked to mean far too much.

There really is a difference between songs intoned by disembodied voices on a soundtrack and songs sung by supposedly real characters in a show, and How Deep is Your Love sounds plain ridiculous when rendered as a fervent duet by two people who have just declared their intention of being only friends.

The other songs, whether new or carried over, divide into two categories. There are the dance- hall numbers, which are indistinguishable. (More Than a Woman is probably the best.) There are the dramatic numbers, which are equally generic but in a far worse genre. They may be sung by anybody who finds him- or herself in a situation of stress, details immaterial.

It really is very rude to start bellowing a ballad at an audience to whom you havent been introduced, and in this show nobody gets introduced. Tonys past and future girlfriends get to emote in this way, as does one of his friends. He himself holds out against it for most of the show, thereby greatly increasing his credibility as hero, but near the end he succumbs. Nan Knighton takes principal credit for the book, and in terms of character delineation and the careful integration of script and score, her work makes Mamma Mia look like Gypsy.

Arlene Phillips' choreography mainly calls for its dancers to be energetic, which they are. But it is very monotonous, number to number, and each individual routine seems to consist of a single step, endlessly repeated. We are expected to be impressed by the sight of a line of performers all raising their left arms, one after the other, in a partial salute to the show's (and the movies) logo. Its like demanding applause for a slow kick line. But then disco dancing, however gussied up, was never meant to be looked at for an evening, any more than disco music was meant to be sat down and listened to.

Nor are things much improved by the occasional lowering of a tilted mirror, in which we can study the tops of the dancers' heads. The routines look just as dull from above as from below. The mirror actually recalls the one the same distinguished designer, Robin Wagner, employed aeons ago in his sets for A Chorus Line, to rather better effect. His work here is unlikely to occupy a proud place in his portfolio (it's surely about time the display on a black backcloth of street signs, store names and even theatre marquees was laid to rest), and anyway seems in this touring production to be in an acute stage of disrepair. At one memorable moment on the press night, it started coming visibly apart.

There is, though, a rather sweet evocation of the Brooklyn Bridge with miniature cars crawling across it. (Which reminds me: the pieces whole premise -- for frustrated or self-deluded Brooklyn youth, the bridge as the pathway to an urban wonderland -- was anticipated in an earlier, similarly titled musical by Stephen Sondheim. Saturday Night, sans fever, was slated for production in 1954, but never actually got on till 50 years later, when it was resurrected in London and New York. It has a charming score, and deserves a small-scale production in Canada. Shaw Festival, Im talking to you. It's set in the 1920s, so it fits your mandate.)

Richard H. Blake does a good dancing-singing-acting job on Tony. Jeanine Myers looks properly cool as his lady, and if she seems to have trouble believing in the words she has to say, it's hard to blame her. Aileen Quinn, who had the title role in the film of Annie, has now grown into a good girl trying to go bad, which is food for thought. Rich Hebert and Suzanne Costallos as Tonys parents exemplify the professions wonderful propensity for acting its damndest, whatever the circumstances. (Just what are they supposed to be doing in the opening number, boogie-ing with all the kids?)

Not all the others are as conscientious, but they probably aren't being paid for their skills at characterization. By the same token, the choreographer is also the director, but she probably wasn't hired for being good with acting.

The show comes to us from a successful run in London, and also brandishing rave reviews from two separate papers in Chicago. London is no surprise -- its nostalgic embrace of anything that might be described as a rock musical has long been a joke -- but who knew Chicago was so easy to fool? Meanwhile, the professional theatre in general, and the musical theatre in particular, takes another merry step toward the grave.
