THEATRE REVIEW

SIZWE BANSI IS DEAD

Enwave Theatre, Harbourfront Centre

- - -

At 82 years old, 60 of which he has spent as the foremost director in the English theatre, Peter Brook has earned the right to coast. And anyone who knows of the spectacular productions that dotted his earlier career might think initially that is what he is doing in his production of Sizwe Bansi Is Dead. It's a simple show with only two actors, but it doesn't even have what you might call the spectacle of starkness. The performers have the support of props, door frames and recorded jazz. It's all very relaxed. It's also tremendously taut: the mature work of a master.

It comes to Toronto's WorldStage from Brook's own world stage, the Bouffes du Nord in Paris, where, since the 1970s, he has maintained an international acting troupe. This has always had a strong African component, and Sizwe Bansi is a legendary South African play. It was created, also in the '70s, by the white playwright Athol Fugard and two black actors, John Kani and Winston Ntshona. Since one of the characters is a photographer, it seems apt to call it a snapshot of life under apartheid. Brook's production (in a French translation, with English surtitles) is, as far as I know, the first major staging with actors other than those who originated it.

The original, staged in London in 1973, derived much of its extraordinary power from its sense of release; Kani and Ntshona (who had to travel as Fugard's servants to be allowed to leave home) were not only testifying to their own lives, but to their own talents. This was storytelling theatre that used humour to illuminate a dire situation. Styles, the cameraman, begins it by recalling his former life on a Ford assembly line where, for just one day, the workers were treated as human beings because the current Henry Ford was paying a visit. After that, it was business as before, but Styles, one of the few workers who could read, had relished the factory boss's brief dependence on his skills. The experience inspired him to quit the line and open a studio. Into it, just as he's finished telling his tale, walks Sizwe Bansi, a worker from another township, needing a portrait. He also needs another identity, as we learn from a series of conversations between Sizwe and an ingenious friend and from Sizwe's letter home: dictated, since he can't write. Sizwe Bansi is dead but he will, at a daily cost, survive.

The play's record, the image of black life in that time and place, is not so much of atrocity as of continual indignity. It is possible, as Brook would apparently like us to do, to extrapolate from the situation on stage and apply it to horrors in our own times, but the piece has lost some of its power. We no longer have the knowledge that these actors came from that situation and would be going back to it. My strongest memory of the original is of Winston Ntshona's baffled Sizwe railing, Lear-like, at the pass laws for blacks: at the "dompas" (it came out sounding like "dumb pass") whose possession governed his life and whose loss would make him a criminal. Pitcho Womba Konga, in Brook's production, is quieter, more stoical, almost wry.

This is altogether a lighter production than the original. (Lighter in the most basic sense--it is, if I can trust my memory, far more brightly lit.) This has something to do with time, something probably with the logical French language and the ironic French temperament. But, if the delivery is light, it carries a heavy freight. Konga, dreaming awkwardly of his future, has wonderful moments of delicate comedy, pipe in his mouth, assuming a manager's pose. The photography theme is constant and vital to the plot. It's a service that Styles, reflecting on early ghastly experiences with family groups ("37 New Brighton smiles on the same photo--never again") has, without knowing it, been setting himself up as a saviour. Habib Dembele gives him a joyous, elfin quality and balances it with good-humoured man-of-the-world exasperation as Sizwe's friend and other rescuer, the man who exhorts him to grab at salvation, even when he finds it literally lying in the gutter. Between them, these actors create a world for us. The play is little like Fugard's unaided works, which are drier and also duller; it's full of pungent detail, shrugged or mocked at, understated to shock. Dembele plays several roles, Konga only one, but they both give the impression of enormous versatility and enormous mutual trust. The level of contact here--actor to actor, actors to audience -- is remarkable. Brook, in the simplest way, has done it again. - Until April 22. Call 416-973-4000 for ticket information.
