STAR CHAMBER

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

---

The Shaw Festival, which over three evenings is mounting all nine of the one-act plays that make up Noel Coward's Tonight at 8:30, is also throwing in a lunchtime supplement. This is Star Chamber, a play written to be part of the original 1936 cycle but only performed then at a single matinee. The Shaw production seems to be its first outing since then.

It's a slight but entertaining piece that, in Kate Lynch's vivacious and good-looking production, comes over as one in-joke piled on top of another. A collection of West End stars and starlets gather together on the stage of a London theatre to fulfill their obligations as committee members of a retirement home for aged actresses. They are all devoted to the cause but their competing egos, added to their late arrivals and early departures when called for anything that isn't a rehearsal or a performance, greatly delay the meeting's getting started and keep derailing it when it does. The original audience presumably enjoyed watching popular performers lampooning themselves or other popular performers. The Shaw audience, certainly at the premiere, gets a comparable kick out of seeing some of its own favourite actors acting actors.

Double incest apart, the play's drawback is that the frustration felt by some of the committee members at the refusal of the proceedings to proceed can't help communicating itself to the audience. We too, once we've realized there isn't going to be a plot, would like them at least to get on with the meeting. Our representative onstage is the home's secretary, a stage-struck civilian on whom Guy Bannerman bestows a nice blend of the businesslike and bewildered; his is really the star performance though the program, which seems to have internalized the characters' own scale of values, denies him star-billing while according it to every one of the luvvies: a word that I hate but one that Coward really does seem to have gone out of his way to justify. Evan Buliung is in excellent form as Julian Breed, a matinee idol with a distinctive line in crisp narcissism. This wasn't, as might have been expected, Coward's own role; he played Johnny Bolton, a genial but unstoppably anecdotal comedian successful enough to own the theatre that houses the meeting and played here, with dead-on geniality and an irresistible waddle, by Neil Barclay.

The female stars in the chamber outnumber the males, while working hard to outsmart one another. Sharry Flett, who seldom gets a smart part, compensates delightfully as Violet Vibart, quick on the drawl and brandishing a stylish cigarette. Costumes have been slyly updated, though the text hasn't, to suggest that these types are eternal. It certainly seems so with Fiona Byrne as committee president Xenia James, an overflowing fountain of heartfelt gush who here comports herself like a blond Barbra Streisand. She's accompanied by a large and beautiful dog who may, in theatrical terms, be the greatest Dane since Hamlet. This is the only play known to me in which someone talks about the inadvisability of sharing a stage with an animal while actually doing so. There's a child too, though an invisible one, brought along in a pram by Jenny L. Wright as a proud and indignant champion of working motherhood, a subject that her fellow actresses seem to find vaguely distasteful. Peter Krantz plays a stage manager whose laconic bearing suggests that he has seen them all come and can hardly wait to see them all go.

The play keeps satire and sentiment running neatly in tandem, mocking its characters' self-aggrandizement while respecting their sincerity. In doing so, it foreshadows one of Coward's last and most heartfelt plays, Waiting in the Wings, which is set in just such an institution as the one these people are supporting and of which, as they remark with a mock-Dickensian relish that hides a real chill, they may one day stand in need themselves. Lynch rounds things off with a musical coda, a Coward singalong that includes a startlingly anachronistic arrangement of World Weary; the lyric's line about being "tired of all those Jumping Jacks" would be very apposite here, if we could hear it. Throwing this sequence in at all is probably a sign of desperation, an acknowledgment that the play -- really a sketch -- hasn't gone anywhere. But it's fun and well-staged, and Buliung's feigned reluctance before launching into a breakneck Mad Dogs and Englishmen is a treat. - Star Chamber plays in repertory through Oct. 11.
