THE INVISIBLE MAN

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

I have seen The Invisible Man. I know that sounds silly, but so does the play. It looks all right, fitted out by Judith Bowden with neat, pretty sets that put a new gloss on an old style: crowded split-level interiors and airy etched backcloths that hark back to the Edwardian theatre, when stories were stories and plays were melodramas. Fair enough, since Michael O'Brien's adaptation of H. G. Wells' pioneering science-fiction novel is set in and around an English Edwardian (well, late Victorian) village with the picturesque name of Iping. The program says it's in Sussex, which is not that far from London, but the inhabitants all speak in the thick, all-purpose brogue known to generations of British actors as Mummerset. To Canadians it comes less naturally, making it very hard to understand.

Well, you don't go to The Invisible Man for the acting, which is just as well, since most of it is atrocious. You go for the effects, and one of them is indeed a stunner. The title figure, who has been going around swathed like a mummy so that nobody will notice his lack of a body (he doesn't want his invisibility to be visible), is swarmed by the villagers and revealed to have a hole where his head should be. They all gasp, and so do we. But most of the other magic tricks in Neil Munro's production are about furniture moving around, apparently of its own volition. This is smoothly done, and it must have taken ages for the technicians to get right, but that doesn't make it especially exciting to watch.

The invisible man is James Griffin, a brilliant scientist who is the victim of his own genius. Having worked the voodoo on himself, he has no means of reversing it, and while trying desperately to find a cure he has to cope with the hostility of his fellow men, who fear him as they would fear anything unknown. Even on a more mundane level he finds invisibility not all it's cracked up to be; you may be able to play all kinds of pranks on other people but you can't stop them from bumping into you, and it's painful. Progressively embittered, he plans to use his powers to achieve universal domination (today West Sussex, tomorrow the world!) and thereby to avenge himself on all who ever slighted him, notably the girl who turned him down in college and the plodding fellow student who had the impudence to marry her. In a program note, O'Brien claims to have added to the novel by making Griffin a working-class prodigy whose prickly arrogance got him banished, first from his school debating society and then from the school. Not having read the book, I take his word for that. But judging from the program note (by a Wellsian scholar, Nicholas Ruddick), most of the vital elements were already in place, including Griffin's egomania, hubris and frank ambition to institute a reign of invisible terror. Also, apparently, a mixture of styles that O'Brien has all too faithfully reproduced. It may have worked on the page, but the stage version swings clumsily between broad farce (never Munro's strongest suit as a director), portentously crude psychology with a dash of politics and plodding detective fiction. It's especially bad when the wires cross. The invisible man's chief adversaries are a quartet of yokels who spend most of their time huddled together in the alehouse snuggery. (This may partly explain the lifelessness of their acting. They barely have room to breathe.) Later on, when the full force of the law is turned on Griffin, these fellows are at the head of the chase. It's hard to get a thrill from a manhunt when the principal hunters are village idiots.

I said the acting didn't matter, but I lied. Acting always matters; and if the play drags the actors down, the actors do it to the audience. The only specks of conviction come from Wendy Thatcher as the pub proprietress, when she isn't either mumbling or gabbling; from Neil Barclay, in a few moments of good, clean camp; from Bernard Behrens, who has all of two minutes on stage and uses them; and from Jeff Meadows, who as virtuous hero and all-around straight man, makes a decent show of being decent. The invisible himself is Peter Krantz, who whether playing the tormentor or the tormented, seems to have checked in his personality along with his corporeal being. To be fair, the script does manage a neat twist at the end, the kind that's just predictable enough to be satisfying.
