CYRANO DE BERGERAC

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

---

Edmond Rostand's Cyrano de Bergerac, the most celebrated romantic drama in the world repertoire, is also its most elaborate hard-luck story. Maybe that's the same thing.

Think about it. We have a swashbuckling hero, possessed of all the talents both martial and intellectual, but robbed of any confidence with women by his self-consciousness about his preternaturally long nose. He falls in love, dares for a moment to hope, finds that the lady has already fallen for a pretty young dunderhead and contrives to lend his rival his own eloquence in a kind of vicarious courtship. It works, until the rival realizes that the lady, brainy on her own account, is more smitten with his borrowed wit than with his native good looks. He resolves to do the decent thing and tell all, but is struck down in battle before he has the chance, leaving our hero, no less honourable, feeling morally prohibited from disillusioning his beloved. She does eventually work it out, but only after Cyrano has undergone a further 15 years of selfless devotion. And then it's too late because he's dying, struck down by a cowardly assassin. (Nobody ever fought him face-to-face and survived.) Rostand twists every possible knife; his Cyrano even finds time to remember, at death's door, that his mother had been too revolted by his ugliness to show him any kindness. It taps directly into everybody's store of erotic self-pity, and achieves a kind of greatness in doing so.

That isn't all it does. It's as funny as it is sad, with Cyrano's verbal gymnastics, brilliantly rendered in Anthony Burgess' translation, keeping pace with his virtuoso swordsmanship. This is a man who insults an enemy to death, composing a perfectly-rhymed ballad about him while they fight a duel; "when the poem ends, I hit," he promises, and delivers. He's also a free spirit, as feared a satirist as a soldier. He's an actual French figure from the 17th-century, raised to fantasy level by a writer from the 19th. Colm Feore, returning to a role he played at Stratford a decade and a half ago, is in command of every mood; his sword flashes, his wit stings and his energy seems inexhaustible. He reaches a kind of apotheosis in the play's seriocomic balcony scene; Cyrano, in a weird mixture of masochism and authorial pride, pushes aside ( "this is getting difficult") his handsome mouthpiece and pours out his own words, with no hope of reaping the benefit. Pathos mixed like this suits him better than pathos unadorned; his death scene brought a passing lump to my throat, but it didn't have me weeping the expected buckets.

For once, though, Cyrano has to share the emotional load. I called Christian, his unsuspecting competitor, a dunderhead, but I didn't mean it. Mike Shara, in a revelatory performance, gives him not only the customary good looks and good intentions, but sensitivity and even a brain; he may lack the gift of Gascon gab (Cyrano and his fellow cadets are countrymen of Alexandre Dumas' D'Artagnan, who puts in a token appearance) but he has a blunter kind of wit, and a poignant awareness of when he's been beaten. The play's a self-sacrifice contest, and Christian's less elaborate renunciation proves at least as moving as Cyrano's.

The production, by the star's wife, Donna Feore, gets off to a mystifying start with a small modern boy (played by their son) trying on a cadet's costume. As a framing device, it's unnecessary, and anyway never appears again. Once that's over, Mrs. Feore surrounds Mr. Feore with an excellent staging that gets the Festival platform swirling with action, as you might expect from an expert choreographer, but also fills it with intelligence. She shows you where to look and gives you things worth looking at and listening to. She only misreads one moment, when she has Wayne Best, otherwise admirable as the protagonist's friend Le Bret, shout his sympathy over Cyrano's rejection by the adored Roxane rather than confide it. Amanda Lisman, as the lady in question, simpers more than a bluestocking should, and their common enemy de Guiche is a more complex figure than the pantomime villain supplied by John Vickery. But there are some very nice small performances: Douglas E. Hughes as a drunken versifier; Robert Persichini doubling as a ham actor whom Cyrano drives from the stage and as the Gascons' commanding officer; Martha Farrell as a refreshment vendor whose adoration would strike Cyrano in a heap if only he could see beyond his nose. John Stead has arranged a fine duel and a great battle. Occasional passages are spoken in the original French, which may not be strictly logical, but is no less so than Shakespeare's anglophone Julius Caesar expiring with Latin on his lips. Besides, Burgess himself admitted that there could be no English translation for Cyrano's own dying word which is, and remains, panache. Which is also a fair summary of the whole show. - Cyrano de Bergerac is in repertory through Nov. 1. Call 1-800-567-1600 or visit stratfordfestival.cafor tickets and information.
