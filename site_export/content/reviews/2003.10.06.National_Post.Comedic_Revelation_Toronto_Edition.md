ARMAGET-IT-ON!

The Second City, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - The response of the late James Durante to warnings of Armageddon was "Armageddon outta here." Jimmy could make anything sound funny, but I think we might agree that the line in itself has an elegance missing from the title of the new show at Second City. As a pun, Armaget-it-on! is even more forced than such previous entries as When Bush Comes to Shove, What Fresh Mel Is This and -- leaving personalities out of it -- Psychodelicatessen.

Here, though, carping stops. Those previous titles merely grabbed at names in the news and left it at that; the shows themselves had nothing to say about Toronto's mayor or America's president. The last one, The Bush League of Justice, had nothing to say about anything. This new show does have a running concern with the idea of apocalypse, and from time to time with the more joyous idea of getting it on. It even seems to be advocating one as an antidote to the other, or at least as a way of taking your mind off it. It gets it on itself by being one of the funniest of recent Second City revues, and much the most thoughtful.

Don't be alarmed; I mean only that the premises for many of the sketches are more original and ingenious than usual. The faces and bodies on the stage are mostly familiar, and so are the satirical targets, but the approaches are fresh.

An early number takes on the Canadian melting pot and the Middle East at the same time, and even involves its performers in a bit of acting, building a relationship as well as firing off gags. Sandy Jobin-Bevans, the company's laid-back straight-arrow guy, has taken advantage of a cheapo TV offer and bought his Jewish roommate, the tousled and insistent Matt Baram, a ticket to Israel: one-way. He thought, or so he says, that his friend would welcome the chance to get back to his roots. "Dude," says Baram, patiently indignant -- he can also be frisky, though not here -- "I'm from Edmonton."

Another double header: The diminutive Jennifer Goodhue, who by length of service and force of personality is now the company's star, plays a motivational speaker exhorting her audience to stop doing the two things that impede success (thinking and feeling), and meeting her own ultimate challenge when asked to find work for a suicide bomber who is not good at his job; i.e., he's still alive.

Naturally, the show eventually arrives at Osama bin Laden himself, shooting a new video under the frustrated eye of an American director who uses Method methods; he gets the old anger going by rekindling his client's early-morning rage at finding there was no milk for his Cheerios. This, I suppose, is an obvious idea -- the video, not the breakfast cereal -- but it's executed with a lot of brio.

The best-sustained item presents Canadian soldiers in the Second World War wondering when, if ever, the Yanks are going to get onside, while expressing their own dark suspicions about root causes. ("I hear we're only here for the wheat.")

Coming up to date, we are reminded of the never-to-be-forgotten trauma of the blackout: 4/11. (The lights, they claim, went out at 11 minutes past 4 p.m., and that does sound about right.) People playing solitaire on their computers lost their games and will never get them back. Others were sent home because their jobs were deemed inessential. Imagine how that made them feel.

With ultimate logic, the show also takes on the Second Coming. Jesus Christ returns, courtesy of MTV or similar, to answer questions and perform a few miracles, which do not include the Blue Jays winning the World Series. "Look", says the host, "she's Jesus, not God." (I should have mentioned that the Gospels got the gender wrong. Also that Jesus, in the person of the feisty and imposing newcomer, Jamillah Ross, is black.) This number gets in good lines at the expense of all the Toronto newspapers including, I confess, this one.

Consumerism in general and The Gap in particular are done over twice. These of course are more conventional targets, but both blows land and both are freshly twisted. Especially piquant is the one about the lady who is persuaded by the store to buy a young Mexican girl, whom she can wear as a stole. Goodhue plays her purchase; she has the right dimensions and the right agility, and seems delighted to be of use. The show even gets away with a sketch about neighbouring couples playing Truth or Dare and discovering predictably unpredictable things about themselves.

Then there are the songs, few but fine. One is a madrigal about a maiden on the local club scene who finds herself the victim of a one- stand knight. That's her line. Mine -- which I wish I'd invented but have actually plagiarized from another song -- would be that her mother never told her there'd be knights like this. Actually the real delight here is the sight of Goodhue, who sings the ditty, lolloping round the stage between verses, deciding that she likes it, and then lolloping again.

Goodhue later gets to play Simon to Baram's Garfunkel, and they both look very well cast. They are planning a Broadway musical that will do for them what Mamma Mia! did for ABBA; i.e., recycle all their hits in the least congruous contexts possible. Baram also has a solo, with the most stinging lyric of the night, in which he apologizes to the U.S. for the ways in which we're better than they are.

Paul Constable, an alarmingly self-assured fella next door, and Naomi Snieckus complete the cast, who as always write their own material. So we can't attribute the rise in quality to the intervention of some outside author, though it's quite possible that Steven Morel, the first-time-out director, was more rigorous in his editing than some of his predecessors have been. The only serious complaint can be that the show bears further witness to the death of the punchline. Nobody can find a neat way of ending anything, and, as always, I sympathize.
