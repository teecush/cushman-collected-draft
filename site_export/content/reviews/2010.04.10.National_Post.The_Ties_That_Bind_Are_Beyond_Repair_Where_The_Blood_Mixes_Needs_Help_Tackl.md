Factory Theatre's Performance Spring is a festival of new works, many of them re-runs of pieces that have already had exposure in a variety of venues elsewhere. For example, Where the Blood Mixes, the current incumbent, started as a co-production by the Vancouver Playhouse and Victoria's Belfry Theatre. It was previously mounted in Toronto in 2008 by the Luminato festival, which must, on the evidence presented here, have been out of its mind.

Kevin Loring's play is rooted in a subject that could hardly be more painful or, at this moment, more topical: the abuse of aboriginal children in residential schools. There is a short speech bluntly describing the kind of thing that went on, and a longer one describing the continuing effects on a whole community of victims when they become adults. These are powerful, the first of them chilling, the second chilling and thought-provoking and guilt-inducing. But the dramatic structure that supposedly supports them is clumsy and dilatory. The same goes for the direction and much of the acting, but the problems the play presents may be insuperable.

We meet Floyd and Mooch, two middle-aged men, lifelong friends and sparring partners, in a B.C. bar of which they seem to be the only patrons. Floyd is avoiding the solitude of home, Mooch is avoiding his girlfriend, who objects, violently if understandably, to his habitual embezzlement of the housekeeping money. Mooch seems at first to be a half-wit, but is later revealed to be merely not especially bright, and shamefacedly aware of it; he does develop a certain poignance. Floyd is morose, oppressed by the long-ago death of his wife and the loss of his daughter Christine, who was taken off into adoption when he proved unable to care for her; though the flashbacks we see of their life together don't look all that bad. Anyway, though the play hardly makes a point of it, we have two generations forcibly estranged from their families, since Floyd and his wife and Mooch and his girlfriend all went through the residential experience and were permanently scarred by it. In Christine's case, the uprooting doesn't seem to have worked out too badly, but this, of course, only increases Floyd's sense of shame. When Christine comes back to find him, he's afraid to meet her. That's believable. But in order to establish this alienation, the play spends most of its length on interminable scenes of Floyd and Mooch interacting, in the bar and on salmon-fishing trips at the confluence of the Fraser and Thompson rivers. There's a lot of watery lyricism, and at one point Christine, who's been hovering as a choral presence, appears as a fish. If that's the kind of thing you like, that's the kind of thing you like.

Glynis Leyshon's production hardly puts a foot right. Transitions between scenes are awkward, and even within scenes actors are frequently left gaping into space. Robert Lewis' blue-tinged twin-levelled set conveys no atmosphere of either river or tavern, though there's some compensation in the latter department from Tom McBeath's discreet, cordial performance as the barman. A musician (Jason Burnstick) plays folk-and-blues guitar, and nobody seems quite certain whether he's meant to be there or not. At one point, he abruptly joins the main action-- perhaps because it's felt that more bodies are called for -- and then as abruptly departs it. His underscoring of much of the dialogue has the same effect that this device has in movies and on network TV: It absolves the actors of the responsibility of acting. The music also makes them look as if they're about to go into a number, although none of them actually does so. I tell a lie; one of them does an impromptu parody of Heartbreak Hotel, but the instrumental backing means it doesn't sound impromptu. It sets up a convention that is never followed through.

Most damagingly, there's little flow or contact between the performers; they talk and then they stop. That the characters have trouble communicating doesn't mean that the actors should. Both leads are monotonous, with Ben Cardinal making little more of Mooch than a comic turn, and Billy Merasty playing Floyd with a heavy hand and an appallingly limited range of expression. The women are uneven, which in this context is almost a compliment. As June, Mooch's madam, Margo Kane is a stock harridan when berating her mate, but surprisingly real when given the task of unfolding the play's backstory and expounding its theme, the two being identical. Kim Harvey's Christine is fresh and strong, except when resorting to generalized teariness near the end. The two ladies share what is, the author being also an actor, the play's best in-joke. Christine says that she's an artist, specializing in "spoken word"; the term baffles June, as it's always baffled me. (Most plays are made up of spoken words). I also liked the unifying motif of smell; people are constantly being judged by their odours, pleasant or otherwise. Many of these of course are alcoholic. The play apparently started as a bar-room duet for the two guys, and sprouted from there. This may explain why the abuse-theme emerges as a kind of topdressing, while making everything else seem like a waste of time.
