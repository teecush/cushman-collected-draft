WINGFIELD ON ICE

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

At the emotional climax of Wingfield on Ice, Walt Wingfield, a farmer with a past career in stockbroking, tells of cradling his just-delivered baby daughter. Newborn babies supposedly cannot see, but this one seemed to be greeting him with what he rather wonderfully describes as "such a look of skeptical appraisal as I've never received in all my life."

I felt like a skeptical appraiser myself as I approached this show. It is the fifth in a hugely successful series of one-man Wingfield shows, all of them written by Dan Needles, all performed by Rod Beattie, all previously seen at Stratford, and all unseen by me.

As a late arrival at the party, I feared I might be in for an evening of impenetrable coziness. My fears were unfounded. True, this is not the most jaggedly satirical evening you are ever going to encounter, but it has an agreeable tartness, is presented with unfailing skill (which I did expect) and is often very funny.

And, of course, there were things that surprised me, even if they were welcomed as old friends by everybody else.

For example, I have been assuming all year that the philosophically bemused, crusty looking old party in brown corduroy jacket, white-rimmed glasses and green eyeshades depicted on the posters (and now on the program cover) was Walt himself. Not a bit of it: He turns out to be Ed, old-fashioned editor of an old- fashioned local newspaper, who acts as a framing device. So, the play and Beattie's performance exist on three levels. Ed introduces Walt (Beattie slips off the jacket, and underneath are overalls), who introduces everybody else. Walt tells his stories and theirs in a series of letters to the cheekily named Ed that the latter must have been delighted to publish.

It is worth noting just how gracefully Beattie gives Walt's discursive prose the illusion of conversational ease without once resorting to forced hesitations or other rhythmic breakages. Delightfully impressive are the dozen different inflections he can bestow on the words "Yours sincerely, Walt," precisely matching each one to the tone of what has gone before.

Lost in the mists of time, or conceivably in the earlier scripts, is any sense of how Walt's citified past affects his position in the tiny township of Persephone, Ont., that he currently inhabits. He now seems as integral a part of the community as everybody else. He does sometimes affect an air of quizzical bemusement, but then, as a comic narrator, he'd have to. He can also, by the same token, be the fall guy; his wife Maggie's pregnancy occasions a hilarious sequence of verbal confusion around the phrase "the waters are breaking." He's talking agriculture, his friends and neighbours think he's talking gynecology, and his apparent callousness gets them very worried.

The first half of the show deals in similar, apparently disconnected, anecdotes. The farmer opposite, now retired, grumbles about his long-estranged brother who married a Catholic girl (this is traditional Orange territory) and has led a financially charmed life: "Lucky? He was carrying a tuba the day it rained gold." (This must be the Protestant obverse of "God help the Irish -- if it was raining soup, they'd be out in the street with forks" attributed to Dominic Behan, Brendan's brother.)

Walt's chum Freddy, who writes terrible impromptu poetry, tries to smuggle a dead goat into town and is stopped by a policeman who doesn't believe he's taking it to the vet and all but says, "this is an ex-goat." This particular escapade is stopped in its tracks by the onset of really terrible weather. A three-day ice storm descends, sealing off the township, making paths impossible and roads impassable, and suddenly justifying the play's title, which up to then we'd taken for a lark.

In the second act, the characters, co-existing in a state of siege, set differences aside and pull together. (Maggie gives birth.) All this is predictable and underlined in the commentary, but believable. Even the tale of the two brothers begins to veer toward reconciliation, though with a dark twist that lies somewhere between Chekhov and O. Henry. Beattie, in the course of the action, wears several hats, all of them Walt's. To summon the other characters, he relies on stance and delivery, all of it cleverly done, though when impersonating the women (Maggie especially) he tends to fall back on a generalized softness of voice. Sometimes the pressure slackens, taking my attention with it, but there is always something worth coming back for: not just good jokes but a humane spirit, a capacity for being surprised by things and sharing the wonder without going pop-eyed over it. The apogee of that, of course, is the line about the baby.

Douglas Beattie directed, and John Thompson, to quote the official credits, adapted the original set. (This is one of Stratford's more economical productions.) I can now join those who look forward to Episode Six, which will conceivably be called Wingfield and Chips. As in: off the old block.
