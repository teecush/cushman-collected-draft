As one of the few people in the world who never saw the movies, I am in no position to pronounce on how faithful Shrek the Musical is to its source. I have gathered that the first film was good and its two sequels weren't. To this extent, the fidelity is high. The stage show is based on that first movie, and its good, too: much better, to be frank, than Id expected.

I'm also sure, from asking around, that the show sticks close to the movie's story (ogre gets princess, after finding they have more in common than either expected) and moral (dont go persecuting people, or other creatures, just because theyre different). What the musical does have, obviously and thankfully, is a brand new score: brand new at least as of 2008, which is when the show opened on Broadway. The composer is Jeanine Tesori; and though her music here is hardly in the same class as her brilliant work in Caroline, or Change, it's a very bright and ingratiating collection of pop and theatre pastiches. David Lindsay-Abaire's lyrics, though not devoid of missteps, have a similar teasing quality; theyre especially fond of spreading a welcome rug and then pulling it out from under, as in the opening number where Shreks fond parents send their seven-year-old little ogre into a big bright beautiful world where "every dream comes true - but not for you." Lindsay-Abaire also wrote the book, which takes the same tack; it tells a fairy tale that it alternately celebrates and undermines.

Actually it tells, or at least alludes to, a lot of fairy tales, plus a few nursery rhymes; in that respect it's a more frivolous but also more earnest Into the Woods, crossed with Wicked and with just a dash of Beauty and the Beast. Obviously, it isn't as great as Into the Woods but its a lot better than Wicked; Beauty and the Beast, at least in its stage version, doesnt count. It's funny and fairly inventive and a bit touching. It's also very well staged and performed.

That last is a surprise, since this touring version has a non-Equity cast: the second in succession that Dancap has brought us. Cheapskate casting ruined In the Heights but this less challenging show stands up well. In fact, if I hadnt been told I wouldnt have known. It also helps that the sound balance is better; you can make out all the words except when the singers are screaming, as they rather too often are. In the title role, grown-up incarnation, Lukas Poost justifies all the sympathy that naturally accrues to a swamp-dwelling misanthrope with a raw Scots accent, thrown into incorrigibly cheerful allAmerican company. Liz Shivener is a bit of a treat as Princess Fiona, the spoiled romantic waiting in her tower for her rescuing prince of her dreams and getting petulantly bossy when he doesn't match her dreams; she may pre-empt the upcoming Veep by suggesting Julia Louis-Dreyfus impersonating Sarah Palin. Andre Jordan's talking Donkey is vocally and physically manic; hes also incredibly irritating but thats probably what's required. Shrek, his reluctant companion, puts it best: He's as chatty as a parrot / more annoying than a mime.

I had something of the same reaction to the villain of the piece, Lord Farquaad, who has banished all fairy-tale creatures from his kingdom because theyre not normal, which given that he himself is the son of one of the Seven Dwarfs and looks it, is a clear case of projection. (Were meant, obviously, to make the connection with right-wing homophobia.) Anyway, Merritt David Janes is fearsomely acrobatic in the role, a human puppet with a voice that's all giggle and squeak and a costume inherited from Laurence Olivier's Richard III - the shows references are nothing if not eclectic. I did grow weary of the Disneyland chorus of his victims, especially when they padded out the evening with a protest number, at once too serious and too silly, called Freak Flag. I did, though, warm to the eviction complaint of the Three Little Pigs that "zey blew our condos down." On the other hand having the Big Bad Wolf lament that they tore my cotton granny dress / and called me a hot n' tranny mess suggests that this supposed kids' show may not be quite sure of its audience, and also that it doesnt always know what a rhyme is. But on the third hand, the show has a Dragon, with a quartet of bedraggled victims who announce, irresistibly, that "she kept us around to sing back-up." Shes a flirtatious beast with fluttering eyelashes, manipulated by four visible puppeteers, which makes her, I guess, a War Dragon.

Out the Window is a documentary, every word transcribed and vouched for, written and directed by Liza Balkan, who in 2000 saw from her apartment, or thought she saw, four policemen beating up a mentally disturbed man who subsequently died. Act One is the trial, Act Two the inquest. In between is a scene, set confusingly later than both proceedings, in which Balkan lunches with a lawyer from each side and wonders whether her play will get her sued for libel; for some reason this is staged as a Last Supper with members of the audience filling in as guests. The play is better, and engrossing, when it sticks to the facts, or rather to the record, since the facts are in dispute and memory, including the author's own, is acknowledged to be erratic. Balkan tops and tails the show in her own person; in the main action she's played by Julie Tepperman, very well though she fades out of the action. The lawyers are composites, swingingly embodied by David Ferry and RH Thomson; Thomson in particular is quite amazingly natural. So, too, are the four actors who play the cops and who are liable to leave you with very mixed feelings indeed. Well worth catching, though you only have a day to do so. Another last-minute alert: Briefly on view at Theatre Passe Muraille (closing tomorrow) is The Vindication of Senyora Clito Mestres, a solo play performed by Dragana Varagic, who when she played it here some years ago, gave every indication of being a great actress.
