For iconic singers in their twilight years, recording an album of duets is beginning to seem like tempting fate. Frank Sinatra's two CDs worth turned out to be the last records he ever made, but at least he was still alive when they came out. Ray Charles' Genius Loves Company (Concord Jazz) has turned out to be a memorial tribute in a more literal sense than anyone can have wished.

It adheres to the Sinatra pattern in teaming its star with a dozen other name-singers from various musical backgrounds. (And some tracks are produced by Phil Ramone, who was responsible for the entire Sinatra project.) It differs from its predecessor in that the performers on each song don't owe their apparent proximity to the miracle of overdub; they were actually there in the studio together.

This, though, turns out to make less of a difference than you might expect. There is a sad lack of interplay on most of these performances. The most exciting -- really, the only one with excitement -- wasn't done in a studio at all; it's a live encounter with Van Morrison on Morrison's own song Crazy Love, for a TV awards show. You can feel the audience spurring them on; more importantly, you feel the two men (and Charles probably has more in common with Morrison than with any of his other partners here) spurring one another on. It's practically the only occasion on the entire disc on which Charles ventures a spoken ad lib.

It's the last cut on the album but it was recorded first, in June, 2003. The studio sessions began the next month and concluded nine months later, and they find Charles not only in progressively lower spirits but also, obviously, in progressively worse health. Charles actually fares worse here than did Sinatra, who was paired much of the time with singers who had no affinity with his very specialized kind of music and to whom, in many cases, he had never been (and indeed never was) introduced. His voice was ragged but, at least on a few songs, still powerful.

Charles gets a more imaginative selection of songs (Sinatra's were all his old warhorses) and a pretty good and varied bunch of people to do them with. And there is a logic, which there never was with Sinatra, in teaming him with singers from across the board because, as everybody has said, he himself was the great defier of genre.

At the same time he was never what you would call versatile; he may have embraced every kind of song but they had to submit on his terms. I don't mean that he desecrated or defiled them, but that extraordinary voice -- with its wails, moans, peals and chuckles and its unfailing sense of narrative logic -- was more individual than they could ever be.

I first encountered Charles in the early 1960s when he was going, though I didn't know it at the time, through his country-and- western phase. I Can't Stop Loving You was a big hit for him, and though I never found the song very interesting, I lived each time it was played for the moment at which he told the choir that was dogging his footsteps to "sing the song, children." A wonderful blend of serenity and chutzpah, a laying on of hands coupled with a wink at the congregation.

After that, a lesser hit but one I preferred, came You Don't Know Me, a sublimely self-pitying number that probably wasn't meant to appeal to my teenage angst but did anyway. Anyway, he gave it dignity and he gave it agony, apparently without trying. He didn't have to emote, his voice was emotion. And though these were both country songs, it never occurred to me for a moment to think of him as a country singer.

I didn't know, either, about Charles the jazz or blues or rhythm- and-blues singer, or Charles the singer of standards. I didn't even know he played the piano. (It wouldn't have made much of an impression through all those strings anyway). I found out a lot, in very short order, when I saw him on -- and I refuse to say "of all things" -- The Perry Como Show. Perry's choral director, of course, was also called Ray Charles (he took, in self-defence, to billing himself as The Other Ray Charles) and the two indulged in a little banter before the first Ray -- the black, blind one -- started singing Georgia on My Mind, which had the same electrifying effect on me that it has had on everybody else who's heard it.

It still does. And I still don't quite know why. Other good singers have sung it very well, before and since (the road, in this case, goes back to 1932) without making the same connection. Charles, meanwhile, doesn't really sing it any differently from the way in which he's sung countless other tunes, and yet something -- some yearning quality, I suppose, though that sounds inadequate -- fuses singer and song.

It's not even as if anyone really knows whether he's singing about a place or a girl; the authors (Hoagy Carmichael and the mysterious Stuart Gorrell) never came clean about it, and neither did Charles. (The state of Georgia had no such doubts and has adopted the song as its own.) All we can be sure of is that Ray longs passionately for something and wants, with every fibre of his voice, to tell us about it.

Something of the same thing happens when he sings the song Ruby (which is about a girl) and which jumps out from an album of songs with women's names in their titles, all of which he apparently treats in exactly the same way. He does tend to sound best when he's singing about the unattainable, and this perhaps has its counterpart in the tone of outraged bewilderment that characterises his best comedy numbers: Hit the Road, Jack or, my own favourite, Busted.

Of all these touchstone songs, the only one to turn up on Genius Loves Company is You Don't Know Me, on which Charles kindles at least the embers of the old anguish. His songmate is Diana Krall and she complements him nicely, being equable but not bland. (And after what the camera did to her in De-Lovely, one is rooting for her anyway.)

Better though is Norah Jones, who on the opening cut, Here We Go Again, sounds -- to my surprise -- like a veritable soul-daughter to The Man; it's a very quiet and easy duet, and Charles has a laugh in his voice of which one would like to hear more.

Natalie Cole, whose father was Charles' earliest influence and who has herself become a very reliable singer of all kinds of standards, joins him for Fever, which, from the opening bass vamp on, can't help sounding like an out of place tribute to Peggy Lee. It might have been better to have heard one of Charles' songs that Peggy featured, Hallelujah I Love Him So or, even better, Tell All the World About You; Brother Ray the considerable songwriter is not represented here. Charles can't sound feverish anymore, and Cole's attempts at backchat don't work.

The saddest pairings are with figures from comparatively contemporary pop. Elton John, bringing his own Sorry Seems to Be the Hardest Word (with melodramatic strings), sounds like the most self- important voice in popular music, at least until the ludicrously spectral Michael McDonald turns up to weigh down Goffin and King's Hey Girl, though Charles himself does manage to emit a few sensual gleams through the general listlessness. He sounds like a very old man when he joins James Taylor on Taylor's Sweet Potato Pie, but the track is pleasant easy listening, with a father-and-son air to it.

The country singers don't do too well. On Do I Ever Cross Your Mind, Charles actually sounds more country, or at least placid, than ever, but when Bonnie Raitt joins in, singing in exactly the same way, it's too relaxed to be interesting. Charles matched with Willie Nelson for It Was a Very Good Year sounds like a good idea, and Nelson's matter-of-factness is initially endearing; Charles, though, sounds genuinely toothless -- even his phrasing is suspect -- and it's distressing. Sinatra (to whose arrangement this one is heavily indebted) was only 50 when he recorded it. You don't want to hear two genuinely old men singing about when they were 35, though their last unison line is powerful.

Happier, bluesier things: B.B. King (Sinner's Prayer) still sounds gritty and powerful, and still plays ringing guitar; Charles' piano doesn't sound as good, but he gets down to business by the end. This is, as expected, the most sympathetic pairing. There's good rapport with Gladys Knight (Heaven Help Us All) as well; she sings good gospel, as of course does he, though the production is chintzy, especially the choir.

And then there's Over the Rainbow, with the melody coming up so overpoweringly in the intro, you expect to hear Charles duetting with the ghost of Judy Garland. What you actually get is Johnny Mathis. You also get Charles singing a song that's almost completely wrong for him. He once, in an interview with Whitney Balliett, spoke of songs he loved but couldn't sing. Stardust was one of them. The reason, I think, is that Stardust, like most of Over the Rainbow, is a contemplative, generalized kind of song. Charles had to sing in the first person; he wasn't a philosopher, he needed to want something, a woman or a state, it didn't matter.

Rainbow only gets that personal on its last line -- actually, its coda -- and then, and only then, Charles makes it really poignant. It's worth it, perhaps, to realize that about him. And there is something touching about this tribute from Charles' peers and disciples, about its spirit if not about its execution. Sing the songs, children.
