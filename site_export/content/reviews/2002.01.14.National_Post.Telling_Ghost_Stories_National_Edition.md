THE DRAGONFLY OF CHICOUTIMI

Factory Studio Theatre, Toronto

- - -

THE DANCE OF THE DEAD

Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Dennis O'Connor gives an extraordinary solo performance in The Dragonfly of Chicoutimi as a man caught between trance and consciousness, between childhood and maturity, and -- most bizarrely -- between English and French.

He plays Gaston Talbot, a middle-aged Quebecois. As an adolescent Gaston witnessed -- may even have caused -- the drowning of an Anglophone playmate. The shock sent him into 40 years of silence. Now, finally, he talks, but in English -- a tongue he never spoke before. At least the words are English; the syntax remains French. The author, Larry Tremblay, is himself a French-Canadian who has never before written in English. (The play was staged in Montreal in 1995.) Playwright and actor together create an effect of tortured simplicity that is troublingly effective.

O'Connor is a round-faced, scant-haired man who looks somewhat Falstaffian; indeed, he played a good Falstaff in Toronto a few years ago, and he seems initially to be beaming benevolence. It turns out, however, that his Gaston has access to deep reserves, not only of confusion, but of hostility. A mask of hatred comes over the actor's face, and waves of it contort both his voice and his body. The speed of his transformations, and their whole-heartedness, are remarkable. He is, he says, "a child in an adult's body." Sometimes it seems to be the other way round.

Delving back into his memories, he recalls himself as a virtuoso artist of the Popsicle stick -- "the Picasso of Chicoutimi." He also remembers, and impersonates, his mother, whom he recalls, terrifyingly, as an implacable guilt-assigning monster. Finally, after much twisting and turning, he arrives at the event that traumatised him.

Kevin Orr directs with admirable tact. This, following Michael Healey's Plan B, is the second play in a week to use the linguistic divide as an image for the riven state of Quebec, though -- at least in this Toronto production -- Tremblay's piece works more potently in personal terms than in political. I wish it a longer life.

You can probably, if you try hard enough, find links between any plays. Gaston is perhaps possessed by the spirit of his dead friend Pierre. The Dance of the Dead, a cheerily named new musical that has also taken seven years to reach a Toronto theatre, gives us a whole stageful of unquiet ghosts, looking for a mortal body in whom to take up residence. It's set in a cemetery, to which a young man has come to commit suicide. However, he has chosen the one night of the year on which those who died with unfinished business (and who doesn't?) reassemble hoping for another chance. There are 20 of them, of various vintages; but once they have done their opening number, all but four take refuge in the auditorium and join us in watching the show. I hope they enjoyed it more than I did.

There are a few nice things going on here. One is a song called Double Your Trouble, supposedly written by a self-described blues singer (actually she seems to have been more of a torch singer) who passed away c. 1930 and who seems, on the evidence presented here, to have been some 10 years ahead of her time. The song itself is all right, but what I liked most is that she doesn't get up, come centre stage, and try to kill us with it. She sits on a bench at the side and sings it to the more recent revenante, whose favourite number it was during life and whose listening is as important as her companion's vocal. In fact, the two of them interacted.

Not much of that goes on, perhaps because most of these living dead are also walking cliches. The chanteuse died of a drug overdose, having previously discovered that the men in her life "didn't want me; they wanted the lady on the stage." Sandra Caldwell, who plays her, wastes little time on subtlety; hers is a relentlessly hands-on-hips performance, even when her arms are hanging down by her sides. Elizabeth Beeler gets somewhat more reality into her latter-day fan, a compulsive gambler who lost at Russian roulette. A third is a repressed homosexual. A fourth is a killer. Each of them pleads to be Edward's beneficiary. He chooses a recipient, but isn't happy with the results. So he changes the rules.

I was never quite clear what the rules were anyway. How come that Edward, now dead, was apparently able to stick around a whole year and monitor the fate of his soul in his successor's body? Why, if it comes to that, are these corpses still so concerned with the world of the living? I can hardly speak for the dead (though in this job ...) but the uncaring graveyard bunch of Thornton Wilder's Our Town seem to me a lot more convincing -- certainly a lot more compelling.

Scott White wrote both the book (which carries what drama there is) and the score, and he uses the characters' various ages as the pegs for an eclectic collection of songs. (To put it another way, he has tried to please everybody.) There are a couple of interesting tunes, but the lyrics -- aside from being carelessly rhymed -- are too clotted and contorted to carry the amount of exposition that has been wished upon them.

This is especially notable in Move On, a would-be gospel showstopper in which Edward, who has been doing some research (how?) tells his flock that all is now well with their survivors and that they themselves can now rest in peace. It must be the only revivalist number whose message is "stay dead." His contempo-pop ballads are as sententious and sentimental as everyone else's; and the whole thing is suffused in do-goodery. Though there is nothing explicitly religious about it, it has the flavour of Christian rock, that forlorn attempt to take back the Devil's music. It talks bold, about suicide and gambling and homosexuality, but it remains ineffably ingenuous.

Vinetta Strombergs' choreography is basic, involving lots of hand- waving, but she seems to have a cast of singers rather than dancers. Having them sit among us, though, is self-defeating as well as disconcerting; it throws the balance. Instead of getting the full effect of a vocal ensemble, you hear a single line being bellowed into your ear. And the words are frankly not the kind to which you want to get that close.
