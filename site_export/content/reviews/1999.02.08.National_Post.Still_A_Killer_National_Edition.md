Chicago

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

Chita Rivera, still a killer, gives a gleaming performance of Roxie Hart, the -- for want of a better word -- heroine of Chicago. Roxie is the failed chorine of the roaring '20s who shoots her absconding lover and finds herself, to her great indignation, up on a murder charge, her loving husband having fumbled in his gallant attempt to take the rap.

"I'm older" says Roxie, surveying her wasted dreams of stardom, "than I ever intended to be." It's a great line in its own right, and it gets an extra muted laugh here, Rivera at 66 being some 40 years older than the woman she's playing. But then, Chicago is hardly a naturalistic piece. It's a gleefully malevolent cartoon, in which all the conventions of musical comedy are explored, exposed, and returned to their most libidinous roots.

The numbers are self-contained parodies, formally announced by the conductor. Its message is that if you've got it, you flaunt it; but only if you can do it without sweat. Rivera has it all -- vocally, physically, and temperamentally. This, after all, is her territory; in the original 1975 production she played Roxie's sidekick and rival homicide Velma Kelly, and she wasn't in her 20s then.

Velma is basic brass, and that is how Stephanie Pope plays her, with a growling delivery and spectacular legs that apparently see no reason to stop. They aren't quite a satisfying pair -- the two women, I mean -- since Pope doesn't have Rivera's aura and Rivera doesn't have Pope's phenomenal height. Their final number, the wonderfully sinuous Nowadays, lacks its proper symmetry.

This, I must emphasize, is quibbling at a very high level. This is a far better company of Chicago than played in Toronto last year, and the 1996 Broadway revival from which they both derive was a knock-out to begin with. It was given special velocity by the deadpan amoral Velma of Bebe Neuwirth, but it also fined down the dark and elaborate vision of Bob Fosse's original staging to something more palatable.

Ann Reinking's choreography is described as "in the style of" Fosse, and at this distance it's hard to tell the difference. Numbers such as the opening All That Jazz, the Cell Block Tango for the massed murderesses (count 'em, six), and the frantic We Both Reached for the Gun in which Roxie quacks out her story to the press as a dummy perched on her lawyer's knee, are vaudeville routines raised to the highest power. The score never adds anything personal to its pastiche but on its own terms it's superb: John Kander's music a resourceful series of period archetypes, Fred Ebb's lyrics crammed with neat, smart jokes. ("She'd ask me, what state's Chicago in? I'd say, ill.")

The show is a triumph of style rather than substance. It tells us over and over how rotten and/or gullible everybody is, and if it weren't done with consistent wit it would become self-righteously monotonous. (That's what happened back in '75, abetted by Fosse's compulsion, ever since the film of Cabaret, to blame show business for the state of the world.)

Marcia Lewis has come up to recreate her prison matron, though she has lost some of the awesome economy she had in New York. Michael Berresse is a very dapper lawyer, though not as stylish in song or dance as he is in dialogue. M. E. Spencer is the best sob- sister reporter I've seen, and Ernie Sabella as Roxie's old man brings off the neat trick of filling the stage with his self- effacement. And Jack Gaughan takes full advantage of the best part ever written for a conductor.
