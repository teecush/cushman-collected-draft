The commercial hook for PU-239 is the presence of George Clooney and Steven Soderbergh as executive producers. Nowadays it seems that everybody and his brother can be an executive producer -- this production lists seven -- but it's quite possible that without Clooney's and Soderbergh's endorsement, the film would never have been made. Which would have been a pity.

PU-239 is weapons-grade plutonium. The film, written and directed by Scott Z. Burns (writer of The Bourne Ultimatum, producer of An Inconvenient Truth) from a short story by Ken Kalfus, is about two Russian men stranded and shafted after the collapse of the Soviet Union: Timofey, a nuclear plant worker, and Shiv, an aspiring Moscow gangster.

There's a malfunction at Timofey's plant and, after trying to prevent it, he is fired without compensation, since to give him anything would constitute an admission of criminal negligence on the part of management. (Truths don't get more inconvenient than that.) He does depart with a dose of radiation sufficient to kill him in just a few days, and a pouchful of plutonium, fissionable material that he hopes to sell so as to secure a future for his wife and young son. Which is how he gets mixed up with Shiv.

Shiv is an enthusiastic young hustler, trying to make his way in a world that has collapsed into one big black market. He's in the protection racket, but finds he's encroached on the territory of mobsters far more powerful than himself. He has a couple of sidekicks dumber than he is but also more dangerous. (Or more dangerous because they're dumber. They are, it turns out, especially dangerous to themselves.)

Shiv takes some convincing that Timofey is actually in possession of an untouchable powder that he's prepared to sell at the bargain rate of US$30,000. But as he is in desperate need of funds himself, to pay off the big guys he unwittingly crossed, he agrees to go into partnership.

Healsogets personally involved. Shiv has a son himself -- by a hooker girlfriend who professes not to be sure if the boy is even his -- and what starts out as a strained thematic parallel turns into a dramatic bond.

The film divides into two parts of which the second is the more engrossing. In the first half, we keep cutting between Timofey's story and Shiv's, and it's an uneven contest. For all the horror of what happens to Timofey, and despite good performances from Paddy Considine and Radha Mitchell (tough and tender as the wife), the treatment is lachrymose.

Oscar Isaac's Shiv, on the other hand, is funny, quick-witted, and desperate in the best gangster movie tradition. He's a small- timer who scams money from street traders supposedly to fight Chechen terrorists, and for whom every walk down the street is an opportunity to lift some windshield wipers.

Shiv is also, naturally, a fan of American pop culture, though he's hazy about some of the details: Sharing with his son, he comes up with a delirious concoction in which the CIA pursue that well- known Chicago hood Jesse James, who in turn gets mixed in with Jesse Jackson, of the Jackson Five ("the most powerful family in America").

When Timofey and Shiv combine, each feeding off the other's desperation, the movie kicks into its highest gear. The two initial stories keep folding back on one another, to dramatic and blackly comic effect. One of the achievements -- an ambiguous one, I suppose, but an achievement nonetheless -- is that we lose our moral bearings. If our heroes' deal goes through, lethal material will be made available to the highest bidder.

Nevertheless, as we watch them shuttle between two pitiless gangland kingpins -- one old, one young -- we find ourselves desperately hoping that they will succeed and that they won't be killed or screwed. The personal hits harder than the political; which may not have been the Clooney-Soderbergh intention. This is an American production, mostly peopled by American actors, plus Steven Berkoff, once the arch "bovver" boy of theBritish theatre, grown unrecognizably fleshy but as terrifying as ever. (His "legitimate" counterpart, though-- the nuclear CEO -- sounds disconcertingly Scottish.) It's full of American gangster tropes which, as most recently demonstrated in David Cronenberg's Eastern Promises, have long since gone international.

Here, though, the genre is really raw; the underworld is the world--gangster-capitalism at its purest with no social safety net. The ending is, all things considered, less bleak than it might be. But not by much. - PU-239 airs tonight at 9 p.m. on Movie Central
