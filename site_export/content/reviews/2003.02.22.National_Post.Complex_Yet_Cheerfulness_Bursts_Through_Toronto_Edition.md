THE SEA

George Ignatieff Theatre

- - -

To see The Sea is to be reminded of what an extraordinarily good writer Edward Bond can be. Or at least how good he could be. The Sea is a 30-year-old play, written when Bond and the professional theatre still had some use for each other.

That relationship continued for about another decade, after which Bond seems to have decided that the theatre was just another irredeemable part of the capitalist world. He now writes only for uncorrupted amateurs, and young ones at that. I haven't seen any of his recent plays, but I do recall that earlier on, even when he seemed to be writing didactic parables, complexity, if not cheerfulness, had an incorrigible way of breaking in.

The Sea, from his earlier phase, is complex to the point of waywardness. Set just before the First World War, it's a comedy, and a truly witty one. Most of the wit is centred in the figure of Mrs. Rafi, a well-to-do widow who is the governing presence of the small coastal town in which she lives. (That town, as Bond wrote it, is in the south of England. In the current production, it has been moved to Newfoundland, with no especially ill effects. Indeed, in that setting, the sight of a Union Jack flying at a funeral carries an especially ironic charge.)

Mrs. Rafi has a natural authority, mainly expressed through bullying. Her most immediate victims are the more timid ladies of the town, most especially a Mrs. Tilehouse, who is her companion and who fears, respects and resents her in about equal measure. Mrs. Rafi is also an enthusiastic purveyor of amateur dramatics (though not of the kind that Bond would later favour) and one of the play's more hilarious scenes shows her rehearsing her colleagues in her own treatment of Orpheus in the Underworld, and putting them, appropriately enough, through hell. Some of her put-downs ("Jessica, do stop trying to look as if you had an interesting past") are inspired. It's difficult to resist a character who talks like that, even if her author would like us to see her as a symbol of everything that's evil in society.

He complicates the picture in other ways, too, because Mrs. Rafi is self-aware. She knows what she does to people, and claims, quite convincingly, that most of them enjoy it. She also prophesies that, when she is an old lady in a wheelchair, they will have their revenge. She has a long speech on that subject, and it's superb: concrete, funny and -- to invent a phrase -- self-pitiless. (Bond was to be less kind to the establishment monsters of his later plays, but they, too, tend to assert themselves in the teeth of his intentions.)

The best of Bond's dialogue has a stage presence equalled among living English playwrights only by Harold Pinter; his lines spring from and illuminate their dramatic situations, but also sound as if they might survive on their own resources.

Hazel Desbarats, playing Mrs. Rafi for the Actor's Repertory Company (a low-key Soulpepper), cannot muster the character's full force or energy, but she is very intelligent and very incisive. She rules the play as she does the village.

One of her less willing subjects is Mr. Hatch, the local draper. "Shopping," proclaims Mrs. Rafi, "is a lost art," and her efforts to revive it are principally at Hatch's expense; she keeps ordering stuff and then sending it back, getting him into deep trouble with his suppliers. She now cancels the biggest order of all -- for a set of blue velvet curtains -- and drives him mad. I still have a vivid memory of Ian Holm, in the play's original production, cutting up the fabric regardless into the required lengths, and then going at his customers with the shears; that intensity of manic obsequiousness is hard to bring off, and Alan Jordan, the current Hatch, manages the resentment but not the fury.

Mr. Hatch is somewhat crazy already: A man obviously ahead of his time, he believes that the Earth, and especially his town, is being invaded by aliens, and he has made some converts among the local male working class. It is this belief that brings him into Mrs. Rafi's irrevocable disfavour.

The play starts with a storm; Hatch is on coastguard duty; he angrily repels a foundered boat, whose two inhabitants he takes to be space invaders; one of them drowns.

The dead man happened to be the fiance of Mrs. Rafi's niece, on his way to claim her; so the chatelaine's wrath is understandable.

This is, indeed, no way for a coast guard to behave, and no amount of special pleading, his own about being too imaginative a man for his station in life, or his author's about the appalling way he's treated by the gentry, can put us on his side. (His later dementia includes an attempt to murder a corpse -- a very Bondian thing to do.)

Hatch, in isolation, is almost as vivid a creation as Mrs. Rafi, and we're meant, I think, to see them both as people too big for their environment, and either crushed or corrupted by it. But the relation between them is never driven through; one wouldn't expect it to be resolved, but here it disappears.

Instead, speechifying takes over. An old recluse (well played by Sam Malkin), who lives in a cave by the sea, delivers apocalyptic rhetoric about the end of a death-worshipping world that could fit a dozen other plays as easily as it does this one. The bereaved niece and her fiance's surviving ship mate fall in love, and they prepare to leave town -- thereby following Mrs. Rafi's advice but not, as is tartly observed, her example.

Both lovers are very high-minded, and there is a suggestion that the girl, who was Eurydice in her aunt's pageant, is likewise ascending from the inferno. But that's the kind of thing that sounds better in exegesis than in performance.

Brenda Bazinet's production is serviceable. It probably goes over the top in the funeral scene but is forgivably funny while doing it.

The opening storm is impressive, and so is the constant punctuation of military guns, practicing up for the coming carnage.

Brett Christopher and Janet Bourgeois bring unexpected life to the lovers, and Angela Fusco's Mrs. Tilehouse has amusing moments, principally musical. Robin Ward, too, is nicely befuddled as the local vicar, recruited first to play Pluto and then to eulogize a man he barely knew. He's quite a sympathetic character until the author remembers that he's an ideological enemy.

The play, like all Bond's best. creates a world and tells a story, albeit in fits and starts. He was a master craftsman, and it is the theatre's ill-fortune that he has, for what most of us would call practical purposes, abandoned the craft.
