THE RAT PACK: LIVE FROM LAS VEGAS

Canon Theatre, Toronto

- - -

A New York taxi driver once told me of a trip to Las Vegas during which he had seen Frank Sinatra, Dean Martin and Sammy Davis Jr. doing their joint nightclub act. He had dropped a hundred bucks for the privilege, which -- this was 1967 -- was very serious money indeed. "But," he said, "for three celebrities like that, you don't mind paying it."

I probably wouldn't have minded myself, given the cash and the opportunity. All the same, I found my cabbie's choice of words perturbing even then, emphasizing as it did the fame of the performers rather than their talents. At least, though, the celebrities he was getting were the real things. The Rat Pack: Live from Las Vegas, which is the latest in the line of necrophiliac tribute shows, asks our indulgence for a trio of imitators. The actors make no attempt to reinterpret either the people or the material; they merely try, with varying degrees of success, to reproduce them. The show started its life a few years ago at an ungainly shopping-precinct theatre in the far western end -- as opposed to the central West End -- of London, and it is an appropriately suburban entertainment, karaoke done for money.

In theory, it should offer more dramatic interest than most of its kind. It has three subjects, so there is the possibility that we will see them interact. That, after all, was the attraction that drew Vegas patrons in the '60s. The premise is that we are watching one of those evening get-togethers at the Sands Hotel. But sitting sober in a theatre is not like sitting sauced in a club; watching an acted show that's been scripted and rehearsed is not like rubbernecking at an all-star party that traded on its freewheelingness and probably did contain a fair quantity of genuine ad libs.

There is also the question of whether the stars are being celebrated for the right reasons. We are being asked to admire all three of them here not for their vocal or comedic skills but for their status as rich, hard-drinking, skirt-chasers: swingers whose connection to musical swing is purely coincidental. Compared to what they actually had to offer, it's an image that's at best irrelevant and at worst rather repugnant.

This show's sense of period, as of everything else, is coarse- grained. The big-band orchestrations sound authentic and are great to hear, but the set is anachronistic, and so are many of the songs. In the interests of delivering as many greatest hits as possible, the evening is filled out with numbers that entered these guys' repertoires decades later: The Candy Man, Mr. Bojangles, New York, New York and Mack the Knife, on the last of which the singer, Stephen Triffitt, seems to have Sinatra's version confused with Bobby Darin's.

That, though, comes late. At first glance Triffitt, an English performer who has made a career out of Sinatra imitation, seems to have his man down cold. He has the insolently cool profile, the cock of the head, the subtly swaying arm, the hooded eyes that close to focus a note or a mood, the clinching rhythmic foot. He also, to a most unusual degree, has the metallic texture of the voice (though not its compensating warmth) and a good copyist's idea of the phrasing.

However, by the second line of the first song, the cracks show. Copying isn't enough; indeed it's antithetical to what the real Frank was about. He essays two Sinatra stereotypes: the imperious Lothario (Luck Be a Lady, The Lady Is a Tramp -- would FS ever have dealt two Ladies back to back?) and the saloon sufferer (Angel Eyes, One for My Baby), but nothing in between. Nor does he have much authority in comic banter -- though in this he may be uncomfortably close to the real thing. Sinatra could be a great emcee, but as an interacting comedian he was a bully.

Mark Adams, when singing, sounds exactly like Dean Martin. This may be no great trick; Dino's voice is one of those anyone can do, like Jolson's and Durante's. Nor do Adams' appearance and manner have much of Martin's endearing cragginess; his face indeed seems to be cut from the same unwrinkled cloth as his shiny serge suit. But he does capture excellently Martin's pivotal position as both the funniest and, in his way, the most courteous of the group; the booze jokes ("the difference between a drunk and an alcoholic is a drunk doesn't have to go to the meetings") still work.

Sammy Davis, master impressionist though he was, could never manage to sound like Sinatra. I offer this consideration as balm to Rashad Naylor, who can't sound like Davis, and is too tall to look like him, either. His singing starts out wrong, with That Old Black Magic done at about a third of the frantic Sammy tempo, and gets worse. His dancing is better, but he doesn't get to do much of it. The supposed dynamo of the act never sparks, and stops the show from working even on its own constricted terms. Three mature and beefy chorines, performing timelessly awful choreography, are another handicap.

Even less relevant is the finale. Triffitt sings My Way, which, apart from being another anachronism and the worst song ever written ("without exemption," says a lyricist friend of mine, without compunction), gives the lie to the rest of the evening. We thought it was about three men and now it turns out to be about only one. There follows what seems an endless series of encores. Well, it sounded like some of the audience wanted them. In London, the show has been running for two years in the real West End. That, whether you judge the event as music or as theatre, is a truly depressing thought.
