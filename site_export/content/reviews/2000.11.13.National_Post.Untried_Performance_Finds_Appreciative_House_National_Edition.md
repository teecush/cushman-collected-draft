FIVE FINGERS

133 Bathurst Street, Toronto

The Five Fingers of Robin Fulford's play come together to deliver a blow. The title may also be open to symbolic interpretation, but I'm happier with the literal.

The blow is struck by a husband, Tuck, to his wife, Anna. The play takes us into their house, like investigators called to the scene of the crime. In the current performance, it takes us there with a bracing air of verismo. Theatres are traditionally described as houses, but this one actually is a house: at Bathurst and Adelaide, a couple of doors down from the Factory Theatre. There are practical reasons for this; the show is a workshop for which the author's own company, Platform 9, has been trying, unsuccessfully, to find a more orthodox home.

The play has existed in some form for the best part of a decade. As a one-act piece with the impenetrable title of Swahili Godot (whose significance may conceivably have been that the two characters are locked together but cannot communicate) it was performed in 1992 in Toronto and last year on the fringe of the Edinburgh Festival. While abroad, it began to develop a second act, which on Friday night -- at the start of a week's run -- received its first-ever performance.

It was also, apparently, receiving what amounted to its first real rehearsal; the director, Sarah Stanley (whose work here is by far the best I have seen from her), informed us beforehand that they had had no time to do more than talk the play's new second half at the table.

You wouldn't have known it from the performance; the two actors - - who carried scripts throughout -- flew. It seems that the only thing as effective as a thoroughly rehearsed cast is a thoroughly unrehearsed one. The first encounter of good actors with a fertile text usually sets off sparks; and for once an audience was there to fan them into a blaze.

About a dozen of us ("I like a full house," said the director appreciatively) were crammed just outside the living room, peering through an archway, for the first act, and into a studio room at the back for the second. The first part is largely alternating monologue. Tuck, a cleanliness freak whose distaste for the mess of married life has been aggravated by the arrival of a baby, variously fantasizes, storms and complains. Anna is drawn in less detail; her most notable characteristic is a wiry intensity that may derive more from the actress than from the writing.

It would be tactless to call her a human punching-bag, and systematic physical abuse is not the issue here, but her principal dramatic functions are to react and reminisce; she seems to have more memory than he of what drew them together in the first place. She recollects and re-creates their first meeting; they were in an elevator, and he wiped a smudge of toothpaste from her cheek. (This scene is evocatively staged underneath the arch, which provides a perfect confining frame.)

Now, in the disintegrating stage of their marriage, toothpaste has become something that he rages at her for forgetting to buy. Most men are likely to feel twinges of sympathy with Tuck's feelings of entrapment, but the author is merciless with him. He hasn't grown up; and when domesticity threatens to interfere with his work schedule -- or, worse, with his play schedule -- he explodes. Everything in that first half leads up to his hitting Anna; and everything in the second deals with the consequences of that irrevocable act. The effect, as we walk away at the end, is that of having witnessed the blow in slow motion.

Anna walks out, with the baby; Tuck begs her to come back; she refuses, though she does make a couple of brief business-like visits. There couldn't be much of a second act if she didn't. Dramatically, in fact, they have far more contact after they have broken up than before.

In the first half their scenes together are brief but compelling confrontations, the more riveting for being islands in a sea of lyrical soliloquy (though in Tuck's case the lyricism tends towards the jagged). We spend most of Act Two with Tuck, and here too he talks to himself a lot: necessarily, since there's nobody else around. But he is now far more concrete, far more focused on the present, and on what he wants but -- however pitifully he pleads -- is not going to get; his scenes with Anna (whose passport, in an incalculable mixture of pique and desperation, he hides) make that thoroughly clear.

I prefer the writing -- or at least the kind of writing -- in Act Two, though in both modes the author tends to repeat himself. What he is repeating, though, is worth saying, and even more worth showing; domestic dramas come and go, but this one is more painfully and individually honest than most.

It is also, of course, great fun to see it staged in this environment, and to see that environment so resourcefully employed. The living room action extends into the hall outside, and what must once have been the dining room; the scheme allows for some atmospheric lighting (by Rebecca Picherak), and when Anna looks at herself in a mirror, a stage manager holds it up for her: rather a Japanese touch. (Where did she glide in from?)

The move to the studio is an invigorating surprise in itself (we cluster in the kitchen while the equipment is being moved), and here, too, we get an extra dimension; some scenes are performed -- to great visual effect, and with perfect audibility as well -- in the backyard beyond; they seem appropriately chilly and groping. Inside, the central feature is a long table at which Tuck seems more isolated than ever, especially when on the phone to colleagues. (He seems be a loans officer in some financial institution. Need more be said?)

The actors, Ross Manson and Tamsin Kelsey, are presumably making up much of the choreography as they go along, just as they are exploring the text before our ears, and they hardly strike a false note; he exudes a boyishness that obviously thinks it's endearing, she manages to be firm and sensitive at once.

The idea is that each performance will build on the one before, and they are off to an excellent start. The play will probably resurface, sooner rather than later, in a more conventional setting, but I advise you to catch it where it is. Or maybe it will find another address (the owner apparently wants this one back) and become the first house-bound Toronto hit since the legendary Tamara. Which, by the way, was nothing like as serious or as affecting a play.
