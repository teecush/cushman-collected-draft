THEATRE REVIEW OUT THERE TONIGHT

Thursday, July 12, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Directed by Alan Johnson Starring Shirley MacLaine O'Keefe Centre Shirley MacLaine's zig-zag smile and precision movement are just two of the attratcions her charming show Shirley goes beyond the confines of fame ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail HERE are two unbroken hours of Shirley MacLaine: no interval, no supporting acts, no false exits. We arrive to find the curtain already up on a set that looks, in an Art Deco hue, somewhere between white and grey, like a bandstand. This it turns out is exactly what it is, though complicated by an arrangement of stairs and railings that might have fallen off the back of an ocean liner. Maybe they were left behind by Anything Goes, when everything went.

Presently the show starts. Musicians wander on and, with purposeful casualness, start tuning up. Then technicians, or guys pretending to be, appear and execute sound and lighting checks. Ah, the thrill of rehearsals. If only it could be captured in performance; but it never is. You start fretting at the artful artlessness of it all, and nerve yourself to applaud the stars inevitable grand entrance. Then suddenly, just when and where you would least expect it, she is discovered. Grudgingly, you admit that this production may know what it is doing.

The first thing the lady does is sing the title song, which is devoted to second-guessing all the things the audience might be thinking about her. It is rather accurate, in a self-confidently self-deprecating way, and the lyric is neatly turned. (The program contains the novel credit: "Writing by Buz Kohan.") When MacLaine did her first one-woman show, 16 years ago, it was all about how amazing it was to find someone we all knew as a movie star appearing on stage at all, let alone singing and dancing and doing it well. In another 16 years she may, I fear, be doing a show about how amazing it is to be doing a show at her age. This time round, she has a running gag about her well-known spiritual preoccupations, and how weird she knows everybody must find them. She also delves into her performing past to do what she calls her hooker-victim medley, composed - or so it struck me - of songs she almost did in the movies: somebody elses big number from Sweet Charity and a ballad she would have sung in Irma la Douce if Irma la Douce on film had stayed a musical.

I like Shirley MacLaine, which I assume puts me in the majority. I think she is entitled to her opinions, however bizarre, and I would back her to defend them against any talk-show host in the hemisphere. I also find her, still, desperately attractive as only a funny girl can be, and I can have little serious quarrel with a show that exhibits her in a black lame leotard. She may not be the world's most spectacular dancer but she moves with zest and precision, no slacking, while her zig-zag smile, piquant nose, mocking eyes and cropped red hair are practically choreographic features in themselves.

Some of her self-satire is very effective. She tells a solemn story about a trip to the Andes, and just as we are becoming bored and embarrassed it turns into a tribute to Jimmy Durante. For a moment she really had us fooled, as she gleefully claims. Indeed I found that she was second-guessing me all evening. "I bet you thought I was Mitzi Gaynor" she said, as if the set raised for her the same associations it did for me. Then, just as I was wondering why she didn't play a role in a real musical - Mama Rose in Gypsy, for example - she went into what amounted to an impromptu audition for it. As it turned out we were both off-beam; maybe Roses Turn could never work out of context but I suspect MacLaines vulnerability is too near the surface for Rose, a woman who only discovers that she has feelings belatedly and by accident. But I would like to see her as Adelaide in Guys and Dolls or Desiree in A Little Night Music or maybe Ella, the Judy Holliday role, in Bells Are Ringing. If she loves the stage as much as she claims I don't see why she has to limit herself to evenings of snippets.

But since she has: The snippets I liked best included a song about the supermarket tabloids and, incredibly but truthfully, the ritual thanks to her musicians and back-up dancers, done as a number and a rousing one. There is also a prolonged tribute to Broadway choreography which starts, spiffily and instructively, with a pastiche of the late Bob Fosse, but then founders on the fact that no other maestro has as recognizable a style and disintegrates in an MTV parody that is generalized and fundamentally (and forgivably) bored. Otherwise Alan Johnson's staging is unostentatiously shrewd, while the music has been composed and arranged by industrial heavyweights: Larry Grossman, Peter Matz, Billy Byers.

The show as a whole swings between exhilaration and queasiness. She is Shirley MacLaine, and the audience knows she is, and thats why both parties are there, and maybe its honest of them to admit it, and especially her. Post-modern critical theory holds that all art is self- referential, which is true, but then goes on to assume that that's the most interesting thing about it, which isn't. Shirley MacLaine is famous for being famous, but she does not have to settle - even in the 1990s - for being famous for being famous for being famous.
