GIRL IN THE GOLDFISH BOWL

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Iris, the Girl in the Goldfish Bowl of Morris Panych's new play, lives in that bowl, figuratively at least, because its former inhabitant no longer does. He died, as goldfish will, and was flushed down the toilet by Iris' mother who has her own problems, as does everybody else in the household.

We are in a small Western Canadian town, one with a beach and a canning factory. It is 1962 and the Cuban Missile Crisis is happening offstage, though we do not feel it impinging on the characters to the extent that the author probably intends. Iris mentions it most, but then she is our narrator so she has the scope.

She is 11 and, at least by her parents' lights, irritatingly bright. Her father, an unemployed war vet who has a passion for geometry and spends most of his time standing at a draftsman's table, asks her: "Isn't it time you ran away from home?" (I should point out that he does love her, but he is having a bad day. He has a bad day every day.)

Iris is also told that she shouldn't use words that have more letters than she has years, a rule that she happily transgresses in nearly every sentence she utters. One of the forbidden words, this year at least, is "reincarnation." Iris is convinced that the body of Mr. Lawrence, her family's new and mysterious lodger, houses the returned spirit of her late pet, whom she called Amahl because he was bought in a mall. One reason for her theory is that when Mr Lawrence first arrives he is wet, or at least his clothes are.

Other people believe other things about him. Iris' mother sees him as a possible love object. Iris' godmother, a lodger of much longer standing who goes by the name of Miss Rose, sees him as a possible sex object. (According to the other females on the premises, this is how she sees every man.) Iris' father sees him first as a threat and then, in desperation, as an ally, a means of staving off the worst thing he can imagine, which is that his wife will leave him. Mr Lawrence himself seems bewildered by everything that is expected of him, and gives virtually nothing away about his identity; indeed the other characters all seem to know, or guess, more about him than he does. He is at various times suspected of being a spy, an escaped criminal, and a mental patient; and he does at one point express terror at the idea of undergoing a lobotomy.

There are shades of Harold Pinter's The Caretaker in the play, while at other points, with its earnest fish-conscious young heroine and its cramped dysfunctional family evokes comparisons as varied as Kristen Thomson's I Claudia and Neil Simon's Brighton Beach Memoirs.

It remains its own animal. Panych writes, here as elsewhere, with an uncommon, quirky blend of humour and compassion. ("I used to read," says the mother, being very precise about her vagueness, "but you know what words are like.") The author skirts whimsy without quite giving in to it, and is capable, as at the end of this play, of a sudden surprising harshness that is never gratuitous. He is not so great on narrative drive; you may be eager to hear what his people are going to say next, but you are seldom greatly concerned about what they are going to do. They also seem to have been somewhat arbitrarily assembled; there seems no reason for the presence of Miss Rose except to provide comic relief for a play that is comic already. Iris' vociferous disgust for the nuns who run her school is funny too (and presumably inspired by Panych's loudly- proclaimed rejection of his own Catholic upbringing) but it hardly connects with the rest of the play. Nor does the play get to grips with the relation, or lack of it, between Iris and her mother. What works surpassingly well, grounding the action with increasing firmness as it proceeds, is the parents' relationship. The woman who took a husband she didn't love, and found that time was unable to work the miracle; the man who hangs around hoping for affection, and dreams of finding it by transporting his wife to Paris where she will be seduced by the mathematical beauty with which the streets intersect and the Boulevards run into the Places.

Panych himself directs the play, splendidly. For design he has his regular collaborator Ken McDonald whose set here is unusually restrained and realistic. Much of the space is devoted to a staircase that boasts some spectacularly untrustworthy banisters; the family is on its uppers, unable to afford a television but with a very evocative collection of LPs.

Above all, though, this is a feast of acting. All five performers excel themselves. It is one of the play's oddities that Iris, the play's central figure, is far from being its most interesting and the repeated theme, that we are witnessing the end of childhood (like Eartha Kitt's on the day that the circus left town), is stated too insistently to be honestly felt. Panych obviously had fun finding her voice and writing her monologues, and we have fun listening, but there is self-indulgence on both sides. There is none though that I could detect in Kristina Nicoll's performance, with its hope and disillusion and querulous indignation. A couple of flash forwards to her adult self do little to enrich the play's texture but are marvellous, shocking contrasts in themselves. John Jarvis, whom I have usually seen being lucid and urbane, makes an extraordinary transition to the dishevelled and woebegone dad, constantly in his pajamas and gaining dignity in the act of sacrificing it. His brief moments of joy are delightful at the time and heartbreaking in retrospect.

It might be interesting to see his discontented spouse played by an actress less brisk than Brenda Robins (or one, as her husband might express it, with fewer sharp angles) but she does finely, and can create a great effect by very nearly smiling.

Tanja Jacobs' talents find outsize release as the louche Miss Rose, moving like run-down clockwork from her job at the factory to a social life as dissolute as she can manage; that is, she's canning by day and canned at night. At home, she seems always to be dragging herself momentously upstairs, in search of bed or bath; she looks like Luba Goy, one size larger.

Finally, Richard Zeppieri as Mr. Lawrence succeeds brilliantly in the daunting task of being simultaneously bewildering and bewildered. There are, one is convinced, scary things going on inside him. Or, more or less in the words of the 1940s song, which we get to hear perfectly updated to the swinging '60s by Frank Sinatra, I think he'd rather be a fish.
