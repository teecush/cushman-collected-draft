DR. CHEKHOV: WARD 6

Factory Theatre, Toronto

After their successes with Chekhov's Shorts and Chekhov Longs, the troupe known as Theatre Smith-Gilmour has come up with a third instalment they have heroically refrained from calling Chekhov Medium. Dr. Chekhov: Ward 6 derives the first part of its title from the Russian writer's parallel career in medicine. Ward 6 is a novella he based on his experiences of visiting and working at a remote hospital in Siberia, and this show is a dramatization of it.

In a life of less than 50 years Chekhov achieved greatness as a writer of both drama and prose fiction (a very rare double), while also working full-time as a courageous and conscientious physician. Indeed, his conscientiousness, as exemplified in that trip to Siberia, helped kill him. And he was a great success with women. Yet he died convinced he was a failure.

To the rest of us, this must seem extraordinary, as well as oddly reassuring. One thing that must strike us now, though, is the continuity between the different areas of his life. It isn't just that there are doctors in his plays and medical themes in his stories; it's that the qualities of shrewdness, kindness and total lack of sentimentality that seem to have characterized him as a doctor all carry over into his writing.

Ward 6 is one of his bleakest tales, though the telling is, as always, cheerfully practical. It's set mainly in a hut, the most decaying part of a decaying hospital, "a hundred and fifty miles from the nearest railway station." This hut is the mental ward, inhabited by five patients and a brutal porter/ attendant. We meet one of the inmates, Ivan Dimitrich, a poverty-stricken gentleman who could never hold down a job, could never form a relationship and became convinced that everyone was out to get him: which, in time, they did, condemning him to his present incarceration with no hope of reprieve.

The doctor in charge of the hospital, Andrey Yefimitch, arrived in town full of benevolent intentions. But he got ground down, became hopeless and listless and has not now set foot in Ward 6 for years. But one day he wanders in and starts a conversation with Ivan. These two defeated men, one of them deranged, have probably the best minds in the neighbourhood (a familiar Chekhovian pattern - - think of Dr. Astrov and Uncle Vanya) and they engage together. Ivan rages, Andrey counsels resignation, basing his stoicism in the fact that we're all going to die anyway. (His own life, though as lonely as Ivan's, is of course comparatively comfortable -- as Ivan doesn't hesitate to point out.)

The story, having hooked our interest with the patient, turns out to be about the doctor. An ambitious assistant overhears him in friendly conversation with a madman. The local authorities urge him to take what turns out to be a disastrous vacation. When he returns, he has no job, no money, and pretty soon nothing at all. He too is consigned to Ward 6.

This is Chekhov anticipating Kafka, and it's better than Kafka, because it is rooted in observed reality rather than the author's neuroses. Ivan Dimitrich, like Josef K, feels he must have committed some unidentifiable crime and that he will be punished for it, but we understand what has brought him to this state and why other people perceive him as they do. We also understand, all too well, the bureaucratic politics that destroy Andrey, and his own hopeless vulnerability to them.

Michele Smith and Dean Gilmour, founders and guiding spirits of the company that bears their names, stay faithful to the story's form while altering its flavour. They are expert mimes who bring the tradition of commedia to everything they do.

Their staging here is, as always, swift, graceful and imaginative. They can, with the minimum number of beds and bodies, create the close-pressed, hopeless hut at a moment's notice. These, in the atmosphere they create of a world with its vital passages blocked up, be the best-staged asylum scenes since Peter Brook's Marat-Sade.

But the production can also be distressingly cute. It's pleasing, when we are told that Ivan Dmitrich was a great reader, and hands unknown fling a book at him, which he insouciantly catches; it's excessive when more volumes are immediately flung at him from all sides, however nimbly he fields them. And it gives a misleadingly playful impression of what his life was actually like.

At the heart of the story is a series of debates. It takes great verbal authority to get these discussions from page to stage, and this is just what Theatre S-G, as a showcase for Smith and Gilmour themselves, is least able to provide. Gilmour gets things off to a fine and steely start as narrator -- it's a shock to realize he is also to play Ivan, a role whose anger he transmutes into something beamishly Dickensian, but swathed in a nightgown, he does give the character a gaunt, compelling physical presence.

Smith as Andrey has the perpetual sad smile of Pierrot; her holy- fool innocence does something to suggest the doctor's plight, but doesn't begin to do justice to a character who is as much his own victim as anyone else's. (Chekhov would never have countenanced it; he was always especially hard on his own trade.) Far more damaging is Smith's heavy French accent, which obscures the sense of the text while dulling the senses of the audience. Even Andrey's most startling insight -- "So long as prisons and madhouses exist someone must be shut up in them" -- goes for virtually nothing.

In Chekhov's Shorts and Longs, some of the dramatic slack was taken up by actors of the calibre of Colombe Demers and Liisa Repo- Martell, but there is no supporting work of that quality here. As the Postmaster, the doctor's nearest approach to a friend, Paul Fauteux works hard, and somewhat successfully, to supply the sense of coarse quotidian reality the show otherwise lacks, and Ann-Marie Kerr, who plays a number of roles, is most prominent as Andrey's housekeeper, forever poking her head around the corner of the proscenium like a cuckoo popping out of its clock. It's nice, but it has nothing to do with the real world.

I should end, though, with a concession, maybe a confession. Among major writers -- certainly major modern writers -- Chekhov is a figure almost uniquely sane and likeable. Therefore, we all want to kidnap him -- that is, to remake him in what we take to be our own image. That is what Theatre Smith-Gilmour have done with him, both before and now. They have as much right to do so as anyone else. And at least they have an image.
