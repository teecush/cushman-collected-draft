LITERATURE

HEAD TO HARBOURFRONT

A reviewer on Amazon.com described Janette Turner Hospital's latest book as "dressed-up pulp fiction." Well, if you'd like to dress up to hear some pulp fiction, head to Ms. Hospital's appearance at the Harbourfront Reading Series (235 Queen's Quay West) tomorrow at 7:30 p.m. The author will be reading from Due Preparations for the Plague, which follows the lives of survivors of a hijacking along with the relatives of those killed. If you're not afraid to leave the house after listening to Hospital pontificate on the history of chemical warfare, cults and terrorism, consider attending the Toronto Women's Bookstore (73 Harbord St.) launch for Womankind: Faces of Change Around the World on Saturday at 7 p.m. Author Donna Nebenzahl and photographer Nance Ackerman travelled the world documenting the lives of such women activists as Joanna Manning, Robin Morgan and Vandana Shiva. Finally, Howl, the spoken word program on CIUT (89.5 FM), will be featuring Star Trek Poetry on Sunday at 2 p.m. James Cowan

VISUAL ARTS

PARR FOR THE COURSE?

Martin Parr's photo exhibit Common Sense opens at Pitko in the Distillery District today. Parr unflinchingly focuses on ordinary people's decidedly lowbrow pleasures. His photographs highlight the ubiquity of crass pop culture -- as in a series of Spice Girls T- shirts around the world -- and the weirdness of working-class approximations of luxury. Subject matter includes tourists' sun- charred flesh, grotesquely greasy meats and working-class vacation destinations that are anything but idyllic. The opening event is tonight from 7-10 p.m. The exhibit runs until Dec. 10. Shannon Proudfoot

POPULAR MUSIC

SHE'S A REAL PEACH ...

How can we miss the Eagles if they won't go away? And why would anyone pay as much as $250 to see them when they could see vulgar gender bender Peaches at the Guvernment on the same night? The band that gave us Desperado and Hotel California bring its 17th annual farewell tour to the Air Canada Centre tonight (416-870-8000), but, with apologies to Don Henley, you're much better off seeing Peaches - - the abrasive, sometimes bearded Canadian vixen of electroclash and rap. Not for the faint of heart. But then again, those with heart conditions should probably avoid the credit card bills that result from a night with the Eagles. Friday and Saturday, the critically revered Constantines play the Horseshoe (416-598-4226). But, it should be noted, future child star drug casualties Hanson play the Guvernment on Friday, while surf rock refugees Smash Mouth play the same venue Saturday. On second thought, maybe that shouldn't be noted. Forget we even mentioned it. Aaron Wherry

FILM

LATIN FILM FESTIVAL

Perhaps a month after the Toronto International Film Festival, take comfort in the little festivals -- the kind where you show up and buy a ticket at the door; so civilized -- that crop up in this film-loving town almost every weekend. From tomorrow to Tuesday the 14th, the Royal Cinema hosts the Toronto International Latin Film Festival. Includes Spanish, Portuguese, French and Italian language films on Latin culture, and the vastness of the mandate makes for some intriguing programming. Of note: About Bunuel, a Spanish/ Mexican/ French documentary on Luis Bunuel; Three Nights of a Saturday, a black comedy set in Santiago during a pivotal soccer match; and Condor Crux: The Legend, the first full-length computer animated film to come out of Argentina. For scheduling, check www.tilff.com. Royal Cinema, 608 College St. Ticketing: 416-364- 3131. Katrina Onstad

THEATRE

SHOWS TO CATCH AT SHAW

Some of this year's best shows at the Shaw Festival have now closed, but much remains. Misalliance (Festival Theatre) is a Shavian talkfest that here survives partly because, and partly in spite, of its director's succession of bright ideas. The Royal Family (Festival) is one of those vintage U.S. farce-comedies, awash with characters, that the Shaw does so well and so often. On the Twentieth Century (Royal George) is set on a luxury train. As a composition, a sublimely tongue-in-cheek comic opera, it's much the finer of the Shaw's two musicals, though Happy End (Royal George) is more easily within the company's grasp. The Festival's best all- round production, though, is of its most modest piece: Blood Relations (Royal George), Sharon Pollock's psychodrama, in which Lizzie Borden, egged on by an actress friend, re-enacts the crime that made her famous. Robert Cushman

Factory Theatre opens its season with Hiro Kanagawa's Tiger of Malaya, based on the Manila War Crimes Trial of General Tomoyuki Yamashita. Runs in previews to Oct. 15, regular run Oct. 16 to Nov. 9. $12-$34. 125 Bathurst St. 416-504-9971.

HELEN'S NECKLACE

Tarragon presents the premiere of Carole Frechette's Helen's Necklace. Starring Susan Coyne and Sanjay Talwar. Helen goes on a search for a lost necklace through a hot, chaotic Middle Eastern city. Previews to Sunday at 8 p.m. ($15-$16), regular run from Oct. 14 ($15-$27). Tarragon Extra Space, 30 Bridgman Ave. 416-536-5018.
