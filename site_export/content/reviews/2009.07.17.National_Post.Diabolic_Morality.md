This year, Shaw at the Shaw means history plays. The festival has two of them in the bill, from opposite ends of the Shavian canon and in totally different modes. From near the end of his playwriting career we have In Good King Charles's Golden Days, which is as discursive as a play can get without falling apart, and from near the beginning we have The Devil's Disciple, which may be the most plot-driven thing he ever wrote. It is in fact an unabashed melodrama, and a good one.

It's not quite as unabashed as all that. Shaw rides the conventions pretty hard; a ne'er-do-well son and a pious mother, surprise revelations at the reading of a will, a wrongful arrest and a man nobly taking another's place on the scaffold, a woman torn between love and loyalty and her unsureness of which is which, a court martial and a last-minute rescue, all yield their traditional quotas of suspense and satisfaction. His setting is the U. S. war of independence, and his hero, the splendidly named Dick Dudgeon, is a serious rebel with a political agenda; he doesn't, unlike his possible inspiration Sydney Carton from A Tale of Two Cities, court martyrdom for purely personal reasons, though his rival's wife would like to think he does.

Dick is taking tea with the young and respectable Mrs. Judith Anderson, when the British army drops by to arrest her middle-aged and even more respectable husband, the local minister; they want, as Dick himself had foreseen, to make an example of the solidest citizen available. Dick lets himself be taken by mistake because, he says, he couldn't bring himself to take his head out of a noose and put another man's in. It's a question of nature rather than moral code. Nevertheless Dick has a code, or says he does. He goes on at great length in the first act about being "the devil's disciple," in opposition to the Christian code espoused by his mother, a puritanical monster whose first and only command is joylessness. Still, however much this production's program note (by the redoubtable Ann Saddlemyer) goes on about Shaw's "attraction to the diabolonian" and his debt to Bunyan and Blake, it's hard to see young Dick as more than the conventional good-hearted scapegrace. And, in fact, we hear less and less of his diabolic principles as the story goes on.

The play's last scene builds up to an execution; Tadeusz Bradecki's production begins with one, unscripted but much spoken of later. The officiating soldiers then step out of the frame, to speak some of Shaw's stage directions and to offer their own commentary on how refugees from the play's New Hampshire came to settle in the very Niagara-on-the-Lake in which we are sitting. Peter Hartwell's set, in which gallowstrees rear up behind the script's timbered interiors, provides another kind of frame. Within it is good storytelling, and more emotional and pyschological interest than you might expect. Much of the latter is due to Fiona Byrne's very fine performance of Judith; her well-bred detestation of the reprobate Dick is strong as well as demure, with no hint of caricature. Evan Buliung is Dick, playing with the dash and swagger and breezy common sense necessary to drive both the play and the character along, though sometimes slowing them with unnecessary vocal and physical curlicues. Peter Krantz gives a decent account of the minister with the soul of a soldier, though he lacks both weight and age. The end in which the two men exchange vocations doesn't convince, but then it never does; the play hasn't made us believe much in either in the first place.

We do believe, with a couple of lapses, in Donna Belleville's implacable self-pity as Mrs. Low Dudgeon, and are taken by surprise--at least I was -- by Jim Mezon as the British General Burgoyne, a Shavian fellow spirit who accordingly gets more good lines even than the devil, let alone his disciple. He may have the best set of sardonic one-liners in dramatic literature; Mezon goes beyond them, indeed even sacrifices a few laughs, to create a desperately world-weary man behind the mask.

Bradecki, a regular visitor from Poland, is one of the Shaw Festival's most imaginative regular directors. I have sadly to mark the passing of another, this one a resident. Neil Munro, who died on Monday, directed Man and Superman, The Petrified Forest, You Can't Take It with You, The Circle, Rosmersholm and the complete works of Granville Barker -- a long line of the best Shaw shows I remember. He was also responsible for a few of the worst, but the two things go together. He had an exceptional command of visual imagery always keyed to the play in hand, or at least to his conception of it, but he never sacrificed ating to environment. He was a rare bird, a loss not just to the Shaw but to the entire Canadian theatre. - The Devil's Disciple is in repertory through October.
