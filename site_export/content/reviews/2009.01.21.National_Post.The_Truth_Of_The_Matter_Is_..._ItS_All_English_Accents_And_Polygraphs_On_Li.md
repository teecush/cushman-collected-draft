I'm not gonna lie to you. Lie to Me isnt good. It isnt all that bad either: just standard-issue. The pilot, which is all I have to go on, is quintessentially pilot in the matters of getting its regular characters together, displaying their quirks, hinting at possible future interactions and giving them a couple of specimen plotlines in which to show their paces. Plots in which we can't get invested because we know we'll never see them again.

Its a new wrinkle on the detective-show formula. Wrinkle" really is the appropriate word here, since the central character, the apparently British Dr. Cal Lightman (Tim Roth) believes, in direct contradiction of Shakespeares King Duncan, that there is an art to finding the mind's construction in the face, and that he's got it. Hes a human polygraph, able to spot a liar by reading the minutest details of their face and body language. He seems infallible at this but, just in case, he has a support team of three: Dr. Gillian Lightman (Kelli Williams), a psychologist loaded with the human warmth that the brilliant Cal conspicuously lacks; Eli Loker (Brendan Hines), a young man whose name practically screams "TV series and who, we are told, never lies himself; and Ria Torres (Monica Raymund), whom we actually see being recruited. Shes an airport security officer whose prowess at spotting suspicious passengers so impresses Cal and Gillian that they hire her on the spot. She even spots them, though frankly they are acting so exaggeratedly suspicious that even I might have tagged them. Have you ever," she's asked, "had any specialized deception training? To which she smartly replies, I've dated a lot of men." There, Ive spoiled the one good line for you. But its worth catching her delivery of it.

We start with Cal himself interrogating a domestic terrorist who refuses to talk. "That's all right, says the doc, nonchalantly letting us in on his personal credo and the show's. I dont have much faith in words. The average person tells three lies in every 10 minutes worth of conversation." Its riveting stuff, but it turns out not to be the beginning of a storyline, but part of a lecture he's giving to the law-enforcement troops. Having no access to the body language at the show's story conference, I cant say whether this is a blatant imitation of the opening sequence of The Lives of Others or just a coincidence. Though I doubt that Cal believes much in coincidence.

He and his colleagues and their expertise are for hire to any law enforcement agency that needs them, though as always in these shows the need is expressed with suspicion verging on contempt. (One FBI guy dismisses them as a carnival show.) The genius team do have an inconvenient habit of suspecting the innocence of those whom the authorities have already determined to be guilty. Their lesser case tonight concerns a U. S. senator suspected of consorting with prostitutes, or at least a prostitute, and surprisingly reluctant to try and worm his way out of it. Most time, though, goes to a male high schooler apprehended at a murder scene, the victim being a lady teacher. The boy comes from a family of Jehovahs Witnesses, and it falls to the two doctors to interview his parents. One looks forward to seeing them questioned in their own home instead of doing it themselves on the doorstep of somebody else's, but no subtle revelations are in store. "Get out of my house, yells the father, when the question is raised of his responsibility for his son's thwarted sexuality. With his nostrils flaring and his fists clenched, he seems rather a waste of Dr. Cals subtle skills. Meanwhile the music swells, to relieve the actors of most of the burden of acting.

Not that they dont do their best, under what must be considerable pressure. They have to live, after all, with the knowledge that the camera is scrutinizing their minutest changes of expression on our behalf. Among the comparatively innocent, I was delighted to see Robert Wisdom, the great Bunny Colvin of The Wire and good, if miscast, as the jail boss in Prison Break's unfortunate Panamanian detour. He's back on the side of the law this time, with one scene as a helpful desk sergeant. Hell presumably reappear, but even that possibility isnt enough to counteract the deadening feeling that we're in for the same bunch of people going through the same predictable moves every week, even while seeming to start from scratch. They may be thwarted on the odd case -- this is 2009, after all -- but even that won't change the predictability of it all. None of them have yet displayed any compelling personal characteristics. Cal, who is divorced with a teenage daughter, is being built up as a misanthrope: I recommend happiness to you," says Gillian, his girl Friday. But that only makes the show seem like the police procedurals answer to House, with Roth the new Hugh Laurie. And at least Laurie gets to show off an American accent.
