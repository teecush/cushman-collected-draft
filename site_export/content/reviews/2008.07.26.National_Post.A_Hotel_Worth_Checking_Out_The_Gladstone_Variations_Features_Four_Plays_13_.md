I don't want to confuse anyone. The Gladstone Variations is a collection of four short plays that are given on-site productions at the Gladstone Hotel on Queen West. We will call them for the moment Plays A, B, C and D. A and B make up a double bill, C and D another. Both bills are performed simultaneously during each night of the run. That's not all; both are performed twice. You may see Play A and then move on to Play B, or you may do it the other way around. You may return the next evening and face the same options with Plays C and D. Or you may see C and D first. Myself, I saw them in strict alphabetical order, as arranged by the management who knew what was best for me.

When you see the plays, you get to do some walking. They take place in rooms, in lobbies and on landings all over the hotel and also on the sidewalk outside. The kicker is that of the 13 characters on board (or is it 14? -- one of the actors may be doubling), six (or is it seven?) appear in more than one play. And even apart from that, the action in one play sometimes impinges on that in another, with some of the evenings' impact depending on that fact. The amount of effort and ingenuity required to get all this synchronized is awe-inspiring.

As an event, The Gladstone Variations unquestionably succeeds. As for the individual plays: they vary. They all play off the implicit contrast between the posh watering-hole the Gladstone now is, and the dodgy hostel it used to be. The protagonists tend to be ghosts from the past who return to haunt the present. The obvious danger here is preciousness. Brendan Gall's The Card Trick (Play C), directed by Alan Dilworth, avoids it best. It has the advantage of a stunningly immediate performance by Richard McMillan as a bloodied down-and-out, desperately searching -- for reasons that are made somewhat but not entirely clear --for a lost little girl. (We get to see her, or at least some little girl, staring unavailingly through plate-glass windows. She's in one of the other plays, too.) McMillan starts the play lying outstretched in front of the hotel, a position from which he is evicted by a highly hostile bellboy. But he makes his way inside, and we follow him, up and down and even into a bathroom; of all the four plays, this is the one that makes the most extensive and ingenious use of the hotel's facilities. McMillan's character is no patsy: intellectually, if in no other way, he's smart. But he's also outnumbered and overpowered by staff and circumstances and what seems to be his own crushing sense of guilt. You can stand a few feet away from McMillan and see the real unblinking face of agony. And he can stand a few feet away from you and never lose it.

Richard Greenblatt, who ranks next to McMillan as the most prestigious actor on this particular block, also starts out supine on the sidewalk. He appears in Rick Roberts' The Tearful Bride (Play A), directed by Aaron Willis, as a concierge. When he gets up, he leads us into a bedroom where he tells us the story of a bride whose groom failed to show on their wedding night, and of an attendant bellhop who turned out to fit perfectly into his attendant tuxedo. These two roles of course acted out before our eyes (Marc Bendavid being nicely nervous as the help), though it's Greenblatt who does most of the talking. It's pretty clear that this is his memory of an early scene from his own career, a tale of broken dreams covered over with a professional gloss that has now become habitual. Partly because of this distancing narrative device, partly because it is for the most part geographically static, the play doesn't fit too well with its companions. It's like a New Yorker story thrust among pieces from The Walrus. But Greenblatt's performance makes a fascinating contrast with McMillan's: a study in how to keep your audience at emotional arm's length even while standing on top of them. It is in its indirect way almost as affecting.

Mike McPhaden's Requiem for a Hotel (Play B), directed by Ruth Madoc-Jones, gives us Janet Amos as a ginger-haired bag lady in a Tweetie-Pie T-shirt whom I momentarily took, when she approached us as we lined up for admission outside the hotel wall, for the real thing. The reality-quotient increased as we trailed her inside and watched her battle with the ATM in the lobby. (Others watched too, and watched us watching her. That's part of the fun: the rubbing-up of official spectators and unofficial, each group seeming to the other like parts of a show. This isn't about blurring distinctions. It's about accentuating them.) She's here for karaoke night, at which she always sings an Elvis song. Guess which one she chooses. I thought this item excessively fey, and I thought the same, only more so, about Julie Tepperman's I Grow Old (Play D), directed by Rebecca Benson, in which a man who, like the title, delights in quoting "Prufrock," comes to the hotel to lay some ghosts and to add to their number. One up for reality, though: At one point a social worker called to his aid finds that her car has been towed from the supermarket lot next door; I'd left mine there too, and felt panicked.

The staff includes Sanjay Talwar as a second, ambiguously courteous concierge, and Dov Mickelson as a second bellhop with skills to learn, being as he's discourteously sardonic in one play and sadistic in another. The sum is more than the parts: The hotel takes on a theatrical identity of its own. The show is a hit from the Fringe, produced by Tepperman and Willis for Convergence Theatre, which for these circumstances could hardly be more aptly named. - The Gladstone Variations runs at the Gladstone Hotel through Aug 3.
