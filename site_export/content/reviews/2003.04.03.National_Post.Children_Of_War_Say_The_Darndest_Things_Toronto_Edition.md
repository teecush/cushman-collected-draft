THE NOTEBOOK

duMaurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

Toronto's WorldStage, which without its duMaurier sponsorship is in imminent danger of being puffed out, seems at least to be going out in style. The fine opening production of its maybe-last season hails from Belgium, though of its four actors, one is Polish and another -- the only woman -- is of Scots parentage, and sounds it. It is based on a novel, also Polish, and is performed, at least for this gig, in English. The cast are responsible for the adaptation and they must also have directed themselves, since no one else is credited as having done it. The spare, stylish and disciplined show they have come up with suggests directors may be a dispensable species.

The story is set in an unnamed country during an unnamed war. It's clear, though, that the author, Agota Kristof, was writing about her own Poland and that the war is the Second World one. We have occupiers who are Germans, supposed liberators who are Russians (and whose first action after conquest is to hang up pictures of their leader and forbid all criticism) and a brutally abused minority who are all too clearly Jews. At the centre of the play are twin boys, whose mother is apparently off to do a spot of collaborating, and who wants to leave them in a border town in the care of her own mother, known locally as the Witch. The grandmother's response to her daughter's request is typically barbed ("For 10 years you didn't give me a thought. You never came, you never wrote") but she takes the boys in, threatening to "put them to work." What she actually does is neglect them, making them sleep on a bench in the kitchen, barely feeding or clothing them, and leaving them unwashed. They don't seem to mind.

In fact, they become chillingly self-reliant. They toughen themselves by exchanging blows and insults; this imperviousness to physical or mental pain comes in very useful later. They also become their own home-schoolers, with the help of a Bible and a dictionary. These endow them with a formality of diction (they scour the dictionary for "synonyms and antonyms") that the neighbouring adults find deeply disconcerting. They explore the forest. They become adepts of the black market, and supplement their income by performing conjuring tricks in cafes. They acquire a kind of sex education, supplied in various ways by the handicapped girl next door, by the housekeeper of the local priest and her soldier-lover, and by the occupying officer who's been billeted with their grandma. They observe the Jews being marched out of town to their deaths. They learn the techniques and the dangers of crossing the national frontier. They write down all their experiences and observations in a notebook, and their narrative gives the play its frame.

They are, to put it mildly, dispassionate. They do seem capable of what might be called kindness. For example, having discovered that the priest has been touching up their young neighbour, they ask him for money in order that they may pass it on to the girl and her ailing mother. ("Have you any idea what you're doing?" he asks. "Yes, sir," they reply. "We're blackmailing you.") They do not seem capable of love, hate and fear. They come to prefer the bracing indifference of their grandmother to the clinging affection of their mother. The extent of both their practicality and their amorality is revealed when they finally have dealings with their father, formerly a POW, now a refugee.

Presumably the twins are meant as studies in the distorting effects of war on childhood. There could hardly be a timelier subject, and it certainly resonates. We recognize the spectacle of children old before their time but immature forever. It's ambiguous, though. These are very special cases, or -- since they are psychologically if not physically identical -- one special case times two. Not every evacuee can have undergone experiences so consistently brutish, or have reacted by going to such extremes of self-regimentation. At times we seem to be witnessing the convergence of two recognizable Central European traditions. One is the fable of the wild child in a hostile landscape. The other is satire of a rigid educational system that's all facts and no feelings. It's a rich mix, but the more poetically compelling the piece becomes, the less you feel like generalizing from it.

By "poetically compelling" I don't just mean that it's written in verse, or at least in brief, unmodulated sentences that are laid out in a line apiece. This does give it a hard, blow-of-the-fist sort of quality, of the kind that's to be expected when inhuman events are relayed in the levellest of tones. But the impact resides more in the visual style, which is swift and almost totally unencumbered by furniture, and in the sense that the actors, having worked up the text and the staging themselves, are making you a present of the results.

As the brothers, Gunther Lesage and Robby Cleiren go through the entire play in shorts and singlets, and with visible bulges in their pants; they are implacable in their innocence. All the other roles are shared, with a fine disregard of gender, between Ryszard Turbiasz, whose thick voice, ranging between a roar and a whine, hits an especially happy medium as the wavering priest, and Carly Wijs, the Scots lady, who when playing the predatory maid adds a dash of sensuality, even of warmth, though the latter quality is turned abruptly off.

There is a fair amount of humour in the play, not quite all of it black. The Notebook, though more or less complete in itself, is only the first half of the story. The second is called The Proof and is being given at alternating performances. I look forward to catching up with it, since the present instalment has left me asking a bunch of questions, of which the simplest -- though by no means the least urgent -- is, what happened next?
