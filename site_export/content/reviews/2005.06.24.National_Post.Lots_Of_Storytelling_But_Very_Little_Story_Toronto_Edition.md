THE DONNELLYS: STICKS AND STONES

Studio Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Diane D'Aquila, leading actress in Stratford's Sticks and Stones, made a charming curtain speech on opening night. She recalled the 1970s, when Canadian drama was beginning to wake up and new plays were tumbling on to stages. Many of them, she disarmingly admitted, were not very good, but she was obviously making an exception for James Reaney, author of the trilogy The Donnellys, of which Sticks and Stones (premiered 1973) is the first instalment. Reaney, she said, had been one of those "leading the charge"; and he was present, now an octogenarian, to receive an obviously heartfelt tribute.

After which, it seems churlish to report that the play now looks like something that was probably very exciting if you were around at the time. It takes the familiar Canadian trope about telling ourselves our own stories to wild extremes; it's so concerned with modes of storytelling that the story itself never gets told. It takes place in the township of Biddulph, not that far from Stratford. Its climax and raison d'etre is the murder-by- incineration of James Donnelly, an Irish-born farmer, and four members of his family. This happened in 1880, but the play has a fluid time scheme that allows the event to be echoed, pre-echoed, forecast, remembered, described and even lampooned. The one thing it doesn't get is dramatized.

This is partly because the author still has two-thirds of his trilogy to go, and he's saving it up; but it's still frustrating. (And, given the free conventions he employs, there'd be nothing to stop him from staging it twice.) Frustrating, too, is the way in which the play piles on background material so that it obscures rather than illuminates. Back in Catholic Ireland, Donnelly had been a "whitefoot," meaning someone who refused to run with the right mob. This label followed him to Canada, and it may have been his murderers' principal motivation. But there are plenty of other conflicts to choose from. As well as Catholics against Catholics, we have Catholics against Protestants, Irish against English, rich against poor, white against black and, for a fleeting couple of sentences, Grits against Tories, Donnelly being a Liberal voter in a region ruled by Conservatives. Donnelly also arouses the jealousy of any number of land-grabbers, and is undeniably bloody-minded on his own account. He kills a man in a brawl, and spends seven years in jail -- one year, as it happens, for each of his children.

As well as being drunk on its own history, the play is drunk on its own style. Back in '73 a professor declared Reaney a better dramatic poet than Eliot or Yeats; that's doubtful, but the text, which is in prose, does contain some pungent sentences, along with chunks of exposition naive enough for a school play. It's folkweave drama in excelsis, with games, fights, dances and even a traditional theme tune, the powerful ballad of John Barleycorn, that has to be manhandled into relevance. Characters switch from playing scenes to delivering first- or third-person narration, and the company keeps forming itself into a chanting chorus. It's very uninvolving, and also very confusing; I kept longing for the actors to thin out a bit, so that those remaining might get a chance to interact, or at least to clue me in on who they were supposed to be. The Donnellys' enemies may have been varied and many, but here they seem interchangeable, like so many snarky shadows.

To succeed now, when we are no longer overawed by Canadian content for its own sake, the play would need ruthlessly lucid acting and direction. Andrey Tarasiuk's production gets lost in the thickets so liberally planted by the author; it looks and sounds as if getting the show onstage had left no time for getting it clear. People start shouting before we can tell what they're shouting about, and they hardly ever stop. Some of the younger actors are lamentable. Occasionally there's a good barbed scene; the best involves an itinerant priest from Kentucky (David Snelgrove, recycling his Cat on a Hot Tin Roof voice) refusing absolution to a suborner of killers (Andrew Massingham) who wants to confess without actually admitting any guilt. Reaney sees Donnelly himself as a figure of tragic intransigence; Robert King cannot make him anything more than a stocky malcontent.

The play's final flaw may be its most crucial: Reaney seems less interested in portraying the Donnellys than in vindicating them. He was apparently roused to write the play by reading a book that takes a far blacker view of his protagonists than he does (and is in fact called The Black Donnellys). He seems, from the noblest of motives, to be fighting a literary battle; he even puts his opponent into the play, in the guise of a travelling medicine showman who mounts a caricatured version of the Donnelly case while the real Mr. Donnelly looks on. This, if you don't know the background, is baffling. It's puzzling enough even if you do. We're offered a parody of something that we never got straight in the first place. There's also a scene involving the Governor-General and his wife that plays like Video Cabaret without the finesse. Actually I'd like to see the VideoCab gang get their hooks into this stretch of Canadiana. And, having a weakness for epics, I'd still be interested to see the remaining two- thirds of this one. As pages of theatrical as well as national history, they deserve respect. But I'm not convinced that they deserve reverence.
