THE FULL MONTY

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

- - -

I doubt if I am revealing anything when I say that The Full Monty ends with six guys up on stage revealing everything. As has been the way with musicals, all the way back to Hair, the show goes coy at the moment of disclosure, which is not so much capped by a blackout as synchronized with it. There are, however, much odder things about this musicals closing number than that.

The strippers, as everybody will remember who saw the English film on which this American show is based, are new at the game: five blue-collar workers and one white or, counting another way, five youngish white guys and one oldish black. They have all been thrown out of work by a factory closure in their impoverished home town of Buffalo or, as it was pronounced in the movie, "Sheffield."

To raise some cash and revive their morale, they get up this stripper act. Comes the big night, comes the big number, and they start it with a song. Now, we have spent most of the evening watching them rehearsing , or just getting up their nerve, and not only have we not heard them practise a note, the prospect of their singing has never even been mentioned.

And another thing: Toward the end of the number, a great light shines. It proves to be the shows title, spelled out in giant neon letters, one word to a line. It looks hideous. It also looks expensive. So what's it doing there? The whole point of the story is that these men are broke. They have just about scraped together the money to hire the hall -- how could they afford all that glitz?

Or is it just that this is a musical, so it doesn't have to make sense?

The Full Monty, as you may have guessed, is a crass and charmless reduction of a funny and touching original. One of the worst things about it is the standard of performance in that closing number. Its too good. These guys are meant to be amateurs. Thats why we should be rooting for them.

A Jerome Robbins or a Michael Bennett might conceivably have devised a routine that would work as part of a musical while still keeping faith with the situation, but Jerry Mitchell, the choreographer here, has simply gone for flash. It isn't all bad flash; there's an audition number in the first act called Big Black Man that legitimately tears the place up, and its capped by another in which the whole gang loosen up their moves, macho-style, by thinking of Michael Jordan. But even that latter one has the feel of something thrown in to get us to intermission. It doesnt go anywhere.

It's impossible, anyway, to enjoy the show as just a series of mindless diversions, because it's trying to tug your heartstrings at the same time. This, however, is where it really degrades its source.

Part of the trouble may be the change of locale. Its not, God knows, that depression, economic and psychological, cant also strike in the United States (though John Arnone's elaborately skeletal set looks little like Buffalo, or like anywhere else). It's that the underlying assumption that an American audience couldnt possibly understand or sympathize with people from Englands post- industrial North is insulting to both parties, and this patronizing attitude seeps through the entire show. It's the same mindset that turned Fawlty Towers into the aptly-named Royal Payne, insisting on the way that Basil and Sybil were really terribly fond of one another.

Here, straightening out the characters' kinks and quirks , ostensibly making them more sympathetic, actually means making them less interesting and far less emotionally involving. Watching the film, one cared about the relationship between the tearaway young leader of the troupe and the son of his broken marriage. Here the boy is just another cute theatrical kid. And it doesnt help when his dad (Rod Weber, too bland for the dramatic weight he has to carry) starts crooning a lullaby at him.

This is one of a clutch of sentimental numbers that back up, one after another, at the end of David Yazbeks score. Its earlier sections are livelier. The opening ensemble, Scrap, a hymn of unemployment, captures a strong, angry mood that never recurs, and there are sharp observations, neat rhymes, and even a couple of genuine laughs in what follows.

Yazbek's tunes are less striking than his lyrics, but they are inoffensive until the home stretch when, for example, a song called You Rule My World, previously sung by a fat man to his beer belly, is reprised as a turgidly conventional soft-rock ballad. The composer has complained in interviews about the prevalence of musical-theatre scores that beg for cheap applause; he's right, but if he and his colleagues think that they have risen above all that, they should get the neon beams out of their eyes.

This show goes for every obvious effect it can manage. Terrence McNallys book dogs the footsteps of the movie, dulling or coarsening its impact at every turn. A sad-funny scene about an ineffectual suicide becomes forced, unfunny farce. The scene in which furniture repossessors are routed by the boys twanging their G- strings is still there; so is the abrupt termination of a rehearsal by a police raid. But neither scene has any force, party because of Jack OBrien's ineffectual staging, partly because the characters concerned no longer mean anything.

As for the scene in which our heroes break uncontrollably into their dance steps while standing in the dole queue -- the one great sequence that everyone remembers, the one that would seem to have real musical potential -- it's been transferred to the presumably less depressing setting of a funeral, where it goes for almost nothing. This may be because the scene is attempting to kill three birds with one stone; it also functions as a gay romance, and as a requiem for a dear departed whom we met, a whole act earlier, for all of two minutes.

Larry Marshalls big black man is energizing, and Daniel Stewart Shermans fat man is an appealing stereotype. Steven Skybell flaps competently about as the ex-manager living in fear of the bailiffs, though his is the most diminished character of all -- a supposed graduate of Wharton, one of America's top two business schools, but one who, if my resident management consultant is to be trusted, is seriously confused about the distinction between niche marketing and competitive edge, applying the former term to the concept of going the full monty when he really means the latter.

(Isn't it strange how glibly that phrase full monty now slides off all our tongues? Before the film came along, none of us had ever heard it. Im not even sure on which side of the pond it originated.)

Gay-friendly and feminist (or at least male-objectifying) though the show professes to be, all its women, save one, are shrieking caricatures, and ineptly played at that. The exception, and this productions nearest thing to a saving grace, is the veteran singer- comedien Kaye Ballard, who plays an elderly piano-lady come out of retirement to accompany the boys in their hours of need and glory. She regards them with a permanent gleaming smile, suggesting both great kindness and utter bewilderment -- personal, that is; professionally, she's almost unfazeable, though sometimes her resource -- "I can't play any slower," she complains of one hesitant strutter -- seems about to give out.

As a link with actual American show business, the character may have been written in to give the musical a link with something it knows about. Anyway, the actress and the script serve one another perfectly; every line is funny. To be honest, she seems to be acting in a world of her own. But it's a better world.
