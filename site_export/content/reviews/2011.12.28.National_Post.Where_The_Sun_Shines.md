THEATRE REVIEW

Quidam

Ricoh Coliseum, Toronto

Cirque du Soleil's Quidam is one of its earlier productions, dating back 15 years or so. It's been revived for a tour, whose Toronto date is the Ricoh Coliseum, an arena that proves unexpectedly cozier than the very big top under which the Circus of the Sun usually shines.

According to a program that's short on information though long on pictures and even longer on price, Quidam means "a nameless passerby, a solitary figure - a person who lives lost among the crowd in all-too-anonymous society." I'm not sure whether that makes him a good or a bad thing.

Anyway, in this show, if he's the character I think he is, he's not only nameless but faceless, indeed headless; between his hat and his coat there's nothing but empty space: not perhaps the show's most spectacular effect but certainly a striking one. He rescues a young girl who's being ignored by her conventionally conventional parents, and leads her, to nobody's surprise, into a wonderland of acrobats, jugglers and clowns.

They, too, are anonymous, unless you're prepared to grapple with some very small print, but they're certainly colourful and, as usual at Cirque, their accomplishments can be incredible. They have skills that most people don't, though I find the implication that this makes them superior beings - superior at least to the heroine's mom and dad - very suspect. Chances are that when they take off the makeup and go home, their lives are as boring as anyone else's.

Circuses are formulaic, almost by definition, and I've never found Cirque du Soleil's attempts to elevate its own brand by applying some kind of mystical narrative thread to be very persuasive. It isn't here, but at least the Quidam story is simpler than most, and the title figure does make a magical reappearance to round things off at the end of the show, though for most of the time it's not just his head that's been invisible.

Quidam has other special attractions, both sonic and scenic. The music in Cirque shows is always good, but here it buoys the spectacle up to an exceptional, even hypnotic, extent. Everything that happens happens in rhythm. Meanwhile, the set features five girders just below the roof, each bending in an arc, and each equipped with a mobile block than can move toward or away from the audience. From each block there hangs a wire, and from each wire there can hang a performer. A quintet of them, with attendants, are as graceful as they are death-defying.

Jugglers toss vessels from one to another, at increasingly impossible angles and velocities. (Cirque, always marketconscious, sells miniature versions of these missiles at intermission. Patrons probably shouldn't try them at home.) An exceptionally beautiful act, devoid of props and trappings, consists of two bodies, one male and one female, supporting and balancing one another at increasingly improbable angles. Other, less rarefied performers skip rope, or roll and spin within what is apparently known as a German wheel. A late act takes apache or adagio dancing - the kind in which a woman gets flung around from one man to another - to surreal heights, with the emphasis on the noun: A small girl, though she might conceivably be a small woman, is thrown, with nonchalant expertise, into the arms of a man who has two other men in vertical formation beneath him.

All this, though breathtaking, is the kind of thing you expect from Cirque. More of a surprise is a clown who's actually funny, one who specializ-es in creative audience participation. In the first half, he inveigles a female customer into an imaginary car and makes more or less imaginary advances. In the second, he gets four punters to go through the motions of making a silent movie, the kind in which a pair of lovebirds are surprised by the lady's husband. To these three impromptu actors, he adds an impromptu clapper-boy.

The comic himself is ceaselessly inventive, while the draftees prove themselves (at least when I went) to be more than just good sports. They don't just enter into the spirit of the thing; they get possessed by it. Maybe this counts as an extension of the main theme; the clown draws civilians into an unexpected realm, like an earthier and more enjoyable version of the mysterious Quidam himself.

Quidam runs at Toronto's Ricoh Coliseum until Dec. 30 before moving on to performances in Oshawa and Windsor in January. For tickets, visit cirquedusoleil.com.
