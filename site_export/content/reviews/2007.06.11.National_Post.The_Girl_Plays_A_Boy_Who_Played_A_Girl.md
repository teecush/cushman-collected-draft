THE BLONDE, THE BRUNETTE AND THE VENGEFUL REDHEAD

Studio Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

---

If there were to be an award for the year's best male performance at the Stratford Festival, it might well go, despite what is already serious competition, to Lucy Peacock.

In The Blonde, the Brunette and the Vengeful Redhead (revived from last season) Peacock plays seven characters, but the most memorable may be the one man among them. He's certainly the most unsparingly drawn, both by the actress and her Australian author Robert Hewett. He's Graham, the husband of Rhonda, the redhead of the title, whose vengeance was directed at the blond of the title, with whom she suspected Graham of having an affair, on the advice of their neighbour, the brunette, also of the title. The redhead ended up in jail and Graham promptly started a new affair with the brunette.

So he has, really, a lot to feel sorry about.

Most of his sympathy, though, is directed at himself. We first see him (from the back) relieving himself, and he continues to relieve himself, verbally. He has, we gather, been very hard done by, and everything is somebody else's fault, mostly some woman's. He does admit to a few twinges of guilt around his old mate, the brunette's husband. (No more neighbourly barbecues for them.) If it took another man to understand this guy so embarrassingly well, it may well have taken a woman to give him so clear and witty an outline. That's not to say that Peacock ever mocks or patronizes Graham. She plays him with the same sympathy and conviction that she extends to the five women and one child who make up the rest of her portrait gallery. But the circumstances of a one-person show almost dictate a certain amount of detachment, for both actor and audience. Peacock is as fully inside each of her characters as any actor can ever be. She also has an individual voice and stance for each of them. But each comes on stage bearing an implicit weight of comparison with the others.

Hewett takes a single traumatic incident, at a shopping mall, and examines a selection of those it affects: a catalogue, we come to realize, that could be extended almost indefinitely.

Rhonda is the only one we meet twice; she's quiet and confused at the start, quiet and steely at the end. There's a capable English doctor, aware that her colleagues refer to her as "that Pommy bitch" (a strange usage, given that the scene has been transplanted from Australia to Canada). There's a bereaved boy, played more precisely and also more movingly than last year. There's the blowsy brunette, whose story differs significantly from Rhonda's even when they're supposedly describing the same events. There's an elderly woman with reserves of compassion that are genuine but limited.

And there's the blond mistress, who's Russian and a surprise. Peacock has lapsed into excessive underlining in the first couple of scenes, but otherwise she remains perfect. The writing takes a sentimental dip at the end, but it's full of believable observations and incorporates one moment of classically packed irony that radiates in memory across the entire play. Geordie John-son's direction is spot-on, both during the scenes and between them. As Peacock changes costumes in silhouette and, more significantly, wigs, we listen to Stephen Woodjetts' musical score, which is like water dropping, and look at projections that suggest that malls, in any country, are accidents that cannot wait to happen. - The Blonde, the Brunette and the Vengeful Redhead is in repertoire until Sept. 2.
