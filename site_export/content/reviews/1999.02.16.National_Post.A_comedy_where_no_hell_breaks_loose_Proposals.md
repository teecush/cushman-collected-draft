Neil Simon's latest play has nine characters, all of them in love to some degree, and by its close it has arranged them into four couples. Simple arithmetic decrees that somebody must be left out; and even apart from that, Simon's Proposals come in for a few rebuffs. This, in intention at least, is a mellow comedy about time and attrition; about grabbing things while you're young and maybe -- though there's no guarantee -- recapturing them when you're old.

Its values are unchallengeably decent, but it plays as soap opera (of the literate Dawson's Creek variety) with a couple of funny scenes. It's the late 1940s, and we are in Pennsylvania woodland country where Burt Hines -- the central character, or at any rate the one played by Al Waxman -- has a summer home. Burt is a retail store magnate, forced into retirement by a weak heart. He lives here with his daughter Josie, and Clemma the black housekeeper.

Burt's divorced wife, Annie, now remarried, is paying a visit. So is Clemma's ex-husband, Lewis. So is Vinnie, an admirer of Josie's. Josie has two other young men in tow. One of them, Ken, is local; the other, Ray, used to be and is now returning with a new girlfriend, Sammii, who's a model. All these people turn up on the same day, and there is much jocular comment from Clemma about the strain their arrival will place on the local roads and on her catering arrangements. But that's just talk; in practice the logistics work with amazing smoothness. Which may be swell for the characters but is a let-down for the audience.

The framework is similar to that of Noel Coward's Hay Fever, another nine-hander in which all the members of a family simultaneously invite summer house-guests, but the tone could hardly be more different. In Coward's play all hell breaks loose over the arrangements, everyone changes partners, and the visitors depart battered and baffled while the hosts sail on, egotistically oblivious. Simon, beneath the surface crackle, is a Broadway softie who wants to redeem his characters and bring them together. He realizes that's not always possible; that, plus the country-house setting, give him something of a Chekhov flavour, but it's Chekhov drastically sentimentalized. A scene in which one character surprises two others kissing is like a replay of Uncle Vanya, minus most of the farce and all of the heartbreak.

This is also a memory play, narrated by Clemma: a device very popular in American plays of the '40s and '50s (The Glass Menagerie, Our Town, A View from the Bridge) and perhaps revived here as a homage to the period. There seems little other reason for it, except to add a gratuitous layer to a slight story. It does dispose of some technical matters; instead of dropping basic information tactfully into the dialogue, always a chore, the author has the maid ladle it out for him. (In the very old days, she would have done it in conversation with the butler; the retrenchments of the last 60 years have left playwrights with their own servant problem.)

When the play proper gets started, Burt reveals an obliging habit of overhearing everyone else's conversations from his window, thus cutting out pages more of exposition. In all, Simon's craftsmanship seems to have gone a bit lax. Characters wander on just as other characters have wandered off, with none of the messy overlap you get in real life. Clemma's announcement that Lewis is coming is met with "Lewis? Your ex-husband?" -- one of those everyday expressions that trip so naturally off the tongue.

There is one set-piece that reminds you how good Neil Simon can be. It's a verbal showdown between Kenny, the buttoned-up young lawyer grappling with being Josie's unsuccessful suitor, and Vinnie, whom he takes to be his rival and who looks, talks and dresses like a junior member of the Mafia. Vinnie gets words wrong, to Kenny's vengeful delight. It then turns out that -- some of the time at least -- Vinnie does his malapropping on purpose (to attract attention, he says) and Kenny is left hopping, though still compulsively punching. This is fine-layered comedy that builds.

Duncan Ollerenshaw's Ken is an entrancingly stiff-necked performance: not just class but class of its own. Nothing else in Steven Schipper's production matches it for humour or humanity, though Art Cohen's Vinnie works pretty well as caricature, and so in a small way does Lisa Ryder's dumb blonde turn as Sammii.

The Hines family too are stock types, with the unfortunate difference that the author wants us to take them seriously. Al Waxman's Burt -- Willy Loman plus success -- is low on energy, even for a man between heart attacks. The best to be said for him is that he goes at his own tempo, unlike his wife and daughter, who are imitation Broadway, all fake pauses and emotional semaphore. Jennifer Gould at least goes through her awakening-princess routine with spirit and even some glimmerings of intelligence; Maida Rogerson's mom is a wooden attempt at glitter. Alvin Sanders (Lewis) is unexceptionable, Graham Abbey (Ray) also unexceptionable but dull.

As for Clemma, who in all senses runs the show: Patricia Idlette is certainly a presence but if acting involves interacting, then she hardly does any; when she confronts her ex, she's so busy scoring points that she barely looks at him. She talks about the house as though it were the real subject of the play; it isn't, but, as designed by John Lee Beatty, it gets to show itself off handsomely from a variety of angles: dark wood slowly revolving in the greenwood. The same designer was responsible for the contrastingly stark but equally imposing set for Chicago just down the block, making him the scenic king of King Street.
