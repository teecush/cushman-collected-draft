AH, WILDERNESS!

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

According to a theory expounded by an American critic in the 1930s and much quoted ever since, the plays of Eugene O'Neill move us not because of their writing but because of the emotional cost to the author in setting them down. Ive never really bought this idea, or at least Ive never seen why it should apply to O'Neill more than to any other dramatist. But it does seem to work, in a kind of reverse gear, with Ah, Wilderness!

The first thing everybody notices about this play is that it is, unique in the O'Neill canon, a comedy, and a gentle one at that. Its a nostalgic piece, recalling comfortable family life in what the stage directions call "a large small-town, in 1904 Connecticut. Its a play many other American playwrights might have written, and several have. All such plays have a strong element of wish fulfillment, but O'Neill was unusually upfront about that element in his. The Miller household of Ah, Wilderness! represents, he said, the way I would have liked my boyhood to have been."

Even without that admission we would probably guess that Richard, the 16-year-old son of the house, with his moods and his shyness and his fondness for what his elders regard as subversive literature, was a stand-in for the author. We can also compare him to O'Neills later self-portrait as the tubercular Edmund Tyrone of Long Days Journey Into Night, with his tyrannical father, morphine-addicted mother and drunken older brother. You don't have to consult the biographies to know the grimmer version is much closer to the facts (even though it piles on the darkness with as much calculation as Ah, Wilderness! ladles out the light) Richard and Edmund have certain things in common, notably a fondness for reciting poetry; Swinburne is a favourite of both. And we follow each of them through a single rite-of-passage day; in Richard's case it happens to be the Fourth of July. Edmund and the Tyrones make us appreciate why ONeill had to will himself into a holiday mood and create Richard and the Millers. Its the implicit contrast that makes the comedy poignant as well as charming and funny.

It is both of those pleasant things in surprising measure. O'Neill turns out to be remarkably adept at writing big, festive dinner-table scenes, at mining the humour in family relationships and especially at turning people around to show off their lovable weaknesses. He could be a Gentile Neil Simon, though without the extractable wisecracks; the laugh-lines here (and there are many of them) come out of the situations. Joseph Ziegler's production is something of a twin to his lovely Soulpepper staging of Our Town, and it contains especially fine work from Norman Browning as Richards father, a newspaper editor torn between the firmness he thinks he owes his boy and the indulgence he is naturally disposed to grant him. The latter, of course, always wins out, but the actor makes every twist convincing. Even the familiar facts-of-life episode, in which father dutifully tries to instruct his son before realizing to his relief he doesnt have to, comes up fresh.

It isn't all idyllic. Nat's brother-in-law Sid is a dubiously reformed alcoholic whose weakness has torpedoed his long courtship of Nats sister. (One of the old songs that the play deploys to rich atmospheric effect is Waiting at the Church. It doesnt go down well.) Sid is a joker, but a sad one; William Vickers cleverly balances the jovial with the shame-faced. He must represent the O'Neill family drinking problem, slightly displaced. He is thus well placed to commiserate with his nephew on his first hangover. Richard has spurned the day's patriotic celebrations, as befits one who dreams of seeing Pierpont Morgan driven in a tumbril, and he feels, temporarily, spurned by the nice girl to whom he has given both his heart and his warmest selection of verses. So he seeks consolation in one of the towns most disreputable taverns (sternly realized in Christina Poddubiuks set design), where he considers the attractions of the juice and the flesh, before deciding against both. This is most unlike the real O'Neill, who was well acquainted with sex and booze at an even younger age; but that's the point. Jared Brown, good in the role throughout, is especially persuasive in his tentative foray into the lower depths, particularly when he tries to defend the virtue of a lady who doesnt want it defended.

The Miller women are slower to come into focus, but Wendy Thatcher steadily establishes herself as Richards mother, sharp- voiced and soft-centred, and Mary Haney proves a subtle presence as his valiant, disappointed aunt. Some supporting performances are coarse-grained (Lisa Norton makes far too hardened a trollop), but there is a forceful cameo from George Dawson as the town bigot, Richard's putative father-in-law, and a delicate one from Graeme Somerville as a barman.

Watching Ah, Wilderness! with its author's biography in mind is a little like seeing the young Judy Garland in the similarly nostalgic setting of Meet Me in St. Louis while knowing what was to become of her. Indeed, the wartime success of that movie set off a cycle that included a musical version of ONeills play, a cult classic called Summer Holiday. O'Neill's dream, set near the beginning of the Depression, had by the decades end become Americas. It's an impossibly rosy picture; it ends, almost as a premature inversion of Long Day's Journey, with Mr. and Mrs. Miller pledging mature love and expressing confidence in their sons future. Its so sweet it hurts. But it feels earned, or at least entitled. It isn't the cost that's paid that moves us; its the cost thats avoided.
