Caryl Churchill is doing A Number, and a very good one, on the subject of cloning. Churchill, an unflaggingly active British dramatist since the 1970s, has written plays that range from the brilliant (Top Girls) to the atrocious (Serious Money). But she has always been an original, nobody's clone.

A Number presents a father and his sons, three of them out of an unknowable quantity: "a number," as the play says in its very first line, a phrase that's arresting and has become downright chilling four lines later. Thirty-five years ago, the father, whose name is Salter, traded his anti-social four-year-old son in for a newer test- tube model. (The original boy's mother, unstable, according to Salter, was dead. "She was one of those people when they say there has been a person under a train and the trains are delayed, she was a person under a train.") The scientists executing the process took advantage of the opportunity to produce more than the one clone. One was sent home with his dad; the others were dispersed.

Now, in just under an hour's playing time, Salter is confronted by three of his sons in turn: the original called Bernard, the replacement also called Bernard and one of the random others, name of Michael. His issue have issues, or at least two of them do. Bernard 1 not unnaturally hates Bernard 2, for whom he was dispossessed. Both of them, also for understandable reasons, hate their father. One of them always has.

Arguments about nature and nurture have presumably been going on since Adam and Eve fell to wondering where they'd gone wrong with Cain and Abel (who, not to give too much away, are relevant). I don't think the play has as much to say on the matter as some critics have claimed. But it has a lot to say about the perils of fatherhood and the needs, fears and resentments of sons, and it says them in short, unpunctuated speeches that come so fast upon one another that they virtually overlap. In these duologues one character will complete or anticipate another's thoughts, sometimes accurately, sometimes not. Audience and actors are kept on their toes.

Salter, the father, is a study in overlapping all by himself. Repugnantly cold-blooded (see again his description of his wife's death), he still wants, like everyone, to think well of himself. He takes some responsibility for his failure with his first son, though he admits more than perhaps he knows. Nothing in the play is more chilling than his telling how he felt perfectly safe leaving the house because he knew that the boy would be too scared to get out of bed. In the same breath, though, he will boast of how hard he tried with his second. And perhaps, by his own lights, he did. He initially sees the cloning revelation as a chance to make money, and he never quite gives up on the idea. Thinking in the play's own terms, one wonders what Salter's own bringing-up was like: Is there any cause in nature, or nurture, that makes these hard hearts?

For the second time this CanStage season (The Goat was the first), a notable play has been given less than it deserves. David Storch has mounted it traverse-style, on a strip of stage with the audience on both sides. This sounds as if it should aid concentration, but in fact it hinders it. With just two actors squaring off at any given time, we spend the evening looking at their profiles and our fellow-spectators' faces. As Salter, Gary Reineke strikes a nice initial balance between the suave and the shifty, but never moves beyond it. The role was created in London by Michael Gambon ("the great Gambon," Ralph Richardson called him), who can switch in an instant from geniality to terrifying cruelty, and the play must have been a whole different experience.

The three sons are played of course by the same actor, and Shawn Doyle does a fine job of differentiating them. With no obvious physical effort, he conveys the in-grown hardness of the elder Bernard, the deepening disillusion of the younger and the cheerfulness of the human experiment Michael. That's the play's kicker; Michael, who presumably was fostered, is happy, and nothing Salter can say will make him declare otherwise. All it took, apparently, was not being raised by Salter, though without Salter, Michael, and the indefinite number of Richards, would not have been. As Michael himself says, as if knowing how out of place he must seem in a Significant Modern Play, "Sorry."

Traverse-staging works better for Return (The Sarajevo Project) which has a larger cast and more action. This show, to which we have been looking forward for about four years, is enjoyable and disappointing in about equal measure. The disappointment stems from it being a small domestic play, born of international co-operation and on a large international theme. Theatrefront, a Toronto troupe that includes some of the best younger practitioners, sent a deputation to Bosnia and worked with actors there on a story that would illuminate both sides. The cast of the resultant production comprises two of theirs and three of ours.

One casualty here is the Western superstition that theatre gets more exciting, and certainly more physical, the further you go East. The most adventurous staging here is also the lamest: a recurring flashback to the carnage of war that gets no more convincing, and no less confusing, however often it happens. The main part of the play concerns the return to Sarajevo of Tarik, a young man who escaped and who now brings with him his new, and pregnant, Canadian wife. He also brings papers that will enable his Serbian family to emigrate to Toronto. Only, they don't want to go.

A huge national tragedy is of course the sum of many small ones. But, even given that, this play (written by the company with a dramaturge, Sue Balint, and directed by Daryl Cloran) gets sidetracked. It can be very acute, and funny, when dealing with cultural differences; the most reverberant line is the one that Tarik's vegetarian bride (played delightfully and touchingly by Holly Lewis) utters when offered Turkish coffee: "Do you have decaf?" Much of the play, though, is taken up with deciding how much of a bastard Tarik is (Christopher Morris deals well with the ambiguities but he doesn't have much to go on) and on how he treated the girl he left behind him, who subsequently took up with his younger brother. It's a story of love and jealousy that could be happening anywhere, and though that may be the point, it isn't a very interesting one.

On these limited terms, the play and production more than hold attention. The visiting players are Alana Dzebo, radiantly angry as the ex-fiancee, and Tanja Smoje, gently luminous as Tarik's sister. Dylan Trowbridge splits that difference as the brother, who's both powerfully angry and enforcedly quiet; he lost his tongue in the war. The same actor doubles (they all do) as a Canadian peacekeeper, whose one scene opens windows through which the rest of the play gazes only fitfully.
