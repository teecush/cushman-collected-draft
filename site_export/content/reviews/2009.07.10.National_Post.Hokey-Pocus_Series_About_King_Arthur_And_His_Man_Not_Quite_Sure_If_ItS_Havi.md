Two weeks in, and Merlin is settling into a pattern. Somebody arrives at the court of King Uther, father of the once and future King Arthur, with murderous intent backed up by supernatural powers. These evil plans are foiled by Merlin, the boy who puts the sauce into sorcery; he's a gauche but game young man, currently hanging around the castle as a physician's assistant. Merlin has his own superior magical skills, demonstrated by his ability to knock things over without touching them, which he has to disguise because it would be death to reveal them. In Episode 1, he defeated a celebrity singer whose performance put the whole courtly audience to sleep. Well, we've all been to shows like that. This chanteuse, though, went further than most, by enveloping her fans in cobwebs. She then tried to stick a blade into the heir-apparent, at which point Merlin, who had kept his ears prudently covered throughout the siren's song, sprang into action and saved Arthur's life. This was very sporting of him since the prince, a bully with an outsized sense of entitlement, had been treating him appallingly. Merlin's reward for his good deed has been a job as Arthur's personal servant: a mixed blessing if ever there was one.

Still, some kind of respect and even affection has sprung up between the two young men, and in Episode 2 Merlin saved Arthur's life again. This time he stopped him being treacherously killed in a tournament by somebody called Knight Valiant, whose shield incorporated a brood of snakes, programmed to spout venom on command. And the pellet with the poison's in the vessel with the pestle.

You don't have to be a student of Sir Thomas Malory to detect some revisionism here. We aren't used to Arthur and Merlin being chums and contemporaries, and certainly not to Arthur growing up at his father's court. In the received versions he's spirited away -- actually by Merlin -- to grow up in humble surroundings, and eventually to prove his parentage and his right to the throne by performing feats unattainable by anyone else. If this new series intends to work in the sword-in-the-stone stuff, I can't see how it hopes to go about it. There's a serving-wench on hand who takes a shine to young Merlin and whose specialized knowledge -- she's a blacksmith's daughter -- comes in very handy for instructing him in which bits of a knight's armour go where. She's called Guinevere, "but most people call me Gwen," and if names, even shortened ones, mean anything, Merlin is headed for a broken heart; though it's hard to see this cub-like Arthur falling for a lady's maid or his even more snobbish father allowing the match. Though of course, dad may be gone by then; he seems pretty lifeless already.

Speaking of names, the series began with the decapitation of a convicted magician who identified himself, in his last headed moment, as Thomas James Collins. This would certainly have jarred with Malory, whose characters, when they had them at all, sported surnames like Pendragon and du Lac. Merlin, by the way, identifies as a warlock rather than a wizard; though I can't fathom what brand of political correctness has dictated the dropping of the familiar title "sir" in favour of Knight Valiant and, obviously looming somewhere in the series' future, Knight Launcelot.

There's not much point in complaining about anachronisms. Malory's Morte d'Arthur, which has conditioned all subsequent treatments of the Arthurian legends, was itself outrageously anachronistic, the imposition of a medieval Christian world view on a figure who, if he existed at all, would have been an early British chieftain and probably pagan. Malory did, though, write great if stilted prose, and most subsequent modernisers have at least attempted a certain stylishness. The text of Merlin, a British series with four credited writers, seems to have been created by authors with tin ears for delivery by actors with cloth larynxes. It aims at jauntiness and misses. "We can't let Valiant get away with it" urges Merlin, urgently, to which Arthur thoughtfully replies "I don't like the guy, but that doesn't mean he's cheating." It would be one thing if they were trying to have fun with the story (I think, longingly, of the very old Robin Hood series with Richard Greene) but they seem to think they're doing it a favour.

From time to time we leave the toy turrets of Spamalot -- sorry, Camelot -- to descend with Merlin into the bowels of the castle, which are very deep indeed and reached by a series of perilous steps left over from an Indiana Jones movie. Here dwells the Great Dragon, who's the only one of his breed left alive by the fanatically ant-magical Uther and who, unlike anyone else in the show, talks as if he might have read a book, though not perhaps a good one. "Just give me a straight answer," begs Merlin, but nobody ever got one of those from an oracle. Bodeful pronouncements apart, the Great Dragon, flapping his wings as he descends from nowhere in particular (his captors have given him a lot of Lebensraum), seems rather gentle, even cuddly. He also does voice-overs, announcing at the start of each episode that "no young man, no matter how great, can know his destiny." It's John Hurt's voice that he uses. The show's only other contact with thespian distinction is the anxious presence of Richard Wilson as the court doctor, Merlin's protector, who alternately marvels at the boy wonder and tells him to clean up his room. Angel Coulby is an appealingly spunky Guinevere; Colin Morgan's Merlin and Bradley James' Arthur are well-intentioned young men, similar except that one wears a smock and a smile, the other chain-mail and a scowl.

Well, Rome-- the TV show, I mean-- wasn't built in a day. It took a whole season to progress from camp to something both serious and funny. So maybe Merlin will do the same. At the moment, though, it's jokey without the wit to know that it's a joke. - Merlin airs Sunday at 8 p. m. on CTV and NBC.
