PICASSO AT THE LAPIN AGILE

Bluma Appel Theatre

- - -

Steve Martin's first, and to date only, play is one of those that brings together famous historical figures who never met, but should have. It ushers in Pablo Picasso and Albert Einstein, both of whom were in Paris in 1905 and who are here corralled in a cafe that the painter is known to have frequented. The physicist just happens to drop by.

What they have in common, of course, is that they are revolutionaries just about to happen, in a new century poised to do likewise. They are also great womanizers: something we have always known about Pablo and are just beginning to assimilate about Albert. So they discuss art, science, sex and genius, with additional contributions from the bar staff and clientele. Along the way they make a lot of jokes, or at least their author does. The result is less than a great play but, as presented by CanStage, is certainly a great evening.

The classic of this genre is Tom Stoppard's Travesties, which capitalized on the simultaneous presence in First World War Switzerland (or in Switzerland when everywhere else was having the First World War) of James Joyce, Tristan Tzara and Lenin. The difference is that Stoppard let the celebrities determine the shape and texture of his play, while Martin merely exhibits them. Martin sticks at simple addition, while Stoppard went in for dizzying multiplication. Also, Stoppard's jokes are strategic parts of the argument, while Martin's are there to make the party go.

Some of them, as you would expect from this author, are very funny. Almost at the top we get Einstein furiously ruffling his hair in order to look more like himself. (This is the professor's second appearance in a free-wheeling take-your-partners drama. Terry Johnson, in his play Insignificance, teamed him up with Marilyn Monroe.)

Later the Lapin proprietress -- who has had a fling with Picasso, like most of female Paris, apparently -- rounds on both men to tell them they only adopted their respective vocations in order to pick up girls. Einstein, who is just a year away from publishing on relativity and has no social life to speak of, calls this absurd, but it has a deeper truth. They both crave recognition, and the carnal kind is the best.The play is very good on sexual relationships, both in discussing them and -- with a swift, cartoon- like touch --displaying them.

When it comes to comparing their respective achievements, actual and potential, the two begin by scoffing at one another and end in deep mutual respect. Each is deeply moved by the other's visionary sense of design. This is very nice as far as it goes, but it leaves you wondering how the piece can possibly develop.

And then Martin surprises us, twice; his play really does get better as it goes on. Our heroes wonder who can possibly complete the new century's transforming trinity. Enter a giggling inventor, in a yellow checked suit, claiming to be the boy. Plainly he is a fraud (though he will probably do well). Then, thunder and lightning, and in blows a visitor from the future. The program just calls him Visitor, and I wouldn't dream of blowing his cover, but I will just hint that he is a pompadoured pop singer from Memphis (Tennessee, not Egypt).

I am not sure he really rates in this company (I mean, he's not Louis Armstrong), but compared to some of the other architects of the 20th century, he is a benign choice. The three of them look at the stars and see their names up in lights, the newcomer's much the biggest of the three. That, as he more or less says, is show business. And then a kitschy picture on the cafe wall disappears, to be replaced by Picasso's Demoiselles d'Avignon, striking awe into everyone. It's a deeply stirring moment. The play's tribute to genius comes alive, and feels felt.

The play looks good throughout: not just good but satisfying. It is directed by Randall Arney, who was responsible for its major American productions, on a gorgeously mellow, subtly raked set (designed by Scott Bradley, sublimely lit by Kevin Lamotte) that provides a superb cast with both a frame and a resting place.

The choreography, physical and verbal, is perfect. Jordan Pettle's Picasso falls a little short -- he is any fiery, attractive young man rather than this particular one -- but he rises to the play's climactic moments. Alon Nashman is a brilliant Einstein, impish and deep; and the supporting cast can hardly be faulted. Eric Peterson, speaking for the ageing and weak-bladdered ordinary man, pungently parades between barroom and bathroom; Geoffrey Bowes is sharp and solid as the patron of the Agile Rabbit (a Symbolist); William Webster is smart and stylish as a buyer and seller of masterpieces; and the two Great Pretenders are Ron Kennell, hysterically hysterical, and Blaine Bray, an unchallengeably authentic Elvis. (Sorry, it slipped out).

But the best are the two women: Cara Pifko, wounded but proud as one of art's recent conquests, and Michelle Fisk as the patroness, a Post-Romantic with all the sad, assured sensual wisdom that that implies. The play is a sketch, but it's a sketch with wings.
