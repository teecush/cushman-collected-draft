EDWARD II

Studio Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Christopher Marlowe's Edward II starts with an exultant soliloquy by Piers Gaveston, the new king's old favourite. "Old," that is, as in "well-established." This is unusual; generally, if there is any exulting to be done at the top of a Marlowe play, it will be done by the title character. Edward the Second, king of England, only appears on stage after Gaveston has graphically outlined the high times --make that gay times -- that they will be having, with a whole realm as their playground. Then Edward enters, bullied by his barons, who aren't having any of it.

That is the way the play goes. Edward is nearly always at the mercy either of his friends or of his enemies; he rarely gets a chance to act for himself, and when he does, the results are nearly always disastrous. It is, all the same, his play throughout; it begins with his accession and ends with his death, hideously murdered in the dungeons of Berkeley Castle.

In the new Stratford production -- the festival's first ever Marlowe and one of its shamefully few ventures into any of Shakespeare's contemporaries -- David Snelgrove plays Edward with a boyish doggedness that seems right at any given moment but doesn't vary much; it leaves unsounded the depths both of Edward's vulnerability and of his suffering, though it excels in showing off his vengeful streak. To be fair, Edward as written does not so much develop as endure. As often happens in Elizabethan plays, theatrical sleight-of-hand allows the years to slide into one another. One moment, Edward is the youthful king, the next he's the father of a 12-year old boy, and not too long after that he's "aged Edward," and it must be hard to know where to mark the transitions. Snelgrove's Edward looks and sounds no older at the end of the play than at the beginning, though he does look dirtier, as well he might given the appalling degradations of his imprisonment. His captors seem motivated partly by simple sadism, partly by the hope that he'll commit suicide and save them the bother of regicide. Here, they're out of luck; Edward hangs on, with a toughness that may, we are led to believe, be the last trapping of kingship. He harps pitiably on the contrast between his present state, but he displays no inner growth, which is why his role is less rewarding, both for actor and audiences, than the Shakespearean Richard II, which it so plainly influenced. Richard attains a sardonic awareness of his own responsibility and of the vulnerability of all mankind; Edward is left complaining that his only fault was to have loved too much -- which, in the world of this play, would certainly have marked him out for destruction.

Richard II is a play about a kingdom as well as a king. Edward II goes through the history-play motions -- the barons complain about the state of the realm and about the king's lamentable failure to make war on foreigners -- but you don't really feel that Marlowe was interested. He wrote plays about aspiring supermen, who may use their societies but aren't really involved with them. In Edward II, probably his last play, he focuses for once on powerlessness rather than power, but it's the same theme in reverse; we merely get to spend more time with those who suffer the pain than with those who inflict it.

The play is not quite as heartless as that makes it sound. When the barons, who don't mind Edward being homosexual but object to his lavishing high offices on a comparative commoner like Gaveston, ask the king, "Why should you love him whom the world hates so?" Edward replies, "Because he loves me more than all the world." It's a piercing moment, but it changes nobody's mind. Also, it probably isn't true; Gaveston has his own agenda, and we never know how much affection may be mixed in with his ambition. Jamie Robinson gives him an effectively insolent performance but without the weight either for his initial dreams of glory or for the sudden access of nobility that in an Elizabethan play invariably accompanies incipient execution. Edward's replacement favourites are nimbly done by Nicolas van Burek as Young Spencer and by Ron Kennell, who is absolutely right as the obsequious scholar Baldock (and extremely wrong as the Archbishop of Canterbury).

There are a few cliches in Richard Monette's production. The play's final scenes themselves can hardly help being harrowing, but the effect is lessened when Edward's jailers and murderers, who are supposed to be minor aristocrats on the make, are played with a coarseness that would seem excessive in an end-of-pier melodrama. It's their refinement that should really chill the blood. I also never want to see another slow-motion battle. Otherwise the play is given all the speed and clarity it needs, plus a brief orgy to show it's up to date. It's an emblematically-styled production in which you can tell that Gaveston is a spendthrift; he has an extra suit of clothes. Scott Wentworth as Young Mortimer goes from junior rebel to king in all but name, without changing out of his red tunic. He gives, as expected, a glittering demonstration of the ruthless ambition that marks him out as Marlowe's favourite; he falters only at his defiant end, which he shouts when he should be taking it coolly for granted. James Blendick and Walter Borden do some expert harrumphing as the king's rougher opponents. Michelle Giroux plays Edward's queen, whose masochistic devotion -- first to her husband, then to Mortimer -- she seems to find it hard to credit. This gifted actress' comic sense often seems to trip her up; there's a constant air of tongue-in-cheek about her. It does work here for the tremulous hypocrisy of her later scenes, wishing her husband dead "so it were not by our means."

Marlowe may not have been much interested in the sweep of politics, but he knew all about realpolitik. It's probably what killed him.
