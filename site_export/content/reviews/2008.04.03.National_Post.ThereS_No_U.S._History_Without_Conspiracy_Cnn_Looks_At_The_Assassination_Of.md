Anew CNN series titled Black in America begins tonight with Eyewitness to Murder, a documentary about the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. It's an excellent program. The presenter, Soledad O'Brien, seems to have talked to just about everybody still living who was present at, or around, the Memphis killing in 1968, and to many who were caught up in what happened later. Her interviews are lucidly interwoven with grainy footage from the day and with some helpful graphics. The sequence depicting the murder itself is about as tense and as scrupulous as any reconstruction could be.

Someone says that King's death marked the end of the Civil Rights era. It could equally be claimed that it marked the beginning, or at any rate the cusp of the progression from struggle to achievement. The point is never explicitly made, but the feeling is conveyed that the killing of King effectively discredited his opponents. That's poetically just, considering the lengths to which they had gone to discredit him. The film provides chilling -- and, at this distance, bleakly comic -- details of the FBI's poison-pen conspiracy against him, exacerbated by his being as fierce an opponent of the Vietnam War as he was of segregation. "I think J. Edgar Hoover had a personal dislike for Martin Luther King" says John Lewis, one of the King's young associates and obviously a master of understatement.

Lewis is now a Democratic congressman for Georgia. Andrew Young, an older and closer King aid and the most eloquent of the interviewees, became American ambassador to the UN. Their progress is some evidence at least for the truth of King's famous sermon about standing Moses-like on a mountain, viewing a territory that he might never reach but that his successors would. The irony of his having said that just a couple of days before his death is duly marked, but we're also told that it was a speech he'd given before -- whenever, in fact, he felt that danger was imminent. Which was a lot of the time; he's quoted as saying, "Every morning when I brush my teeth, I know this day may be my last." What really set the fear in motion was the killing of President John F. Kennedy, which brought assassination back into the American arena. There's a bloody bookend here: Two months after King's death, John Lewis was in a Los Angeles hotel room, awaiting a meeting with Bobby Kennedy. The meeting never happened. When he heard about the new assassination, Lewis tells us, "I broke down and cried." And he does again, on camera.

Two days later, King's presumed killer, James Earl Ray, was captured at Heathrow Airport in London. And the film is more about him than it is about King, which may seem to belie the series title, but is in accord with all studies of an assassination, documentary or dramatic. The climactic event happens, at latest, halfway through: The victim becomes a symbol and the perpetrator a protagonist. Ray was a poor white southerner with an extensive criminal record; his brother, interviewed by O'Brien, says that, however unprepossessing in appearance, "as a criminal he was fearless ... smart and intelligent ... smart enough not to get caught." His major crime, prior to the murder, seems to have been a bank robbery from which he walked away with US$30,000 in $20 bills -- thereby creating a literal paper trail that helped undo him. Ray was undoubtedly a racist (he wrote in a statement "I wouldn't say I hated King ... I think most preachers are a little phony") and he was undoubtedly in Memphis on the fatal day -- staying in a flophouse directly opposite the hotel balcony on which King was gunned down. That same day, Ray left Memphis, arriving two days later in Toronto. (There's a momentary shock here for locals; we see the street signs for Ossington and Argyle and wonder, till the narration clears it up, whether there's some American town with the same intersection.) His eventual aim was to get to some white-dominated country in Africa; he had the money for the trip, and the forged papers, but not quite well forged enough. Hence his arrest. The FBI were as zealously on his case as they had been on King's -- which, for the conspiracy-minded, may be suspicious in itself.

Ray confessed, then retracted the confession, but was convicted anyway. He claimed to have been duped by a mysterious man named "Raoul" who was never found. Certainly there are troubling gaps in the evidence. Ray's brother believes in his innocence; so, more startlingly, does the King family. We see footage of King's son Dexter visiting Ray in prison in 1997 and pledging solidarity. Ray died in jail in the next year, but we hear from lawyers who took up his cause and from those who opposed it. Your opinions when watching are likely to bounce about: convinced first that he was guilty, then that he was framed, then that he was involved but was part of a conspiracy (involving, it's alleged, the FBI, the Memphis police and the U.S. military). We're also told by a lawyer that "the fact that Ray lived all those years proves there wasn't a conspiracy." That sounds like the last word, but of course the conspirators could have been being super-subtle. If this were TV fiction, we'd know there was a conspiracy, a bottomless one. As it's fact, meticulously presented, we're left in fascinated uncertainty. - Eyewitness to Murder airs on CNN tonight at 9 p.m.
