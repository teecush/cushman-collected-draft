It's tough being a bad girl, and especially being a bad girl on a television "reality show." (I can no longer bear to type those last two words without quotes.) Last year, in Rich Kids: Cattle Drive, we saw a posse of them dispatched to earn their keep -- and a substantial catch prize -- in the Wild West. In The Girls of Hedsor Hall, another bunch of spoiled American females is sent off to an equally mythic, and possibly even tougher, environment: an English finishing school for young ladies.

In fact, the kids in this new show aren't all rich though, such are our expectations of the genre, it takes some time for that realization to sink in. There are a dozen of them, and introductions in the first episode tend to be rushed. (Confusion is compounded by the fact that the ranks include a Jennifer, a Jenna and a Jen. Couldn't the selection process have been rigged a little more constructively?) We may think we have them pegged as a bunch of Paris Hilton wannabes, but it turns out that many of them are don't-wannabes; they have abuse problems, substance or sexual, of which they want to rid themselves. This makes them more sympathetic, and the show more offensive.

Add to its many faults that the whole thing is set up on, literally, false premises. Research suggests that "Hedsor Hall," the English country home to which The Girls are consigned, is actually called Hedsor House and that, despite claims made in the script about a hundred-year pedigree, it's generally used as a convention centre. In fact, the whole idea of "the English finishing school" is probably a myth; when young English ladies have been "finished," they've been sent abroad, usually to Switzerland. The Girls of Hedsor Hall turns out to be a U. S. spinoff of a British show, Ladette to Lady. (There's a male equivalent, From G's to Gents, which is being given a simultaneous spin on MTV. Some bad ideas seem inexhaustible.) The two principal teachers at Hedsor Hall have been carried over from the parent production; they are Gill Harbord, headmistress, and Rosemary Shrager, "disciplinarian," an educational title unknown outside highly specialized establishments. Both ladies may be playing themselves, but they come across as very bad actresses. Ms. Harbord (I think I heard her title right) is toothy and mostly addresses her charges in tones of pained disapproval; Mrs. Shrager (ditto) is beefy and barks at them. Both are very easily offended. There is a third instructor in the shape of Tara Conner, a former Miss USA and a protegee of Donald

Trump, who is the show's executive producer and can add it to the list of things for which he stands accountable. Conner greets her trainees by telling them that she was once every bit as bad they are--bad meaning disrespectful or foulmouthed or drunken or slutty or some combination thereof -- but that Hedsor Hall has changed her. Her actual duties are a little vague.

The girls, having been treated to a ride through the countryside in a fleet of black cabs (renting a bus would probably have been cheaper but less picturesque), are decked out in school uniforms and provided with a pearl necklace apiece.

This makes some of them begin to feel like real ladies and makes others burst out laughing. They will have to surrender them if they're expelled, and it is of course built into the show that every week at least one of them will be. That will apply whether or not any of them has deserved it. That's both cruel and stupid. It makes American Idol, which at least professes to involve the deployment of actual skills, seem positively classy.

As a treat, and a test, the girls are let loose on their first evening at a cocktail party with some young men announced as -- I couldn't make such language up, but the show can and does -- "dignified members of British royal society." (They're so dignified that their dialogue comes with subtitles.) Some riotous behaviour ensues; one of the boys tells one of the girls that she looks like a horse, and for some reason we're meant to hold it against her that this makes her angry. Actually anyone who believes that the youths of the English upper class have anything to teach anyone about manners and morals is living in a snobbish -- and peculiarly American -- dreamworld. Maybe it's because I'm a Londoner, but I would like to state that any show that begins, as this one does, with establishing shots of Big Ben has no idea what, or where, it's talking about.

Next comes the first round of judgements, first in one-on-one interviews in the principal's office ( just them and us and the cameras), then in more public humiliation sessions before the whole school. Margie, a black girl with a big ass (her own description) and a rage problem (not her own description, though she wouldn't disown it) is told that all the other girls are in mortal fear of her. She breaks down in tears, saying that she desperately needs the chance; I wouldn't be surprised if she were the eventual winner. So I've revealed that she isn't kicked out tonight. Sorry. Jenna (I think it's Jenna), when asked what she does for a living, says that she strips and has an adult web-site. Miss Harbord ("how simply awful") is shocked beyond measure that she should be showing off her body and unmoved by the excuse that if she didn't she'd be homeless. She's even more appalled when the girl slams the door on her way out. Reality shows cannot bear too much reality.

"By tomorrow morning," the students are chillingly told, "we want you to conform" -- in other words, to change from televised objects to fantasy stereotypes. One of them, Amanda, is asked, "What are you doing about your cursing?" Since the offending words have been bleeped out, it's possible that she's merely being requested to swear more imaginatively in future, but probably not. Anyway, Amanda's unrepentant. Asked what she'd like to say to Mrs. Schrager now, she says, "I'd tell her to go f---herself." It's hard not to cheer her on. - The Girls of Hedsor Hall airs tonight at 10 p. m. on MTV Canada.
