BERNARD SHAW VOLUME II: 1898-1918 THE PURSUIT OF POWER BY MICHAEL HOLROYD Random House, 422 pages, $36

The Shavian mask

BERNARD SHAW VOLUME II: 1898-1918 THE PURSUIT OF POWER BY MICHAEL HOLROYD Random House, 422 pages, $36

Saturday, October 21, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN The first panel of Michael Holroyds official Shavian triptych was subtitled The Search For Love, and brought its subject as far as marriage. The last instalment, due in two years, has already been tagged The Lure Of Fantasy, and here we have The Pursuit Of Power. The divisions, though necessary, are arbitrary. The three goals were constant and indivisible.

Shaw, believing himself unloved in his childhood and achingly unforgiving about it, was astonished to discover in his late twenties that he had power over women, and was never thereafter able to relinquish it. The mastery he sought was mental rather than physical, but it was certainly sexual. His marriage, contracted when he was 40, was unconsummated, and he drew the line at adultery, so fantasy increasingly ruled. The biggest fantasy of all was his pose of being above emotion. He described vehement sexual passion as "an experience which it is much better, like the vast majority of us, never to have passed through, than to allow it to play more than a recreative holiday part in our lives." That's a neat trick, if you can manage it; Shaw, despite his public protestations, never could.

With his erotic life on hold and his playwriting career still to take wing, Shaw immersed himself in politics. He had done time in London local government, but he increasingly devoted himself to the Fabian Society, the intellectual pressure group that saw itself as the responsible vanguard of socialism in Britain.

Fabianism - pressing its advice on any politician, regardless of party, whom it deemed worthy of it - was presided over by the mole-like Sidney Webb, buried deep in bluebooks and pamphlets, and his censorious wife Beatrice. The most respectable of revolutionaries, they seem in retrospect a slightly risible pair, and must have done at the time. They especially disapproved of H. G. Wells, Shaw's junior rival as a popular prophet, whose public image was as lascivious as Shaws was puritanical. Shaw, who regarded Wells as his protege, was torn between admiration and disapproval.

Wells was perfectly capable of quoting Shavian scripture for his own diabolic purpose: You have it from Mr. Bernard Shaw, he told the Fabians, "that poverty is a crime, and if so, then by the evidence of your balance-sheet ours is a criminal organization." Orthodox Fabianism was for Wells the politics of fantasy; and Shaw was more than half-inclined to agree with him. With his customary self-destructive love of paradox, he campaigned for the removal from the Fabian executive of the Old Gang, himself included. In the end, he went, but the Webbs stayed.

Frustration upon frustration. But love, power and fantasy were all aspects of the one compulsion, which was fame. And in this quest, on the face of it, Shaw was triumphantly successful: spectacularly so within the confines of this volume. Over 20 years he went from aging young coterie playwright to unquestioned contemporary champ. The period yielded Man And Superman, his first success; Fannys First Play, his biggest initial success; and Pygmalion, his most durable success. There were also such key works as Major Barbara, John Bull's Other Island, and Androcles And The Lion. Shaw found a use for the mask he had been constructing all his adult life: G.B.S. the philosophic joker, illusionless, ruthless, heartless.

Unfortunately for him, he wasn't heartless. Two things in particular breached his defences. One was the First World War. He opposed this from the start. He might have got away with that, but he ridiculed it as well. Even those who sympathized intellectually thought his tone misguided. (They included his fellow playwrights, who had similarly deserted him over the smaller matter of theatrical censorship.) Once again the Shavian myth took over, so much so that when peace came he had to issue a book called What I Really Wrote About The War. By this time public opinion had caught up with him. And what he really felt about the war is summed up in a letter he wrote to a bereaved mother: Oh, damn, damn, damn, damn, damn, damn, damn, DAMN DAMN! And oh, dear, dear, dear, dear, dear, dearest!

The recipient was Mrs. Patrick Campbell, the original Eliza Doolittle and Shaws other nemesis. A monster by most accounts, she emerges here as a victim who extricated herself just in time: when Shaws intentions, after a long teasing (and largely epistolary) pursuit became avowedly carnal. She was a straightforward egotist; he played it hypocritically and self-defeatingly cool.

When he gets in Volume III to discussing The Apple Cart, Michael Holroyd will doubtless have much to say about the way that play reflects this affair. King Magnus there is one of the more transparent Shavian masks, with his tempestuous (and platonic) mistress Orinthia, whose letters he shows to his motherly queen Jemima. "I believe," says the royal secretary, that they make me as sick as they do her. Shaw played the same unlovable trick on Mrs. Pat and his wife Charlotte, who emerges as something less than the all-wise and all-forgiving protectress of earlier biographies and of Shaw's wishful thoughts. The union in fact seems pretty miserable. Neither woman lived up to Shaw's exalted standards of civilized behavior; nor did he.

Holroyd makes few value judgments on Shaws writings (it is barely possible that he dislikes them all), but he is scrupulous in referring them to the life and thought. The test case here is Man And Superman, in which the Artist Man falls prey to the Mother Woman. The terms are Shaws own, though they only become clear in the long and detachable dream sequence Don Juan In Hell. This introduces us to the Life Force, Shaw's synonym for God, and ends with the heroine rushing off crying, "A father, a father for the superman." In waking life, though, she remains unconscious of her biological destiny (it isn't even her dream), and simply wants to get her man. So Shaw has it both ways; but the messianic view of human reproduction comes oddly from a man who had just contracted a marriage plainly destined to avoid it.

It used to be claimed - and Shaw often made the joke against himself - that the characters in his plays were merely mouthpieces for his ideas. Now we see that they are indeed Shavian masks, but emotional rather than intellectual. They body forth his own conflicts. He was a tormentedly autobiographical playwright, closer to Strindberg than to Shakespeare - except, a very large reservation, in his unquenchable sense of fun. The ecstasy keeps the plays alive, but the agony helps.

George Orwell held unarguably that any life seen from the inside is a series of defeats. Holroyd helps us to see that Shaw, despite his posing, was no exception to the rule. Which is not to denigrate the man - with defeats like his, who needs victories? I sometimes missed in Holroyds massively scrupulous account the gossipy involvement that you get from St. John Ervines less responsible life of 30 years ago, but I have only one real complaint. At the end of Holroyd's first volume, and intended to serve for this one as well, is a long list of acknowledgements that includes the name of almost every extant theatre critic. Among this is one John Cushman. I am in a position to assure you that this person is a mistake. That's all right - just don't let it happen again. Robert Cushman writes on theatre for Saturday Night magazine.
