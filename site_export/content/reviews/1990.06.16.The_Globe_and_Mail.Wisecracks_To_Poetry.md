SOME FREAKS BY DAVID MAMET Viking, 180 pages, $24.95

Wisecracks to poetry

SOME FREAKS BY DAVID MAMET Viking, 180 pages, $24.95

Saturday, June 16, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN ONCE, greatly depressed, and in New Haven (but I repeat myself). . .  That is playwright David Mamet writing, the same David Mamet who in the play American Buffalo had one of his semi-criminal characters advise another to arm himself on a break-in in case, God forbid, something inevitable should happen." The first line is a lovely wisecrack; the second is comic poetry.

Mamet is the most distinctive stylist to appear in the English-speaking theatre since Harold Pinter. They have plenty in common. Both were actors and have since become screenwriters and directors. Mamet has done especially well at this, with two Hollywood directing credits under his belt. Both write enclosed plays for small casts. Both are verbal experts with the knife and the needle; their texts always seem to carry the threat of physical violence, and sometimes unleash it. Mamets plays have a more direct line to the outside world than most of Pinter's. They are set in specified, recognizable locales. His latest, Speed-the-Plow, takes place in a movie executive's office; he, like Pinter, is going up in the world, maybe not with the happiest results, though God knows Speed-the-Plow is funny.

Pinters masterpiece remains The Caretaker, a tour-de-force of scruff. Mamets twin peaks are American Buffalo, which happens in a junk shop, and Glengarry Glen Ross, from the suspicious end of the real-estate trade. They are the best plays of the last 10 years.

Also, both Pinter and Mamet are Jewish, though neither has made an issue of it. Pinter did write (and has even played) the virtuoso role of Goldberg in The Birthday Party. Mamet's ethnic coloring has always seemed Italian. He grew up in Chicago, and his work is usually set there. In the film The Untouchables, which he wrote, it's Capone Chicago. In Things Change, which he co-wrote and directed, its present-day mobsville (with a side-trip to Nevada). And in Buffalo and Glengarry, the hoods may be just around the corner. The characters, small-timers all, act like they have connections, though if those connections were actually to connect, our guys would probably drop dead from fright.

So I have always believed that Mamet was Italian Catholic, and it has taken Some Freaks, his new collection of essays and occasional items, to disabuse me. He has, of course, the right to write about whomever and wherever he pleases. The thing is, in Some Freaks he has a couple of pieces about American Jewry being ashamed of its own identity, ready to champion every minority cause except its own, proud and envious of the Jew who succeeds without obvious Jewishness, the Jew who "passes. In his plays Mamet is passing: passing, I should say, with honors. The two essays are perceptive, though they are essentially the same piece. So let me splice together a few sentences from them: The lesson of my Reform Temple was that metaphysics is just superstition - that there is no God. And every Sunday we celebrated our escape from Judaism. . . . For my generation, Jewish culture consisted of Jewish food and Jewish jokes, neither of which, probably, were very good for us. . . . Yes, I know that its funny. It is the funniest humor in the world, it's how I have made my living all my life, and it is self-deprecating."

It is funny even, as Mamet has proved, in the mouths of ostensibly gentile characters. He uses it better than Neil Simon, maybe better than Woody Allen. Unlike Simon's and Allens, Mamets are context jokes, not readily quotable, something I find admirable in general if awkward at this moment.

Mamet compartmentalizes in this book too. There is no mention of Jewishness in other contexts. Much of it is insistently all-American. Mamet celebrates the small-town Vermont community in which he now lives, and such manly pleasures as cards, guns and hanging around with the boys, but I get a disarming sense of a shy guy watching himself playing tough.

On his own turf Mamet is thoroughly assured. He writes engagingly on schools of acting, pointing out why Stanislavsky and the Method were exciting and necessary, and why they have ossified. His conclusion - that the theory has lost touch with the life it is meant to reflect and the plays that reflect it - is not new, but it is elegantly and authoritatively stated. He tells us, fascinatingly, how he planned and bluffed his way through the direction of his first movie, House Of Games. ("I found Eisenstein's theories particularly refreshing, as they didn't seem to call for any visual talent.) He writes about women, apparently because a magazine asked him to, though he said that he didnt know anything about the subject. His wife agreed with him about this. Women are notoriously absent from most of his plays. He does know about his own perception of women. Basically, its that they're nicer than men, certainly stronger, and - especially as film and theatre producers with power over playwrights - ruthless. (Speed-the- Plow does have a female, an idealistic bimbo who might develop into a manipulator if not checked as, by a couple of shabby males, she is.) I have seen women do things that the worst man would never entertain the thought of - I do not imply that he would be stopped by conscience, but he would be stopped by the fear of censure." Even if that's wrong about women, its all too right about men - each of whom, as Mamet has repeatedly shown us, yearns to remain one of the boys.

"Over the years, I have come to rely completely on my wifes judgment of character; and am coming to rely on my daughter's ability to assess a situation completely, because she cried when he accidentally stood on her foot. I don't want to be chauvinistic, but a boy might be trusted to do that too. But I like the sentence anyway. And I love this one: As long as Im coming out, I might as well add that I think Mozart couldnt adjust Bach's shoe buckles - an opinion which can be of interest to no one, and which I include solely because I have been trying to work it into cocktail conversation for many years and I have recently stopped drinking."

The book is called Some Freaks because Mamet started by thinking that artists were privileged oddities, and then came to realize that all people are. Robert Cushman is a Toronto writer.
