LIQUOR GUNS KARATE

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

You have to wait until the end of Morwyn Brebner's new play -- a short evening that seems long -- to encounter the title. Apparently, signs offering Liquor, Guns, Karate can be found dotted among the drive-through emporia in the strip malls of Los Angeles. Since my own acquaintance with L.A. is very slight, I'll take the author's word for it. The play seems to subscribe to the belief -- usually more prevalent in U.S. drama than in Canadian -- that if you stick a phrase in your title and intone it at a crucial point of your text, it will be very meaningful.

Let's see: You can kill yourself with liquor, you can kill other people with karate, and you can do both with guns; and of the play's four characters at least two -- the male ones -- might be desperate enough to try one or the other. It seems, though, that it's the sheer arbitrariness of the menu that has taken the author's fancy. The play's last line is something about the folly of going through life on the assumption that one thing will link up with another.

The play itself may conceivably be about the arbitrariness of life. Philosophically it's valid enough, but it's difficult to dramatize. It makes a play seem, well, arbitrary.

We begin in a bar: something of a coup de theatre since, when we arrive and look Sue LePage's creamy set, it looks like it will represent the living room of somebody's ritzy house. So indeed it will, most of the time; but first off, when the lights go down and then up again, we discover that its back wall was a false one and that we have been transported to somewhere dull and smoky in another part of town. A young Canadian woman and a middle-aged American man are trying to get sufficiently crocked to pick one another up. They make barbed, flippant conversation that varies from the effortfully whimsical (she recalls going to bed with a man who couldn't pronounce his r's and wondering if she would catch "a sexually transmitted speech impediment") to the genuinely funny (he claims to be a failed writer; she says this town seems to be full of them, and vaguely adds "you can't swing a cat..." It's the way her voice trails off that's hilarious.) We pay attention because there is something recognizably at stake here: The scene appeals to the basic human desire to see if two people are going to get it on together. Much later in the play, the woman, whose name is Laurel, will tell us -- quite acutely, I think -- that what she really likes about sex is the moment at which she knows it's going to happen; and it seems to tie in. I have a quite separate, and probably unworthy, suspicion that the first scene was written in very late; it seems out of style with the rest of the play, in which neither the characters nor the story seems to have any drives whatever.

They talk as if they have. The plush house turns out to have been the abode of Laurel's just-deceased father. She has come down from Toronto to collect what she hopes will be a sizeable inheritance. With her comes her boyfriend, Chuck, a stockbroker who needs the break -- both kinds of break -- since back home he's about to be had up for malpractice. Irving, Laurel's bar-room encounter of the previous night and a man who has failed at many things besides writing, comes in quest of her, smitten And Irving's long-estranged daughter, whose name coincidentally is Lauren, comes in quest of him, bearing divorce papers from his equally long-estranged wife.

And round and round the four of them go. Things do happen -- a car crash, a suicide attempt, the reading of the will, some kind of seizure -- and if you could draw a graph of the play's action it might well come out as a straight line, but if feels circular. And there seems be no point at which the audience can break the circle and get involved. The characters keep making long speeches about their previous lives but we don't believe in them because we don't believe in the people. Indeed, the more we learn of them, the less we believe. Whatever spark of language or insight it takes to bring characters to life the moment they step on a stage, this play not has not been able to provide. Some sharp moments do rise above the morass. Chuck's doomed efforts to get himself a new life by selling a movie idea down the phone are painfully entertaining, though prolonged beyond the limits of credible masochism. Most of the time, though, one waits for it all to stop.

The actors, under Jackie Maxwell's direction, play at full throttle throughout, which is probably the only way to do it. Victor Ertmanis as Irving comes off best: He's a burly jelly, and the mere fact he's older than the others helps give him weight, and even texture. Laurel is Waneta Storms, best known for her skill at waifs (Faust, The Diary of Anne Frank); here she plays a waif with ribs of steel, and gives some life to a character who is really an intellectual construct (she's described as "a poster-girl for apathy"). Tom Barnett is agreeable as Chuck, a loser in winner's clothing. Ann Baggley as Lauren has the hardest job; the moment she comes on, she's required to orate, in the centre of what suddenly seems a very big and empty stage, and she goes on to make detailed accusations against a man she hardly knows. The near-duplication of the two women's names yields nothing usable except a joke about changing the last letter on a tattoo.

The play takes place in the wake of an atypical Los Angeles ice storm. At its close, Laurel and Chuck share a humorous fantasy about skating their way to the airport, past the malls and past the postings of the play's title phrase. It seems to be intended as comment on the aimless sterility of Californian or maybe just modern life: In other words, a received idea of monstrous proportions. If the play is meant to be about Los Angeles, iced-up or otherwise, then it hasn't worked at it hard enough. We seem to be in Sam Shepard's spiritual territory, which is not a territory I've ever much cared for. But at least with Shepard, you feel as if you're at the heart of the desert; here, you're stuck in a suburb.
