BOY GETS GIRL

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Rebecca Gilman, author of Boy Gets Girl, has been hailed as the best new American playwright since David Mamet. This is unwarranted. She is a clever and intelligent writer (the two words are not synonymous) who dramatizes interesting subjects, and she may be developing a distinctive viewpoint. She has not matched it, however, with a distinctive voice.

Her expository dialogue is indistinguishable from anybody else's, and when she goes in for humour, the audience can usually guess her comebacks before the actors can utter them. So much for style. As for substance: as she says herself, her most successful plays have been about issues. Spinning Into Butter, the play that established her in New York, is her racism-on-campus play. Boy Gets Girl, which followed and is now receiving its Canadian premiere, is her stalking play. Its the more absorbing of the two, partly because of its novelty value. There havent been many stalking plays, though there have of course been several stalking movies.

Gilman has staked out an interesting area for herself -- her protagonists are capable women with moderately successful careers, pushed to their limits. She takes the workplace as her central setting. Theresa, in Boy Gets Girl, is a magazine writer, lately unattached. A friend fixes her up on a blind date with Tony, a computer engineer, slightly younger than herself, apparently charming, and very attentive: Disconcertingly so, in fact. When she declines to pursue the relationship, he refuses to take no for an answer. Theresa finds herself deluged by phone calls and flowers. It's embarrassing for her, and also for the audience -- emotional ineptitude is always painful to watch on stage, perhaps because it's so easy to identify with. We grow to dread the voice on the phone as much as Theresa does, which is a compliment to the play, even if a back-handed one.

What follows escalates, speedily, way beyond embarrassment. The calls become obscene, to be followed by threatening letters and worse. A restraining order is placed on Tony, but when he violates it, the police are unable to enforce it. Theresa is advised to leave town, get a new job, change her name: to sacrifice her identity, in fact. She is faultless, except perhaps in having discouraged Tony with insufficient firmness to begin with, and she is trapped. In Miles Potters production, the play makes us share her terror. This is a play with many scene changes, and after each revolution of Peter Hartwells smart, suggestive set we expect to find Theresa's nemesis lurking somewhere in the newly-revealed location. The denouement is as frightening as we have expected, though not in the way we have expected.

Seasna McKenna, perfectly cast, conveys all of Theresa's intelligence (which includes some wit), all her integrity, all her professional assurance and all her private panic. The last element she injects with great economy; it courses underneath, mounting in intensity but still kept down with just one devastating, hysterical moment.

Note this is not really a play about stalking; it is a play about being stalked. Kevin Bundy is a Tony suitably unsettling in his very blandness, but the mechanics of the piece mean he has to spend a lot of time offstage. The play isnt very interested in what might make him tick, settling for a few generalizations from a cop whos never met him. So there's a lot of stage time still to fill, and it's devoted to broadening the theme, contextualizing it, examining other ways in which men may objectify women.

Theresa has two very supportive male colleagues: Her editor, who offers her a refuge, and another journalist, who is the first to call the law. They could hardly be more loyal, but each -- at least according to the other -- has some kind of emotional design on her. Her fellow scribe is even insensitive enough to suggest writing a story on stalking, using Theresas ordeal as the hook. This makes her furious beyond words: literally, she refuses to discuss it. One sympathizes, but it means that nobody ever gets to ask the obvious question. Suppose she had never been involved, and he had brought a similar idea to her. He could hardly have written it without going into actual cases. Would she have minded then about whose privacy was being breached, whose trauma was being probed? And doesnt this go beyond male-female relationships? Don't we all project on to other people the things we want from them?

The two guys here do much of the play's legwork, functioning as they do as buddies, protectors, sleuths and sounding boards. They hardly have time to be people in their own right, and the actors have their work cut out to humanize them. Peter MacNeill slouches in comfortably pudgy mode through the role of the editor; John Jarvis has the edgier character, and sometimes pushes him too hard.

There remain three lively episodes in which Theresa, for interview purposes, visits an ageing film director famous for his obsession, offscreen and on, with womens breasts. Here, if you like, is the arch-objectifier, and Theresa gets very cross with him. She ends up quite liking him though, partly because he is engagingly shameless, and partly because he is both personally and professionally desperate. The role is played by Eric Peterson, who gives his best performance in ages. In recent roles he has been all over the place but here he is obliged to play all his scenes confined either to a desk or to a hospital bed. Thus restrained, he focuses his extravagance, channels it through the character, and not only relishes his generous percentage of the plays best lines but allows us to enjoy them too. I'm not sure what this character adds to the argument, beyond giving another twist to the kaleidoscope, but he's certainly good for the plays flavour, and for letting us see another aspect of the heroine. Its only with him that she's allowed to be both affectionate and in command.

This is, I suppose, a more prosaic play than the Bluma Appel's other recent Broadwayish imports, Wit and Proof. To put it another way: It has fewer pretensions, spiritual or intellectual, and is better off without them.
