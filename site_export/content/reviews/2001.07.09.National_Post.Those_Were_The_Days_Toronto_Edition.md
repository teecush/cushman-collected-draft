SHADOW PLAY

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE - Since Noel Coward was both a notable dramatist and a notable songwriter, there is a general assumption that he combined his talents in a body of first-rate musicals. It isn't true. His most substantial work in the genre, Bitter Sweet, is now sadly faded, and his subsequent operettas and musical comedies were not successful even when new. Many of his best songs were written for revues.

That leaves us with the sequence of one-act plays he wrote as vehicles for himself and Gertrude Lawrence, which were first produced in 1936 under the umbrella title of Tonight at 8:30. These nine playlets contain some of his best writing, and three of them are mini-musicals: Family Album, which is Coward in his lace- valentine mood; Red Peppers, a sketch about embittered music-hall performers whose effectiveness depends on whoever happens to be playing it; and Shadow Play,which is being revived as a lunchtime show at the Shaw Festival, and which is in part a musical about musicals.

Simon and Victoria, well-off and apparently brittle, have been married for five years; the gilt is rubbing off and the guilt is setting in. Each has another admirer waiting in the wings; Victorias is a harmless young man called Michael, and Simons is a more predatory young woman whose name (shades of Private Lives) is Sibyl.

Victoria has become dangerously addicted to sleeping pills, and on the night we meet her she is feeling especially low or, to put it another way, excessively high. She and Simon, with an older couple, have been to the theatre, and Victoria finds herself bitterly resenting the ease with which the people onstage could deal with their crises; when the pressure mounts, they could simply sing a song or go into a dance. Under the influence of the drugs, she starts re-imagining her own life as if it were a musical, sorting through her memories in an effort to discover what went wrong.

What she actually comes up with is a rerun of what went right: memories of her first meeting with Simon, their courtship, their honeymoon. There are no clues here to later disasters, other than our built-in awareness that young people grow older. We end up pretty much where we started, and the play does not dig as deep as it pretends.

Its surface, though, is very intriguing: sharp and economical in its depiction of the characters' over-privileged and over- stimulated milieu; highly suggestive in the way it intercuts past and present, dream and consciousness, speech and music.

Three of Coward's best songs weave through the action, whole or in fragments: Play, Orchestra, Play, which is in his finger-wagging Dance Little Lady-Poor Little Rich Girl vein of frenetic gaiety, and which later served as the signature to his cabaret act; You Were There, one of his most sinuous and insinuating ballads; and Then, a lesser-known number in which you can almost hear the masters voice, high-strung, keyed-up and reminiscent.

David Savoys production admirably captures the mood, with William Orlowski's choreography and Judith Bowden's designs making amusing, economical use of simple elements. Four couples -- the principals, the dalliances, the confidants, and a pair of domestics - - switch from characters to ensemble and back again.

Patrick R. Brown's performance of the Coward hero suffers from the same tentativeness that afflicts his impersonation of Coward himself in The Man Who Came to Dinner, but Patty Jamieson's Victoria is excellent. Jillian Cook and Guy Bannerman are the solid, sympathetic older couple who unnervingly advise their juniors to grab their happiness while they still can, and Karen Wood makes a notably pert maid.
