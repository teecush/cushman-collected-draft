There are two obvious ways in which Banana Shpeel differs from all the previous Cirque du Soleil shows that have been seen in Toronto. One is that it's staged in a theatre rather than a tent. The other is that it has dialogue, lots of it, that can be both heard and understood. These are not good things.

There have been spoken words in previous Cirque shows, but they have generally sounded like gibberish, either because of big top acoustics or because they actually have been gibberish. There have also been rudimentary plotlines that were difficult to follow but usually seemed to involve someone being captured or placed under a spell or both, and finally rescued and redeemed. Clowns figured intermittently, as supplementary goodies or baddies or just as relief. This time, though, there's a story that crowds everything else offstage and has clowns at the centre of it. Three tiers of buffoon are involved. At the top there's Marty Schmelky, the showman whose show this supposedly is. He's a short man in a flecked, shiny green jacket, which gives him the air of an ambulant pistachio ice cream with mint chocolate chips. Like so many legendary showmen and/or circus ringmasters, he's a tyrant and shouts at everybody. In the course of the evening he reforms, then re-reforms, and finally has an epiphany involving Paris, a city the mention of whose name has been driving him to distraction all evening. We're meant to wonder why this is, and we don't. Whether acting nasty or nice, Marty is an obnoxious presence, and our resentment of him rubs off on Danny Rutigliano, the perfectly efficient and doubtless amiable actor who plays him. He has a tall, beaky female foil, in the person of Shereen Hickman, who would probably be hilarious with better material.

Their seconds-in-command are two vocational clowns, one (Wayne Wilson) irresponsible, the other (Daniel Passer) officious -- the classic duo -- and both, through no identifiable fault of their own, clogging up the evening. They have even more text to carry than Marty and even less character.

Finally, there are three zanies who erupt onto the stage from the auditorium as part of a supposed talent contest and refuse to vacate it. These guys are really good, each of them specializing in a different variety of smile. There is Gordon White, a familiar Cirque figure, self-announced as "the oldest mime in the world" and exploring new horizons of happy, hopeful decrepitude. There's Patrick de Valette, a lank-haired "modern dancer" with a devilish, unconquerable grin. And there's Claudio Carneiro, a smirking gigolo who does a protracted silent number that's the best thing of the night and depicts him going out for dinner and a drive with a young lady selected from the audience. Though if his date on the press night wasn't a plant, she must have had quite amazing skills of instant adaptation.

Talented though they are, the intruders get nothing but grief from Marty, who carries on as if they were the lunatics taking over the asylum. In a way, he's right. But what he doesn't acknowledge -- and what the show itself, written and directed by David Shiner, catastrophically fails to realize -- is that Marty himself is part of the problem, indeed its biggest symptom.

This "vaudeville with a twist," as it calls itself, is all frame and no picture. Traditionally, people have gone to the circus to be thrilled by the sight of performers in peril, in danger of either being eaten alive by wild animals or falling to the ground from immense heights. Cirque's humane instincts have always eliminated the first possibility, and the dimensions of their current playing space put paid to the second. There can be no tightropes or trapezes at the Canon. Indeed there are few specialty acts of any kind. What we are offered, when the clowns kindly leave the stage, is an underpopulated third-rate variety show. There are two helpings of fast but unexceptional tap dancing. There are showgirls. There are two jugglers: a lady (Vanessa Alvarez) who does it, impressively, with her feet; and a gentleman (Tuan Le) who, more traditionally but even more impressively, uses his hands. For some reason these fine but not dissimilar acts are placed close together in the first act. The show can't even leave its best talents alone to dazzle us. It clutters the stage with lesser performers, either looking on in dumb admiration or executing paler versions of the main event.

What the production most signally lacks is the one quality that Cirque du Soleil could always, even at its most maddeningly pretentious, be counted on to provide: beauty. Perhaps it was inevitable that in going to Vegas, Cirque would also go Vegas. Here it's gone cut-price Vegas, the worst of all worlds.
