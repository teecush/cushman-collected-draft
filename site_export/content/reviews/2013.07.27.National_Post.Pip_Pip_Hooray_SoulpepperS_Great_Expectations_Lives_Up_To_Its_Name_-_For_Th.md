With a running time of two and a half hours, Soulpepper's stage version of dickens' Great Expectations, adapted and directed by Michael Shamata, clocks in between the royal Shakespeare Company's two-part eight-hour Nicholas Nickleby, one of the great theatrical experiences of my lifetime, and the trim and perennial Soulpepper-Shamata Christmas Carol, based on a short story. Both those productions were matched in scale and feeling to their sources; they left you with the satisfying feeling that nothing had been left out. Great Expectations is, by dickensian standards, a medium-sized novel, and this dramatization doesn't have to skimp very much in terms of plot. The first half never feels rushed; it is in fact beautiful. The second lacks breathing space; the story is all there, but it becomes a concertina of coincidences.

Bless, however, the central performance. dickens' heroes often prove livelier onstage than on the page; and Pip, the narrator-protagonist of this one, starts with the advantage of having an inner life. He's the orphaned blacksmith's apprentice whose life is transformed when a mysterious benefactor gives him the money to move to London and become a gentleman. Snobbery overcomes him; he becomes ashamed of the people who have always loved him. If anything Pip, who's more corrupted in his manners than in his instincts, spends too much time beating up on himself for what seem like normal human failings, failings that are told rather than shown. Jeff Lillico, however, keeps us with him in an unshakably clear, lucid and responsive performance. He alternates at switchback speed between commentator and participant, rough and smooth, man and boy. As narrator, it's his prerogative to play himself at all ages, and he gives an especially vivid account of the frightened child in his parents' graveyard, surprised by the escaped convict Magwitch who will, in more ways than one, hold Pip's life in his hands.

Other characters are governed by different rules, so a child actress and an adult one divide between them the roles of Pip's two female contemporaries, the warm and sympathetic Biddy and the decidedly cold and unsympathetic Estella. young Naomi Agard, finds more to chew on in Estella, the ultimate spoiled little girl; with Leah doz, her grown-up successor, it's the reverse: She's better as Biddy. But then the grown Estella is a literary contrivance: a woman raised to be selfish, "to break men's hearts", who is yet unselfish enough to warn Pip about it.

There are some flavourful performances among the full-time adults. Kate Trotter forsakes the usual she-dragon accounts of Miss Havisham to give us a self-punishing faded beauty whose "sick fancies" are hauntingly believable; she does, though, run out of variations. Oliver dennis, who makes modesty magical, was obviously born to play Joe Gargery, ever the best of Pip's friends, embarrassed by his own embarrassment. He was equally born to play Mr. Wemmick, the lawyer's clerk who's all humanity at home and all business at business; sensibly, he plays both of them in the most notable of the evening's ongoing quickchange routines. C. david Johnson is punctiliously powerful as Wemmick's employer Jaggers, dickens' great embodiment of a decent instinct in an indecent system. Paolo Santalucia's Hebert Pocket has warmth and energy, if not quite enough of either, and Oliver Becker's Magwitch is suitably scary, though so vocally rough-hewn as to be often incomprehensible.

Shawn Kerwin's set, on a stage that bisects the auditorium, consists of some chairs and a mysteriously unnecessary pillar. Pip's arrival in London is bustlingly well staged but the fatal fire at Miss Havisham's is confusing. So, more damagingly, is the climactic steamboat tussle between Magwitch and his nemesis Compeyson, here transposed to dry land with Compeyson, a shadowy character to begin with, escaping alive, resulting in a lack of closure all around. Indeed the show gives short shrift to all the book's villains; old Orlick, who does for Pip's shrewish sister, is barely present and not at all accounted for, while Bentley drummle's, Pip's amorous rival and symbol of the society that engulfs him, is more talked about than seen. This is not an overpoweringly joyous event; but then Great Expectations isn't an overpoweringly joyous book. It's one of the late, great dark novels. dickens intended to give it an unhappy ending, but was talked out of it. It could, I think, be made ambiguous; and Shamata's staging of the last scene seems initially to be heading in that direction. Maybe next time.

In Anything Goes, by contrast, joy is pretty much unconfined. This 1934 Cole Porter musical takes us back to the days when all the best shows were set on Transatlantic luxury liners. Actually there was no such time, but there should have been. This show has established itself as the quintessence of all the song-and-joke-heavy products of its depressed decade, exuding what the great critic Eric Bentley once described as "a cockeyed devil-may-care philosophy that is certainly attractive and may even be rather useful." It's epitomized here by the moment at which rachel york as "nightclub evangelist" (get that) reno Sweeney gazes down on a scene in which a humble Wall Street runner has been mistakenly hailed - and celebrated - as a gangster, Public Enemy Number One. Cocking her own eye, she gives the only possible response: she goes into the title number which, as staged and choreographed with unstoppable verve by Kathleen Marshall, remains the definitive comment on celebrity culture.

Anything Goes was a work-inprogress even at its premiere, its book enforcedly rewritten at the last moment. The present script (by Timothy Crouse and John Weidman) has just the right amount of self-mockery and has been standard since Lincoln Center presented it in 1987. Marshall's direction now doesn't have the wit and style of Jerry Zaks' then, but it beats London's overstuffed National Theatre production. The book works in all but one of the songs (which include five standards) from the original score, plus five from other Porter sources, including one, the magnificent Easy to Love, which was meant for the show but replaced because the leading man thought it too difficult. (I think the song that replaced it is even harder, but maybe he didn't dare complain twice.) Its intended status may explain why it works so well now, dramatically as well as musically. It seals the love plot.

If anything there are too many songs, or at least too many extended treatments of them, but that seems a grudging complaint when they're all so good. More serious is the disconnect between york's singing and her acting, which sounds like an impersonation of Peggy Lee impersonating Mae West (Miss West in fact crops up here in two of Porter's list songs, suggesting even he could run out of ideas), and the further disconnect between her and the rest of the show. Both she and the audience seem to be under the impression that it's her star vehicle, a sort of Hello Reno, though it's an ensemble piece, and was so even when Ethel Merman played it in '34. If anything the central character is Billy, the playboy broker, who's both the romantic and comic lead; Josh Franklin is not quite to the debonair manner born, but you can't see him defeated by any song, any dance, any gag. Fred Applegate does wonders through understatement as his farcical foil, Public Enemy No. 13 (I wish they'd kept the original gag in which he was superstitious and wanted to bump somebody off to improve his rating), and dennis Kelly does similar as Billy's myopic employer. I initially doubted the wisdom of Edward Staudenmayer's playing the bemused Wodehousian Englishman with such devout seriousness, but he'd won me over by the end. The energy level is fabulous throughout; and who can resist the way the portholes change colour, several times in a number, in sympathy with the sky. Everyone go.
