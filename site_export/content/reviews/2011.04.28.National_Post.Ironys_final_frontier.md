THEATRE REVIEW

The Cosmonaut's Last Message to the Woman He Once Loved in the Former Soviet Union Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

An especially fine scene in The Cosmonaut's Last Message . (we can call it Cosmo for short) involves Vivienne, a Scots speech therapist, and her neighbour Claire, a policewoman. Earlier, we've seen them together socially, as awkward new acquaintances, but this is official. Vivienne's husband Keith, a civil servant, has disappeared, leaving only a neatly folded bundle of clothes by the side of the ocean. Maybe he's drowned; maybe he's pretending. Although as Claire rather tactlessly reminds Vivienne, "one way of seeming dead is to be dead."

Claire also says "Nobody knows him, Viv. That's the awful thing." David Greig's brilliantly woven play, first staged in Britain in 1999, is about not knowing people, and about the forcible efforts, frustrated and frustrating, to get through to them.

One thing we know about Keith that Vivienne doesn't is that on his trips to London, he's been seeing a Russian stripper by the name of Nastasja. "The impossibility of communication" has been a staple of playwrights at least since Chekhov, but Greig's play is the first I know to make it the explicit subject, rather than something to be inferred. Another thing we know about Nastasja is that she is the daughter of a cosmonaut, the partner of the one referred to in the title.

The two of them have been circling the Earth for 12 years, unaware that the Soviet Union that sent them has become former. The object, apparently, was to determine how long they could survive such isolation without going crazy, and it's surprising they've lasted as long as they have. In fact, both men have women on their minds whom they loved; for one it's his daughter, for the other it's an almost-girlfriend with whom he missed his chance by not knowing what to say. We undoubtedly meet the daughter down below; we may very well meet the girlfriend.

The irony in Cosmo, satisfying for us if not for the characters, is that in a play about not connecting, everything connects. One of the cosmonauts is trying to make radio contact with Earth; he only succeeds in ruining the reception on Keith's and Vivienne's TV set. A later attempt is misinterpreted by a scientist who believes in aliens. This man, Bernard, has previously suffered a stroke; Vivienne, because of her work, recognizes the symptoms. She's there because she's following the one clue left by her husband: a Cezanne painting imprinted on his tie. Unfortunately, neither she nor Bernard speak a word of the other's language, barring the odd international phrase like Star Trek. When they hover on the brink of a love scene, it's very moving.

Each of Cosmo's six actors plays two roles: very apt for a play in which everything doubles and everything echoes. There's barely a line that doesn't reverberate somewhere else.

Jennifer Tarver's production, unfortunately, doesn't do the play full justice. On the unforgiving Bluma stage it, too, feels lost in space; as the action moves from country to country, and from terrestrial to extraterrestrial, the production seems to trundle after it.

The acting is very uneven, notably in the matter of accents. Some are at least approximately appropriate; others don't even sound like they're trying. Fiona Byrne's Vivienne, first sheltered and then shattered, is linguistically and emotionally pitchperfect; and she finds a fierce contrast in Sylvia, Nastasja's dogged friend and self-appointed protector.

Sarah Wilson does similarly fine dual delivery as Nastasja, speaking largely in what a lobby warning calls "mature language," though it might be more accurately described as immature language. Raoul Bhaneja combines a succession of cynical barmen with a powerful but vestigially vulnerable Erik. David Jansen hardly differentiates his Keith from his Bernard, though he seems better suited to the former.

The spacemen, too, fall flat with Tony Nappo and Tom Barnett almost indistinguishable, not so much physically as psychologically. There's a desperate playfulness here that the production misses.

The play comes through, though, wittily and even poetically. After all the jumped-up soap operas trying to pass themselves off as serious drama, this is the real thing.
