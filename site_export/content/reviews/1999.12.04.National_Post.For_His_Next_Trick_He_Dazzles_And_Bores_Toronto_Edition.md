THE CONJUROR'S SUITE

Royal Ontario Museum

A couple of years ago David Ben presented a magic show which he called The Conjuror. He performed, in costume, a selection of feats originated by the great illusionists of the 19th century. The tricks were amazing but the presentation stiff. The mixture is pretty much the same in the sequel, The Conjuror's Suite.

The premise of the new show is that, rather than witnessing it in a public theatre we are assisting at a private display in a Victorian or Edwardian salon. Frankly, I didn't notice too much difference. Ben performs in a smaller room at the ROM than he did last time, on a set with some scrambled elements of the drawing- room, but the audience still sits in serried rows and it still feels like a theatre.

There is perhaps a subtle change in the way he talks to us. Before, he acted the Victorian magician. Now, he speaks more or less in his own person about what it was like to be or to see a Victorian magician. He refers back rather too often to the previous show and seems to be trying for a mixture of old and new, formality and chumminess. The strain tells.

He still dresses in period, sporting a frock-coat and cravat, and with a lock of his floppily abundant black hair dropping down over one eye. (By the end of the show it was two locks over two eyes.) When he speaks, his rhythms are halting. He is not an actor and he doesn't carry the vocal or temperamental weight of an old-time prestidigitator. He is not a comedian either, and his banter with the audience -- especially with those, children and adults, whose assistance he enlists -- is forced.

For me, the best magicians, like the best jugglers, are the funny ones.

There is, after all, a built-in montony to the form. You know in advance that they are going to bring off whatever they attempt, and humour is the best way of lubricating the process and involving the audience. It can also, of course, distract the audience, taking its mind off the mechanics -- and in that sense Ben, possibly to his credit, is working without a net.

Not that I could do what he does, or have the first idea how he does it. There are too many card-tricks, montonous for those of us seated farthest from the stage, who are forcibly reminded that we are not in a salon. But there are plenty of more-involving feats. He causes balls to move invisibly underneath a trio of cups: a familiar routine but still a baffling one. In tribute to Harry Houdini he swallows a needle and thread. He robs a lady of her wedding-ring and then presents it to her, mysteriously ensconced in a box within a box within a box. (This, it struck me, was a Houdini escape stunt turned inside out.) This is genuine -- i.e. triumphantly false -- sleight of hand, and the young magicians in my company pronounced it to be all much better than David Copperfield, whose array of smoke, mirrors and dancing-girls they dismissed as high-tech fakery. The only Ben trick that had them thoroughly flummoxed -- not even guessing what kind of process was nvolved, let alone how they worked -- was one in which he identifies a specific word in a specific line on a specific page of one of half-a-dozen volumes of poetry. As he says himself, in one of his happier pieces of patter, this is the kind of thing for which.you might once have been burned at the stake.

I was dazzled, but also rather bored. And pulling this off may be the conjuror's neatest trick of all.
