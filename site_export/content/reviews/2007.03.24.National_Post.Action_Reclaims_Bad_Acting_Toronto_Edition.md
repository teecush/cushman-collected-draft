Last week I tried to describe a recurrent form of bad acting that I called "passion in excess of the object." I also referred to "nagging and pleading." This week my vocabulary in this matter received a welcome boost.

I attended a rehearsal of Action, a Sam Shepard play from the 1970s, that opens next Thursday at the Equity Showcase Theatre. It's a play whose four characters exist in largely unexplained states of obsessive despair. Toward its end we find a man called Jeep (played in this production by Brendan Gall) concentrating

all his energy on gutting a fish while meditating on the frightening fact that he always seems to be eating. Meanwhile, another man, Shooter (Greg Palmer), is crouched under a chair that he's pulled down on top of himself and from which he declares that he will never move, ever. It would be hard to claim that these two guys and the women (Maev Beaty and Aviva Armour-Ostroff ) who share their space communicate with one another, but they do in some sort converse.

Brendan Healy, the director, working on a scene between Jeep and Shooter, gave Palmer a note that struck me as very perceptive. "Can you," he said, "not play deny? Just set the record straight." The concept of "playing deny" is not one that I had encountered before, but it seemed to sum up much that had been bothering me for years. An actor states a feeling or an opinion but inside of just stating it ("setting the record straight"), which can be very powerful, he speaks as if he expected to be contradicted. He is denying something that hasn't actually happened, refuting an objection that hasn't been made. A querulous note sets in. It happens all the time.

Bad acting, as I noted last week, can happen to good actors. The interesting thing about Healy's production in this context is that it sets out to reclaim Bad Acting, to give bad acting a good name. Of course, it all depends what you mean by bad acting. The kind that I've been going on about is something for which Healey and his actors have, I discovered, as little time as I do. Bad acting can only be defined in opposition to what we think of as good acting, and it's these players' contention that we've come to equate good acting with "naturalistic acting." And that this, especially in the constricted form in which we encounter it in films and television, may represent surface reality but does not represent "truth."

I've heard that song before, and I have problems with it. For a start, it seems to me that surface reality -- how people actually talk and behave -- may not be the whole of human life but is an important part of it; if you ignore it you are halfway to treating people as symbols, which is often bad theatre, or to regarding "reality" as some kind of bourgeois mystification, which is always bad politics. I think, too, that if you asked most people what they thought of as good acting, they would say that it was "natural acting." Here I have to quote, yet again, from Kenneth Tynan who once wrote: "the rise of the natural style is an event of ubiquitous date that has taken place roughly twice in every century from the sixteenth onward [Garrick] staggered the public by the naturalness of his manner [Edmund Kean's] command of natural speech revolutionized a theatre inured to Garrick's rantings Breaking decisively with the past, the Moscow Art

Theatre under Stanislavsky inaugurated an acting method founded

on the cataclysmic idea that actors should act natural."

Acting changes because behaviour changes. It's very largely a matter of rhythm. We can respect history but when it comes to judging something new, we respond to what makes us laugh, what makes us cry, what makes us believe.

What we believe depends on the kind of play we're watching. We can accept people expressing their emotions in song or in blank verse, but we want those forms to be personalized. Audiences are very good at identifying conventions and adjusting to them (though much ink has been wasted by theorists who don't believe that they are or should be). The kind of Bad Acting Healey & Co. are trying to reclaim is not bad at all. It's extravagant acting for extravagant circumstances. Palmer made a very good point when he said that what passes for naturalism on TV is actually unnatural. It's circumscribed. When an actor on screen receives bad news, he tries to react to it as quietly and subtly as possible. In real life, we don't actually do this. We cry, we scream, we explode.

The characters in Action are at the end of their respective tethers, and Healey is encouraging his cast to express their desolation as graphically as possible. Gall is breathing differently from the way actors usually do -- "pushing out all your air before talking" -- which, as he says, "puts you in a dangerous place." Let me confess that I'm not convinced that Shepard's play needs the extreme treatment to which this production seems bent on subjecting it; I hear it lighter, more conversational, in line with the old principle that the way to play an absurd situation is to treat it as absolutely normal. That makes it funny, which I suspect is what will happen anyway once it gets in front of an audience. But what I saw was powerful in its own terms, and the more I saw, the more it grew on me.
