(parenthetically, there is always something pretentious about putting a play's entire title either in lower case or in brackets. this one does both. what's it supposed to prove? and how are you meant to pronounce it?)

(nod) is about a family. There are a mother, two sons and a daughter. There was also, once, a father, but one day he got up, left the house, climbed into his boat, sailed it across the local lake and drowned. Mother, too, has just met a watery end -- in the bathtub -- as the play starts, but that doesn't stop her from playing an active and voluble part in the succeeding action. Her children, all now adults, strive to come to terms with their parents' legacy, which was pretty damaging. The house is in a rotting state of disrepair, and so are the children's psyches. They were all disappointments to their mother, and she was a nagging burden to them. They remember mama, and she comes back to haunt them.

Father is not afforded the same corporeal presence, probably because of mysterious, unknowable circumstances in both his life and his death. He is merely a shadow, intermittently projected on to a video screen. It must also be in his honour that the cast, at regular intervals, sway and hurtle around the stage as if they were in the opening scene of The Tempest. They also have a habit of humming What Shall We Do with the Drunken Sailor, which they do with the air of people who are furnishing us with a vital clue to something.

The show is produced by Theatre Gargantua, a troupe with a commitment to mime, music, mixed media and such. But however much of a collaborative effort the production might have been, its text is the work of a single author, the actor and playwright Rick Roberts. Roberts gave a terrific performance in last season's Hotel Loopy, in which he delivered a monologue that was a marvel of gratification perpetually delayed. He never, when writing for other actors, gives them comparable opportunities to delight. His previous play, Fish/ Wife, was trim but desiccated, its two characters patronized abstractions. This new one is less shapely, and outstays its welcome even at 80 minutes, but it strikes more chords on the way.

None of the family members is favoured with a name; they are identified as Mother, Sister, Brother, Young Brother and Tenant -- the last a young girl who has taken up residence in the basement, and whom even the other characters treat as some kind of symbol. All this stylization affords some very effective shortcuts; the family pressures are served up neat, often as sour jokes. Brother (Michael Spence), who can hardly get out of bed, pursues a fitful career as a janitor, this being the nearest he can come to realizing the maternal ambition for him to become a famous scientist. Younger Brother (Aaron Willis) was in the womb when his father departed, and emerged with a hunched back or, as his mother prefers to put it, with very bad posture. Both of these performances have an individual wit, as well as the required ensemble enthusiasm when it comes to reeling, writhing and fainting in coils.

The women are less fortunate. Erica Buss (also the production's sound designer and not, I suspect, a performer much at home with words) finds little in Sister beyond aggressive self-pity. This is a quality she seems to have inherited from Marjorie Campbell's Mother, who dispenses it with a relish that grates on the audience as much as it does on her relatives. (It certainly explains why Father left home, though the place's crumbling state is apparently his fault.) The Tenant (Lori Nancy Kalamanski) makes little impact and has little chance.

The play is billed as "for anyone who has ever had a family." That takes in most of us, and most of us will find something to respond to. Not enough, though. I've managed, so far in my critical life, to resist quoting Tolstoy's celebrated observation about every unhappy family being unhappy in its own way, but the time has come to throw in the towel. This unhappy family has certainly found its own way, but the show's insistence on mystification and -- if there is such a word or even if there isn't -- mythification means that the specifics are barely established and never explored. I appreciate that the play doesn't want to be a soap opera but all the jumping around in time and space or between present and past, fantasy and actuality, can not make it into anything else. And those trappings aren't necessarily as thrilling as they're often assumed to be. Jacquie P.A. Thomas' direction is well-drilled but not especially imaginative.

(As for the title. It's more than possible that the whole play is a dream. And Sister, who's narcoleptic, is the likeliest person to be dreaming it.)
