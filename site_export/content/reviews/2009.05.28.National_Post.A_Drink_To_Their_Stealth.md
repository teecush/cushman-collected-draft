Jim Mezon, in the Shaw Festival's production of A Moon for the Misbegotten, gives the most riotously funny performance I have ever seen in a Eugene O'Neill play. That, admittedly, is not much of a contest; in fact, I can't think of a single competitor. But if it's a departure in my experience of O'Neill, it's also a departure in my experience of Mezon. I associate him with chillingly powerful level-toned characters, men who don't need to raise their voices in order to command respect. I've sometimes wished that he would shout a bit, that he would, literally and metaphorically, get his hands dirty.

Well, here he does, plenty. He plays Phil Hogan, an Irish-American tenant-farmer, tilling unforgiving soil in 1923 Connecticut. Phil is a roaring, swaying drunk most hours of the day and night, except for maybe a few hours, say late morning, when he's just gearing up. Phil has three sons, who have understandably left home; and a daughter, Josie, a strapping lass with a self-nourished reputation as the town tramp. Josie isn't about to leave, for complicated reasons that furnish some of the play's comedy and more of its tragedy. She nags at Phil, he storms at her, but they're locked together and they know it. Jenny Young isn't built to Josie's physical scale, and you sometimes have to close your eyes and imagine somebody else. But what she lacks in heft, she makes up in tenacity. This is a generous performance, in rage as much as in love. Josie has a lot of both, though dispensing the former comes easier. She also has a lot of pain, some secrets and much pride, and the elements combust.

Father and daughter might easily have been transported from an actual Irish play; they have the brogue and the blarney though not, mercifully, the whimsy. Writing Irish seems to have released a richness and sharpness in O'Neill that you don't find elsewhere in his work, and the actors here seem to rejoice in it: "You're worse than dacent," goes one choice insult,

"you're vartuous." If the play were just about Phil and Josie, then it might be a masterpiece and Joseph Ziegler's production an unqualified delight.

But it isn't just about them. It's also about their landlord, Jim Tyrone Jr., a character based on O'Neill's alcoholic actor of an elder brother. Fearing that Jim may be about to sell the place from under them, Phil comes up with a plan to surprise him in bed with Josie so that he will marry her or, at the very least, refrain from evicting her. Jim has always admired Josie, especially -- as he confirms more than once -- her big breasts. A man with extensive experience of whores, Jim seriously doubts Josie's claim to be of their number. It's reckoned by all parties that romance and whisky will do their work, once the two young -- well, younger -- people are alone together under the moon from which the play derives its title.

Moon is one of O'Neill's very late, very long plays: three hours and up. There are acres of repetitive exposition and two minor characters who aren't really necessary. It's true too that by the time the moonlight rendezvous rolls around, we've been made exhaustively aware of the characters' tangle of conflicting expectations, and the scene is potentially richer for it. It turns out, though, that for O'Neill, still obsessed with his own family, the Hogans' predicament is secondary to the self-torment of Tyrone. There are several drawbacks to this: a) he isn't our brother; b) though he too has Irish blood, he speaks in the Yankee gin-mill slang that O'Neill never managed to make sing, interspersed with wiseacre aphorisms like "our land of the free and the get-rich-quick;" and c)-- the fatal one-- he's played by the spiritually clean-cut David Jansen, who at one point manages a passable physical approximation of the delirium tremens but gets nowhere near their despairing essence. The production still manages to squeeze some tenderness out of this strangest of love scenes, but it's a shadow of what it should be, which in turn makes the play's ending less bittersweet than frustrating. I should say that it beats any other production I've seen. It's still O'Neill, great through all his faults, and Mezon's performance is at once toxic and intoxicating: a cocktail of brute cunning and shamefaced compassion, with the ingredients contending for passage through every grimy pore. Not that Phil would ever lower himself to anything as adulterated as a cocktail. - A Moon for the Misbegotten continues until Oct. 9.
