THEATRE REVIEW

Exit the King Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

Eugene Ionesco's Exit the King is a play that seems, provokingly, to have set out to be a masterpiece. Even more provokingly, it succeeds.

Ionesco, the supposed maestro of the Theatre of the Absurd (a gimcrack term used to bestow a false uniformity on many disparate playwrights, and one that he to his credit disliked) wrote the play in 1962 when serious illness brought him face to face with his own mortality. (He got better.) Solipsism, or maybe just narcissism, was an affliction not unknown to Ionesco; he once said that he could write about everybodys problems by writing about his own. But in this play he did create a universal fable. He covers the whole spectrum of a human beings attitude toward death. He takes in both the physical and the metaphysical. He makes it funny and he makes it moving. Soulpepper's new production does the same.

The play's method is to collapse time and to create a metaphor. King Berenger, Ionescos recurring little man/everyman figure but with a crown on, is informed at the beginning of the play that hell be dead at the end of it. That gives him two hours and 20 minutes, with intermission. At first he refuses to believe it; of course he's going to die some time, he knows that, but today? He tries to prove his continuing strength, but at every step his body betrays him. He even cries from his balcony for one of his subjects to exchange lives with him, but nobody answers. (The kingdom's severely depopulated anyway.)

His counsellors remind him that everybody dies; "nobody living has," he replies, which is quite a comeback really. He clings to every sensation, every taste. He cant decide whether he wants to be remembered or forgotten.

Berenger has been alive for 400 years, and king for most of them. His reign, apparently glorious to begin with, is now in ruins, as is his kingdom. The throne room, where the play is set, has an ever-widening crack in its wall and, at least in this production, a fearsome slope in its floor. As a dramatic device, Berengers kingship combines the symbolic (every man the centre of his own universe) and the satirical; indeed the state of Berenger's world, where the environment is really acting up, seems more topical now than when the play was written.

The precedent kings of whom he most reminds us are Canute and Lear. Like the former, he tries under pressure to stop nature in its tracks; like the latter, he comes to realize that he has neglected his poorer subjects. Talking to the maid-of-all-work who seems to constitute the palace's entire domestic staff, he inquires, solicitously and for the first time, into the details of her daily round. Unlike Lear, though, he feels sorrier for himself than for her; what really concerns him now is the memory of the stew she used to serve him. He clings especially to the wonder of carrots.

A play that starts by announcing the protagonists death and then devotes itself to showing it happen is unlikely to be a riot of suspense. The only comparable piece I can think of is the medieval morality Everyman, of which Exit the King is really the secular version; instead of admonishing us to make our peace with God, it tells us to let go of selfishness because, well, really, we wont have any choice. The only previous production of the Ionesco that I have seen was a starkly ceremonial one starring Alec Guin-ness, and it was very boring. Albert Schultz's production, which in this context is something of a revelation, uses a sparky new translation by Geoffrey Rush (who played it in Australia and on Broadway) and Neil Armfield, and is crammed with comic detail, outrageous but never gratuitous. His Berenger is Oliver Dennis, whose resume pre-Soulpepper includes clowning with Theatre Columbus.

Exit the King here starts with the king entering, crossing the stage, nose in air, trailing the very longest of trains; I half expected him to turn a couple of corners and appear again behind it. This most sympathetic of actors never plays for sympathy; he doesn't slight on the pettiness or the peevishness, and he is superb in the symphony of collapsing limbs. He is a very ordinary man, whose weaknesses are magnified by his position and who achieves grandeur by enforcedly transcending them; before then his unceasingly fluctuations, as he clings desperately and indignantly to life, are so consuming and so recognizable as to be suspenseful in themselves.

His subjects, pulling and pushing him both ways, accentuate the tension; all excellent, they are Brenda Robins as his imperious first wife; Karen Rae as his younger and coexistent second; William Webster as a doctor whos all brisk professional protocol; Trish Lindstrom as the universal slavey; and Derek Boyes, who relays the kings ricocheting moods as a series of public-service announcements. It's the cruellest characters, the older queen and the physician, who turn out to be the kindest; they're the ones who counsel Berenger to accept his death - Robins literally sees him through it - while Rae, whose seductiveness is a desperate kind of selfishness, has to leave the field defeated. Lorenzo Savoini's set is a triumph of dilapidation; you half-expect it to fall apart before your eyes.
