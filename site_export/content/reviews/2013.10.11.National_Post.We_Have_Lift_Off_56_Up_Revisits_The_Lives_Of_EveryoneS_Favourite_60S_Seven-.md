Ive been catching up with Up. I'm referring to the British documentary series that started in 1964 when World in Action, a current-affairs program presented by the independent company Granada TV, interviewed 14 seven-year olds from a variety of social and geographical backgrounds. The resulting segment was called 7Up. Seven years after that, Michael Apted, a young director who had been a researcher on the show, had the idea of checking in on the young participants at age 14. Every seven years since then, he has repeated the process. The latest instalment is 56Up. It was aired in the U.K. last year, and is being screened on PBS on Monday. All eight shows are also available in a box set, which is how I've been watching them, over three days, after hearing about them all my adult life. Its an experience both inspiring and heartbreaking.

Its also been somewhat indigestible. The episodes get progressively richer but also longer as each new instalment includes, for purposes of both comparison and exposition, plentiful footage from the old ones. Again and again, we flash back to the seven-yearolds skipping down streets, riding bikes, acting up in class. It's hopeful and it's sad, as the sight of children playing always is - nobody ever gets to be that happy again - and the knowledge of their future lives makes both feelings the more intense. Here, a voiceover tells us as the kids enjoy a group outing to the London Zoo, is a glimpse of Britains future, the CEOs and shop stewards of tomorrow.

As it happens, none of them has grown up to be either of those things. Some of them have fulfilled their initial ambitions. The posh prep-school boys who intended to be a barrister or a solicitor have become those things, very successfully. At the opposite social extreme, or near it, is Tony, the East End boy who realized his childhood dream of being a jockey but lost every race and settled at 21 for his backup plan of being a London taxi driver- which he still is. Hes done well at it; he has a sideline as an actor (usually playing a cabbie) and he's become a celebrity, the public face of the Up series - something of which, he told me on the phone from London, he's very proud. "Im accessible, and Im enthusiastic about it- I know the importance of the films." Also the importance of his own honesty in them I've gotta make it truthful-there's no mendacity in there. As voluble and irrepressible now as he was at seven ("the mould was there") he may be the participant whos changed the least.

The one whos changed the most, the most troubling and the most troubled, must be Neil, the middle-class boy from Liverpool, the most open and enchanting of the sevenyear-olds, who dropped out of university, became a squatter and then homeless, admits to mental health issues (I'm not just afraid of going mad, he said at one point, "I know I am") but has somewhat reinvented himself as a, mostly unpaid, local councillor in a succession of localities. Alone among the 14, he has never married and has no children.

Neither Tony nor Neil expresses any doubts or regrets about being in the programs. Plenty of the others have, especially the three East End girls - Jackie, Lynn and Sue - who are in some ways the heart of the enterprise. All have had problems with relationships, health, work; all have had lives far beyond the limited horizons they first envisaged and that Apted may initially have prophesied for them. As the series proceeds they talk back to him, criticize the show itself for reducing their lives to a recurring set of soundbites, threaten not to come back. But they, and most of the others, always do; they seem committed to it. (Also perhaps, though the show doesn't make a great point of it, to one another. Tony told me that theres always a private screening for all the subjects and everyone always claps after each interviews finished. That's show business. But it's also humanity. And the sense of belonging to something big.) Suzy, the upper-class daughter of a broken marriage (that there are only four women in the lineup is something that Apted has publicly regretted, as well he might) has compared the show to a bad book she cant stop reading. Hers has been another transformation story: from a bitter, chain-smoking 21-year old to, at 28 and ever since, a happy, grounded (if admittedly privileged) wife and mother.

Theyre all articulate, all intelligent, all likable: even John, the barrister, who at first seems intolerably snobbish, and quits because he's been made to look that way, but returns to reveal that he's had his struggles too. He also at one point compares the program to a reality show "like Big Brother." Unfair, I think; the Up films arent competitions. (The only permanent dropout has been Charles, who became a TV producer himself. Conflict of interest?) The projects power is marvellously summed up by Nick, who grew up on a farm in the north of England, and moved to the U.S. to become a physics professor: The idea of looking at people over time is nifty ... it's a picture of everyman ... not an absolutely picture of me, but it's a picture of somebody. Tony, when I spoke to him, said much the same thing: "Were pioneers of what everybodys going to go through; good, bad or indifferent, it's going to happen to everyone." The Up series is television doing what no other medium could; a documentary comparable in sweep and emotional effect to what The Wire achieved in drama. And it's nice that it should have happened almost by accident.
