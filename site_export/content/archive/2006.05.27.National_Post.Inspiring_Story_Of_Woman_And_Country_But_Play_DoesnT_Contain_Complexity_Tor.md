rcushman@lycos.com; Golda's Balcony runs through May 28 at the Elgin Theatre, 416-872-5555.

About the most surprising thing concerning Golda's Balcony, a monologue for and about Golda Meir, is the identity of the playwright. The credit goes to William Gibson, author of the 1960s hits Two for the Seesaw and The Miracle Worker, and it's a shock to find so distinguished a name attached to so bad a play.

Or perhaps that's a naive response. The Miracle Worker, the repertory perennial about the childhood of Helen Keller, is an effective piece of theatre, but its effectiveness depends heavily on imbuing the audience with an unearned sense of accomplishment. We watch Helen buffeted into learning to speak, and we identify so much with her teacher that we are left at the end feeling that we ourselves have worked the miracle. Golda's Balcony goes for the same effect, even more crudely. Nobody could accuse The Miracle Worker of lacking conflict; its two main characters are there, duking it out. Golda's Balcony doesn't even show us the struggles. It merely talks about them, and from only one point of view.

We meet Mrs. Meir at the end of her life, juggling two sets of memories. One covers the Yom Kippur War; the other covers the rest of her life: as a Jewish child in Kiev at the height of the czarist pogroms, as a young immigrant in Milwaukee horrifying her parents by becoming politically (i.e. Zionistically and socialistically) aware, as a pioneer kibbutznik in Palestine along with her somewhat reluctant husband, as a mature woman swearing "never again" after the Holocaust and standing by that vow, eventually, as Israel's first female prime minister. It's an inspiring story: an indomitable woman, and country, surviving and succeeding against huge odds. But we knew that before we went in. The play supplies some biographical and historical details. What it doesn't supply is any complexity or ambiguity.

It does make a few gestures. The title turns out to refer to two different balconies. One is attached to Golda's home outside Tel Aviv with a nice view of the sea. The other, less consoling, overlooks Israel's nuclear facility, where it's building bombs. There, Golda says, elucidating a chicken-soup image that she used at the beginning of the play, "blood at the bottom of the pot." During the 1973 war, she agonizes over the possibility of unleashing devastation on whole cities of innocent people. But the matter is raised very late and to little effect. We see Golda on the phone to her ministers, telling them to get on the phone to Nixon and Kissinger, threatening to use the bombs if the U.S. doesn't come through with more conventional aid. It works.

But there's remarkably little urgency about these scenes; they exist on the same, flat anecdotal level as everything else. And as a dramatic mainspring, it falls short. A character's remorse over something she was forced to do might be very powerful. Something she feared she might be forced to do, but thankfully didn't have to, carries less weight. Even more perfunctory is her last-minute acknowledgment (last-minute in dramatic time, anyway) that the Palestinians also have a claim to a state of their own. It feels like a gesture, and the playwright's rather than the character's. It would probably have been better not to raise the matter at all.

So we have a Golda who admits to a certain "intransigence" (the polite word, she says) and, very much in passing, to a certain sexual recklessness in her early Palestinian years, but otherwise never says, does or thinks a wrong thing. This is psychologically implausible and theatrically impossible. The lack of light and shade is reflected, and possibly exaggerated, in the performance of Valerie Harper, whose range as a stage actress seems very limited. She stresses everything, which amounts to the same as stressing nothing. She plays, according to the publicity, 44 different characters. I'll take its word, but none of them comes to life, with the partial exception (in the play's best scene) of the courtly King Abdullah of what was then called Transjordan. Otherwise, one would like either to meet the people concerned in their own right or to get a lot further into the protagonist's head. The direction (by Scott Schwartz) features the regulation amount of recorded mortar- fire and projected portraits; it does nothing to shape or to enliven the writing or the performance. There was a first ovation to the subject for existing and maybe to the actress for showing up and remembering all those lines.

MATHILDE

Young Centre

for the Performing Arts

55 Mill St. - 416-866-6666

The depth and texture of a marriage are present in this French play (by Veronique Olmi, excell-ently translated by Morwyn Breb- ner) as in few others. In Kelly Thornton's flawless production, Martha Burns plays a writer who has served 15 months for seducing a minor, and Tom McCamus is her ruffled, apparently pragmatic oncologist-husband. Each is an expert at finding the other's tenderest points; tenderness flares up as well. Ends tonight.

CARE

Tarragon Theatre

30 Bridgman Ave. - 416-531-1827

A clear example of the theatri-

cal gulf between accuracy and

truth. Diane Flacks and Richard

Greenblatt play a mature couple

with their first baby, plus assorted friends, helpers, lovers and -- the most compelling character -- the wife's dying father. The issues are compelling and a few of the jokes good, but the effect is sketchy. Ends tomorrow.

WINGFIELD'S

INFERNO

Royal Alexandra

260 King St. West - 416-872-1212

The sixth instalment of the Walt Wingfield saga might also be called Wingfield's Insurance. The rural township's Orange Lodge burns down and stockbroker-turned-farmer Walt is put in charge of its reconstruction. Anecdotes, apparently self-contained and mostly farcical, come together at the end, like a team of shaggy-dog stories on a single leash. Rod Beattie, playing everybody, is reliably amazing. Through June 4.

BOYGROOVE

Diesel Playhouse

56 Blue Jays Way - 416-971-5656

An amusing and endearing deconstruction of the boy-band phenomenon -- groups whom nobody, including the participants, think are any good. Most of the songs, unsurprisingly, are filler, but the script, direction and the four boys (who play many other roles as well) are fast, astute and friendly-with-attitude. Through June 4.

SONG AND DANCE

Danforth Music Hall

147 Danforth Ave. - 416-778-8163

Piotr Stancyk (National Ballet) replaces Roberto Campanella, who sprained his ankle after replacing Rex Harrington, who injured his Achilles tendon. This has been a bad-luck show. But the dance half, with Evelyn Hart partnering whoever and with a fine corps, remains bracing. The song half, despite some of Andrew Lloyd Webber's better ballads and the presence of Louise Pitre, is not very dramatic. Through June 18.

10 DAYS ON EARTH

Berkeley Street Theatre

26 Berkeley St. - 416-368-3110

In Ronnie Burkett's new puppet-play, a mother's love is felt, overwhelmingly, through its forcible removal. Darrell, a 50-year- old mentally challenged man, is orphaned, but doesn't know it. The effect is heartbreaking, though the supplementary material, despite Burkett's technical virtuosity, feels like padding. Through June 24.

THE HEIRESS

Royal George Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Tara Rosling gives an extraordinary performance, rising to tragedy, as Henry James's shy, plain heroine. Mike Shara, smooth and gauche at the same time, is the suitor who tantalizingly may not be faking it. Taut direction by Joseph Ziegler. In rep through Oct. 7.

ARMS AND THE MAN

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Jackie Maxwell's production of Shaw's anti-romantic comedy is as cute as a whole row of buttons; this has its charms but ends up selling the play short. Peter Hutt and Patrick Galligan give entertaining performances, but the exaggerated Balkan sets and costumes dominate the evening. In rep through Oct. 29.

HIGH SOCIETY

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

The show of the movie musical of the play (that was also a movie). It never worked, and this busy production is particularly coarse. The augmented Cole Porter score is made up not of songs the characters just have to sing, but of songs it's not quite impossible they might; it's dehydrating. Dan Chameroy and the sets are good. In rep through Nov. 19.

THE LORD

OF THE RINGS

Princess of Wales Theatre

300 King St. W. - 416-872-1212

At its best, it's wonderful; at its worst, it's far better than doubters ever thought it could be. Scenically and technically, it's a marvel; and the adpatation of Tolkien's trilogy is outstandingly intelligent. The musical component varies, in style and effect. Notable performances by Evan Buliung, Michael Therriault and assorted hobbits. Indefinite run.

LAST WILL AND VIDEOTAPE

Mysteriously Yours

2026 Yonge St. - 416-486-7469

The corpse in the latest interactive comedy-thriller from Mysteriously Yours dinner theatre is billionaire William Randolph, but he isn't a spoof Hearst or even a spoof Citizen Kane. The show parodies nothing but itself, which is a pity. It's coasting, but Jean Daigle (as a Newfie cop) has as much zest and authority as ever, and the dinner is much better than might be expected. Indefinite run.

SECOND CITY: RELOADED

The Second City

51 Mercer St. - 416-343-0011

This is the troupe's first revue in their new space and one of their best shows, with a manic quality we seldom see from them. Especially good are Anand Rajaram, as a Sikh taxi driver who discomfits his Toronto passenger by revealing a Scots accent, and Matt Baram, a walking nervous breakdown. Improv, previously confined to the end of the evening, now crops up within the show itself. Indefinite run.
