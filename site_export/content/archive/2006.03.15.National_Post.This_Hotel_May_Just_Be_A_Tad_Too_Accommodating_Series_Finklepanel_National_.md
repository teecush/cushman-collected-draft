The next episode of At the Hotel airs March 28 at 8 ET on CBC.

Ken Finkleman, the TV auteur behind The Newsroom, Foreign Objects and More Tears, turns his acerbic lens on the hospitality industry in his new six-part series At The Hotel. Our three Finklefans will check in after each new episode to see how the service measures up to his past wry romps.

OUR GUESTS:

- J. Kelly Nestruck, Arts & Life reporter

- Vanessa Farquharson, Arts & Life reporter

- Robert Cushman, the Post's theatre critic

ROBERT: So, this week's musical number wasnt from Guys and Dolls. It was Always True to You in My Fashion from Kiss Me Kate. But what is the point? Spoof? Commentary? Or just a way to liven things up by reflecting someone elses glory? As Cole Porter himself might have put it, anything goes.

And I think that's the problem over all; the trouble with a hotel is it's too -- whats the word? -- accommodating. Anyone can stay there, so anything can happen there. And so you have a grab bag of one-off story-lines stuffed into the shell of an ongoing narrative. Thats what soap opera does, but this show's style implicitly claims to be above soap opera. That said, there were some nice parallels going on this week: A comedy routine set in a funeral home, and a flash-forward to a real funeral; The comics getting deserved applause that turns out to be cued; The corrupt politician (or is that a tautology?) getting undeserved applause that's shamefully spontaneous. Maybe it will all come together somehow.

KELLY: I dont know. Judging by the melodramatic way the chambermaid and the writer fell into each others arms and the sudden death of the precocious girl's mother, I'm not particularly optimistic that the overall narrative arc is being put together, bit by bit, into a work of art. Having a visions no solution. Everything depends on execution ... Whoops, sorry. This breaking into musicals is catching.

I was more interested in the stand-alone stories from episode two. The old double act reuniting for a television special was quite touching, with real comic chemistry between Maury Chaykin and Kathy Lasky. The story of the closeted gay National Post columnist outing a closeted gay politician during an election was a little inert, however. Given the number of out and not-so-out pols weve had in Cabinet over the past few federal governments, it felt like a plot visiting from the United States.

VANESSA: At least in this episode the musical number cut to commercial immediately after the final beat, so the couple didn't have to awkwardly transition back to their banal conversation about the "cashmeeya sweater. But I agree with both of you in that, considering we're a third of the way through the series, there hasnt been much direction or development. And its bizarre how some characters -- namely Chaykin and Lasky -- can be so engaging, while others -- like pretty much all the chambermaids and politicians -- are so trope-y.

ROBERT: I guess the most obvious reason that Chaykin and Lasky's bits worked the best is that the show treats them with a certain amount of affection. And accuracy; they seem to have been a homegrown Nichols and May, with Lasky's adenoidal inflections in the funeral-home sketch a dead ringer for Elaine Mays telephone operator. (Bell Telephone does not need your di-ime, sir; Bell Telephone has plenty of di-imes of its own.") Sorry, Ive been wanting to quote that for decades. So, apparently, has Ken Finkleman. I guess he's a showbiz baby at heart. And Chaykin, as a comic whose subsequent career has been described as a downward trajectory directly opposite to Mike Nichols', won sympathy without ever pleading for it.

But no acting could redeem the political plot where the writers obviously feel such unquestioning contempt for the characters -- the journalist, "a self-hating Jew" as well as a sexual hypocrite and a careerist -- that theres no way they can bring them to life.

KELLY: Well, Finklemans character on The Newsroom was a hypocritical, careerist, self-hating Jew, too, but he was actually believable and very funny, especially in the original series.

There may well be a group of bellhops and chambermaids following this series with as much relish as my journalist friends followed the original Newsroom. I doubt it, though.

VANESSA: I think Robert's right: the detestable roles are the ones that fall flattest, that are the most disposable. They're not even close to being the type of characters viewers love to hate, theyre more like stereotypes we wished we cared enough about to hate. But Im still holding out for a big resolution at the end, or at least some insane twist by episode five. And in the mean time, I'm content with the talent there is, especially Chaykin and Lasky, but also little Piper (Samantha Weinstein), whose sombre phone conversation with her aunt after her mother dies is one of the most stirring performances I've seen a child give on television.
