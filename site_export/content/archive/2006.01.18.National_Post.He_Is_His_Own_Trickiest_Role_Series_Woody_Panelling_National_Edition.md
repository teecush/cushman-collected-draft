Tomorrow: How bad are his recent films, really?

Match Point, the new Woody Allen film opening on Friday, is said to be his best in years. To mentally prepare, we're reviewing the highs and lows of his career all this week. Today's panel: - Marni Soupcoff, member of the Post's editorial board

- Robert Cushman, the National Post's theatre critic

- Chris Knight, the National Post's chief film critic

TODAY'S SUBJECT: The 1980s and 1990s in Allen's oeuvre.

MARNI Broadway Danny Rose, Hannah and Her Sisters and Crimes and Misdemeanors are wildly different movies. The first is a wacky comedy, as told by Borscht-belt comedians; the second is a sensitive family drama and the third is a dark exploration of guilt and conscience (or the lack thereof). Yet all three films are dominated, if not completely overtaken, by the one thing they have in common: their central Woody Allen character.

As Chris pointed out yesterday, it hardly matters what his name is in each film, because the Woody character is always really just Woody -- a man who runs on guilt and anxiety and does what he can to insulate himself from a harsh outside world where the unscrupulous get ahead. By the '80s, we all knew this fellow so well that he was easily reduced to stereotype, and I'd argue that his presence got in the way of, rather than enhanced, the stories he was telling.

ROBERT I think Marni's right, at least about two of them. In Broadway Danny Rose, I simply think Woody's miscast himself; I just don't believe in him as this small-time actors' agent. He may know that world, but he doesn't belong in it. He's too sensitive, too intellectual, too Woody (and the fact that Mia Farrow's transformation is amazing -- she really is a phenomenal actress) only makes that more apparent. In Crimes and Misdemeanors -- well, he's his usual nebbish, funny self, so his failed-artist story doesn't match up to the supposed weight of the parallel Martin Landau murderer story. Of course, Woody's onscreen persona is always irresistible in itself, but you know something? -- I think his best picture is Purple Rose of Cairo, which he doesn't appear in. And I like Sweet and Lowdown a lot, too.

MARNI I think it's true that Woody was the wrong man to play Danny Rose. When you're looking for someone to believably portray a somewhat naive, trusting person who doesn't see betrayal coming, Woody is just not a very good choice. We can't help but know that he would have seen all the back-stabbing coming well in advance (and even imagined some that wasn't there).

People who went to see these movies in the theatres may have been expecting and craving the whole Allen schtick. But in retrospect, it is sad to watch them and see the shadows of the better films that might have been had Allen only shown a little more restraint with his own persona. Of course, I'd also say that Crimes and Misdemeanors could have used a little more directorial restraint in general. But it nonetheless had the potential to be an excellent film.

CHRIS I always felt the Woody subplot in Crimes made a nice counterpoint to the film's more serious aspects (Woody supplied the misdemeanour, Landau the crime), like the players in Hamlet. But Robert's got a point -- there's an entire sub-genre of, er, Woodyless films by Allen. Purple Rose of Cairo and Radio Days are my favourites from this period, although he did kind of appear in the latter, as the narrator and in the form of a little red-haired kid. Purple Rose works so well because unlike so many of the films in which Woody doesn't act, he hasn't cast a stand-in for himself (a la Will Ferrell in Melinda and Melinda or John Cusack in Bullets Over Broadway).
