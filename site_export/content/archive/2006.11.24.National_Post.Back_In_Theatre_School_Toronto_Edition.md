Our theatre critic Robert Cushman reviewed The History Boys at Bradhurst Theatre when it was being performed on Broadway in June.

- - -

Alan Bennett made his playwriting debut around 1968 with a comic allegory of England's past and present, set in a boys school and entitled Forty Years On. Forty years further on, hes back at school, and with comparable intentions. The History Boys was staged to acclaim at London's National Theatre and on Broadway, where it received more raves and just about every award available. It deserves all the praise and all the prizes.

Bennett brilliantly shows eight budding history specialists, attending a northern English school in the 1980s, whose headmaster is set on improving the place's position in the higher education league-tables. Opposed to him is a pudgy, near-retirement English teacher called Hector. Under his tutelage, the boys learn poetry but also learn about old movies, of the Now Voyager and Brief Encounter vintage, and about old songs. There are performances in this play of Bye Bye Blackbird and Bewitched, Bothered and Bewildered that are more poignant and more entertaining than songs from any new musical in years.

The play keeps jumping between past, present and future, between enactment and commentary. It keeps you on your toes, but it never confuses. Nicholas Hytners production backs up the onstage action with grainy video footage that brings the whole school indelibly, and often hilariously, to life. The fluency of this production, its quicksilver mastery of mood, is exhilarating, and so is the precision of the performances. There isnt a false note in the show. Richard Griffiths, a master of quiet comedy that transpires of quiet heartbreak, is the embodiment of Hector, solid but sagging. And not only is it hard to imagine this play done by non-British actors; it's hard to imagine it done by British actors other than these. The eight boys are astonishingly good, individually and as a group.

To be honest, the writing gives only four of them much chance to be individuals, so an epilogue in which we learn of their subsequent careers has only half its intended impact. Indeed, the entire state-of-the-nation aspect is only fitfully effective; as indeed was the case with Forty Years On. But the more immediate picture is perfect; and if the bittersweet ending doesn't quite justify itself in terms of structure, it does so overwhelmingly in terms of character and atmosphere. Its tempting to put the show in the embarrassment-of- riches category, except that theres nothing embarrassing about it. But as for richness and sheer pleasure: I doubt that there is currently a better example, anywhere, of what the theatre is for.
