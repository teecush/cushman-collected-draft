popcorn@nationalpost.com

Quentin Tarantino has said the sign of a good film is that it makes you want to go home, eat some pie and talk about it. With that in mind, our Popcorn Panel features film buffs feuding in this space each week.

THIS WEEK'S PANEL:

- Vanessa Farquharson, Arts & Life reporter.

- Robert Cushman, theatre critic.

- Craig Courtice, a short filmmaker who isn't very tall

This week's pie: Steak & kidney.

This week's film: Match Point.

VANESSA Woody Allen's career had come to the point where it was either come back or die. Thankfully, with Match Point, he's proved there's plenty of creative genius still brewing in his septuagenarian head and it goes beyond both the artistic and financial boundaries of New York City.

As Craig remarked last week, this film is much like a visit from our favourite neurotic uncle. This time, he's being less funny and more intense, but he's still entertaining. The story he tells involves not the usual suspects but a new lineup of faces: Jonathan Rhys-Meyers, Scarlett Johansson, Emily Mortimer and Matthew Goode. Their characters are all living the life of tennis, opera and adultery in contemporary London -- they are a breed who pick out art for their walls at the Tate Modern, and who shame one another for ordering chicken ("bo-ring!") at a restaurant.

I really enjoyed this film for its subtle eeriness, the overriding sense of suppressed desperation and the sort of Slim Aarons-does-London style of cinematography. But did you guys miss the zingy one-liners? And were you convinced of the Hitchcockian storyline?

ROBERT I didn't miss the one-liners. I would have if the film had been set in New York, where I would also have expected Woody to deliver them; if he had, and they had, I would certainly have laughed, but it would have harmed the film, as it did Crimes and Misdemeanors, of which Match Point is the improved, more suspenseful and far less portentous version. I didn't make the Hitchcock connection, but it's certainly valid; I found myself thinking of The Talented Mr. Ripley (handsome amoral outsider) and Room at the Top (erotically torn, up-ward-marrying outsider). And the nod to Crime and Punishment at the start turns out to be very specific. My belief strained a bit at the more violent twists, but the aftermath justified them. And there is at least one very good joke. When Rhys- Meyers takes his wife to see The Woman in White the music cuts out after two seconds -- as opposed to the luxuriant lengths of real opera that otherwise fill the soundtrack.

CRAIG Thank goodness for Mr. Cushman to fill us young 'uns in on the deeper levels on which this film works. It was I who felt like the senior Allen fan at the screening I attended. Lots of twentysomethings there to gawk at Johansson and Rhys-Meyers, expecting a light comedy. But by the end, the kids were shocked, which is kind of shocking itself considering their generation grew up on Jackass and its ilk. Who knows, maybe we'll see a spike in rentals of old Woody films as the kids understand what the fuss was about.

One point about Scarlett -- this is a movie star. Her entrance in this film is what screen legends are made of. That white dress, the smoky voice, the sexy dialogue. I just hope she survives the Hollywood gossip machine, because she's got the goods. And isn't it nice to see a real woman portrayed in a film these days. The sex scenes in Match Point celebrate curves and hips, and Scarlett deserves credit for baring these bits considering the obsession we seem to have with skinny.

VANESSA I have to disagree with you. I like Johansson, but I thought she gave one of her weakest performances here, especially in her entrance -- her delivery of that line, "Who's my next victim?" fell flat. But I assumed this was all part of Woody's plan: Johansson's character is a bad actress, so perhaps he wanted her to act the role poorly to make it more meta. The best performance was by Matthew Goode playing the gay-vague, argyle-and-equestrian- loving Tom. His comment on how Nola was looking "rather hard" these days was dropped with just the right amount of fake shame.

I wonder, though: What will the Brits make of this? I have citizenship to the Land of Bad Teeth, but I believe Robert lived there for quite some time, so he could probably wager a better guess.

ROBERT I'm with Craig about the Scarlett woman. I thought she was great, and a very believable detour for Rhys-Meyers: sensuous enough, and chipped-away at enough by life, to distract him from his career and his too-perfect wife. And I don't think you can play a bad actress by acting badly, or that Woody, who's blessedly old- fashioned about these things, would have wanted her to try. It struck me that this may be the only Allen film to have real sex in it -- as opposed to people endlessly talking about it, or the absence of it. As for London -- if I still lived there, the film might well strike as touristy but, as it is, it made me nostalgic in the same way his Manhattan films made me nostalgic for New York. Anyway, he gravitated to the new parts of London that I like --the Tate Modern, the refurbished South Bank -- so I shared his romantic view, so at odds with what happens within it. It will be interesting to see if he learns to take the place for granted. At the moment, he's plainly in love with it.

CRAIG I'm going to make a bold prediction: Match Point will surprise when Oscar nominations come out on Tuesday. Woody is an American icon, and the image of him and Scarlett together will be too much for the Academy to resist. The film followed the slow release pattern of Million Dollar Baby, and Woody even travelled to Hollywood to promote it. I think it will be up for best picture, best director and best supporting actress, and I think it will win for best screenplay.
