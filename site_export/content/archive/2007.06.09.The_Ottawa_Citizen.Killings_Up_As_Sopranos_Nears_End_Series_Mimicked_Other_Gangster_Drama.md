The Sopranos, 9 p.m., tomorrow, The Movie Network

- - -

Spoiler alert: This article discusses last week's episode of The Sopranos. (The mob drama airs its final episode tomorrow night.)

With its penultimate episode last week, The Sopranos turned into a gangster drama. Or rather, it became like other gangster dramas.

Just as The Godfather closed with Michael Corleone disposing, in montage, of all his enemies, so in this one the body count went way up, as the bosses of New York and New Jersey finally turned on one another.

One of the accusations that Tony Soprano's mother and uncle flung at him (behind his back) in the very first episode was that he was "thumbing his nose at New York." It's been a long road, but that struggle finally came to a head. Only, this being The Sopranos, things did not go so smoothly, at least not on the Jersey side.

They started out all right, with Silvio paying a social call on Burt Gervasi, the snitch who'd been trying to persuade him to cross over to Phil Leotardo's bunch in New York, and efficiently garrotting him. So, no qualms about Silvio's loyalty.

Phil knows it, because he promptly placed Sil on his hit list, along with Tony himself and, to his subordinates' surprise, Bobby Bacala, the apparently harmless former driver.

Tony got wind of this, via FBI Agent Harris, and lay counter- plans, calling guys from Italy to execute his New York rivals. But maybe he laid on too many intermediaries, or maybe you can't trust outside help, because the guys who go gunning for Phil ended up shooting an innocent lookalike and the man's daughter.

Cunning Phil has gone to ground. He despises the Sopranos for not being a real Family at all, just "a jumped-up crew," and his delegates certainly seem more efficient than Tony's. This episode ended with Bobby dead and Silvio in a coma. Bobby, in a prolonged but effective sequence, got shot while shopping for one of his favourite model trains. Silvio was bushwhacked in his car, just outside the Bada Bing. There was a memorable shot of half-naked girls and their customers fleeing as he bites the dust.

Lest we feel too sorry for Sil, the sight of him crawling on the ground couldn't help but raise memories of Adriana in a similar position as he disposed of her a few seasons ago: by common agreement, the most shocking whacking ever on The Sopranos.

Remember how good Steve Van Zandt was in that scene, making clumsily nice with Adriana up to the moment of drawing his gun? Have we seen the beginning and end of Van Zandt the actor? He was quoted in Vanity Fair as saying that he'd never acted before The Sopranos and might well never do so again.

Last seen running for his life was Silvio's car-mate Patsy Parisi. We can probably assume that he was a loyalist, not a rat. But when he gets his breath back, Patsy -- as the Soprano empire crumbles around him--may feel a modicum of satisfaction for the death of his twin brother. Previously, he had to slake any thirst for vengeance by urinating in Tony's pool.

This episode saw Tony covering that pool. Perhaps it's a seasonal thing, perhaps it's to discourage A. J. from a second suicide attempt. By episode's end, Tony was out of the house, having sent wife and kids off separately and ensconcing himself in a safe house with Paulie.

Paulie wasn't on Phil's list; maybe his loyalty is compromised; maybe (and more likely) Phil, rightly, didn't think him important enough. Paulie has had his stab at changing sides, and it worked out bitterly for him. And though Tony, a few weeks ago, thought seriously of killing him, it seemed to be more for his loose mouth than for any suspicions.

Anyway, Tony last week had to face a far more wounding rejection. Dr. Jennifer Melfi abandoned him. At a dinner party full of psychiatrists, Dr. Elliot Kupferberg, the shrink's shrink, repeated his admonitions about therapy for sociopaths serving only to enable them, and even let fall the identity of her patient. This seemed appallingly unprofessional, and I had a hard time believing it. But it cues what is presumably Tony's last consultation. The Duchess of Melfi, as I like to think of her, told him that, after seven years, she can do nothing more for him. Tony reacted with furious self- pity. Every word he said fits in with what Melfi has read about sociopaths' hypocrisy and sentimentality.

Tony's love for his family may count for little against his other activities, but it seems real. And isn't Melfi just angry with herself for having, as she now sees it, been taken in for so long, and for having made no difference? (In the latter case, she's wrong. The differences were for the better. The worse would have happened anyway.) She tried to be clinical and impersonal in dismissing him, but what came out is the opposite.

The scene played like a marital breakup, as bitter and petty as the one Tony and Carmela had. Only, they got together again.

But there is, come to think of it, no guarantee that Tony and Carmela will set eyes on one another again, at least not alive. Would The Sopranos dare do that to us? I doubt it. But I wouldn't take bets on it.
