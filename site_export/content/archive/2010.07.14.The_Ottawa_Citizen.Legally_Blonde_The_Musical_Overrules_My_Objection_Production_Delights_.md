I have an instinctive hostility to any show entitled Whatever: The Musical, but in this case I have to relax it.

It may well be my good fortune that I never saw the movie, but having now beheld the stage version, I am unable to imagine Legally Blonde as anything but a musical. Its a good one, too: silly and sensible in about the right proportions.

For any fellow ignorami out there: Legally Blonde tells of pink-clad golden-locked Elle Woods, Californian major in fashion merchandise who at curtain-rise is all set to get married to the boy of her dreams, to her own deep satisfaction and the squealing delight of her chorus-load of girlfriends.

But instead of a proposal she gets a rejection: Her prospective fiance -- whose name, Warner Huntington III, pretty much sums him up -- tells her that he is off to Harvard Law School and is dumping her for someone more befitting his wealth and social standing.

Elle is undaunted. Shes already demonstrated her educational attainments by sending back a dress she knows at a glance to be last year's model. Now, assisted by a high LSAT score attained in double-quick time (and also by her father's money), she follows Warner to Harvard Law, hoping to win him back.

Why she should want to is a question the show never quite answers; one of my lasting regrets is that she doesnt just slug the guy halfway through the first act. To be honest, she hasnt even done it by the end of the second, though she has seen the light in other ways.

Still blond, and still pink right down to her laptop, Elle is at first a laughingstock in class, but she proves to have a quick mind plus an intuitive sense of justice and how to attain it.

The feared Professor Callahan, notorious for humiliating vulnerable students, invites her to join his team of interns, where she helps win an apparently unwinnable murder defence case. She also finds love.

The story is a little like Erin Brockovitch Lite. It might also be called the photo-negative of Wicked, in that it shows that a rich and beautiful girl can overcome her disadvantages as effectively as a poor and plain one.

And, I have to say, with a lot less whining.

The music and lyrics are by Laurence O'Keefe and Nell Benjamin, a husband-and-wife team whose male half was previously responsible for the underrated score of Bat Boy The Musical. (I should know; I was one of those who underrated it.) Their pop-flavoured tunes here are cheerful rather than distinguished, but their lyrics are excellent, technically and dramatically.

The opening Omigod You Guys perfectly captures the overheated enthusiasm of privileged female West Coast youth. Chip on My Shoulder is a pungent, narrative-propelling number begun by Emmett, the prof's up-from-poverty top student and Elles guide, philosopher and predestined more-than-friend, and subsequently taken up by nearly everybody else.

The shows first half works, delightfully, as a cartoon. The second half sometimes deviates into unwelcome solemnity, with the obligatory inspirational title number. But it mostly keeps its temper, not to mention its tempo.

As for the cast: Becky Gulsvig's Elle is bright and charming, in a standardized kind of way, while Natalie

Jay Johnson is comparably cookie-cut as her man-handled hairdresser buddy giving off indistinguishable squeaks of joy and frustration. Both ladies have pet dogs, one of whom can yelp on cue.

The greatest individuality, though, comes from the male humans. D.B Bonds' Emmett is an offbeat hero for whom one can genuinely root, while Jeff McLeans Warren is an antihero whom one can cheerfully despise while still enjoying his singing voice.

The most vivid of all, though, from his first impregnable entrance and first ringing note, is Michael Ruperts Professor; this performer, once the male ingenue of his Broadway generation, has matured into a singing Alec Baldwin, sneering magnetically.

He later declines into outright villainy, which the show may think sharpens its satiric point but actually blunts it; the things Callahan represents are too insidious to be passed off as melodrama.

This isn't a dyspeptic show like Urinetown, but it has a brain. When a lyric mentions Thomas Hobbes, you can believe that somebody has read him.

Legally Blonde The Musical runs at the Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto until Aug. 8.
