THE BLONDE, THE BRUNETTE AND THE VENGEFUL REDHEAD

Studio Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

There are more characters than you might expect from the title of the Australian play The Blonde, the Brunette and the Vengeful Redhead but fewer players. The author, Robert Hewett, has written a play built around a single incident, to be performed by a single actress, who is however required to play seven roles, with seven different hair-colourings. It is, obviously, a succession of monologues, which could easily be a recipe for boredom. In fact it turns out to be compelling and even moving. It is also, in its Stratford production, superbly performed. Lucy Peacock does the honours and, even allowing for the lack of on-stage competition, walks deservedly off with them.

To start with the title roles: The carrot-topped avenger is a suburban housewife whose dark-haired friend and neighbour informs her that her husband is carrying on with a flaxen floozie. The wife seeks a confrontation, with dire results. The catastrophe spreads outward, and we get to meet some of the others caught up in it: an English doctor on an emergency ward who finds herself with a personal stake in one of her cases; a bereft young boy; an aged female friend, sympathetic but judgmental, even in the act of refusing to pass judgment; and the husband, who seems to deserve all the terrible things that get said about him. And there is still another character, never heard from but much spoken of, whose development over a period of years turns out to be crucial.

The action has been transported from Australia to Canada with only one textual glitch. (The doctor says that her colleagues call her "that Pommy bitch". That is not a phrase we use around here. Not the adjectival part of it anyway.) The implication, plausible enough, is that this is a story that could happen anywhere, or at least in any place with shopping malls. That is not in itself an earth-shattering observation, but in Geordie Johnson's production, designed by Michael Gianfrancesco, it's given a powerful reality by a pitiless series of video stills, projected while the actress, in silhouette, changes costumes and, of course, wigs. There is a constant counterpoint as the writing and performance particularize the characters in an environment which, if not certifiably dehumanizing, is certainly anonymous.

There are times when the play skirts caricature, notably with the nosy neighbour who turns out to have her own sexual agenda, and whose censorious hypocrisy, though certainly funny, is a little too complete to be credible. (How enviable to be that free of doubts about yourself.) And there may be some sentimentality in the depiction of the boy, whose situation can hardly fail to pull at your heart and so should, by the highest standards, be treated as rigorously as possible. He also muddies the convention. The others seem to be speaking their thoughts; he seems to be talking to someone.

Peacock, in fact, is required to play not just a variety of roles or even of moods, but of styles. Her triumph is, among other things, one of brinkwomanship, since she never undersells a character but never overstates an emotion. Her most obvious tour-de-force is of course as the husband, who is introduced in an outstandingly witty piece of staging befitting what turns out to be a devastating portrait of a man whose capacities for self-delusion, self-interest and self-pity outstrip even those of the lady next door. (It's consoling to reflect that this merciless unveiling of male solipsism was actually written by a man.) The unavoidably light vocal texture actually lends an edge; Brecht would have approved.

But it's the Vengeful Redhead herself who cuts deepest, even though on first encounter, she doesn't seem capable of depth. She's placid, level and accepting: strenuously so. And it's all too believable that, when she does lash out, terrible things will ensue. If a central theme emerges through the monologues, it's repression and its consequences; it gives a face, or faces, to the production's emphasis on urban (or suburban) anomie. The redhead is the only character given a second speech, which here translates as a second chance. It's about wisdom through endurance; it's spoken by a person we barely recognize as the one we first met, but put the two together, add in the complete breakdown and reconstruction of a personality, and it's credible. It may be presumptuous to say so but in her plumbing of this last scene Peacock seems to grow, not only as an actress, but as a woman.

The play has anecdotal rather than universal force, but that's enough to be going on with. After a succession of plays, monologues and others, that make demands on our respect by advertising their authenticity, it's quite a relief to find one that is written, unabashedly, from the outside.
