CAT ON A HOT TIN ROOF

Avon Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Tennessee Williams' Cat on a Hot Tin Roof, superbly revived at Stratford, is a three-act play unlike any other. Its action is continuous -- the first line of each new act is a repeat of its predecessors last -- and maybe some hardy director will one day pick up on that and play it straight through without intermissions. It might then shake down into a seamless whole, a single rushing stream on which the lights keep shifting. As it stands, and has often been pointed out, it keeps changing shape and focus while maintaining a ferocious hold on its story.

Act One is a duet between Brick, the crippled, alcoholic football- hero son of the Southern millionaire Big Daddy, and his wife Maggie, self-surnamed the Cat. Or rather its a solo for Maggie, who is desperate that she and Brick should have a child so that they can grab a major share of the family fortune, with sullen interjections from Brick, who wants as little as possible to do with Maggie, especially sexually, and is drinking to forget.

Act Two brings Brick up against Big Daddy himself, whose birthday it is. This act is more truly a double, for though the father does most of the talking, he does goad his son out of his cage. Brick's shame is that a relationship with his best friend Skipper, that may or may not have been homosexual, led Skipper to kill himself. Big Daddy has terminal stomach cancer but thinks he's been reprieved; Bricks revenge is to tell him the truth -- an unaccustomed thing in these latitudes or indeed, as the play repeatedly tells us, anywhere. Act Three is an embattled ensemble for the whole family, including Bricks lawyer-brother Gooper and his wife Mae, who have five kids with a sixth on the way. If there's a central figure here it's Big Mama, who, having spent two acts in volubly happy denial, has to cope with the facts and, after taking time out to melt into a piteous puddle, does so.

What binds all this thrillingly together, in the writing and in Richard Monettes direction, is character conflict and atmosphere. The scene throughout is Brick and Maggies bedroom, where they get no privacy. Lorenzo Savoini's set is a series of tall shuttered window frames, open to a darkling sky, with a balcony outside that, we assume, runs right around the house. Here Gooper and Mae's brood - - the no-neck monsters" as their aunt Maggie unfondly calls them -- run wild; they also erupt into the room itself. Interruption at tense moments is a residential hazard in this house since Big Mama, whether from hospitality or paranoia, forbids locked doors. Its a brilliant theatrical device, which emerges here as a series of perfectly timed explosions. The culmination, one of the great grotesque scenes in modern drama, is the kids unctuous rendition of Happy Birthday, with the parents who coached them looking on: a real all-American moment.

Monette's estimate of Williams in general, and this play in particular, is higher than mine; I don't feel that were in the presence of Greek tragedy, modern style. Id call it high-grade melodrama -- a form I like -- with a human face. But the idea of playing the longer, lyrically charged speeches downstage and out front works very well; each major character gets to reflect or suffer in solitude before returning, newly enlivened to the fray. (Fray, in this play, is just another word for life.) Both Cynthia Dale as Maggie and David Snelgrove as Brick seem freed up by their Southern accents. Hers sometimes courts caricature, but she projects a very fetching mix of daintiness and sensuality, aiming her body like an arrow at her husband, and faltering only when she reaches the peak of rage that provokes him into throwing his crutch at her. He, alternately brooding and taunting, gives his best performance to date.

The gap in the electric fence is James Blendick's Big Daddy, a perfectly intelligent reading of the lines minus the crude elemental force for which they cry out. (Williams thought the character his best-written, and he was probably right.) Without that power, the old man's arraignments of "mendacity" -- the plays last, best chance of establishing a universal theme -- seem like special pleading. Its not the actor's fault, though, that Williams' decision to bring him back in the last act seems more mistaken than ever; he would be more powerful, as first envisaged, as an offstage howl of agony. Lally Cadeau's Big Mama, dolled-up and smothering, is perfect; you see just where she is coming from and just why her husband can't stand her. Brigit Wilson is a properly screeching Mae, and Thom Marriott a superbly deceptive Gooper; at first just a set of dentures in a suit, he comes into his professional kingdom at that infallible theatrical standby, the squabble over the will.
