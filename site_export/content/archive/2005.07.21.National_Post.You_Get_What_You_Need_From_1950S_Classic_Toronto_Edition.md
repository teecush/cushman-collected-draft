Bus Stop is at the Royal George Theatre until Nov. 27; 1-800-511- SHAW.

BUS STOP

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

Everybody is nostalgic for roadside diners, and the one designed by Sue LePage for the Shaw Festival production of Bus Stop looks good enough to be franchised. The play itself, William Inge's Broadway hit of 1955, also calls up memories of a neater if not happier time, a period in which dramatic characters got together to interact in confined spaces for tightly limited periods -- and emerged the wiser for the experience. Truths are told, destinies are linked and even the remaining loose ends have a tidiness about them. Of its kind, Bus Stop is about as good as it gets.

It has the double advantage of seeming both fresh and familiar since, though famous as both a play and a film, it is not often seen, at least not in these parts. A blizzard brings a small busload of people -- a driver and four passengers -- to an overnight halt some 30 miles west of Kansas City, which, as the local sheriff has patiently to explain, isn't in Kansas even though they themselves now are.

First to disembark is young Cherie, a self-pronounced "chantoose" who claims that she is being abducted. Bo, an equally youthful cowboy from Montana who was in K.C. for the rodeo, caught her club act and was dazzled. She liked him, too; at any rate they went to bed together. He woke up convinced they were in love and engaged and wouldn't hear otherwise. So here they are, en route to Bo's ranch, with his protective older buddy, Virgil, along for the ride. The remaining traveller is Dr. Lyman, an old soak of an ex-teacher, who quotes Shakespeare and comes on to young girls, in this instance to Elma, high-school student and part-time waitress. She too gets dazzled but only temporarily, thanks to the good advice of Grace, whose diner it is and who herself is taking advantage of the extra- long stopover to have a fling with the bus driver.

All in the course of the night discover something about love and tell us what it is. Grace learns to settle, Elma learns to wait, and the doctor learns to give up.

Bo and Cherie, the central couple, share a more complicated educational process. He, with some assistance from his friend's wisdom and rather more from the sheriff's fists, discovers that you can't always have what you want, especially if what you want doesn't want you. She, finding that he's more vulnerable and less experienced than he let on, melts. Her decision is also influenced by her realization that neither her past nor her probable professional future bear much thinking about. (Her performance of That Old Black Magic doesn't suggest any kind of threat to Doris Day.) So the expected Broadway happy ending has its dark underside.

The buckaroo and the chantoosie-floozy are American legends, with Cherie carrying additional mythic baggage from being played on screen by Marilyn Monroe. It's a tribute to the two actors here that each of them can embody the archetype and still find the person within it.

Nicole Underhay, in a fluent and firmly centred performance, explores all Cherie's permutations of hurt and pride; Martin Happer's Bo has a blithe swagger that's a very plausible anagram for innocence. He's even, by the end, managed to make the character's boorishness likeable, which is quite a feat.

Jackie Maxwell's production gets the personal rhythms and the rhythms of the diner flowing smoothly into one another, with Grace and Elma brought firmly to life by Mary Haney, shaking her feathers like a satisfied mother hen, and Diana Donnelly, a perceptive listener and eager participant. Michael Ball is a fine drawling sheriff and Norman Browning a good self-hating lush. Peter Krantz supplies a melancholy ground-bass (actually, he plays guitar) as Virgil, who, like Antonio in The Merchant of Venice, bows out of his young friend's life when a lady bows in.

Virgil may supply a literary subtext as well as a homosexual one. He shares a name, after all, with the Roman poet who was resurrected to become Dante's guide, philosopher and friend through the first two-thirds of The Divine Comedy.
