THEATRE REVIEWS"Among London's many offerings are the flawless ensemble productions of David Hares Racing Demon and Arthur Millers After the Fall, featuring a brilliant performance by black actress Josette Simon as Marilyn Monroe National Theatre struts its stuff

Saturday, November 03, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail ARTHUR Miller's 25-year-old play After the Fall has finally been given its London premiere at the National Theatre. This, whatever the author's protestations, is autobiographical, self-justifying drama, and its protagonist-narrator hardly seems to stop talking. James Laurenson, who plays him, probably deserves a medal for remembering his lines, let alone making them lucid and urgent.

But within this exasperating framework are scenes of startling immediacy, reflecting their authors continuing obsessions. One is betrayal, as exemplified in the McCarthy era, here dealt with directly instead of by historical refraction as in The Crucible. Another, more crucial, is family relationships: not, for once, between father and son but husband and wife. Millers first two marriages are laid remorselessly, but not cruelly, bare. The second of course was with Marilyn Monroe: the reason for the play's notoriety but also for its fascination. Miller tried to distance himself by writing her as a singer rather than an actress; this production goes further by making her black. She is played, brilliantly and mercurially, by Josette Simon. The autobiographical interest remains (sometimes we seem to be watching a play about what would have happened if Arthur Miller had married Billie Holiday) but the endless cycles of elation, guilt and recrimination ring universally true. The exquisitely detailed direction is by Michael Blakemore, than whom there is no better in Britain.

Indeed, both After the Fall and David Hare's Racing Demon are pretty well flawless ensemble productions: a credit to the National Theatre. There was riveting acting in everything I saw there: even in a generally scorned production of Racines Berenice, a noble play of pent-up passion, culminating here in a long whispered speech of renunciation by Lindsay Duncan in the title role which had a skeptical house spellbound.

Berenice was the only example around of something called Designer Theatre, which is supposed to be killing or saving the drama according to taste; on this occasion, it looked as if somebody had delivered all the elements of a stunning set and then assembled them in the wrong order. The costumes, too, mixed periods pointlessly, whereas the Nationals King Lear mixed them pointedly. Here again, though, the main consideration was the acting - and the assembly (miraculous, in the context of recent British theatre) of a company that could do justice to every role save one in the most taxing ensemble drama ever written. Deborah Warner, the director, has emerged in the last couple of years as a director who never cuts a line and gets her actors burrowing into every one of them. The breakthrough moment came in the Dover scene where the blind Gloucester (Peter Jeffrey) breaks down in tears at the sound of his mad master's voice. The text prescribes this but I had never really registered it before. It put my own tear-ducts on alert for the rest of the play, and they duly erupted during Lear's reconciliation with Cordelia and his passion at her death. Brian Cox, a craggily honest actor with a streak of Celtic craziness, was the player responsible. He is now one of the two prime classic actors in Britain. The other is Ian McKellen who was playing Kent.

The following night, the compliment was reversed, with Cox playing Buckingham to McKellens Richard III. This is state-of-the-art British Shakespeare (as opposed to the makeshift offered by the Renaissance Theatre Company) and though we dont have to fall down and worship it we could use it as a measuring rod. Richard III is a bravura modern-dress production, directed by Richard Eyre, the National's supremo, also responsible for Racing Demon and clearly back on form after a period of treading water. McKellen's Richard is a warped British Royal with a blistering contempt for anybody to whom he is not related and a consuming hatred of anyone to whom he is. He is Edward VIII reborn as Hitler: not an exhaustive interpretation of the role and rather short on humor, but savagely convincing as the begetter of a police-state. Here the production backs him up with imaginative relish; one scene shows us one of the Princes-not-yet-in-the-Tower playing with a toy train. After his exit, it whirrs all alone across the wide empty stage. We know what the boys fate will be; this makes us feel it.

These productions bring the National Theatre as close as it has ever come in its 27-year history to doing its duty by the national playwright. Generally, it has consigned that responsibility to the Royal Shakespeare Company, which in London is in one of its periodic troughs, financially buffeted (to the point of closing its Barbican Theatre for the winter) and artistically under par.

There is no point in assessing the RSC at present. For one thing the current Stratford-on-Avon season, the basis for next year in London, is reportedly excellent. For another, a new artistic director is about to take over, with a new executive structure. The director in question, Adrian Noble, is currently represented in London by a production of Three Sisters which inaugurates a new but obviously limited trend: dynasty Chekhov. A trio of Redgraves will be performing this same play in the West End in December. In the meantime, imported from Dublin to the Royal Court Theatre, we have a quartet of Cusacks: the sisters Sorcha, Sinead and Niamh as the sisters and, as the drunken doctor, their father Cyril. Cusack pere is the patriarch, not just of this family but of the English-speaking theatre; his capacity for just existing on a stage, unruffled and mesmeric, is the ultimate in relaxation. Beside him, all other actors look hypertense. The production takes its tone from him; it is unhurried almost to a fault, but within its unfolding length is an immense amount of felt life. The older one gets, the more painful this play seems, and the actual familial bonds on view here make the snapping seem all the uglier.

I feel fairly amazed at the things I didnt see. A new Alan Ayckbourn play. A trio of star actors in well-received productions of modern classics: Glenda Jackson as Mother Courage, Richard Harris as Pirandello's Henry IV, Derek Jacobi as Sartre's Kean. An epic nine-hour staging of Malorys Morte dArthur in a West London suburb. The first ever London revival of the Elizabethan history play, Sir Thomas More, with one scene reputedly by Shakespeare. (I'm really chagrined at missing that one.) I was too late to catch the London production of Sondheim's Sunday in the Park with George and too early for his Into the Woods, produced by David Mirvish. It almost makes me credit the theatrical tourist view of London as the centre of the world, and it increases my fury and frustration at the financial jeopardy most of it is in. I even begin to wonder why I was so crabby when I was a London reviewer.
