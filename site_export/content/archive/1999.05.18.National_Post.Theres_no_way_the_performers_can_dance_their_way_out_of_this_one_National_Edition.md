Anything Goes

Jane Mallett Theatre, Toronto

First-act curtain of a 1934 musical: The captain of an Atlantic liner arrests a young insurance broker under the impression that he is a gangster, Public Enemy Number One no less. He is not, however, about to put the lad in irons. He intends to honour him as the celebrity passenger the cruise so badly needs.

Later, when he discovers that his star criminal is a mere stowaway, he will be far less indulgent. Cue for Reno Sweeney, nightclub evangelist (you heard), to let loose with a blithely sardonic musical commentary on modern manners that also happens to be the show's title number Anything Goes.

A famous song out of context, it is even more pungently effective within it: exactly the right statement at the right moment, relevant to its own time and to ours. It suggests that Cole Porter, who wrote the music and lyrics, had a more specific dramatic sense than is generally acknowledged. The opposite impression has been fostered by Porter himself, who liked to react with debonair unconcern when a number was axed, and by his executors who permit revivalists to transfer songs wholesale from one score to another.

A case in point is the version of Anything Goes that was successfully mounted in New York some 10 years ago and was subsequently seen on tour in Toronto. It has now been revived by Reprise as one of their "Great Musicals in Concert." It has a streamlined and vastly rewritten book (by John Weidman and Timothy Crouse, son of one of the original authors) which for commercial purposes may well be -- no, definitely is -- superior to the 1934 text.

But Reprise's purposes are not commercial, or shouldn't be. Its actors perform, with the scripts in their hands, for four nights only. It is playing to an audience that is pre-sold on the opportunity of seeing Cole Porter's Anything Goes, in any form, and might welcome the chance of experiencing the original. It couldn't hurt them.

What we get is the reduction of a reduction. The Broadway revival featured a full band, stylish Art Deco sets, a lot of zippy dancing, and crack comic timing. The Reprise production, though tidily drilled by its director Anne Wootten, can afford none of these except the last, which it only fitfully exhibits.

Striving for wit, it generally settles for roguishness. A line of dialogue prompts the pianist to strike up Night and Day, at which the actors yell "wrong show." They then proceed to sing Friendship, which is also from the wrong show, though I found myself wondering if anyone at Reprise knew this. It has been interpolated from another Porter musical of the '30s, as have several other songs, and lacking either context or staging none of them make sense, not even the nonsensical sense this kind of show is meant to make.

Goodbye, Little Dream, Goodbye -- a little-known but totally beautiful lament -- means nothing when the character hasn't earned the right to sing it. The songs from the original -- they include I Get a Kick Out of You, You're the Top, and Blow, Gabriel, Blow -- all work handily. As those titles suggest, this is a great score. If they want to do the Cole Porter Songbook instead, then why bother with a script?

There are some good voices around, most notably those of Barbara Barsky as Reno and Brian Hill who has some lovely high notes as the impromptu convict (or, as you might say, neo-con). But neither of them exactly sets the stage alight; she shies away from her character's forthright sexuality (the role was written for the young Ethel Merman) and he tends to disappear even when present. By miles, the best performance comes from Scott Walker as a silly-ass Englishman; the role is a brazen caricature but he finds its centre and that allows him to be as funny and tuneful and even touching as he likes.

Lost Musicals -- the London enterprise from which all these revival groups derive -- won its reputation by reviving shows from all periods in scrupulously authentic versions; they have had particular success with '30s Porter. Reprise's next season derives entirely from the '50s and '60s. They have announced Guys and Dolls, the best of all musicals but hardly in need of a concert performance; I Do, I Do, a syrup-bath; and Promises Promises, the one excusable choice, a renowned but little-seen show with a good book and a terrific score.

Reprise's policy ought to be re-thunk. They should be offering us more than just dinner-theatre with unlearned lines.
