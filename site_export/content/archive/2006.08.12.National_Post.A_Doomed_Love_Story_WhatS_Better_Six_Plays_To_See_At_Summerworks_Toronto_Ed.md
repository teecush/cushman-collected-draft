robertcushman@lycos.com; Summerworks closes tomorrow. 416-973-4000, summerworks.ca.

A small selection of this year's shows at Summerworks:

1 The pick of last summer's Summerworks was Essay, by Hannah Moscovitch. The pick of this year's is The Russian Play (Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace), by the same author. Both plays are three- handers, but in very different modes. Essay was a barbed, elegant comedy about contemporary academe. The Russian Play has moments of biting wit, but it is also moving, a state to which its predecessor never aspired.

It's a doomed love story from the age of Chekhov, transferred to the age of Solzhenitsyn; a young flower-shop assistant is involved in turn with two married men, a poor gravedigger whom she loves, and a rich peasant who loves her. It's an open question as to which of the two hurts her most, but the kulak has the Stalinist secret police to help him when things turn sour.

The play is frank about our "Russian" expectations and its own artifice, admitting them through the mouth of a sardonic narrator, who doubles as the love's-martyr heroine. Michelle Monteith slips back and forth between roles without cheating on a moment of either. Her triumph mirrors that of the play itself; it's a superb balancing act in which irony only intensifies emotion. So it is Chekhovian.

The men, Aaron Willis and Shawn Campbell, are as worthy of her as their characters are not; and there is a fourth presence: a guitarist, Tom Howell, who supplies atmosphere even as he mocks it. The music is the clinching element in Natasha Mytnowych's perfect production, designed by Camellia Koo and superbly lit.

It's rumoured that Moscovitch is going to "develop" this play further. Frankly, I can't see what needs to be developed; it's a perfect fable as it stands. Similar whispers were abroad last year concerning Essay, which could conceivably use a second act. But a double bill of both plays, just as they stand, would outpace most of the new writing we see in Toronto.

2 The Review (Factory Mainspace) is another three-hander, this time with two musicians added. It is in fact a musical, and its more- or-less opening number is a corker: A playwright stands at the first night of his new show (also a musical), welcomes the applause of "family and friend" but waits, in vain desperation and in vain ("Stand up!"), for an ovation.

Allana Harkin and Adam Pettle are credited with book, lyrics and direction, Jonathan Monro with music, additional lyrics and musical direction. Whoever wrote what, that song is a jewel, with perfect, funny rhymes that make points and jokes that develop. It got the Standing O. The show itself didn't, because thereafter it loses its way.

As second best, the playwright invites a notoriously cranky critic (if you can imagine such a person) back to his flat, for reasons that both he and the show seem to be making up as they go along. The subject seems to be the universal need for validation ("the only five-star review worth having is the one you write for yourself" -- final chorus) but only one later number really fizzes: I Didn't Know You Could Do That -- ask for a date, that is -- sung by the playwright to the actress he has hitherto worshipped, geekily, from afar. Well, she is about twice his height; she's Sarah Cornell, the enchantingly statuesque Ulla of The Producers, who still has her imposing presence but seems to have lost her diction along with her amplification and her Swedish accent.

Andrew Kushnir is neurotically delightful as her admirer, but he, too, is fitfully indistinct. Acoustics at the Factory may be lousy, but come on, the space is small and so is the band. Only George Masswohl as the reviewer, rumpled but resonant, comes through splendidly, a credit to his own profession and to mine.

Critic and playwright have an intermittent father-son thing going: Guys -- authors, I mean -- don't go there. Audiences: Go by all means; you'll be entertained, but you may fall short of ecstasy.

3 In 4:48 Psychosis (Passe Muraille Backspace), British playwright Sarah Kane offers a front-line report from the far reaches of depression; she, in fact, committed suicide before the play's first (2000) production. But the play doesn't come across as self-pitying or self-indulgent. It is self-lacerating, but not beyond measure or proportion. It's authentic, not just because it presumably happened but because the language, which is beautifully calibrated, convinces that that is how it happened, how it felt.

The desperate, withering scorn in the author's view of the world and of herself come through frighteningly well in Elizabeth Saunders' performance. Maggie Huculak is a first-rate foil as the doctor she both needs and reviles, their feelings being not so much mutual as complementary. There is a limit to how far you can go with this material, and the play, like all I saw except The Russian Play, gets to seem long. Short plays often do feel longer than long ones; they have fewer ways of avoiding monotony.

4 Hip-Hop: Who Stole the Soul (Passe Muraille Backspace), by Joseph Jomo Pierre, has a fine and powerful central conceit. Jason, a young black man, is trapped by the tropes of rap, which teach him that he's both the victim (of white men) and the aggressor (of black women). Inside himself, he's mild, but he can't admit it.

The author himself plays Jason and does justice to both sides of this gangsta without a gang; Cara Ricketts's Black Woman is blazingly provocative; Colin Doyle's White Man is lacking in suave; and a fourth character, a DJ, functions like the Auditor of Samuel Beckett's Not I: a necessary silent witness for a man who wants to be the hero of his own hip-hop record. This one gets stuck in a single groove, but it's a stimulating one.

5 Go Forth and Multiply (Factory Mainspace), by Kate Lynch, takes place outside a laundromat in a cactus-strewn desert. If that sounds odd, so is the play, a fable about pregnancy -- some women just can't stop conceiving, others can't start -- that also takes in racial prejudice: against Martians.

It's whimsical, and I don't really get the point, especially of the dance interludes, but it plays out according to its own logic and is funny and a little scary on the way. It's also very well played, by Gemma James Smith as a barren lawyer (is that meant to be a tautology?) and by Gina Wilkinson as an earth mother, and grandmother, for whom "fecund" is a bewildering understatement.

6 Wilkinson is the author and director of Whistle Me Home (Factory Mainspace), a monologue that's a case of talented writing and spectacularly good acting (by Tom Rooney) in search of a subject, or at least of a shape. Rooney spends the entire play in his bath; not to give too much plot away, he reverses an old line by getting out of a dry martini and into some wet clothes.
