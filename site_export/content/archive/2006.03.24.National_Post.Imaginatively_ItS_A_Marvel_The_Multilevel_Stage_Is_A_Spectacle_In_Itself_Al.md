THE LORD OF THE RINGS

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

At the very end of The Lord of the Rings, after the last actor has taken his bow, the stage gets up and takes a well-deserved bow of its own. It has been giving a flawless performance all night, and a breathtakingly versatile one. Not only does it revolve, which perhaps is no big deal, it also rises, falls, divides and subdivides, frequently doing all these things at the same time.

The multiplicity of levels and compartments is great for battle scenes, of which there are many, and for overall speed; the various strands of a complicated story can overlap, which is exciting in itself.

The theatrical version of J.R.R. Tolkien's trilogy has its faults, most of them coming unshakably with the territory, but scenically, technically and imaginatively, it's a marvel.

Under Matthew Warchus' implacably disciplined direction, hardly any of the effects are gratuitous. The bad guys look really bad. The first Black Rider, suddenly unleashed from nowhere, is as majestically scary an apparition as I have seen in a theatre, a giant gaunt amalgam of horse and man.

The virtuous people get their share of spectacle as well. When Aragorn, the returning king, finds himself short of living soldiers to fight the armies of the Dark Lord Sauron, he calls on the Warriors of the Dead, who arise, shrouded in mist, from what looks like a huge vat of seaweed: a thrilling finale to the second of the show's three acts and a really smart recruiting idea. One can think of many places now where it might come in handy.

Rob Howell's sets, a near-literal forest of invention with bare branches as the governing motif, pull not just the stage but the auditorium into Middle Earth, as does Paul Pyant's lighting which, in its beauty and variety, calls for the invention of new superlatives.

With three books to get through the show can hardly afford to waste time, so it starts before it starts with its real heroes, the hobbits of the Shire, already on stage, indulging in simple hobbit pleasures, such as trapping fireflies and releasing them again.

We have a solid base to start from and, some three and a half hours later, to come back to, since the show includes the culminating episode of the Scouring of the Shire, which the movie left out. It's a noble choice, but as a payoff it doesn't pay off. It feels rushed, not so much in tempo as in lack of weight. That's something that keeps happening, and it's the main flaw.

The adaptation, by Shaun McKenna and the director, is outstandingly intelligent. Resourcefully, it brings in shadow-play and a disembodied narrator to handle the exposition that's left over from the earlier book The Hobbit; and it later proves neat at telescoping: cutting out huge swaths of narrative and binding the remaining bits together.

It keeps its eye firmly on the essential object, the Ring of Power itself, the original weapon of mass destruction; on the quest of the hobbit Frodo to destroy it; and on the torments, physical and mental, of Gollum, who's enslaved himself to it.

James Loye and Peter Howe, the two principal British imports, play Frodo and Sam, and are admirable, though the show skates over Frodo's inner struggles on Mount Doom (which, given the books' exclusively male worldview, might not be too far from Brokeback Mountain). I'm not sure that what happens at these climactic moments would be clear to anyone who didn't already know.

Some of this has to do with the absence of an important character, the title character in fact. The lord of the rings is Sauron, who forged them and whose power depends on them. He doesn't make any personal appearances in the book, either, but he lives there in what people say of him.

We are told that he has shrunk his being to a single, malignant, all-seeing Eye. That's quite an image, and one that you would think a production high on visual coups would want to capture. But it doesn't even try.

It proceeds to miss out on the one really moving scene in the book, the one in which the new king and all his court kneel to the dazed heroic hobbits. As an unsatisfactory substitute we get a wedding ceremony at which the guests look like a convocation of beekeepers.

But that's a rare aberration. In this show the human element fights hard and sometimes wins. The mechanical element, which after all is a human achievement itself, is wonderful.

BY THE NUMBERS

Here are some numerical breakdowns from the production:

$27-million Cost of The Lord of the Rings.

3.5 Running time of the production in hours.

55 Number of actors in the cast.

500 Number of pieces of armour in the show.

150 Number of weapons.

7 Weight of the screen at the back of the set in tons.

40 Weight of the computer-controlled stage floor in tons.

30 Amount, in tons, of concrete workers had to remove from under the old stage to install the complex network of new steel supports to take that 40-ton weight.

17 Number of elevators in the stage floor.

20 Number of 4,000-cubic-foot sea freight containers used to ship the set pieces from England.

73 Number of people working behind the scenes on each performance.

808 Number of shoes worn by hobbits, orcs, elves and ents.

280 Number of gloves worn by the hobbits, orcs, elves and ents.

504 Total number of costumes in the show.

387 Costume changes in each performance.

5,000 Yards of fabric in the costumes.

37,155 Man-hours spent making the costumes and hats at Toronto's Seamless Costumes.

400 Number of individual metal motifs hand-sewn on Elrond's costume.

240 Number of individual Dwarf-Runes sewn into Gimli's doublet.
