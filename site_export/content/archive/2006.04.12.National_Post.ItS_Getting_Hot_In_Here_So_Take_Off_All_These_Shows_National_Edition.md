The next episode of At the Hotel airs Tuesday at 9 p.m. on CBC.

Ken Finkleman, the TV auteur behind the Newsroom, Foreign Objects and More Tears, turns his acerbic lens on the hospitality industry in his new six-part series, At the Hotel. Our three Finklefans will check in after each episode to see how the service measures up to his past wry romps.

OUR GUESTS

- Vanessa Farquharson, Arts & Life reporter

- Robert Cushman, the Post's theatre critic

- J. Kelly Nestruck, Arts & Life reporter

VANESSA: Hot hot heat was the theme of this week's instalment of At the Hotel, with temperatures at a crazy high of 35 degrees (Celsius, natch). The power failure, in turn, triggered plenty of Finklematerial, from an extra-sinful elevator affair to aggressive joke-telling to sweaty bellhops having to carry less-poofy-than- usual pomeranians up multiple flights of stairs.

Now that we only have one episode to go, I think I can safely say that Robert was right -- there probably wont be any big twist at the end. But Im OK with that; I'm sated as is. Whether or not the murder is solved or the hotel gets sold to the evil developers, I could care less -- it's the transient stories and characters that are leaving the biggest impression on me.

As Finkleman said in an interview a little while back, this is a series thats grounded not so much in the who or what but the where. Its less about a story than it is about a place where a bunch of stories are told, some of which end and some of which don't. Obviously this can be frustrating but, after five episodes, I'm finally starting to appreciate the concept of a hotel having a personality, of the venue being the star of the show.

ROBERT: I think Ill take Fawlty Towers, thank you very much. Now, there was a hotel with personality. And with personalities, too.

Actually, Im with Vanessa some of the way. The place does have an aura of its own. Lush and dehydrated, even before the exceptional weather conditions of this week's episode. But I disagree in that I think it's the staff who keep it running. Im warming, if thats the right word, to Albert, the imperturbable front-desk man with the untraceable London-cosmopolitan accent who seems to work 24-hour days. He embodies the place and had the week's funniest lines -- in Italian. Well, the actor's name is Carlo Rota.

His services to guests include placing quotations from Dantes Inferno, which seemed to be where this weeks episode was set: a priest who sins and then spontaneously combusts and a Catskill comic who jumps from a window when his treasured jokes don't get a laugh - - ironically, since they were rather good.

But maybe that's all in the shows spirit of anti-comedy. Ill grant Finkleman and his collaborators the right not to finish their stories. What bothers me is that so many of them don't even seem worth beginning.

KELLY: All I ask for is a little consistency in approach. This episode was, again, like three 20-minute episodes from different shows shuffled together randomly. If the hotel was having problems with its (meta)physical plant, why wasn't anyone affected by the sweltering heat in the babies-for-sale or suicidal-comic subplots? Also, since the main chambermaid is having a romantic relationship with the writer, shouldnt we feel, if not outraged, at least curious about how her elevator tryst with the priest will affect that?

Its not that each episode seems to exist in a vacuum, or even that each scene seems to exist in a vacuum that bothers me; it's that we're being told through the framing this all takes place in the same universe and as part of some grand story when it clearly doesnt. Im not terribly convinced that this hotel has a personality, but if it does it's seriously split. If this series were any longer than six episodes, I'd check out and move to George F. Walkers Suburban Motel.

VANESSA: Oooh, zing! OK, Ill grant you that Walker is a playwright Finkleman could stand to emulate a bit more, but come on, who wants to go to the suburbs? And the suicidal comedian was indeed feeling the heat -- not only was he in an undershirt the entire time, he killed himself.

Anyway, what I was trying to get across was that these inconsistent plots are not, as you say, part of some grand story. Yes, there might be some character overlap and a murder hanging over everything, but essentially they all stand alone. That's the thrust of it: The hotel is the only thing holding the stories together, and even so, most go bursting back out the front door whether or not we like it.

Don't get me wrong -- I dont love the series, but I feel the need to defend it because theres so much formulaic crud on the air; even the best Canadian programming still gets wrapped in a neat, linear package. So when something comes along that's just a little arty and doesn't blatantly try to appeal to every segment of every demographic, it makes me happy.

ROBERT: I dont know how many points you can give a show simply for not being something. And, besides, its hedging its bets. It has these ongoing plot lines, and it begins every week with a flashback to the root situation years before. (Incidentally, every week when the music starts up over that black-and-white pool sequence, I keep thinking it's the theme from A Summer Place, even though I know by now that it isn't; its something equally slurpy and evocative.) So they seem to make us want to think its important; if not the story itself then the fact that these people are so rotten.

But then it keeps telling us that about everyone; the creepy selfishness of the old comedian (the second one we've met in four weeks) was real overkill, even before the word became literal. But since there's no moral centre (or even an immoral one, as in The Newsroom), it feels like unearned gloating.

KELLY: It certainly was strange that this was the second episode with a washed-up, curmudgeonly comic. Also perplexing was that the introductory flashback was a repeat of an earlier one and, for the first time, didn't tell us anything new. What can be said about a series that already feels like a rerun in its fifth episode? Say what you will about the priest's spontaneous human combustion, at least that was something new and interesting. (Though, come to think of it, I do seem to remember one of the mayors on Picket Fences spontaneously combusting after committing a serious sin.) If not the suburbs, can we at least consider moving out west to Robson Arms?
