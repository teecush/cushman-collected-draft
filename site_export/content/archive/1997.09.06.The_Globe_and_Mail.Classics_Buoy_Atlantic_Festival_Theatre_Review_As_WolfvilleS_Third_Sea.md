Classics buoy Atlantic festival

THEATRE REVIEW / As Wolfville's third season of classical drama comes to a close, its three plays confirm a reputation for simple sophistication.

Saturday, September 6, 1997

Special to The Globe and Mail

Wolfville, N.S. -- THE Atlantic Theatre Festival is in an enviable position. Wolfville is a charming location, and the new theatre has perceptibly energized it. The Maritimes have never before had a stage dedicated to the classics, and this too is an advantage. It means that the festival can present a standard repertoire and still feel fresh and adventurous. Here are the famous plays, and a receptive, unjaded audience. That combination, for any theatre, is a privilege -- and a responsibility.

It is also a danger. It means, for example, that standing ovations can come too easy. (Though that's hardly peculiar to Wolfville.) Getting these complex texts off the ground requires strong, flexible and experienced performers, and there are not enough of them to go around: not in Canada, not anywhere. At Wolfville, which tries to include a high percentage of native Maritime talent, the situation is even more acute than at the richer and more prestigious Stratford and Shaw festivals. But for all that, the theatre has maintained a fair degree of company continuity, and it has made a few striking discoveries. It also has top-of-the-line direction. Michael Bawtree, the Atlantic Festivals founder and chief, is an ex-Stratford director and dramaturge, and as a consultant he has Michael Langham, the doyen of Canadian classical theatre, who has staged a series of distinguished shows here, either alone or in tandem with his wife, the actress Helen Burns.

In the season that wraps up next weekend, the company is running Molières Tartuffe, Oscar Wilde's The Importance of Being Earnest and Chekhov's Uncle Vanya : all in some sense comedies (like all the plays in the two previous seasons) and all making stiff formal and technical demands. Thats especially the case on Wolfvilles son-of-Stratford thrust stage, that pushes the actors forward right into the audience. Such a stage can sometimes put proscenium-arch plays into sharp and rewarding relief: the best production of Uncle Vanya (or of anything else) that I have ever seen was presented on the British open stage at Chichester. But the wide open spaces can also leave plays and actors exposed, and encourage a kind of all-purpose animation. Michael Bawtree's production of Tartuffe, for instance, begins with an offstage burst of laughter -- a strange overture for a play whose characters first appear locked in a vicious family row. Madame Pernelle, mother of the Parisian bourgeois Monsieur Orgon, is lecturing her son's family on the virtues of Tartuffe, the puritanical evangelist whom Orgon has taken beneath their roof. The scene swings in Ranjit Bolts new translation, in racy rhyming couplets (Molières own medium, though it's taken English translators 300 years to come to terms with the fact.) But much of the wit is flattened out by actors desperate to stress every word.

The show begins to grip when Orgon comes on. Leon Pownall -- a reliable actor transformed through three seasons at Wolfville into an inspired one -- plays a calm, bearish paterfamilias who performs tyrannical acts quite peacefully in the name of humility. And of course when he finds that his idolized Tartuffe has been duping him -- that the holy man has in fact been intent on seducing Orgon's young wife -- the dam furiously and farcically bursts.

Molière, no shrinking violet, wrote the part of Orgon for himself -- Tartuffe does not appear until nearly halfway through. He is played by Bill Carr, the festivals most impressive local recruit: a fine Lopahin in the Cherry Orchard of the festivals inaugural season, and, in last year's She Stoops to Conquer , the only really funny Tony Lumpkin I have ever seen. But his Tartuffe is disappointing: a simple con-man, and tight-lipped at that, rather than a complex hypocrite who half-believes his own rhetoric. He only comes to full, vicious life when he turns on his protector; here the stage temperature rises and the play becomes, and remains, serious business.

Tartuffe ends far stronger than it begins, showing how a great play can gradually take charge of a theatre when both cast and audience pay it the basic compliment of trust. The same is true of The Importance of Being Earnest , whose epigrammatic prose is even more challenging than the couplets of Tartuffe -- which at least impose their own rhythm.

"I don't play accurately, says Algernon Moncrieff after taking an erratic turn upon the piano in the plays opening moments, and adds disparagingly anyone can play accurately." No way: accuracy takes practice. Jonathan Watton, who plays Algy here, was very promising in gauche comedy roles two years ago. He has an interesting conception of the part -- a very young dandy, exquisitely groomed, prolonging his adolescence by throwing around words and attitudes -- but he has terrible trouble phrasing it. The other young persons have more technique but less originality, except for Kate Hennigs strident Gwendolen, which has neither. We know that Gwendolen will grow up like her Gorgon mother Lady Bracknell, but she shouldn't have got there yet . As it happens the veteran Jane Casson-Redlyon makes mama a rather restrained battle axe, scoring her points through incisiveness rather than volume. This pays off in the last act where she becomes an unchallengeably chilling Voice of Society, calling the errant Miss Prism to account -- "Prism! Where's that baby?" -- with the aspect of an avenging Fury.

It helps that shes dressed in red, while everyone and everything else on stage is in cream or off-white. Setting in this last scene is reduced to a door and two chairs, the culmination of a progressively (and stylishly) Spartan staging by young English director Dominic Cooke.

Paring the scene down is one effective way of using the thrust stage. Another is to build it up, which happens in Uncle Vanya , directed by Helen Burns and Michael Langham, the only one of the three plays with a script that might be called naturalistic, as well as the most elaborate production of the three and the most accomplished. You feel the rambling beehive of a Russian house where no-one is truly comfortable. The staging is crammed with revealing detail: Leon Pownalls Vanya, the smitten farm-manager, offers the beautiful visitor Yelena a drink, but his timing is off and she doesn't notice; Later, he all but makes love to her dropped scarf.

Pownall is not the best-defined of Vanyas, and his attempted shooting of the Professor, whom he blames for his misfortunes -- the great tragi-comic moment in modern drama -- achieves only half its effect. We laugh but we don't cry. Ideally we should do both at once -- Vanya, who dreams of all he might have achieved and then curses himself for his foolishness, is a mirror for all but the securest of us. Maybe the production, in driving for unsentimental comedy, is reacting against those mythical mopey productions of Chekhov which everybody castigates but few have actually seen.

The company's layers of strength are most apparent in this production. Megan Follows, whose work at Wolfville has confirmed her as a star, makes a supremely moving Sonya, Vanya's companion in misfortune, equally exploited, equally unloved. Peter Donat, a Canadian star of an older vintage manages an intriguing double focus as the Professor, presenting him as a monster of selfishness who still has legitimate grievances.

These three plays, for all their occasional faults, have a simplicity that at its best can encompass considerable sophistication. This suggests that the festival, after only three years in existence, already has an identity -- one that suits the territory. Wolfville is a tiny town with a high level of style. Building a major theatre there seems alternately a crazy idea and the most logical thing in the world.


  


Tartuffe runs until Sept. 11. The Importance Of Being Earnest and Uncle Vanya both close Sept . 13. 1-800-337-6661 for information.
