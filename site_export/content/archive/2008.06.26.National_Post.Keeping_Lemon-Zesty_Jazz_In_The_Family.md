CONCERT REVIEW

Sandy Stewart and Bill Charlap

The Old Mill, Toronto

There are some standard songs that are, paradoxically, so well-known that nobody sings them. It takes an extraordinary singer to make something fresh of Tea for Two or Smoke Gets in Your Eyes. Sandy Stewart, who is an extraordinary singer, did them back to back on Tuesday night, thought them through with no trickery, and took a rapt audience along with her.

She reclaimed the Tea song with what I would call a gentle garden swing, or perhaps a soft hammock rock: a languorous, hypnotic reading

that found space for a gently comic disgust for the line in the verse about being discontented with homes that are rented. It conveyed real pleasure, just as the Smoke song, an even tougher assignment, communicated real pain. She did the whole song out of tempo, first philosophically, then shocking us by sounding out the lyric's cruellest words -- "I am without my love," "now laughing friends deride" -- like a tolling bell.

In the early '60s, Stewart was the girl singer on thea Perry Como TV show. She's now a woman singer, physically compact, musically and dramatically mature, with a velvet voice, an understated wit and a subtle sense of dynamics secured by superb intonation. (She can bring off the occasional Judy Garland big finish, though I rather wish she wouldn't.) She also, with admirable forethought, gave birth to a son, Bill Charlap, who is the crown prince of the younger jazz pianists. Accompanying her, he's in more of a conservatory mode than when he himself is leader (though he's always been a musician who plays songs rather than chords); his instrumental choruses amplify and expand the moods his mom's created: notably on After You, an alarmingly grown-up Cole Porter number about anticipated heartbreak -- one of those "this is too good to last" songs. Its flip side -- "too hot not to cool down" -- is Porter's Just One of Those Things, another of those warhorses that Stewart resuscitates by playing down the flipness. It's another of her rubato ballads, every word and image freshly thought, with a hint of Sinatra world-weariness at the edges.

A song that Porter should have written but didn't -- Jerome Kern and Oscar Hammerstein did -- is All in Fun, a gentle romantic spoof of Manhattan sophistication that Stewart phrases beautifully -- "our feet have left the ground -- a bit." She once recorded a wonderful Kern album, and from it she also included Remind Me, a blend of sentiment and commonsense: not so much bitter-sweet as tart-sweet. She and Charlap seem justifiably proud of one another, without overdoing it. Their encore could not have been lovelier or more fitting: Two for the Road, a collaboration of Henry Mancini and Leslie Bricusse, and the best song either man ever wrote.

Stewart and Charlap's performance was part of the Toronto Jazz Festival's Cabaret at the Old Mill series, which mostly features Canadian musical-theatre performers: tonight it's Patricia Zentilli, a Dora nominee for Little Shop of Horrors, and tomorrow Louise Pitre. It's an initiative Toronto greatly needs. But we need visitors too. Stewart is a beacon, quite possibly the best singer of this repertoire alive.
