Martha Henry, the beloved First Lady of Canadian theatre, died on Thursday morning at the age of 83, just twelve days after completing her run in the central role of Edward Albee's Three Tall Women in the Stratford Festival's Studio Theatre. Her performance was universally acclaimed The play, in the words of Stratford's artistic director Antoni Cimolino, "is about the process of leaving the world"; on the last night she was, he told me in a phone conversation, "incandescent."

The play was a personal epilogue for an actress who had always been good at endings. A great tragedienne and comedienne, she could move and tease us at the same time. She had done it a few years before as a female Prospero in The Tempest, whose last farewell seemed to be the character's and the author's and the actor's, all at once. Shortly before that she had brought real vocal grandeur, tinged with affection, to Prospero's summoning of and farewell to, the spirits who have made her "rough magic" possible. And before that she had been pure whimsical mother, taking her daughter Miranda to pay a call on the savage Caliban and singing her lines as she skipped along (her own idea). It was fun; it was also seriously protective. And it was the fruit of a lifetime spent teaching, studying and acting Shakespeare. It could manifest itself in long, beautifully built orations, or in startling single lines, as when her Lady Macbeth, in a superb sleep-walking scene, put a sardonic edge on "the Thane of Fife had a wife; where is she now?"

She had a wicked taste for mischief. One of my fondest memories is of her Lady Bountiful, "homeopath and sex-therapist" (Cimolino's description; he was the director) in the Restoration comedy The Beaux' Stratagem gleefully brandishing a giant zucchini and rushing off to inflict who knows what atrocities on a hapless intruder. And she could be ruthless with herself; Cimolino recalls that in his production of Coriolanus she, playing the hero's mother, spent a whole long scene kneeling on the hard Festival stage while pleading with her son to have pity on Rome. Kneepads were suggested but she wouldn't hear of it.

Mamie Zwettler as C (left), Martha Henry as A, Andrew Iles as The Boy, and Lucy Peacock as B in Three Tall Women. (Photography by V. Tony Hauser)

She died of cancer. When Three Tall Women opened she was using a walker; by the end of the run she was in a wheelchair. But she went out as she would surely have wished, capping a career whose very beginning, 59 years earlier, had been the fulfilment of a dream. This Canadian icon was actually born in Detroit, in 1938. On a school trip across the border in 1957 she saw Stratford's first Hamlet starring Christopher Plummer. Already stage-struck she was now Shakespeare-struck. She decided that a country that could house such a theatre was the one for her. In 1960 she enrolled in the brand-new National Theatre School in Montreal. She left after a year, thus becoming the first NTS graduate, having been offered a place in the 1962 company at Stratford. She was where she wanted to be and where she would remain, spiritually and often physically, for the rest of her life.

Nor, in her first season and aged 24, was she restricted to walk-ons. Her debut role was Lady Macduff in Macbeth: only one scene but it's her scene. Two nights later she was Miranda in The Tempest and audience and critics fell in love. She was a barefoot enchantress, who sounded as good as she looked.

She arrived just at the right time. In its nine years of existence Stratford had developed its own corps of strong male actors; it had also played host to some notable actresses, but none of whom had put down roots. It was Henry who broke that mould; she stayed, for good.

In her second season she was Cressida in Troilus and Cressida, giving an unsparing account of an innocent who fatally fancies herself worldly-wise. Production photographs from this period show her as stunningly beautiful; and she remained, onstage when required and offstage always, supremely elegant.

Despite her looks, she was never an ingénue; she was a character actress. A turning point was her being cast, in 1968, as the super villainous Milady in The Three Musketeers; she plunged into full-blooded melodrama (after, apparently, some initial hesitation in rehearsal) and came joyously out the other side. Something was released in her. She revelled, too, in the occasional low-life roles that came her way, Doll Tearsheet or the managing maids of Moliere. On a more elevated plane she had a serious triumph as Isabella in Measure for Measure, the novice nun shocked and embittered by the world outside her convent. On the other end of the sensual scale, it's a crime that she never got to play Cleopatra, for whom she would have had the authority, the passion and the allure.

Martha Henry as Lady Percy in Henry IV (Henry IV, Part 1), 1965. (Photograph by Peter Smith)

Still, what she accomplished at the Festival between 1962 and 1980, with just a couple of seasons out, was enough to make her a company treasure. She also began directing, starting with Brief Lives, a solo vehicle for her second husband, the Stratford stalwart Douglas Rain. Her first husband was another actor, Donnelly Rhoades Henry, whose surname she adopted; she had been born Martha Buhs. Both these marriages ended in divorce. A third was to the actor Rod Beattie; they separated but remained close, indeed constantly in one another's company. He survives her, as does her daughter Emma Rain.

She came close to running, or part running, Stratford herself. She, the directors Peter Moss and Pam Brighton and the dramaturge Urjo Kareda were the so-called Gang of Four, slated to take over the festival in 1981 and then, in one of the great scandals of Canadian theatre, dismissed by a board set on hiring a prestigious British director (who, as things turned out, couldn't get a visa).

For the next thirteen years she acted and, increasingly, directed all over Canada (and occasionally in the U.S.). Her credentials as a theatrical leader were burnished, and her theatrical horizons widened, by a seven-year term (1988-1995) as Artistic Director of the Grand Theatre in London, Ontario. She finally returned to Stratford in 1994 as the haunted Mary Tyrone in Eugene O'Neill's Long Day's Journey Into Night; she had started her career as the daughter of William Hutt's Prospero, now she was playing his wife. Her final drug-propelled monologue, re-inhabiting her youth and taking us right there with her, was sublime; and the production became instant legend. The director was Diana Leblanc, Henry's close friend since NTS days, with whom she did much of her best work, all the way to Three Tall Women.

Not everything in Henry's second, twenty-year term at Stratford was lovely; she could be a mannered performer, notably as the Chekhovian chatelaines of The Seagull and The Cherry Orchard. As a director of Chekhov, though, she was magnificent; her Three Sisters was almost shockingly moving in its intimacy. Her productions of Timothy Findley's historical fantasia Elizabeth Rex and of Arthur Miller's All My Sons were comparably fine. They were all staged in the small Tom Patterson Theatre, where she also gave some of her finest performances. Maybe the renovated Patterson, due to open next year, could present a tribute to her.

She was also a dedicated teacher. Younger company members admired her acting and found her directing careful and considerate. More formally, she was director first of Stratford's Birmingham Conservatory, dedicated to training new generations of classical actors, and then of the Michael Langham Workshop for Classical Direction. Her juniors loved her as her peers did: because she was warm and kind and generous and funny. I was lucky enough to become her friend, and I treasure the times we spent chatting, mainly in coffeehouses, and the correspondence we exchanged. When we discussed theatre, which was most of the time, she was forthright and unyielding about work that displeased or disappointed her, unstoppably and precisely eloquent about things she enjoyed. She was notably quick to praise actors who had succeeded at roles in which she felt she herself had fallen short.

I even got to direct her once (via email). The occasion was a reading of the Jacobean tragedy The White Devil. She played a bereaved and crazed mother, and as madness took hold I felt, suddenly, in the presence of greatness. I've never much cared for the phrase "a wonderful human being" but I've heard it applied to Martha by, on consecutive days, a prominent entertainment lawyer and a receptionist in the Stratford Festival office, and I knew that this time, or these times, it was the truth.
