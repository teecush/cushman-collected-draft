DANCE

FRESH BAKED STEPS

If your taste in dance inclines toward nouvelle cuisine, Toronto Dance Theatre is offering an appetizing four-course menu of fresh creations. Four at the Winch is TDT's annual spring choreographic invitational. This year, company artistic director Christopher House has retained a notably strong quartet of established and emerging dancemakers to work with his very fine 12-member troupe. Two of the choreographers, Louis Laberge-Cote and Jessica Runge, are themselves TDT members. Kathleen Rea and Yvonne Ng, the outsiders, are among the best of the city's swelling throng of independent choreographers. Winchester Street Theatre, until Saturday, at 8 p.m. Tickets from $15, 416-967-1365 ext. 24. Michael Crabb

VISUAL ARTS

WITH A SONG IN THEIR ART

For 30 years now, Art Metropole has been championing the multiple formats of contemporary art. Started by the artists' collective General Idea in 1974, Artmet publishes, promotes and sells everything from artists' multiples to mail art to catalogues to electronic media through its gallery and online store. Saturday's Art Metronome:01 bash brings the not-for-profit's multiple formats to life with live illustration, performance artists, turntablists and rock stars. Headlining the show is Antony (of Antony and the Johnsons fame), a crushingly sweet crooner who has toured with Lou Reed and is still fresh from headlining the Whitney Biennale in New York. As the queenly Laurie Anderson put it, "When Antony sings, it is the most exquisite thing that you will hear in your life."Also playing are the ever-favoured the Hidden Cameras with Camera member Luis Jacob on turntables along with Andrew Zealley. Raffle tickets offer sponsored swag and actual art (a numbered edition by General Idea and a "sound ball" by artist Marla Hlady), and cost a mere $5. Tickets for Saturday's audio-art fundraiser range from $25 to $100 and are on sale at the Harbourfront box office (416-973-4000). For more information visit www.artmetropole.com. Julia Dault

THEATRE

CHOOSE FROM A NUMBER OF MUSICAL NUMBERS

Even diehard musical comedy fans may be beginning to feel pummelled by song and dance, at least in prospect. In recent years both the Shaw and Stratford festivals have doubled their annual output of tuners: They each used to do one per season, but this year they will be offering two apiece. CanStage has gone even further. It has announced two musical shows (both anthologies) for the 2004-05 season; meanwhile, 2003-04 plays itself out with a total of three musicals to its credit, four if you count the eagerly awaited Urinetown, which has been scheduled for the summer interregnum. (Poetic justice that this show should reach us before Thoroughly Modern Millie, which is widely believed to have robbed Urinetown of the best musical Tony because it was considered a safer bet for the road.)

Cookin' at the Cookery, CanStage's big hit from earlier in the season, reopens next week at the New Yorker, with its two acclaimed performers, Jackie Richardson and Montego Glover. Tonight, Pelagie, the first Acadian musical with score by the ubiquitous Allen Cole, opens at the Bluma Appel. Still to come (at Berkeley Street) is The Next 5 Years, with a score by Jason Robert Brown. He wrote Parade, and is one of the new generation of off (and occasionally on) Broadway writers, all of whom write both music and lyrics, and most of whom have three names.

The upcoming Mirvish subscription season is loaded to the gunwales with musicals, and even before that gets started, we'll have Hairspray -- not to mention The Producers and the apparently unclosable Mamma Mia!. Robert Cushman

LITERARY

TALK ABOUT COOKING THE BOOKS

Books are not only informative and entertaining -- they're also a great source of roughage. At least, that's the premise of the International Edible Book Fest, which makes its debut in Toronto this weekend. Since 1999, groups across North America and Europe have been creating cakes, sandwiches and even fajitas in the shape of books, and then consuming their literary (and culinary) masterpieces. The first Toronto edible book event takes place on Sunday at This Ain't the Rosedale Library's Gallery Space (483 Church St.). The cooked books are on display starting at 2 p.m. The dining begins at 4.

Meanwhile, the National Post's own Anne Kingston appears at Innis Town Hall (2 Sussex Ave.) at 7:30 this evening to discuss her new book, The Meaning of Wife. The author and columnist knows a thing or two about edibility herself. Her previous work includes Dave Nichol's biography, The Edible Man. James Cowan
