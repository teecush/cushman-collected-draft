THE BLONDE, THE BRUNETTE AND THE VENGEFUL REDHEAD

Studio Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-511-7429

An Australian play, but it could happen anywhere with shopping malls. Lucy Peacock plays, superbly, seven roles (including a man and a boy) all caught up in a confrontation that turns into a catastrophe; by the end she seems to have grown not only as an actress but as a woman. The play has anecdotal rather than universal force, but thats enough to be going on with. In rep through Sept. 24.

MONTY PYTHONS SPAMALOT

Canon Theatre

244 Victoria St. - 416-872-1212

There is some sublimely funny dialogue, most of it taken directly from Monty Python and the Holy Grail. But it's also a parody musical, and what is there in the form left to make fun of? Nor does the musical form permit the unceremonious shifting from one subject to another that was the joy of the TV shows and movies. Some of it is the purest Python, even if it isn't the full Monty. Through Sept. 10.

THE COMEDY OF ERRORS

The Dream in High Park

416-366-7723

The citizens of Ephesus sport gaudy coiffures and multi-coloured costumes that suggest a fancy-dress parade rather than, as apparently intended, the day after tomorrow. But there are laughs, and charming smiles. Both pairs of twins are admirably played, though the wittiest performance is given by a free-standing and delightfully mobile door. Through Sept. 3.

DESIGN FOR LIVING

Royal George Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Noel Coward wrote this menage-a-trois comedy as a vehicle for himself and his friends, the Lunts, and it shows. Morris Panychs fine production, however, refuses to indulge the central threesome, losing a few laughs but gaining immensely in emotional reality. Nicole Underhays Gilda is a star performance without star tactics, a generous woman scared of whom she may be hurting; her partners, David Jansen and Graeme Somerville, are more mischievous, while Lorne Kennedy, the outsider, marvellously demonstrates the impotence of being Ernest. In rep through Nov. 18.

LOVE AMONG

THE RUSSIANS

Court House Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

From Russia, with varying amounts of love, comes this year's Shaw Festival lunchtime show, a double bill of Chekhov's early one-act farces. In Eda Holmess high-voltage production of The Bear, Blair Williams goes from unassuageable anger to complete erotic capitulation in 20 minutes, opposite Diana Donnellys young widow; weeds become her. In The Proposal (also about a proposal), the energy deteriorates into shouting and gesticulation. In rep through Sept. 14.

THE MAGIC FIRE

Court House Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

Lillian Groag has written a presumably autobiographical play about a young woman who, like herself, grew up in Peron's Argentina and moved to the United States. Her seven-year-old self is watched by her narrating self, which splits the focus. Her immigrant family (Austrian and Italian) fill up the stage, mostly talking about opera, their defence against the world around them. The point is made many times. Lively compensating performances by Jennifer Phipps, Michael Ball, Ric Reid and others. In rep through Oct. 8.

THE CHAIRS

Young Centre for

the Performing Arts

The great virtue of Jim Warren's Soulpepper production is that nobody seems to have told it that Eugene Ionescos minor classic is Absurd. Oliver Dennis and Kristen Thomson play the nonagenarian couple with a message for the world, absolutely for real. They are also whizzes at the farcical mechanics of filling the bare stage with real chairs for imaginary auditors. The supposed demonstration of the meaninglessness of language is dated and unproved, but its a superb portrait of a marriage. In rep through Sept. 6.

HARLEM DUET

Studio Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Djanet Sears's self-directed play about a modern New York Othello who leaves his black partner to marry his Desdemona. Being mainly domestic (with superfluous historical flashbacks), it doesn't sit too well on the thrust stage, but its superbly written, with highly charged allusive dialogue and a compelling central relationship, excellently played by Nigel Shawn Williams and Karen Robinson. Other performances are lacking, and a layer of Canadian content seems like an add-on. In rep through Sept. 22.

SOUTH PACIFIC

Avon Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Cynthia Dales Nellie Forbush is her best performance, getting right inside her songs and making us feel her happiness. Director- choreographer Michael Lichtefeld keeps Rodgers and Hammerstein's most ostentatiously romantic musical crisp. From a distance it seems a stodgy show, with formulaic comedy, but in actual performance we are conquered by its craft and awed by its self-belief. In rep through Oct. 28.

MUCH ADO

ABOUT NOTHING

Festival Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

One of the least showy, but most humane and entertaining, of recent Stratford Shakespeares. Set in 1910 (as the program tells us, with unusual precision), it has a Benedick and Beatrice who play beautifully together: Peter Donaldson a bluff barker of a misogynist, with perfect throwaway timing, and Lucy Peacock a refugee from Bloomsbury, whose love is a refuge from anger. They come together when her fury is hottest, and it's very satisfying. In rep through Oct. 22.

THE LORD OF THE RINGS

Princess of Wales Theatre

300 King St. W. - 416-872-1212

At its best, its wonderful; at its worst, its far better than doubters ever thought it could be. Scenically and technically, it's a marvel; and the adaptation of Tolkien's trilogy is outstandingly intelligent. The musical component varies, in style and effect. Notable performances by Evan Buliung, Michael Therriault and assorted hobbits. Through Sept. 3.
