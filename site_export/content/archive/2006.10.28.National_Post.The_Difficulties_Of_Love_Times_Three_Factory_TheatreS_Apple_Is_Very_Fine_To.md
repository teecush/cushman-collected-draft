Coincidence in drama can be, and often is, a cheap way of getting out of a tight corner.

But it can also, as a sentence of execution is said to do, concentrate the mind wonderfully. It can make fortuitous character connections into the strands of a universal cat's cradle, comic or tragic. Apple, by the Alberta dramatist Vern Thiessen -- his first play to be seen in Toronto though hes been winning prizes all over and is very big in Poland -- works just that way and works just fine. Very fine in fact.

It has three characters: Evelyn, a real-estate agent; her husband Andy, just sacked from the civil service; and Samantha, who progresses in the course of the play from medical student to oncologists intern. Here's how they interconnect: Andy has an affair with Samantha; Samantha engages Evelyn to sell her condo (a flash joint for someone in her position, but there's an explanation); Evelyn develops breast cancer, and Samantha not only turns out to be her specialists assistant but virtually takes over the direction of her treatment. Belief falters at this last convergence -- there are any number of ethical reasons for Samantha to opt out of the case and she would surely have done so. But the contrivance is forgivable for the way it throws the threesome not into conflict but into contrast.

The play, which calls for no realistic settings, moves between two homes and a hospital, but comes most to rest in a park. Andy and Evelyn used to frequent it and will do so again; Andy, jobless, comes there for solace; Samantha, whose room overlooks it, sees Andy sitting on his favourite bench and comes down to talk to him and eventually to take him home and then take him.

This isnt a cancer play," though it's both more forthright and more dignified on the subject than any other I have seen. It uses the disease partly as a metaphor for the ways in which the cells of a relationship can self-destruct or regenerate. That probably sounds too fancy for comfort, but in fact the play functions most of the time as domestic drama pared down not so much to essentials as to extremes. Thiessen seems to have an enviable ear; he can glide from controlled lyricism into bleak confrontation. Evelyn, a workaholic with a grievance against almost everyone, begins the play by railing against her husband's domestic fecklessness; his disclosure that hes been fired doesnt earn him much of a reprieve, and his attempts to find new employment don't win him much sympathy, either. Evelyn claims at one point never to have loved Andy; we may not believe her (I didn't), but something in their childless 16-year marriage has clearly rusted. Later of course she needs him and then she loves him, and he reciprocates; hes probably afraid not to.

Previously he has sought love, as well as sex, from Samantha, who has not reciprocated; her work, or the things shes seen there, has soured her on commitment. She changes her mind, but too late. Thiessen's touch can be both astonishingly light and painfully honest, as when Andy glancingly acknowledges his financial straits.

Ken Gass's production is designed by Marian Wihak and designed by Kimberly Purtell, with music by Wayne Kelso; their collaboration matches the script in tact, elegance and power. The play is performed behind a scrim, which is sometimes used for projections; this distances the action but never muffles it.

The actors are excellent. Paradoxically, none of them is quite right; even more paradoxically, it doesnt, in the end, matter. The most troublesome is Niki Landaus Samantha who comes off not just cool but cold, even when taking Andy to bed. A problem for her is that she functions not just as a character in the play but as a frame for it, lecturing a new generation of students about cancer, and this clinical detachment colours the rest of her performance; the white coat never comes off. Sarah Orenstein, by contrast, has the warmth and wit of a star, so Evelyn's hardness, brilliantly though she plays it, never seems to go all the way through. Her anger, whether at her despised co-workers or her patronizing specialist, is so fierce and funny as to be endearing. There's humour, too, in her final bleakness, but there its exactly right; its her kind of love. Orenstein, who has sometimes seemed deracinated since leaving the Shaw Festival, is here in her finest form. Kevin Hanchard's Andy, vulnerable to a surprisingly strong core, is perfect apart from being, or at least seeming, too young; it isn't credible that he should be seeking work from people half his age. There, by the way, is another built-in contrast between him and his wife: He responds to questionnaires with rueful patience, she chews them up.

What other love is going around? The last words on disillusion and self-deception are said, and the last notes sounded, in Mozarts Cosi fan tutte, the first offering of the first regular season in the Canadian Opera Companys new house. (The Ring was a gigantic hors d'oeuvre.)

This staging, which dates back 14 years, isn't one of the COCs best; its old-fashioned design entails thunderous set-changes, behind a gold traverse curtain, that sometimes threaten to drown out the music. But its still the purest distillation of the comic spirit, and the most even-handed; men may think that women are deceivers ever, but in proving it they show themselves to be frauds as well, and self-satisfied ones at that.

The second-biggest laugh in this production comes when one disguised gallant lords it over another whose fiancee he has seduced; the biggest is when his friend returns the favour. Meanwhile, they have been torn between two incompatible desires: They want both to be thought irresistible and to think their girlfriends incorruptible. The double wedding at the end is a celebration of human frailty. It can be played for bitterness, as here, or for wise acceptance, as would be preferable. The participants, after all, are very young. Or they should be.

And if you think women are scurvily treated in Cosi, take a gander at a love-story told some 250 years earlier by the pioneering Elizabethan playwright Robert Greene. His Friar Bacon and Friar Bungay, one of the three plays in the Shakespeare and the Queen's Men project, has a charming heroine named Margaret, a.k.a. The Fair Maid of Fressingfield (that's in Suffolk), who is wooed and won by a nobleman who then dumps her by messenger and with some cash thrown in as a sweetener. She is all set to get her to a nunnery, when her betrothed appears and says, with no appearance of remorse, that it was all meant as a jovial test of her fidelity. She swallows it. Now I wouldnt claim that this is charming, though the original audience probably took it in their stride.

What is notable is how Julian De Zotti, a young actor with a knack for playing virtuous and spirited maidens (hes Cordella in the same team's King Leir) manages to play both the hurt and the acceptance without fuss and with the slightest hint of a twinkle. I doubt that many contemporary actresses would bring that off, or would want to try. De-sexualizing these plays, though I wouldn't want to see it become the rule, certainly solves some problems.

The production, during the early performance when I saw it, was slow, but had a good performance by Jason Gray as the magician Friar Bacon, who can out-conjure any mere foreigner but then gets a conscience and retires, and a riotous performance by Alon Nashman as his un-industrious apprentice whom we last see being carried off to Hell on his masters orders and liking it. (The devil who transports him apparently hasnt heard of the Friar's repentance. Or maybe some spells just stick.)

The first part of the project -- The Queen's Men in London -- wound up last week at the Glen Morris Studio; the second part -- The Queens Men on Tour -- has been taking the troupe this week to places as far flung as the Tranzac Club and even Hamilton.

HOSANNA

Young Centre

for the Performing Arts

55 Mill St., Building 49

416-866-8666

Michel Tremblay's 30-year-old play survives on the wit, passion and scabrous detail of the relationship between the drag-queen hairdresser and his/her biker boy-friend. Salvatore Antonio, pictured below, misses out on the verbal glitter in the first half; but he looks a peach when impersonating Elizabeth Taylor impersonating Cleopatra, and is moving when stripped of his finery at the end. Ends tonight.

SOUTH PACIFIC

Avon Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Cynthia Dale's Nellie Forbush is her best performance, getting right inside her songs and making us feel her happiness. Director- choreographer Michael Lichtefeld keeps Rodgers and Hammersteins most ostentatiously romantic musical crisp. From a distance it seems a stodgy show, with formulaic comedy, but in actual performance we are conquered by its craft and awed by its self-belief. Ends tonight.

GENEROUS

Tarragon Theatre

30 Bridgman Ave. - 416-531-1827

Michael Healeys new play, about the costs and rewards of virtue, lives up to its title: in the social and emotional ground it covers, in its barbed laughter, in its sharpness and compassion about relationships. It's also a generous play structurally, though not as complex as all that. It's a good play about politics and an even better play about love. A cartooned first scene is a one-off that affects all that follows, if only philosophically. Superb production and acting, with Fiona Reid and Jordan Pettle outstanding. Ends tomorrow.

OLIVER!

Festival Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Donna Feore directs a stage-filling production of Lionel Barts epic crowd-pleaser of a musical, one that even manages in its opening scenes, and sometimes later, to sneak in some of the nightmare atmosphere of Dickens original non-stop chronicle of crime and child abuse. Bart, whose genius was instinctive if short- lived, spoke showbiz like a native, resulting in a self-believing score in which even a rank bad song like As Long As He Needs Me sort of works. Colm Feore revels in Bart's comical Fagin; Tyler Pearse's Oliver is splendidly acted and sung; Bruce Dow and Mary-Ellen Mahoney are perfectly matched workhouse tyrants. Ends tomorrow.

OF MICE AND MEN

Bluma Appel Theatre

27 Front St. E. - 416-368-3110

John Steinbecks classic novel-into-play is devilishly well constructed, and Dennis Garnhums production is excellent, with magnificent sets, alternating stunning vistas of golden corn with timbered interiors. Shaun Smyth is an edgy George, Ashley Wright a trusting one-track Lennie, and there's a bunkhouseful of other good performances. The story shows the destruction of all best-laid plans, but play and production are proof that they can sometimes work out. Through Nov. 11.

DESIGN FOR LIVING

Royal George Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Noel Coward wrote this menage a trois comedy as a vehicle for himself and his friends the Lunts, and it shows. Morris Panych's fine production, however, refuses to indulge the central threesome, losing a few laughs but gaining immensely in emotional reality. Nicole Underhays Gilda is a star performance without star tactics, a generous woman scared of whom she may be hurting; her partners, David Jansen and Graeme Somerville, are more mischievous, while Lorne Kennedy, the outsider, marvellously demonstrates the impotence of being Ernest. In rep through Nov. 18.

HIGH SOCIETY

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

The show of the movie musical of the play (that was also a movie). It never worked, and this busy production is particularly coarse. The augmented Cole Porter score is made up not of songs the characters just have to sing but of songs its not quite impossible they might; it's dehydrating. Dan Chameroy and the sets are good. In rep through Nov. 19.

WICKED

Canon Theatre

244 Victoria St. - 416-872-1212

A sort of parallel text to The Wizard of Oz but one in which narrative consistency goes by the board, music and lyrics are bombastic apart from a couple of nice tunes and sets and costumes are cluttered. It's preachy but doesnt have the guts to follow through to the logical unhappy ending. Ding dong, the wicked old witch is dead -- but this one isnt wicked, old or even dead -- just misunderstood. Through Dec. 31.

DEAD AIR!

Mysteriously Yours

2026 Yonge St. - 416-486-7469

The plot and characters of the new Mysteriously Yours show are the same as the plot and characters of all their previous shows. But this time there's a new wrinkle; this, as its subtitle says, is a musical murder mystery that sharpens the element of parody, here directed at old-time network radio presenting cowboy stars in things with titles like Withering Heights. (A Brontesaurus?) The songs are neat and all the cast have voices, including Jean Daigle doing his customary interactive turn as both corpse and sleuth. Indefinite run.
