robert.cushman@hotmail.com; Seussical: A Musical runs through Dec. 31 at the Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People, 416-822-2222; Songs for a New World runs through Dec. 16 at the Young Centre, 416- 866-8666; Thom Pain runs through Dec. 17 at the Tarragon Extra Space, 416-531-1827.

So what's a Seuss? In North America, the question is probably rhetorical, but that isn't necessarily the case elsewhere. I grew up far away, and the first time I ever heard mention of Dr. Seuss was in the lyric to Little Green Apples. And I was in my 20s when I heard that. But even here and now I feel like asking the question. I have just seen Seussical: A Musical, a show inspired by the Doctor's stories, characters and whimsical language, the latter reflected from the very first song, which is called Oh the Thinks You Can Think. On the very last note it turns out that one of those thinks is the name Seuss itself, a point reiterated at the final curtain. Despite all the effort the cast put into it, it comes across as bald, unsupported and what-are-they-talking about. Perhaps they should have spelled it out in coloured lights across the backcloth.

Or maybe that isn't necessary, and the word actually makes the climactic point it's supposed to make. Maybe. But it suggests to me that what is being hawked here is less a subject than a brand. I suspect that this is what has dogged the show ever since its first incarnation as a full-scale musical on Broadway, where it had a modest run after a fraught tryout. It takes the audience's interest too much for granted. And this is the case even in the modest, and sometimes charming, version that is being staged at the Lorraine Kimsa. It's an economy edition, prepared by the still-underrated team of Lynn Ahrens and Stephen Flaherty, who wrote the score for Ragtime and are here credited with score and book and with having, along with Eric Idle, "co-conceived" the show, whatever that means.

Streamlined though it now is, it still has bits sticking out. What, for example, is the Cat in the Hat doing there? Well, of course he couldn't be left out. He's the most celebrated character, with the most recognizable visual signature in the Seussian canon; even I know that. But all the show can do is give him a master of ceremonies function whose main effect is to delay the arrival of the main narrative and to get in its way once it's started.

That story does work. It concerns Horton the elephant, who persists in protecting the bunch of clover that contains the planet's worth of infinitesimal people called the Whos because, as he keeps singing, "a person's a person, no matter how small." George Masswohl plays him with a deliberation and concern that are impeccably and gracefully elephantine, if you are willing to admit that an elephant can have grace.

All Horton's altruism gets him, though, from most of his fellow Creatures is the reputation of Biggest Damn Fool thrown at him in a savagely repetitive rhythm-and-blues number of that name: a derision increased severalfold when he spends about a year sitting on a bird's egg whose feckless mother has abandoned it for the bright lights of Palm Beach. When it eventually hatches it turns out, in a clear example of poetic justice (or, the Seuss-source being what it is, light-versical justice), to look half like Horton. It's an elephant bird; it, too, must have been co-conceived.

Luckily, Horton has a real bird on hand to help bring it up: his neighbour, Miss Gertrude McFuzz, who has long sought his attention, first growing and then shedding a long tail to aid the process. Corinne Koslo plays Gertrude, and her account of her odyssey, a Tail of Woe, decorated with the odd spasm of hysterical accusation, is the incontestable highlight of the proceedings.

Nobody else in the cast reaches the level set by Masswohl and Koslo, and some fall painfully below it. There isn't much magic or invention in Allen MacInnis's production, while Nicola Pantin's choreography and Philip Clarkson's costumes are strictly from hunger. Most of the songs from the full-length score are still present, though some have suffered internal damage. How Lucky You Are, a cheer-up number with a diabolically catchy Flaherty tune, has lost the smartest of Ahrens's lyric lines ("and when worse comes to worst, as we all know it must"), as if such passing cynicism were thought -- wrongly -- too much for the kids to take. Or maybe I just didn't hear it; on the press night a muddy sound system meant that many words were lost. But I don't think these were among them.

There is more musical excitement in Songs for a New World than in any show to hit Toronto since (as opposed to during) The Last Five Years. Both scores are the work of one of the current generation of off-Broadway prodigies, Jason Robert Brown. The current one was his first and is far from his best; it's a song cycle whose unity and theatricality owe more to wishful thinking than to any perceptible continuity.

The style is pitched somewhere between Sondheim and The Fantasticks, with a coating of folk and rock, though its most immediate progenitors may be Richard Maltby, Jr., and David Shire, a team whose perceptive and propulsive revue songs haven't usually been matched by their book shows. Brown's progress has been in the opposite direction; his later scores have character and action but those qualities are lacking in most of these early songs. They also have an -- almost -- uniform inspirational quality that gets monotonous. Melodically, though, they are adventurous if not often distinctive; and rhythmically they are killers. It is this last quality that, propelled by four superb singers, takes the show through the roof.

The spine tingles from the very first note, sung by Tracy Michalidis, whose voice and smile are about equally intoxicating. She later shares a love duet with Jason Knight, in which both parties look and sound authentically enchanted. Sharron Matthews fires off two full-throttle black-comedy numbers: One (Just One Step) has a fur-clad Park Avenue lady contemplating throwing herself out the window; the other (Surabaya Santa) has Mrs. S. Claus lamenting in the accents, if not the accent, of Brecht and Weill. The ideas of both are funnier than the actual words, but that isn't the actress's fault. She's contrastingly and surprisingly restrained in the show's best-known song, Stars and the Moon, about a woman who turns down poets and marries riches and -- of course -- regrets it. Once again I wish for more cynicism: Why couldn't she have decided that rich was better, like in real life?

Thom Allison combines a devilish grin, a voice that peals and aches and caresses and a personality that conquers. On the too-rare occasions when the quartet join forces, most notably on the pulsating The River Don't Flow, the theatre vibrates. The musicians, under Noreen Waibel, are magnificent, and this time the sound (by John Lott) is impeccable. Chris Abraham's direction is, for so essentially static a piece, amazingly stylish -- great credit to Beth Kates's lighting -- and inventive; every move means something. The production does fall down, though, on clarifying a couple of songs that seem to have historical settings; the show's title may mean more than is apparent.

An American critic described Will Eno, author of the monologue Thom Pain (based on Nothing) as "a Samuel Beckett for the Jon Stewart generation"; the phrase is much quoted in the Toronto production's publicity, and it seems very apt. I imagine that the Beckett part is what attracted the director, Jennifer Tarver; she handles the disquieting aspects very well. She's less successful with the wise-cracking parts, possibly because her performer, Tom McCamus, is a finer actor than he is a stand-up comic. He does very well alternately taunting and embarrassing us, and the character he plays, from behind a pair of horn-rimmed glasses, but he isn't quite light enough.

The script keeps circling back to an anecdote about a boy and a dog that isn't as compellingly traumatic as it thinks it is. Conceivably, though, that's part of the point. This is the theatre of wrong-footedness; the piece plays with our expectations about theatre, life and narrative logic: " 'You've changed', she said, the night we met." It is -- and this, at least, is predictable -- stimulating and exasperating in about equal measure.

INSOMNIA Buddies in Bad Times Theatre 11 Alexander St. - 416-975- 8555

Two of the characters in Daniel Brooks's Insomnia suffer from the titular complaint; a third from its opposite, narcolepsy. The fourth seems to have no sleep disorders. Mesmerizingly staged by Chris Abraham, deals with a breaking marriage, on which subject it carries complete conviction, and with society at large, all of it, on which it is a lot chancier. Brooks himself is a journeyman actor whose own belief in the central role nevertheless ensures ours, and his three colleagues are irreproachable. Closes tomorrow.

THE OXFORD ROOF CLIMBER'S REBELLION Tarragon Theatre 30 Bridgman Ave. - 416-531-1827

The roof climber is Lawrence of Arabia, a fellow of All Souls College, post-First World War. Robert Graves, poet, is his contemporary, and Stephen Masicotte's play deals with Lawrence's anger and guilt, Graves's shell-shock, their possible homosexuality, Graves's marriage and his wife's feminism, the reactionary Lord Curzon, a father's bereavement, and the birth of the modern Middle East. That's a lot to get in and the result, though comprehensive, lacks both weight and style. Good performances from Tom Rooney, Jonathan Crombie and especially Michelle Giroux. Through Dec. 17.

PIPPIN Royal Alexandra Theatre 260 King St. W. - 416-872-1212

Stephen Schwartz's best score is affixed, like all the others, to a mawkish, pretentious show. Its hero (a charming Joshua Park) is the son of the Emperor Charlemagne in search of "a higher purpose." This revival suffers from de-Fossefication; Bob Fosse, the show's original director, pursuing his own higher purpose, gave it a brilliant staging that he then proceeded to tear down. Without his inventiveness and his obsessiveness, the piece is pointless. Through Dec. 3.

THE CARETAKER Young Centre for the Performing Arts 55 Mill St., Bldg. 49 416-866-8666

Soulpepper's best new production this year. (Our Town was a revival.) From the moment he comes on stage, twitching and blinking, Diego Matamoros's tramp, the cuckoo in Harold Pinter's nest, is indelible. Damien Atkins is delicate and Matthew Edison ferally frightening as the brothers he tries to play against one another. Albert Schultz's direction of Pinter's 1960 masterpiece makes the power shifts brilliantly clear. Through Nov. 25.

DESCENT Theatre Passe Muraille 16 Ryerson Ave. - 416-504-7529

The descent is that of Susan, whose desire for a pre-marital stag night, or its female equivalent, leads her eight years later to a nightmare night in an abandoned church in the company of four men, two of them named Randy and all of them lusting after her. Tom Walmsley's latest Skid Row excursion feels like a five-way monologue and takes for granted characters and relationships it hasn't established. Deborah Hay has style, especially in her jaded diatribes. Ends tomorrow.

WICKED Canon Theatre 244 Victoria St. - 416-872-1212

A sort of parallel text to The Wizard of Oz but one in which narrative consistency goes by the board, music and lyrics are bombastic apart from a couple of nice tunes, and sets and costumes are cluttered. This play is preachy but doesn't have the guts to follow through to the logical unhappy ending. Ding dong, the wicked old witch is dead -- but this one isn't wicked, old or even dead -- just misunderstood. Through Dec. 31.

DESIGN FOR LIVING Royal George Theatre Niagara-on-the-Lake 1-800- 511-7429

Noel Coward wrote this menage a trois comedy as a vehicle for himself and his friends the Lunts. Morris Panych's fine production, however, refuses to indulge the central threesome, losing a few laughs but gaining emotional reality. Nicole Underhay's Gilda is a star performance without star tactics, a generous woman scared of whom she may be hurting; her partners, David Jansen and Graeme Somerville, are more mischievous, while Lorne Kennedy, the outsider, marvellously demonstrates the impotence of being Ernest. Ends tonight.

HIGH SOCIETY Festival Theatre Niagara-on-the-Lake 1-800-511-7429

The show of the movie musical of the play. It never worked, and this busy production is particularly coarse. The augmented Cole Porter score is made up not of songs the characters just have to sing but of songs it's not quite impossible they might. Dan Chameroy and the sets are good. Ends tomorrow.
