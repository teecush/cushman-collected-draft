Diego Matamoros is a comic virtuoso in The Government Inspector, and Robert Cushmans actor of the year; Michael Levine, right, gets top nod for designer of the year.: A few of the best Who Moved To The Greatest Effect

This, in Toronto theatre, has been the year of real estate. The important events have been the opening of the Four Seasons Centre and the Young Centre as homes for the Canadian Opera Company and Soulpepper respectively and as actual or potential facilities for other groups as well. Both are a pleasure to visit; the Young Centre has the warmest, most welcoming lobby in Toronto, and the Opera House, with its clean architectural lines and crystalline acoustics, is closer to perfection than one had dared to hope.

Moving from places to people, here are a few of those who in 2006 moved and shook to the greatest effect: Director of the year This has to be Peter Hinton, whose Stratford production of The Duchess of Malfi was the dramatic high point of the year, a continuous flow of visual and aural invention, keyed with absolute accuracy to the text itself. (This puts it ahead of Hinton's production the previous year of Into the Woods, which was almost equally imaginative but had far less to do with the piece in hand.) At the opposite extreme, he also staged -- and wrote -- Stratford's Fanny Kemble, which was creative far beyond the customary call of duty for one-person plays, and provided both a frame and a spur for a stunningly resourceful performance by Domini Blythe.

Hinton then went on to take charge of the English company at the National Arts Centre, where he kicked off with Allen Coles musical The Wrong Son. This, even in the concert version that reached Toronto, was a superbly exciting show, in score, narrative, performance and -- as we could gather from back projected pictures of the Ottawa original -- staging. Indeed, about the only thing wrong with it was the title. On the evidence of the past few years, no Canadian director works better with actors than Hinton, and none works as well with designers.

Unless you count the Designer of the year, Michael Levine, who this year, making his directorial debut with Rheingold, worked superbly well with himself. This crowned the COC production of the Ring, of which he -- along with Richard Bradshaw as conductor, impresario and driving force -- was the unifying factor. Just getting the opera house would have been triumph enough; opening it with a world-class climb to the summit of the repertoire was a mind- blower. Thats not to mention the emergence of Adrianne Pieczonka as a home-grown world-class soprano whom even I, a lifelong divasceptic if not divaphobe, could appreciate. Actor of the year Diego Matamoros. This is becoming predictable, but it's hard to deny the title to a performer who can kick off with a delicious cameo in Our Town, then move on immediately to full-fledged comic virtuosity in The Government Inspector and come back just as funny, while also flapping with a vindictive kind of dignity, in The Caretaker, having stopped off en route to deliver a performance of Lear's Fool that was immensely striking at first encounter but dwindled later on. Nobodys perfect. Actresses of the year There were two, a veteran and a comparative newcomer. Martha Henrys Mrs. Alving in Ghosts had, in addition to the expected emotional clout, enriching quantities of watchfulness and wit. (They're native to her, but I think she may have honed them with her all-but-silent performance in last year's Vigil.) She was also no slouch as Volumnia in Coriolanus. And -- can we bring in TV here? -- she was a riot, as well as a redeeming feature, in Ken Finklemans anti-serial At the Hotel Meanwhile, at the Shaw, Nicole Underhay more than fulfilled earlier promise with star-level work in Too True To Be Good and Design for Living, the latter a show in which she reached star level by eschewing star tactics. Company actors of the year Scott Wentworth ( The Duchess of Malfi, Henry IV: Part One) at Stratford. Peter Hutt ( Arms and the Man, Rosmersholm) at the Shaw. Playwright of the year You mean there was one? Well, yes, there was: Michael Healey who demonstrated a kind of versatility by giving us both The Innocent Eye Test, which, if only by virtue of its high-profile commercial production, has to be rated one of the worst plays of the year, and Generous, which was the best: a first-rate production, too, by Daryl Cloran, with an especially fine acting duet by Fiona Reid and, in a role that might have been written for him, Jordan Pettle. The years other good new plays were Leo by Rosa Laborde (who also distinguished herself this year by drowning at the beginning of every single episode of At the Hotel), Vern Thiessen's Apple, Sky Gilbert's very funny Bad Acting Teachers, Alex Poch- Goldins Cringeworthy and, at Summerworks, The Russian

Play by Hannah Moscovitch, who is definitely one to watch. We all moan, but in truth thats not a bad record. Both Generous and Leo (a pitch-perfect production by Richard Rose) were at Tarragon, a good record for a theatre in the accident-prone business of producing new plays. Its worst accident was Past Perfect, a lesser Michel Tremblay given an agonizingly pretentious production that crippled some perfectly good actors.

SOLO PHENOMENA OF THE YEAR

D'bi young, extraordinary in her own play, blood.klaat, which started as part of a new-voices season that this year provided Theatre Passe Muraille withits main reason for being. Daniel MacIvor, equally extraordinary in his own (revived) play Here Lies Henry, in which he justified his legend. He also wrote and directed A Beautiful View, another of the best new plays (and productions). His constant collaborator, Daniel Brooks, who directed him in Henry, came back as author and actor in Insomnia, directed in rivetting Brooksian style by Chris Abraham, who, what with this and Cringeworthy and the musical Songs for a New World, had a year.

THE DROWSY CHAPERONE OF THE YEAR

A Canadian musical is a hit on Broadway, and we all feel very patriotic. It was fun, too. Though, except in terms of performance and production -- admittedly two very large exceptions -- it was actually more fun on its last Toronto outing some years ago at the Winter Garden. Maybe it's just the historic proximity of the real thing, but the pastiche songs in this pastiche musical now seem very hit-and-miss. Still, Bob Martin as the narrator-protagonist is a joy. There was more musicaltheatrical excitement in The Lord of the Rings, a roughly equivalent transplant (British writing and direction, Canadian casting), but it was a commercial flop so were not allowed to say so.

CLASSICAL COMPANIES OF THE YEAR

We still have three, but one of them has expanded exponentially. Soulpepper has mounted nine plays in its new home, with the same recognizable core of actors, and maintained an overall air of excitement. That, in Toronto, is an extraordinary, indeed unprecedented, achievement. So bear that in mind while I do some carping. The Soulpepper year was bookended with two great productions, Our Town and A Christmas Carol, but they were both revivals of past successes. Between them, a company that established itself with bold choices of large-scale classics played it comparatively safe by playing it modern. Individually, all the play choices were reasonable; collectively, they seemed small. The two biggest texts, King Lear and The Government Inspector, both fell short, though they came closer to success than was generally acknowledged. Next seasons repertoire looks considerably more exciting. Note, too, that in male leading actors, Soulpepper's strength is unrivalled; Albert Schultz, Joseph Ziegler, Diego Matamoros, William Webster, Oliver Dennis, all on more or less permanent call.

The Shaw Festival, barring a couple of populist stinkers, had a good year, with real eye-openers in Rosmersholm, The Heiress, Design for Living and The Crucible. The new regime seems indistinguishable from the excellent old one except that it does more plays by women; a shame they aren't better ones.

Stratford had a rewarding and, believe it or not, an enterprising year. Apart from shows already mentioned, there were The Glass Menagerie, South Pacific, Harlem Duet and The Blonde, the Brunette and the Vengeful Redhead, a multiply transforming solo by Lucy Peacock. There were also four decent Shakespeares, including a truthfully comic Henry IV and a Much Ado About Nothing with a Benedick and Beatrice who made complementary sense. One wouldnt claim, though, that they were transformingly imaginative productions. Are such things still possible? Apparently, yes. Britains Royal Shakespeare Company, recently given up for dead, visited the University of Michigan, in Ann Arbor, with a good Julius Caesar, a superb Antony and Cleopatra and a truly stunning Tempest. The last two starred Patrick Stewart, who was a Shakespearean before he was a Star Trekker, and he was excellent. All the same, the company's overall acting standard was no higher than ours, and maybe not as good. But it's plain that in terms of investigating meaning and making it theatrical, we have some ground to make up. Antony and The Tempest will be playing in London next year; if youre passing through, dont miss them. I'll be writing about them in greater detail. In the meantime, let me, while straying far afield, recommend the film of Alan Bennett's moving and hilarious The History Boys, with the original British cast and director. I havent seen it, but if theyre as good on screen as they were when I saw them onstage in New York, you're in for something quite shamelessly enjoyable.

RADIO CITY CHRISTMAS

SPECTACULAR

Hummingbird Centre

1 Front St. E. - 416-872-2262

The Rockettes have been performing their Parade of the Wooden Soldiers for decades, and it's some kind of high-precision classic. Everything else in this lavish Spectacular is utterly generic; its like nothing, and everything, youve ever seen. There's a sliver of continuity, about Santa Claus and how hard-worked he is; something called Santa's Gonna Rock and Roll might be described, if one were feeling unseasonally uncharitable, as an abomination. The finale is ultra- traditional involving three real camels (or are they dromedaries; as Ogden Nash wrote, "Im never sure, are you?"). Ends tomorrow.

WICKED

Canon Theatre

244 Victoria St. - 416-872-1212

A sort of parallel text to The Wizard of Oz but one in which narrative consistency goes by the board, music and lyrics are bombastic apart from a couple of nice tunes, and sets and costumes are cluttered. Its preachy but doesn't have the guts to follow through to the logical unhappy ending. Ding dong, the wicked old witch is dead -- but this one isn't wicked, old or even dead -- just misunderstood. Ends tomorrow.

SEUSSICAL -- A MUSICAL

Lorraine Kimsa Theatre

for Young People

165 Front St. E. - 416-862-2222

An economy version of Lynn Ahrenss and Stephen Flahertys Broadway musical based on the characters, stories and linguistic contortions of Dr. Seuss. It takes the audience's interest toomuch for granted (the Cat in the Hat seems to be there only because he couldn't be left out) but it can be charming, and it works once the basic narrative gets started. Lovely performances from George Masswohl and Corinne Koslo as Horton the elephant and Miss Gertrude McFuzz, respectively -- both, in their different ways, paragons fidelity. Ends tomorrow.

DEAD AIR!

Mysteriously Yours

2026 Yonge St. - 416-486-7469

The plot and characters of the new Mysteriously Yours show are the same as the plot and characters of all their previous shows. But this time theres a new wrinkle; this, as its subtitle says, is a musicalmurder mystery that sharpens the element of parody, here directed at old-time network radio presenting cowboy stars in things with titles like Withering Heights. (A Brontesaurus?) The songs are neat and all the cast have voices, including Jean Daigle doing his customary interactive turn as both corpse and sleuth. Indefinite run.

BIRD FLU OVER THE CUCKOOS NEST

Second City

51 Mercer St. - 416-343-0011

The groan-title has a modicum of relevance; there is a number about avian flu, an East Indian calypso. Political satire fails, as usual, to materialize, but there are several goodies including Naomi Snieckus radiating every variety of shock and delight as a mother hacking into her son's MSN account. Indefinite run.
