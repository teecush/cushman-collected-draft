Quentin Tarantino has said the sign of a good film is that it makes you want to go home, eat some pie and talk about it. With that in mind, our Popcorn Panel features film buffs feuding in this space each week.

THIS WEEK'S PANEL:

- Craig Courtice, a short filmmaker who isn't very tall

- Robert Cushman, the Post's theatre critic

- Prof. John Marshall, University of Toronto expert on early Christianity and author of Parables of War: Reading John's Jewish Apocalypse, a 2002 nominee for the Canadian Society of Biblical Studies Book Award

THIS WEEK'S PIE: A picnic de video.

THIS WEEK'S FILM: The Da Vinci Code.

CRAIG: When Martin Scorsese's The Last Temptation of Christ was in pre-production, Christian protests forced Universal Studios to put the kibosh on it for five years. When it was finally released in 1988, over 3,500 theatres refused to show it. Radical French Catholics even threw Molotov cocktails into a movie house, injuring 13 people. Why? Because it had a scene in which Jesus, while he was on the cross, dreamed of a life with Mary Magdalene. The Da Vinci Code posits that not only was Jesus mortal, but that he married Mary Magdalene, had a child with her and that his bloodline continues today. Half-hearted religious protests had little or no effect on Code -- Columbia took in an ungodly US$77-million on its opening weekend. I think the studio should send a cheque to Universal for clearing the way, no?

JOHN: Can I get in on that cheque? In fact, can we get two centuries of historical-critical study of early Christianity in on that cheque? While I wouldn't want to sign on to the particulars of Code's claims, I would emphasize that the notion that the story of Jesus has been tampered with has roots deeper than Scorsese. The director may have paved the way cinematically, but the broader cultural thirst that Dan Brown taps into also feeds on impulses we see mirrored in the success of the Left Behind books and their critically panned movie versions. People of all sorts of faiths, or of none, seem to be hooked on creative retellings and countertellings of Christianity's fundamental narratives.

ROBERT: It's a tribute to Christianity, surely, that people keep talking about it. But we're confronted here with a wholesale critique of the Catholic Church that only holds water in one particular -- the Church's misogynistic record in its persecution of witches -- which is only mentioned for a moment. The rest is just laboriously fanciful; I notice that even here the movie hedges its bets -- when Tom Hanks keeps saying, "Whoa!" to some of Ian McKellen's more fevered allegations -- in a way the book, I'm told, doesn't. There, I've said it: I haven't read the book. But I gathered it was a page-turner, however badly written; the movie is poorly written, directed and edited, and photographically -- given all those locations -- it's a long litany of missed opportunities. Still, it must be the best cinematic publicity Leonardo has had since he was a Ninja Turtle.

CRAIG: I bought my copy at the drugstore. It was either a pack of gum or the book. The gum package was probably better written, but The Code did give me lots to chew on. I thought the screenwriter did a fine job, considering the source material and considering he was answerable to Brown. The one choice Ron Howard made that strayed from the book was his use of flashbacks to portray historical events. Big mistake, Opie. I laughed out loud at the scene in which they debated the gospels. Is this any way to get folks interested in religious history?

JOHN: I'd never tried to imagine the Council of Nicaea visually, but the flashback struck me more like the witches in Macbeth than anything else. The issue of getting people interested in the warts- and-all history of Christianity on the basis of a movie is a tricky dance of mutual exploitation. The movies and the media try to generate interest and controversy to sell tickets and papers and soap commercials. Scholars try to pull people back from the most outrageous and unhistorical elements of the controversy and push people towards the interesting human and historical elements of the development of Christianity. I've been in this tug-of-war on The Passion of the Christ, The Gospel of Judas and now this. That's why I'm eating popcorn on the National Post's dime. Can we do Life of Brian someday? There is arguably more substance and better writing there.

ROBERT: Well, it's Life of Brian and Monty Python and the Holy Grail, isn't it? And probably The Meaning of Life, as well. I thought the historical flashbacks were necessary but unfortunate. Unfortunate in that they looked like outtakes from past Hollywood epics; necessary in that audiences with only vague ideas about who the Emperor Constantine was might have needed the nudge. McKellen seemed to enjoy doing all that manic exposition, and I enjoyed watching him enjoy himself. Though he seems to have reached the Olivier-Gielgud plateau for highly-paid-great-British-actor shamelessness a little early in life. I also thought Tom Hanks put up a good fight against total miscasting, both as intellectual and as action hero. I should think it was frustrating, too, that he was denied any romantic payoff because his leading lady turned out to be a descendant of Christ. Though the film is telling us that Jesus' descendants have always had romantic interests -- or they would have stopped descending.
