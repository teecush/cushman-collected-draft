HIGH SOCIETY

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

Already this new season the Shaw Festival has given us the good (The Heiress) and the indifferent (Arms and the Man). Here, in unwished-for symmetry, comes the bad.

There have been at least three attempts to make a stage musical out of High Society, and none of them have worked. There's no way they could. The given elements not only fail to jell; they cancel one another out. The immediate source is a 1956 movie, from the twilight years of the MGM musical. It has a pleasant but unremarkable score by Cole Porter, who was also in his twilight; its significant that the best number, Well Did You Evah, is a recycling of a Porter song from 20 years earlier. Its also significant that the number is remembered not for its intrinsic qualities but for being a duet for Bing Crosby and Frank Sinatra, stepping outside the story and virtually playing themselves. This really is the decadence of the movie musical; it celebrates not the performers' skills but -- to coin a word we undoubtedly need -- their famousness. It is also, of course, a hard act for later performers to follow. (Louis Armstrong's in the picture, too, literally playing himself. Thats an impossible act to follow, and none of the stage versions have tried.)

The movie is itself based on a famous play, Philip Barrys The Philadelphia Story (1939), which became a classic film in its own non-musical right. Cary Grant and Katharine Hepburn are in it; that means we have two generations of legend to contend with. The play, and all its derivatives, concern the melting of an ice-maiden; Tracy Lord, beautiful and self-righteous society heiress -- all this year's Shaw shows seem to be about heiresses -- is about to marry George Kittredge, so humourless he's funny, who has worked his way up from the bottom (of a coal mine). No one approves, least of all Tracys once and obviously future husband Dexter, who is -- well, he was -- Cary Grant and who lives conveniently next door.

Barrys play is not so much an American classic as an apology for one. It's neither as wise nor as witty as it thinks it is. But it's well-made, and doesnt take too kindly to having songs poked into it. The High Society movie gets around this, just about; there are Porters songs, generic and few in number, and there is the story, and they go their separate ways. ("End of song" says Satchmo at the start of the picture; beginning of story. And so it goes, throughout.) But you can't write a show that way, not these days, so adaptors, with the blessing of the permissive Porter estate, raid the catalogue for additional songs in the forlorn hope that they will fit the action. The inevitable dehydrating result is a score made up not of songs that people just have to sing in these circumstances, but of songs that it's not quite possible they might. The styles dont match, despite the authors shared social context; Barry was genteel, Porter was rude. It's even worse when the song is a standard; the present version (which was done without much success on Broadway in 1998) throws in Just One of Those Things and It's All Right with Me, and its as disorienting as hearing Macbeths "Tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow" in the middle of Othello. The obscurer songs are more fun, though I'm Throwing a Ball Tonight and You've Got That Thing are so dragged in you can practically hear their screams Their titles get heavily dropped in advance, allowing buffs extra time to squirm.

Occasionally, gold is struck: I Worship You, a beautiful song well sung (by David Leyshon) works miraculously well as an expression of Georges feelings about Tracy; Im not sure where a self-made, self-proclaimed philistine gets off with his references to the goddesses Isis and Astarte, but the imagery is apt for the subject if not the singer. In fact, given the hopelessness of the enterprise, Arthur Kopit's script is a credible job; he has shuffled the movie songs into a new and sensible order, and he gives a sizeable portion of the second act a coherent identity as a post- midnight pastoral after the fashion of A Little Night Music, with couples rearranging their destinies in the stately home's stately grounds. If the show has an unsung heroine, its Susan Birkenhead, whose "additional lyrics" to some of Porters tunes actually match the originals for wit and far surpass them for dramatic relevance. She has turned what used to be the High Society Calypso into a running commentary by the Lord family's servants on the antics of their employers. It's a good unifying idea.

Its debased at Niagara by Kelly Robinsons busy, coarse production. So is much else. Who Wants to be a Millionaire, one song from the film that might stand a theatrical chance, is performed by the scenery rather than by the two important characters (Patty Jamieson, who is adequate, and Jay Turvey, who is reptilian) to whom it is supposed to introduce us. About the only things to praise are William Schmuck's sets, imposing indoors and magical out, and the Dexter of Dan Chameroy, whose acting flows nearly as effortlessly as his singing. But all depends on Tracy. Camilla Scott is a decent character actress and she can sing a ballad in an anonymous kind of way. But her fizz is forced; and she has the emotional vulnerability and comic finesse of a sledgehammer.

High Society is a famous title. That must be why it's there. But if the Shaw Festival is committed to doing mainstage musicals (which seems to me a dangerous gambit), it cannot let famousness govern its choices. There isnt enough to go round.
