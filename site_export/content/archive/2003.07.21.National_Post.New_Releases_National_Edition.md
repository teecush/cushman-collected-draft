POP

HOLLY COLE

Shade

(Alert)

Holly Cole's latest is a disc whose time has -- finally -- come, being a collection of songs about hot weather, and how either to escape or to revel in it. Some selections are far-fetched, even by concept-album standards (We Kiss in a Shadow?); and God only knows what God Only Knows is doing in there, unless any song with Beach Boys connections automatically qualifies as summery. Cole sings it very well, but then she has usually been at her best when applying vinegary jazz inflections to rock-era songs. Here, though, she concentrates on show tunes and such, with mixed results. Songs urban (Manhattan) and rural (Cottage for Sale) are ruined through sheer rhythmic insensitivity, and the opening Heat Wave is gimmicky fun, but nothing more. Her vocal range is unashamedly limited; rather than navigating round her flat notes, she sails right into them. Sometimes, though, her vocal nakedness -- a cracked, husky, orphan- girl quality -- can be riveting, allowing her to redeem some potentially pretentious songs: Her Lazy Afternoon is a bewitching incantation, and her Something Cool must be the most moving and believable on record. She's supported on these by her two perennial sidemen, Aaron Davis (piano) and David Piltch (bass). Other tracks feature strings that sound synthesized, even though they apparently aren't. She should have included Two Ladies in de Shade of de Banana Tree, not to mention Summer in the City. Robert Cushman

ROCK

54-40

Goodbye Flatland

(Divine Industries)

After 23 years on the Canadian music scene, these Vancouverites still have a tendency to to fly a bit under the radar. Not that we haven't all heard their radio-friendly tunes a million times, but I've yet to find anyone who'd describe 54-40 as their favourite band or even among their top five. That said, there's something reassuring about a collective that can go more than two decades without dramatically changing its sound, and still sound reasonably up-to-date. Goodbye Flatland finds Neil Osborne and company in their finest form since 1998's Since When, if not before. Going back to their rock basics after mellowing in their approach to middle age, they charge out of the gate with five solid tracks punctuated by Animal in Pain, the album's terrifically ragged first single, and the more polished Hope the Hell I Haven't Died. Unfortunately, they cede much of their momentum with the uninspired Who Is Sylvia and two forgettable ballads that follow. But they end nearly as strong as they began, with the anthemic Seventeen On and the retro-ish Wish I Knew. "I have been afraid that I might settle for what it is I have become," Osborne tells us in Ride. No need to fret just yet. Adam Radwanski

JANE'S ADDICTION

Strays

(Virgin)

Reunions -- family, school and/or band -- are almost always fraught with peril. People have changed, memories have faded and Aunt Gladys has only become more belligerent. Best then, not to force things in hopes of reclaiming past glory -- if ever there was glory to begin with. Jane's Addiction have actually been re-uniting for years now with various gigs or one-off recordings here and there -- but this, their first proper album since 1990's Ritual de lo Habitual should have been just such another disaster, a painful exercise destined to tarnish the legendary reputation built more than a decade ago. But, though not as obviously exciting as their earlier work, this is a worthwhile effort that holds up well against the alt-rock spawn currently polluting the airwaves. Much of what you'd expect with spacey bits (The Riches) bluesy bits (Wrong Girl), heavy bits (True Nature) and quiet bits (Everybody's Friend). Altogether all right, if destined to be little more than an epilogue. Aaron Wherry
