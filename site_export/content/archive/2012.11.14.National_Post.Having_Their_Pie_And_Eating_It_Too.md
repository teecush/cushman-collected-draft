THEATRE REVIEW

ALLIGATOR PIE

YOUNG CENTRE FOR THE PERFORMING ARTS, TORONTO

Soulpepper has a new subgroup, known as the Creation Ensemble, that consists at present of Mike Ross (actor-musician), Ken MacKenzie (actor-designer), Ins Choi (actor-playwright), Raquel Duffy (actor-actor) and Gregory Prest (ditto). Their initial presentation is Alligator Pie, a freewheeling musical entertainment based on the Canadian children's verse of Dennis Lee.

It's an invigorating, sometimes enchanting show whose weakest element is its source material. The poems left me feeling dissatisfied, or maybe just disenfranchised. Having been written in the 1970s, they could hardly have been part of my own childhood (which wasn't Canadian anyway), but they weren't part of my children's childhoods, either. Encountered now, by someone who grew up on Lewis Carroll and tried to pass it on, they feel sub-Seuss: longer on whimsy than on wit, without the Seussian rhythmic insistence and, most of the time, without the narrative strength. That they mention Toronto landmarks in unexpected contexts doesn't quite compensate. Besides, setting existing verse to music is a dodgy enterprise at most times; the texts here are easier to follow than the e.e. cummings poems that were the basis of an earlier Soulpepper show, but even these comparatively simple words tend to be swamped rather than enhanced by the accompaniment.

No director or choreographer is listed; the show was staged, very inventively, by its performers. It's done in the round, with the space envisioned as an adventure playground for grown-ups; or for the child in every grown-up. The famous five begin proceedings by clambering up through a trap door, initially one at a time, though the pattern gets varied and syncopated. Their wear looks of mischief and wonder, and they keep wearing them throughout. They also wear elaborately strange coiffures (Ross and Duffy especially) and these do change in the course of the proceedings.

The staging makes much use of found objects, or of objects left purposefully lying about, never more effectively or infectiously than in a concluding dance on bubble-wrap, proving that squeaks can be as joyful as taps. (Further expanses of the same material are placed at the exits so that the departing audience can feel the joy.)

Homemade and original, too, are many of the musical instruments, many of them played by Ross, who presumably wrote most of the music. There's more orthodox instrumentation, too, some of it played by unlikely people in unlikely circumstances and prompting unlikely thoughts. For some reason I found myself convinced, while watching the lanky Prest blowing trombone, that here was a future Hamlet. And not so far in the future, either. This is momentous. One of Soulpepper's problems over the years has been finding -- and holding on to -- young male actors likely to inherit from their formidable list of founding members. Prest, who has succeeded in one demanding role after another in the last two years, demonstrating equal intensity as tragedian and clown, seems to be the man.

Ross is wickedly delightful as a superannuated wizard in the evening's most sustained sequence of storytelling. Duffy does some impressive instant re-stylings of hair and personality, and Choi looks wonderfully self-satisfied as a phenomenal infant. Music sounds predominantly country, theoretically incongruous for an urban poet, but in fact working nicely and varied in itself, taking in everything from ballad to square dance. But there are also excursions into Latin and rap, as well as an extended interlude of boyd percussion in which four-fifths of the cast turn themselves into so many Bobby McFerrins. The mention of "Mulligan stew" prompts a tone of sweet solemnity that is pretty much the show's signature. The title poem bookends the proceedings. It's hard to resist the quintet's rhythmic choral declaration that if they don't get their pie they're surely gonna die, and pleasant to welcome it back at the end. I wish the filling of the pie were more nourishing. But the flavour's appealing.
