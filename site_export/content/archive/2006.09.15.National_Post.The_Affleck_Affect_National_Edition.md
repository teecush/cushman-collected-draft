Quentin Tarantino has said that the sign of a good film is that it makes you want to go home, eat some pie and talk about it. With that in mind, our Popcorn Panel features film buffs feuding in this space each week.

THIS WEEK'S PANEL:

- Craig Courtice, a short filmmaker who isnt very tall

- Marni Weisz, editor of Famous magazine

- Robert Cushman, the Posts theatre critic

THIS WEEK'S PIE: Leonore Lemmon

THIS WEEK'S SUBJECT: Hollywoodland

CRAIG: Back in the heady days of Matt and Ben, my sister and I debated which of the Good Will Hunting auteurs was better. She based most of her case on Afflecks charm and good looks; I thought Damon showed himself to be more devoted to his craft. Nine years later its pretty clear who won the family argument. Matt makes consistently excellent movies, such as Syriana and The Talented Mr. Ripley, while Ben bounces between, well, Bounce, and Surviving Christmas. Matt's superhero, Jason Bourne, kicked the crap out of Ben's Daredevil. Even Matts stinker, Stuck on You, smelled better than Bens (Gigli is now synonymous in pop culture with a box- office bomb). My sister long ago switched her boy-toy allegiances to Matthew McConaughey.

But now comes Hollywoodland, a picture about a pretty-boy actor who yearns to be taken seriously. For his role as George Reeves, TV's first Superman, Affleck won the best acting prize at the Venice Film Festival. But is this a case of right man, right tights or can we start treating Ben as a Matt?

MARNI: I want to like Ben, I really, really do, which is funny because a lot of people intensely dislike him for some reason. But I didn't like him in this film. He was wooden and marble-mouthed, and it seemed like hed taken his 1950s intonation from watching a high school production of Guys and Dolls. I was shocked to hear that hed won best actor at Venice. Then it occurred to me that Affleck may be a better actor if you don't speak English as your first language.

I wondered whether Affleck was purposely turning in a bad performance because he was playing a bad actor -- the tragedy of George Reeves, particularly in this film, was that he aspired to be better than his talent allowed. But I couldn't figure out how going meta in that way would add to the movie, so I dont think that was the case.

ROBERT: Ive never been able to share in the general contempt for Affleck. Maybe it's because the first movie I saw him in was Shakespeare in Love, in which I thought he stood up well to some very heavy acting competition. And I think he's more than fine in Hollywoodland. (And I think my English is pretty good). Hes absolutely convincing as a somewhat talented actor -- and that is not meant to be any kind of back-handed compliment -- who has achieved a moderate success of a kind he doesnt value and is torn between gratitude and frustration.

And it's not as if Affleck is all there is to the movie. There's a wonderful performance by Diane Lane as the older woman who keeps him in a kind of Pal Joey relationship, and is scared of losing both him and her youth. When Bob Hoskins, as her jug-eared, movie exec- husband Theres also Adrien Brody as the private eye investigating Georges suicide/possible murder. Brody's as watchable a movie actor as there is, but though I can see what the parallels are meant to be, I'm far from sure the stories marry up.

CRAIG: Perhaps Lane, Hoskins and Brody are all exemplary. Perhaps, perhaps, perhaps. But the whole of Hollywoodland felt terribly derivative to me. The score, the period pieces, even the style of acting reminded me of L.A. Confidential. There was even a wink to the hush-hush methods studios used to keep gay actors in the closet.

Thats not to say Hollywoodland isnt watchable, just that Confidential, with Kim Basinger, James Cromwell and Russell Crowe, is much better. Perhaps it has something to do with the James Ellroy novel it was based on, something to keep in mind should you be forced to choose between Hollywoodland and this week's The Black Dahlia, which is also based on Ellroy material.

MARNI: There were several great scenes, such as the surreal, very civil lunch between Lane's and Hoskins characters and their respective extra-marital partners. But, overall, the first 45 minutes seemed really awkward -- like they were trying too hard to place the action in the 1950s. With films like Capote or L.A. Confidential you instantly feel like youre back in the '50s; with this film they hit you over the head with period cars and little boys with huge ears wearing striped shirts. I get it!

But then the script picked up the momentum that drove it toward an ending so good it shifted this movie from disappointing to not- so-bad. I loved the way the present and the past were seamlessly intercut, with no special effects to indicate a time change. It was simple, clean and unsettling at the same time.

ROBERT: I also liked the intercutting of the two stories and the two periods -- you were always being caught slightly off-guard. I had no objection to the '50s evocation; in fact I thought it was first-rate. And I liked what you might call the sub-plot within the Brody sub-plot; the creepy guy who hires him to prove his wifes (totally imaginary) adultery. Only I didnt think it quite fulfilled its function. It seems to be meant as a moral wake-up call for Brody. The trouble is, I wasn't convinced he needed one.

I found by the end that I didn't care whether he found out the truth or not, though I did care what the truth was. History dictates that we dont find out, but I found that frustrating rather than satisfyingly enigmatic. So I disagree with Marni. I thought it began better than it ended. But theres an awful lot of good stuff in there. I think it deserves better treatment than it's been getting.
