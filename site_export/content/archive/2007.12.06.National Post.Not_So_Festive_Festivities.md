Tonight is the 300th episode of ER. And the fictional characters seem to be in on this extra-textual fact, because they're celebrating by having a betting-pool on admissions: 142 -- if we keep them cranking, we could hit 300, says one of the team in the expository tones so typical of medical staff at the beginning of a show.

Those betting it will happen get a windfall in the shape of a whole convention of food-poisoning victims. Even this doesnt quite bring the numbers up to the required level. Will there be a last- minute casualty that will push them over the top?

Well, I wouldnt want to spoil it for you.

The official reason for all this gambling in the ranks -- not to mention the gastric disorders -- is that it is, in the premature way they do these things in TV land, Christmas. So the episode is doubly celebratory.

All departments are doing their seasonal bit and "surgery drew the nativity scene." This means that, due to a complicated chain of events, we get to see somebody trying to take a swab from a camel.

The festivities also account for the guest appearance of Reiko Aylesworth, a pleasure to see since she's unlikely to be coming back to her old role on 24 any time soon. (She was Michelle, who is fairly definitively dead.) She plays the hospital chaplain, and her presence gives rise in the script to such unaccustomed words as proselytizer and "acolyte," as well as more colloquial phrases like are you banging the chaplain? Both levels of discourse are understandable; who wouldn't want to be Ayleseworths acolyte?

Shes also there to serve a higher purpose by offering counsel to troubled regulars, and presiding over a final scene in which she exhorts the personnel to remember the patients who have touched their lives (cue a succession of split-second flashbacks) and to feel justifiably proud of themselves.

The other visiting star is Peter Fonda. He plays a father reunited with his Down's syndrome son on the very day that his terminally-ill wife is

to have her plug pulled. Fonda gives this all he's got (this includes a vocal delivery full of calculated hesitations, but how else do you act this kind of thing?) and the situation is as undeniably affecting as its manipulative.

It also reminded me of why, though I can always get sucked into shows like ER while actually watching, I can never get hooked. Medical soaps dont have any of the elements that might keep me tuning in: They aren't funny, they don't have suspense from one episode to the next and their characters are uniformly superficial.

And they are numbingly formulaic: Every week theres a life to be saved, of someone we hardly know. (The same formula of Six Feet Under, only in reverse.)

The most believable medical series ever was The Sopranos, which had a lot of scenes set in hospitals. But they were seen, always, from the point of view of the patients, which made them a lot easier (and also more painful) for most of us to identify with. Maybe medical professionals feel the same bond with ER and Greys Anatomy but I wouldn't bet on it. Not even at Christmas.

Also involving a nativity play -- in fact starting with one -- is The Pagan Christ on CBC. A voice-over tells us that one third of the world's population subscribes to basic Christian beliefs, including - - in fact dominated by -- that Jesus was a real person.

The film charts the growing disbelief in this of the

Canadian religious journalist Tom Harpur, who nonetheless still considers himself a Christian, an allegorical one. For him "the Jesus figure stands for [everybodys] higher self." The film is based on his recent book of the same name.

What sparked his historical doubts were strikingly detailed resemblances between the Christian legend and the myths surrounding numerous Egyptian gods: the birth, the miracles, the resurrection. (He also identifies the Egyptian goddess Isis as the black Madonna.)

Whether this makes Christianity a plagiarism or just another example of the confluence of myth is left unexplored, but Harpur has plenty of other ammunition. He suggests that Christianity in its official form was imposed by Constantine, the first Christian Emperor of Rome who, to hold the Empire together, insisted that the new state religion be accepted, lock, stock and fairy tales. As in Communist Russia, to disbelieve meant banishment and death.

This is very plausible, and the film strengthens its hand -- in what may be an unfairly emotive way -- by juxtaposing scenes from "The Holy Land Experience," a Christian theme-park in Orlando, Fla. (yes, next door to Disneyworld), guaranteed to turn off any rational person.

It also gives pundit time to genial Christian traditionalists of a less fundamentalist aspect; but its hard not to concur with a pair of English scholarly sceptics. According to them, the orthodox argument amounts to "those other stories are just stories, ours really happened," which is not intellectually respectable.

The Harpur faction are also emphatic that there are no historical references to Jesus till 140 years after his death; the Gospels are self-justifying oracles (as well as contradicting one another) and other texts are fragmentary and ambiguous.

It all comes as a cold shower, even to non-Christians like myself who have always taken for granted that Jesus was a real and admirable -- and revolutionary -- man. It also raises questions: If he didn't exist, then what did the early Christians believe, and why? They were persecuted, before they started in persecuting others. It's a fascinating program nonetheless.
