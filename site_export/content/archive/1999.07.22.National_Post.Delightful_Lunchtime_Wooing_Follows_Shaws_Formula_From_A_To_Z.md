VILLAGE WOOING

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

The Shaw Festival traditionally tops up its season with a couple of lunchtime productions, one of them drawn from their patrons own considerable corpus of one-act plays. This years Shavian choice is as good as it gets.

Village Wooing is a comparatively late (1933) Shaw play that recaps, in delightful miniature form, the preoccupations of his best- known full-length comedies. As in Man and Superman and in Pygmalion (at least in the romantic, happy-ending Pygmalion that everyone except Shaw himself is convinced he wrote) a man who considers himself invulnerably self-sufficient finds himself falling into marriage with an irresistibly vital woman. Presumably convinced by now of the universality of his theme, Shaw, this time round, simply called the man A and the woman Z.

A is a widower, a self-proclaimed poet who makes his living churning out travel books with a catchy brand-name; he is, as he confesses with a grimace, "the Marco Polo man." Z is unmarried and works in a village store and post office; she has won some money in a competition and is spending it on a world cruise. Which is how they find themselves sitting side by side on ship (economy class to judge from the deck chairs, which look like instruments of torture) while crossing the Red Sea which, to Z's indignant disappointment, is blue. Her perky, non-stop conversation is a torment to A, who is trying to compose his daily 2,000 words; nevertheless, he cannot stop himself replying.

A second scene finds Z back home minding the store, and A coincidentally dropping by for supplies in the midst of a hiking trip. He has no memory of their previous encounter, but she recalls every detail. By the end of their conversation, she has talked him into buying the business from its current owner and keeping her on as an employee. The third and last scene sees their relationship converted, to nobody's surprise (least of all A's, though he wriggles a bit) from strictly commercial to commercial plus personal; it ends with Z, whose responsibilities include operating the local switchboard, placing a call on her own account: to the church.

The main demands made by the dialogue, as it progresses from A to Z and back again, are for fluency, pace, lucidity and ebullience, with all of which both the players in Nikki Lundmark's production are loaded. Neil Barclay may be a trifle too pettish, but he admirably conveys the writer as philosopher, revelling in the acquisition of knowledge -- knowledge in all spheres and hemispheres, from the glory of Greece to the going price of tea. Camille James, just as inquisitive but less contemplative, gives us a woman possessed by a demon of practicality; once she has set her sights she can be hurt, but not deflected. She is also able to switch delightfully from an English West Country accent (not quite accurate for Wiltshire, where the domestic scenes are set, but never mind) to the bright, clipped BBC tones of a professional telephonist; this is partly a play about love across class barriers, and she is already living out that conflict every day of her working life. (She does not, however, seem oppressed by it; she seems to enjoy ringing the changes.) Both performers, she especially, have the essential trick of playing Shaw: that of hitting humorous climaxes that are also emotional ones. He may be wise, but she is clever; so they need one another.

There is actually a third, silent character: a deck steward (Duncan Stewart) who serves soup by the sea and sticks around to set up the furniture on land, doing both jobs impeccably. The store that he helps usher into existence -- designed by Barbara Gordon -- is a nostalgic delight in itself.

Shaw, socialist though he was, had a passion for commerce; in both writing and presentation, this rural establishment is a visual ode to the days, which may even have existed, when service was personal and unhurried, customers dropped by on bikes and food stood neatly on shelves, actually looking like food, smelling like food, and quite possibly tasting like food when you got it home.
