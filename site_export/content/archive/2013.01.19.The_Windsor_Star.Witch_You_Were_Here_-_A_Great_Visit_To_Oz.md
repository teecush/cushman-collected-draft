BOX OFFICE

The Wizard of Oz, starring LaSalles Danielle Wade, is playing at the Ed Mirvish Theatre in Toronto. For tickets and more information, visit mirvish.com.

When I first heard that Andrew Lloyd Webber and Tim Rice were adding songs of their own for their stage adaptation of The Wizard of Oz, my initial reaction was, Isn't that illegal?"

Probably it should be, but in fact, apart from a near-to-closing inspirational anthem that's more like the worst of Disney than the best of MGM, the new numbers they have tagged on to the movie originals arent bad. Rices lyrics, indeed, may be the best he's ever written, with an accuracy and ingenuity in wordplay that are astonishing from the man who once tried to get away with rhyming "biscuit and district." He even manages to write some additional lines for Over the Rainbow and If I Only Had a Brain without embarrassing himself. The spirit of Yip Har-burg must have rubbed off on him.

E.Y. Harburg, to give him his official billing, wrote the 1939 movie lyrics to the music of the great Harold Arlen. He was also one of the platoon of writers who worked on the screenplay; and to hear him tell it (and he told it a lot) he was the one responsible for giving the picture its tone: a mixture of heartfelt whimsy and social consciousness. The latter may not be too obvious now, but The Wizard of Oz is a story that starts in the Kansas dust bowl, filmed in the wake of the Depression and on the brink of war. Harburg saw it as his tribute to the New Deal, with FDR as the man who had restored brains, heart and courage to a nation of scarecrows, tin men and cowardly lions: a country that no longer knew its own strength.

It's this conviction, plus Judy Garland at her nervously pluckiest, that gives the film its urgency. Without that, its just a story, a spectacle. Which is what it becomes in the new stage version that originated in London and is now in Toronto, with a Canadian cast and its sights set on New York.

The movie Wizard, of course, was itself an adaptation; and the original L. Frank Baum book has been the subject of stage musicals, quite independent of the film, both long before it and long after (The Wiz was a pretty good show). But its the movie that's stuck, and Jeremy Sams' production (hes also the co-adaptor, with Lloyd Webber) is, like all such enterprises, torn between reproducing it and re-imagining it.

It starts out rather well in Kansas, with Dorothys Uncle Henry and Auntie Em given a more detailed relationship to their orphaned charge than we're used to. (They're well played by Charlotte Moore and Larry Mannell, the latter doubling as the gatekeeper of Emerald City, British-accented like in the film.) But then we get to the twister, the hurricane that blows Dorothy to Oz; apparently deciding it cant be staged, the production team try to evoke it with a projected montage of flying images. In other words, they film it, which of course has already been done. The only time here when the new technology justifies itself is at the end, when Dorothy seems to be finding her way back to Kansas with the help of Google Earth.

Meanwhile, we have had set and costume designs (by Robert Jones) more garish than stylish. The sharp-suited inhabitants of Emerald City suggest Guys and Dolls gone green, but the effect is done to death in Arlene Phillips choreography, which is lumpen almost throughout. The low point is reached by the snarling slinking of the Wicked Witch's underlings, which might just about do for a musical of Star Wars, but here only spoil her song, a covetous confection about the ruby slippers that finds Rice matching "clueless with shoeless" and "footwear with put where." No, it isn't Harburg, but its feet are in the right place.

But theres another thing. In the movie, the witch doesnt sing. In fact, the last third of the movie practically stops being a musical; it has too much story. The stage show, obliged to keep shovelling in songs, slows everything down.

Hollywood was so self-denying in this regard that it cut out a first-rate Arlen-Harburg rhythm number, The Jitterbug. I was rather surprised that the show hasn't reinstated it; I'd gladly sacrifice the Lullaby League and the Lollipop Guild in exchange. (To be honest, I could do without the Munch-kins altogether. Theyre full-grown in this production, either for PC reasons or so they can be doubled with everyone else.)

Im philosophically opposed to the idea of casting shows by TV talent contests, but it works out as well here in practice as it did in Sams' (far superior) production of The Sound of Music. As Dorothy, Danielle Wade - plucked from the CBC's Over the Rainbow talent show - is a troubled teen rising above her troubles, and does much to keep the show on the Yellow Brick Road (one of the better design elements, incidentally). It isnt her fault the song Over the Rainbow is now so crusted in mythology that its impossible to accept as part of a story (and the rainbow itself looks very cut-rate).

Jamie McKnight is a charming Scarecrow; his solo dance is the most delightful thing in the show. Mike Jackson's Tin Man is all right but no more, while Lee MacDougall's Lion is much less. Harburgs inspiration of fashioning the role for Bert Lahr has made the role unplayable by anyone else, but turning him into a pussycat whos seen too many musicals (and proudly pronounces himself "a friend of Dorothy) is a terrible solution. I did like the annoyed reaction of the other two when Dorothy announced she'd miss the Scarecrow most (I'd often wondered how they must feel) and I loved Lisa Horners Wicked Witch greeting Robin Evan Willis Good Witch with a hello, Glinda," like Jerry Seinfeld acknowledging the existence of nemesis Newman.

Horner seems to be having a good time, and shares it with the audience.

Willis, meanwhile, is a Good Witch who's post-Wicked, and far more entertaining in her fairy godmother complacency than the sugary trilling of her movie prototype.

Cedric Smith is a pleasing Professor Wonder and a booming eye-flashing Wizard, whose exposure is weakened by being confusingly staged.
