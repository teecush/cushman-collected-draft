musicals much

Contrary to rumours of their demise, megamusicals are here to stay ... though one is tempted to make a few suggestions

So what, other than an exceptionally ugly word, is a megamusical?

Garth Drabinsky claims that there is no such animal; and, since his Live Entertainment Inc. have brought The Phantom of the Opera and Sunset Boulevard to Canada, he should know. According to Drabinsky, "Hal Prince will go to his grave" denying that there is anything especially grandiose about Phantom. As Prince directed the show, which most people would regard as the very model of a modern megamusical, the whole existence of the phenomenon seems in doubt.

But it does exist, if only in the popular consciousness. Journalists slap the megamusical label on any show that has a pronounced technical gleam and that obviously cost a ton of money to mount: on the Livent revival of Show Boat, a classic American musical in the book - music lyrics tradition; on Crazy for You, a free adaptation of another classic American musical, the Gershwins' Girl Crazy; and on Kiss of the Spider Woman, which in my judgment is a new classic American musical. But a purist definition of the megamusical would exclude all these. The megamusical is the Euromusical.

That means Cats, Starlight Express, Les Miserables, Chess, Phantom, Miss Saigon, Aspects of Love, Sunset Boulevard: i.e. any musical involving composer Andrew Lloyd Webber, director Trevor Nunn, producer Cameron Mackintosh, or some combination thereof. Let us call these works the hard - core canon. Among them they have set new standards for commercial success and international marketing -- standards that have inevitably affected the way in which the more traditional shows (Crazy for You, in particular) have been staged, sold, and received. They have also inspired emulation from other sources: notably The Who's Tommy and Beauty and the Beast.

Large - scale musicals have come to dominate the commercial theatre through - out the world. In Canada they are the commercial theatre, which was reinvented when the Toronto production of Cats proved that a show could settle down in one place for a considerable time: a lesson reinforced by the phenomenal run of Phantom. The failure of subsequent shows to duplicate this longevity has been taken to mean that the public has tired of the megamusical. But it takes a journalist or a pollster to believethat an audience buys or rejects a genre. People go to specific shows of which they like the sound. Hype can help, hugely, but it cannot create.

The hard - core canon is the work of a very small group of people, whose productivity was bound to slow down. But megamusicals have taken up a lot of space, and the phenomenon isn't going away. It's worth examining the salient features.

1. Provenance. As many an American practitioner has bitterly noted, the megamusical is a British invention. Webber, Nunn, and Mackintosh are all British, and it was Britain's Royal Shakespeare Company that developed Les Miserables, after its French creators, Alain Boublil and Claude - Michel Schonberg, had done it oratorio - fashion in Paris. Boublil and Schonberg's subsequent work, Miss Saigon, was launched in London -- as will be their next, Martin Guerre, the only pedigreed megamusical on the immediate horizon.

This Anglocentricity is hardly reflected in the shows' style or subject matter. America is the ancestral home of the modern musical, and even these authors who have supposedly supplanted the American product like to strut in its feathers: to prove themselves American by association, with Miss Saigon and Sunset Boulevard. Though the musical idiom of the megashows isn't American, nor is it identifiably British or French. It's Europop: the toothless international style that turned middle - of - the - road into middle - of - the - ocean. It was typified by the Swedish super - group Abba, two of whose members duly composed their own megashow, the supremely rootless Chess. The one megaballad to have achieved the eloquence for which they all so laboriously strive is Lloyd Webber's "All I Ask of You," from Phantom.

2. Dialogue, Lack of. The oldest plays we have are musicals. Ancient Greek tragedy and ancient Greek comedy told their stories through a mixture of speech, song, and dance. It's the purely sung or purely spoken drama that's an aberration.

Pop opera, the form to which the megamusicals aspire, wants you to hear the words but makes you wish that you couldn't. One achievement of the Boublil - Schonberg duo has been to reduce Herbert Kretzmer, a competent lyricist, and Richard Maltby, Jr., a brilliant lyricist, to writing doggerel (for Les Mis and Miss Saigon respectively). They use song to dispense information that could be conveyed with far more ease and elegance in dialogue. In Sunset Boulevard the supposedly sharp Hollywooders, chanting exposition at one another over endlessly repeated vamps, sound like earnest half - wits. Don Black, another experienced lyricist, and Christopher Hampton, an intelligent playwright, are the authors; possibly they blush on their way to the bank.

Audiences today are too sophisticated, the theory goes, to accept transitions from speech to song and back again. It's a weird idea of sophistication, and there's absolutely no evidence for it. The through - sung technique only works in Webber's early Joseph and the Amazing Technicolor Dreamcoat, a short and breezy show whose narrative links are funny. Which brings us to

4. Humour, Also Lack of. Stephen Sondheim has described his latest musical, Passion (not mega, by any definition), as "the world's first humourless musical." He was being wry, possibly defensive, and probably disingenuous. He may have meant the first intentionally humourless musical. Most shows try at some point to be funny. Many fail.

There was wit in all the shows Webber wrote with Tim Rice, in Jesus Christ Superstar and Evita as well as Joseph. Rice's later work, though, has been as pompous as any in the genre. (There's a yawning gap between the flatulent additional lyrics he contributed to the film Aladdin and the stage version of Beauty and the Beast, and the sprightly words of his predecessor, the late Howard Ashman. It affects the music too.) There is one entertaining song in Les Mis and one that tries hard in Miss Saigon. Nothing resembling a good joke is allowed to profane the solemn portals of Phantom, Aspects, or Sunset. It weighs especially hard on the last, whose source, otherwise faithfully followed, was in large part a black comedy. Crazy as it sounds, there is probably a commercial logic to this. A lot of people suspect humour, especially the irreverent humour that was the hallmark of the Broadway musical in its prime. Consider this early (1917) lyric by P.G. Wodehouse, working with Jerome Kern:

Napoleon was a little guy, they used to call him Shorty, He only stood about so high, and his chest was under forty... But they couldn't faze Napoleon, when he started in to slap; He was five foot high, but one tough guy, and I take after Nap.

There are similarly profane songs about Josephine, by Noel Coward and Cole Porter. Napoleon was also a comic character when he turned up in the title role of Canada's home - brewed megawannabeof a couple of years back: an idealistic loser whose fatal mistake at Waterloo was to dream about his discarded wife on the eve of battle. (Not tonight, Josephine.) Unfortunately, the authors didn't know they were being funny. A sense of humour is vital in musicals as in most other things. It teaches you how and when to be serious.

4. Performance. A peculiarity of the megashows is that nobody knows or cares who's in them. Michael Crawford's truly haunting performance of the Phantom, in London and New York, was a bonus but not a necessity. The only exceptions are Evita and Sunset Boulevard, two shows about stars, aspiring or actual, that require to be cast accordingly and have kept columnists happy for years. Or, in the former case, decades. That the movie role of Evita should finally have been won by Madonna, not so much a star as a parody of stardom, is true poetic justice: probably good casting as well. On film, Sunset Boulevard was an acrid examination of the star phenomenon; on stage it's a traditional showbiz hymn. Whoever the leading lady may be, the audience is required to worship her. It's why she spends so much time coming down staircases.

5. Sources. Most megamusicals are based on something else. That is not a crime. So are most old - style musicals. So are most operas and movies. So, at one time, were most plays. (Look at Shakespeare.) All the successful shows tell time - proven stories, except for Cats, which makes a point of not having one. If the story is bad (Tommy), confusing (Aspects), or just unmanageable (Napoleon), there is trouble: artistic and even commercial. And if the natural progression, from page to stage to film to television, is violated the results are almost invariably grisly. Turning a film musical into a stage musical is self - evidently pointless, even if the show's a hit.

The terrifying case of Beauty and the Beast finds people who may go to the theatre only once a year, putting down money, lots of it, for an inferior replica of something they have already seen -- and can continue to see -- on a screen. On the stage, Beauty is the spin - off of spin - offs, a long, slothful reminder of how good the movie was. Not one Toronto critic commented on this production's amateurishness, its merry villagers grouped around its trilling heroine in immobile clumps like extras in a provincial English pantomime. (And if you say that a live show cannot hope to rival the invention and agility of acartoon, then that's a very good reason not to try.) The Toronto version has one thing going for it: a remarkably sensitive and flexible performance by Chuck Wagner as the male lead. Of course he loses appeal when he sheds his fur and whiskers at the end, but that's the nature of the Beast.

6 (and inevitably). Spectacle. I suggest a moratorium on dismissive sentences about chandeliers and helicopters. It's a lazy journalistic shorthand, supposed to signify mindless extravagance and in no way supported by the facts. I loathed Miss Saigon, but the helicopter wasn't what I loathed it for; it was a graphic element in the fall of the city, itself a vital part of the story. The descending chandelier in Phantom is an amusing and momentarily scary trompe l'oeil that contributes to narrative and atmosphere.

People confuse spectacle with mere lavishness. Spectacle (check out Aristotle) is an aesthetic quality; it's the way a stage looks, and the better it looks the better. Phantom, with its candlelit voyage to the protagonist's lair and its explosion of colour in the "Masquerade" number, is spectacular; so was the brilliant alternation of spare and cluttered staging in Nicholas Hytner's production of Miss Saigon; so, despite lousy material, was Des McAnuff's high - tech vision of Tommy. Sunset Boulevard is lavish; Trevor Nunn is the best director of plays -- certainly of classic plays -- in the English theatre, but his work on this show is stodgy, however much the set goes up and down. Beauty and the Beast is the dumping on the stage of expensive materials by people who have no idea what to do with them.

The hard - core megashows have their merits, and if they entertain people I'm glad, but they are a limited form -- more limited than the musical theatre need be, and historically has been. (You want to know what I love most about musicals? Smart lyrics. I've yet to hear one in a megamusical.) So, if Mirvish Productions have left the field, closing Tommy and renting their new theatre to Disney, I don't repine; to have first - class plays reinstated at Toronto's Royal Alex is a blessing. Livent remain committed to musicals and to developing their own. On their stocks are a version of E.L. Doctorow's Ragtime, with a score by the richly promising Broadway team of Stephen Flaherty and Lynn Ahrens; The Sweet Smell of Success, with music by veteran Cy Coleman; new productions of Candide and Pal Joey: all, intriguingly and encouragingly, in the American rather than the European mould.

There was a delightful moment in Crazy for You, when one tradition met the other, and tweaked it: a moment of what hip literary critics call intertextuality. The show's Ziegfeld figure surveys a stage piled high with furniture and declares that it looks like the French Revolution. He alludes of course to the barricade scene in Les Miserables. The joke is even better than the authors may realize, since Victor Hugo's novel deals with the Revolution of July, which took place some forty years after the 1789 affair. The audiences for Les Mis think they're getting a history lesson, and Les Mis wouldn't dream of disabusing them. Musicals will continue to dominate the commercial theatre in Canada. Let there be some intelligent ones.
