popcorn@nationalpost.com

Quentin Tarantino has said the sign of a good film is that it makes you want to go home, eat some pie and talk about it. With that in mind, our Popcorn Panel features film buffs feuding in this space each week.

- - -

This weeks panel:

- J. Kelly Nestruck, Arts & Life reporter

- Robert Cushman, the National Post's theatre critic

- Tara Ariano, the editor of the Web sites Fametracker.com and TelevisionWithoutPity.com

This week's pi: 3.1415 ...

This weeks subject: Proof

- - -

Kelly: Proof, adapted from David Auburns Pulitzer Prize-winning play of the same name, features Anthony Hopkins as a once-great mathematician gone mad and Gwyneth Paltrow as his daughter, who worries she may have inherited his neurological tendencies along with his numerical talent. Though well within the parameters of the crazy-genius genre, Proof moves to the head of that class for depicting mental illness as the enemy of creative thinking rather than its quirky ally.

And yet, while the film avoids some (psycho)tropes, other cliches -- like the yuppie sister with jojoba for brains -- abound. Is all that talk about imaginary numbers and Sophie Germain just a disguise for an inert drama about a quarter-life crisis? Does Proof follow old formulae or is it a unique prime of a film?

Robert: Well, the play's original author is on record as saying the play could just as well be about any kind of genius, so I guess the math is merely top-dressing. I wasn't crazy about the play -- it seemed the kind of safe, semi-serious thing that gets Pulitzers and these days represents Broadways nearest concession to original drama. For a while, I thought Id like the film more as a glossy TV movie-of-the-week, and with about as much visual oomph. But that wore off and irritation set in.

The thing is: The actress I saw play the lead in Toronto was believable, because she was young and unpolished. I thought Paltrow was very good, but she was wrong. She's a star, undisguisably; her face and her spirit catch the light. They can make her up and dress her down as much as they like -- she still looks more beautiful than her supposedly elegant sister, who wasn't just a nag but a frump. And of course they pared away what edges the play had. I came to the end thinking: What was that?

Tara: I think I must be getting old (or am past my quarter-life crisis, at least), because I rebelled against the films manipulations and stood firm in my annoyance with Paltrows Catherine, identifying instead with Hope Davis's level-headed Claire. I get that we're supposed to feel so sympathetic toward Catherine, the delicate genius-cum-self-sacrificing saint, but the truth is that its extremely tiring to deal with a depressed person who wont or can't help herself. Reviews I've read have made Claire out to be this bourgeois nightmare, oppressing Catherine with her inadequate care and stupid suggestions, but to me she seemed, if insensitive, at least well-meaning.

Having experienced her fathers spiral into mental illness (that drove away or killed the wife and mother no one mentions even once - - what did happen to her?), its natural for Claire to try to "square away" Catherine however she can, and with the limited resources at her disposal. To say that depressed people are self- involved due to their illness gives us a reason for Catherine's resistance to her sister's ministrations, but its a shoddy excuse for her acting like a sullen teenager. Since the movie starts a week after the death of Catherines beloved father, that might be unfair - - but it's no less fair than the movie's treatment of its one functional, employed non-genius. Ive got your back, Claire! And I also appreciate a nice conditioner!

Kelly: Well, Catherine was just treating others the way her parents taught her. And by parents, I mean screenwriters Auburn and Rebecca Miller (daughter of playwright Arthur, by the by). They treat Claire with disdain because shes not like them and pretend as though the mother never existed because she's inconvenient to the plot. They make math geek Jake Gyllenhaal run after -- and briefly overtake! -- a car so that they can pretend Proof is worthy of being filmed. And they make Catherine even more intransigent than she is in the play so that they can include a scene of her dashing through an airport.

While I don't think I can prove that Proof was a bad movie, I do think it is possible to show conclusively that it was not a very good one. QED, yo.

Robert: Well, they also threw in a party scene and a hitting-the- sack scene, both of which are only narrated in the play. But I cant say I felt a wiser person for having seen them, or for having met a peculiarly stiff representative of the math faculty. Its a desperate play-into-movie technique. Meanwhile, Claire's character and back story are pared down quite ruthlessly. I didn't share Taras sympathy for her -- there just wasnt enough to her to arouse any feelings -- but maybe I should have because the cards are so stacked against her. I mean, how dare she be nasty to Paltrow?

Meanwhile, Gwyneth and her boyfriend get the few mildly funny lines, which will always predispose me in someone's favour. The flashbacks drove me wild --just as I'm getting vaguely interested in the story, Im whisked back to Anthony Hopkins being Celtic and mystic (with a line written in, I noticed, to explain his origins). I cant see what was added to his first appearance by his second, third, fourth or whichever.

Tara: Yeah, the father character just goes back to my main point, which is how dicey a proposition it is to build a production of any kind around a character who's mentally ill: He couldn't add much to the proceedings because of all his crazy. Add him to Catherine and you end up with a film half-anchored by characters whove lost their moorings (Ill forgive your math puns if you'll forgive my boating metaphors), which, naturally doesn't work.

I never saw the play, so I cant compare it, but the film felt plenty stagey on its own -- the settings were claustrophobic, the dialogue was stilted and the staging was generally just static. The running and the party and the sex didnt make it feel any more as though we were in a three- dimensional universe. And the point of the enterprise was to remind us that it's important to believe the people you love, even if they don't shower regularly or probably know how to balance a chequebook? Talk about a story problem with extraneous information.
