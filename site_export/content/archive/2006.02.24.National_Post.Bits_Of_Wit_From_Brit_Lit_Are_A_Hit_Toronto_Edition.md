popcorn@nationalpost.com

Quentin Tarantino has said the sign of a good film is that it makes you want to go home, eat some pie and talk about it. With that in mind, our Popcorn Panel features film buffs feuding in this space each week.

THIS WEEKS PANEL:

- Robert Cushman, the National Post's theatre critic

- Tara Ariano, the editor of the Web sites Fametracker.com and TelevisionWithoutPity.com

- Craig Courtice, a short filmmaker who isn't very tall

THIS WEEKS PIE: Mincemeat.

THIS WEEKS SUBJECT: Tristram Shandy: A Cock & Bull Story.

ROBERT: Every review I've read of Shandy has referred to the novel as unfilmable. It doesn't seem to me any more unfilmable than any other novel, and in the first half of the picture Michael Winterbottom and co. have made a good straightforward job of it. Of course "straightforward" here doesnt mean what it would to Merchant and Ivory shooting E.M. Forster. Sternes novel has a narrating protagonist who keeps detaching himself from the narrative and wondering about the feasibility, or even the sanity, of writing a book at all. The movie has a narrating protagonist who keeps doing the same thing and in doing so he has to identify himself as Steve Coogan, the actor. It's an exact filmic translation of the book's style. Very funny, too.

Then in the second half it becomes something else -- a movie about the problems of shooting the movie; not an adaptation of the book but a parallel to it. Everybody quotes Coogans line about Sternes novel being post-modern before there was any modernism to be post about. That's great, because it shows that post-modernism is a crock. Its only use is to be funny; if it isn't, it belongs to the Sybil Fawlty school. It points out the artificiality of what no one thought was real in the first place. Sterne was undermining the English novel in the 18th century, when the English novel was still getting started. Shakespeare did it. The Marx Brothers did it. This movie does it, very well. And it doesnt stop the story itself from working when it has to.

TARA: It did seem as though the only people in the movie who kept insisting that it was unfilmable were the ones who hadnt read it. And speaking of people who haven't read it ... well, I did, 10 years ago, in school, and it kind of blends in with Clarissa and The Mill on the Floss and all the other books I had to read, so I was less involved by the textual issues and whether the film did them justice. Actually, the first half of the movie -- where we see the events of the novel rolling out onscreen more or less as they do in the book -- was a bit of a drag and much less successful than the straight-up satire of the second half. Reality shows are populated by famous people, and sitcoms like Curb Your Enthusiasm have famous actors playing annoying caricatures of their public personae. We nobodies in the audience love to get this sort of backstage glimpse, especially when it's as funny and mean as what we see in Shandy.

CRAIG: It seems The Office has spawned a new British humour, seen in films such as Its All Gone Pete Tong and Tristam Shandy. Gone is the silliness of Monty Python and Benny Hill and the scrappy- underdogs-do-ridiculous-things-to-survive-Thatcherism of The Full Monty, Brassed Off, Calendar Girls, etc. This neo-Anglo school of comedy skewers self-important boobs using the mockumentary technique.

The effect is best described by The New Yorkers David Denby, who writes of Shandy: "It's maddeningly repetitive, and dependent on the the kind of strained English whimsy that leaves your throat sore from laughter that dies in the glottal region."

While Britain is hailed as the birthplace of this form, it is Canada that really deserves the credit/ blame. Pete Tong was directed by Calgary's Michael Dowse, who also made F.U.B.A.R., one of the best mockumentaries. I also seem to remember a guy by the name of Finkleman, who made Escape From The Newsroom back in 2002. Shandy has so many similarities to Escape, Im almost certain Winterbottom was influenced by it.

ROBERT: Well, he may have been, but it still seems to me right in the mainstream of British humour. Literary allusions mixed in with slapstick, juxtaposition of mock fact and mock fiction. Very much Monty Python. And the Goons before them. And its international; see early Woody Allen. It's all around us. Really basic ancestral comedy. Which is why the term post-modernism always seems to me so suspect. Matter of fact, I had my own post-modern moment with this one. I saw Shandy on Sunday, then came home and watched Bleak House on TV, and there was Gillian Anderson -- who is not quite in and not quite out of Shandy -- giving exactly the same performance, or at least using exactly the same accent. And to get back to where I started, that Bleak House, which is superb, is another example of providing a parallel to a novel, which is all an adaptation can hope to do.

TARA: Craig's mention of the British version of The Office reminded me of another show Shandy echoed for me: Extras. Created by Ricky Gervais and Stephen Merchant, the show stars Gervais as a struggling would-be actor relegated to nothing but roles as a "background artist," and his efforts to win the kind of parts he thinks Robert De Niro only gets due to luck. The show also features stars (Patrick Stewart, Samuel L. Jackson, Kate Winslet) on movie sets, playing the most unflattering versions of themselves with the effect that we end up thinking they must be that much more easygoing if theyre willing to send themselves up that way. Image rehabilitation through image destruction; what could be more postmodernistically ironic?

CRAIG: Weve talked a lot about post-modernism and styles of humour, yet we haven't touched on the themes in the movie. Shandy is a Freudian goldmine with graphic examples of castration complexes, Oedipal complexes and primal scenes. Isn't this a failure of the film, that we skim the bull of the story without getting to the cock?

ROBERT: I dont know. The film starts, more or less, with the infant Tristrams painful genital injury, courtesy of a careless nursemaid and an out-of-control sash-window, and it ends with Parson Yorick's cock-and-bull story, graphically illustrated. In between, Coogan-as-Coogan seems to be having virility-and-fidelity issues, reflected in his professional jealousy of Rob Brydon. What more do you want?

Oh, and we do get the scene with Gillian Anderson's Widow Wadman wondering about Brydons -- Uncle Tobys -- equipment. Which reminds me: Just like the book stops but doesn't finish, the movie leaves you wondering what the movie was like. We see Brydon's scene with Anderson , and we think "Did that scene get into the finished picture, or did the jealous Coogan find a way of nixing it?" And then you think, well, what finished movie? Insofar as one exists, its the movie Ive just seen. Very Shandean.

TARA: The longest stretch of book that we see on film has poor Keeley Hawes, as Tristram's mother, in unspeakable agony giving birth to our hero because his father, an amateur scientist, doesn't trust his son-to-be with a mere midwife, and insists that Mrs. Shandy be attended by a real doctor ... who proceeds to laze about, then terrify us with his clumsy forceps. Meanwhile, Hawes has to spend days doing nothing but crouching in bed screaming. The book may be full of phallic references, but the movie makes up for it with the yonic; the only cock in evidence is Walter.

CRAIG: While it seems we all agree Shandy was a hoot, I hope it doesnt encourage others to try filming historic novels. Lets not forget Orson Welles and Terry Gilliam both found Don Quixote too much to handle. I can already see the program guide at next year's Toronto film festival: "The Great Plague. The Fire of London. One man lived through it all and recorded it for posterity. And then he went to bed. Anthony Hopkins is Samuel Pepys in The Diary."
