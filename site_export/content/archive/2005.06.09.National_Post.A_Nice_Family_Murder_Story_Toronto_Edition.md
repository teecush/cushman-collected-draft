THE BROTHERS KARAMAZOV

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

- - -

The theme of fathers and sons is the whole motor of The Brothers Karamazov, where at least three of the four title characters have some motive, personal or philosophical, for offing their dad. I have a hunch, unsupported by research, that Dostoevsky's novels rank second only to Dickens' in frequency of dramatization, mainly because each of them is, among other things, a murder story.

This is the aspect that Jason Sherman has seized upon in an ingenious adaptation that gradually reveals itself as a continuous trial-scene, with the evidence presented as a series of flashbacks and flashbacks-within-flashbacks. And even then there turns out to be a false bottom to the whole thing.

There is also a sporting, though probably foredoomed, attempt to do justice to the book's ideas along with its story. Briefly: Dmitry, the prime suspect in the murder of Fyodor Karamazov, is trying to find his own tortured way between the belief-systems of his older and younger brothers. Dmitry is admirably played by Jonathan Goad, who gets a drunk scene that summons all the quicksilver self-hatred of the Dostoveyskyan hero. He is obviously the most dramatically viable character, unless you count the father himself, red-bloodedly rendered by Scott Wentworth. Like the Big Daddy character in Tennessee Williams' Cat on a Hot Tin Roof (also currently playing at Stratford), Karamazov pere is coarse and vital. Ivan the agonized atheist (Shane Carty) and Alyosha the monk-in- training (Peter van Gestel) are merely mouthpieces, though Ivan's famous fable of the Grand Inquisitor, his case against official Christianity, is at least given a chance, and a deftly-staged one. But you feel that it's included because it's too famous to leave out.

The fourth brother, the bastard Smerdyakov, is also the narrator; he's played by Ron Kennell, in sneering mode, and is maybe one device too many. The women, apart from a dependably fastidious and dependably mysterious Michelle Giroux, are woeful. It's a mystery how an actress as good as Dixie Seatle could end up so thoroughly patronizing the character she plays, while Dana Green's Grushenka, the whore desired by two generations of Karamazovs, seems inspired by the legend that this was a role once coveted by Marilyn Monroe.

These lapses apart, Richard Rose's production is first-rate, an endlessly resourceful re-shuffling of chairs and tables to create instant new locales, peopled by an ensemble who start out resembling a church congregation, then morph into courtroom spectators and participants, and are always specific and always involved. Sherman's ear is liable to go tin (somebody says "uber-bad"); still, this is probably as good a three-hour version as anyone could come up with. But it can't shake -- probably no version could -- the feeling of a book hanging in the wings and denying the play any chance of autonomous life.
