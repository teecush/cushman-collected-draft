It's hard to say which is the more depressing: the 2005-'06 Toronto theatre season, or the slate of nominations for the Dora Mavor Moore Awards that are supposed to commemorate it. On balance, though, I'd have to vote for the Doras. The season may have been desperate, but it wasn't that desperate.

Look, for a start, at the nominations for outstanding production of a play, general division. (General division, as I have to explain every year, refers to Toronto's bigger, more established theatres. It doesn't just mean the commercial sector.) This is probably the most important award category of all since, if its name means anything, it means best all-around show of the year, or perhaps most satisfying theatrical experience.

On one of the five nominees I cannot comment, since I was away when it opened. This was blood.claat, one womban story, written and performed by d'bi.young, and presented as part of the Stage 3 "other voices" season at Theatre Passe Muraille. It may well have been extraordinary; young's performance in Da Kink in My Hair the previous season, in a scene she seems to have written herself, certainly was.

Of the others, Leo (at Tarragon) was a superb production of what may have been the year's best new play. A Beautiful View (Buddies in Bad Times) showed author-director Daniel MacIvor at his best. So no complaints there. Soulpepper's Our Town was not only a fine production, showing off many of their, and the city's, best actors but, as the opening of the company's spanking new theatre complex, a great occasion. It will probably win. But it wasn't Soulpepper's best production. That was The Wild Duck, done back at the beginning of the season in the old premises at Harbourfront, and the year's best show anywhere. It has gone unnoticed, not only in this category but in all others save one.

And what is The Monument, the fifth nominee and one of the dreariest evenings of the year, doing in that section, or indeed in any section at all? (It's in six.) If it's because it was produced by Obsidian, our most prominent black company -- and I fear it must be -- then that's tokenism at its most outrageous. It also does a disservice to a lower-profiled Obsidian production, Born Ready, also part of Stage 3, which was far livelier and introduced us to a promising new writer, Joseph Jomo Pierre. The Tonys in New York are at least sort of honest; it's understood that the big prizes will go to the shows that stand to make the most money. Here, where there's no money to be made, we're supposed to be above that sort of thing. We shouldn't be saluting mediocrity.

I suspect that a similar reverence for the idea of collective theatre may be responsible for the presence among the best new play candidates of The Long Valley, adapted from some Steinbeck short stories by a troupe called This Is a Bird, and presented by Soulpepper. I liked it -- more, I think, than most critics -- but I would never have thought it a candidate for high honours. But then again, what's the competition? I'm sorry I missed blood.claat and also Territories, Niki Landau's play about the Middle East, which sounded exciting. This, by the way, was also at Passe Muraille, generally reckoned the poor relation among Toronto's new-play theatres and currently in serious financial trouble. It had, all the same, a far better track record this year than its more esteemed cousins, Tarragon and Factory. (So, in other spheres, did Buddies in Bad Times and the Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People.)

CanStage distinguished itself by not mounting a single uncontestably good show. Tarragon did redeem itself somewhat with Rosa Laborde's Leo, which will probably win and which probably deserves to. Ronnie Burkett's 10 Days on Earth had its great moments, but was not up to his best standards (which, I'm afraid, is what we've been saying about his last three shows). I'm bemused by the absence from the list of A Beautiful View, given MacIvor's prestige and the fact that even I liked it. I wish I could think of another play that deserved to be there, but I can't.

Oh yes, I can. Better Parts, Nicole Stamp's delightful solo piece, yet another part of Stage 3, and much the most entertaining. Which may explain why it has gone unmentioned.

Here's a revolutionary idea. If there aren't five outstanding new plays in a year -- and who but a mad optimist would expect there to be? -- let's simply nominate fewer. And while we're at it, let's give up the idea that good new plays can simply be workshopped into existence, and that it's somebody's fault if they aren't.

As always happens, the same shows reappear in one list after another. Best direction: I wouldn't know how to choose between Joseph Ziegler's Our Town, Richard Rose's Leo and MacIvor's A Beautiful View, so varied in their physical and psychological demands, so alike in texture and tension. I note again the absence of Laszlo Marton's The Wild Duck and pass on.

Best principal actor: That has to be Albert Schultz in Our Town, though I'm surprised that he wasn't listed also -- or even instead - - for his Hamlet. I'm also surprised that Jeff Lillico in Our Town is nominated, since there's no way his was a principal role. It's a strong field all around, with Tom McCamus in Mathilde my favourite for runner-up (not that they ever tell us).

Best principal actress: If it isn't Martha MacIsaac's heartbreaking Hedvig in The Wild Duck, the world's gone mad, though I'm pleased to see the undervalued Christine Brubaker getting a nod for The Amorous Servant.

So much for plays. Now for musicals. Or rather the musical, since -- though I'm well aware others like it less than I do -- I can see no honest way in which The Lord of the Rings could lose for best musical (though why are the authors listed but not the composers?) or best production of same (what's the diff?) or best direction (Matthew Warchus's achievement is amazing) or best set and lighting (Rob Howell and especially Paul Pyant, ditto, though they are both up against some operas).

As for best actor: This is bound to be Michael Therriault's Gollum, though in its less flashy way Peter Howe's Sam may be as good.

The only holdout musical category for Rings should be best actress, since the show, like the books, really isn't interested in women. Carly Street and Rebecca Jackson-Mendoza can only be there to make up the numbers: ironic, since either of Street's non-musical appearances, in The Dispute and The Merchant of Venice, should have qualified her for a nod in the independent theatre division (of which more in a moment) but didn't. I'm sorry I missed Corinne Koslo in Bunnicula (Young People's Theatre) but, no, it will have to be Louise Pitre in Song and Dance, obvious though it seems and miscast though she was. As for the other musicals, what were they? Snow White, a panto, and Boygroove, a revue-sketch. Both OK of their kind, but no. Still, at least nobody seems to have voted for Wicked. Not that that'll stop it coming back.

One of the few categories in which play and musical -- and indeed male and female -- are allowed to go head-to-head is best supporting (or, as we so democratically and meaninglessly put it, best featured) performance. Usually, and understandably, this is bestowed on somebody dramatic, but this year I fancy that it will, and think that it should, go to Evan Buliung for his Aragorn in Lord of the Rings, a performance that is, among other things, a first-rate audition for Henry V. Though why it should count as featured rather then principal beats me.

Of his non-singing competitors, I note that Gord Rand (The Innocent Eye Test) and David Storch (Habeas Corpus) are both deservedly nominated for salvage work in catastrophic productions. I think the wholesale citing of two "ensembles" (in The Long Valley and Banana Boys) is a cop-out; what good are awards if they aren't even ego-trips? The dramatic performance I thought would get it in a walk, William Webster's Polonius, wasn't nominated. Neither was any individual woman; how about Nancy Palk in Our Town?

The independent theatre division covers the small shows of which I can see only a smattering. This year, though, it was an impressive smattering, including three of the best things anywhere. One was Enoch Arden, which has gathered a nomination for its author, Judith Thompson, but unfairly not for its director, Maria Lamont, or for its amazing actor, John Fitzgerald Jay. Another was Alex Poch- Goldin's Cringeworthy, which has a claim, though not perhaps a conclusive one, to be the best new play, period. (Richer language than Leo; poorer structure.) The third, and a tremendous evening, was The Last Days of Judas Iscariot. I don't honestly know how you choose between David Ferry's epic direction of that one and Chris Abraham's exquisite miniature work on Cringeworthy, but I'd vote for Ferry. And he had an extraordinary cast, of whom three -- Philip Akin, Diego Matamoros, Irene Poole -- are up for prizes.

Matamoros, of course, should also have been nominated for his big- stage work in The Wild Duck and, even more, The Government Inspector, but I imagine his mantelpiece is overcrowded by now. I'm glad that Gavin Crawford has been nominated for his glittering turn in Sky Gilbert's Bad Acting Teachers, a show that deserved at least a single nod. I'm sorry that Alon Nashman got nothing for his more sombre virtuosity in Kafka and Son. I'm aghast that there should have been a nomination for Jani Lauzon in The Merchant of Venice, an inept performance even apart from the stupidity of playing Shylock as a woman; the independent jurors seem even more agenda-driven than the general ones.

In the opera division: I didn't see enough to make comparisons, but I thought Gotterdammerung one of the great nights of the year.

In the theatre for young audiences division: The best thing I saw was Hana's Suitcase, a distinguished production by any standards, and I find its complete neglect inexplicable.

HELLO AGAIN

Tarragon Extra Space

30 Bridgman Ave. * 416-531-1827

Enterprising production of a musical based on Schnitzler's La Ronde, by one of the best of the new breed of American lyricist- composers, Michael John LaChiusa (who also writes his own good, spare dialogue). Some wonderfully abrasive musical scenes, especially the first one. Like its source, this sexual daisy chain gets monotonous by the end, but there's much good stuff on the way, in an adaptation that sets everything in America, but jumps around between periods. Performances are uneven, though most of the singing is good, and Suzanne Bennett, as the whore who opens and closes the circle, is haunting in every way. Through July 1.

10 DAYS ON EARTH

Berkeley Street Theatre

26 Berkeley St. * 416-368-3110

In Ronnie Burkett's new puppet-play, a mother's love is felt, overwhelmingly, through its forcible removal. Darrell, a 50-year- old mentally challenged man, is orphaned but doesn't know it. The effect is heartbreaking, though the supplementary material, despite Burkett's technical virtuosity, feels like padding. Ends tonight.

OLIVER!

Festival Theatre

Stratford * 1-800-567-1600

Donna Feore directs a stage-filling production of Lionel Bart's epic crowd-pleaser of a musical, one that even manages in its opening scenes, and sometimes later, to sneak in some of the nightmare atmophere of Dickens' original non-stop chronicle of crime and child abuse. Bart, whose genius was instinctive if short-lived, spoke showbiz like a native, resulting in a self-believing score in which even a rank bad song like As Long As He Needs Me sort of works. Colm Feore revels in Bart's comical Fagin; Tyler Pearse's Oliver is splendidly acted and sung; Bruce Dow and Mary-Ellen Mahoney are perfectly matched workhouse tyrants. In rep through Oct. 29.

ARMS AND THE MAN

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Jackie Maxwell's production of Shaw's anti-romantic comedy is cute as a whole row of buttons; this has its charms, but ends up selling the play short. Peter Hutt and Patrick Galligan give entertaining performances, but the exaggerated Balkan sets and costumes dominate the evening. In rep through Oct. 29.

HENRY IV, PART ONE

Tom Patterson Theatre

Stratford * 1-800-567-1600

Richard Monette's production understands that the prose and verse parts of this great play are equally comic and equally serious. Adam O'Byrne's gangling, impetuous Hotspur is an especial revelation, witty in himself and the cause of wit in others (to quote Part Two). Scott Wentworth's sonorous King is both calculating and devout, two faces sporting one guilty conscience. Falstaff and Hal are less exciting, but there are many vivid minor performances and some brilliant strokes of staging. In rep through Sept. 24.
