The Dora Mavor Moore Awards, Toronto's annual theatre prize- giving, are never going to compare with the Oscars or Tonys for sleekness. Nobody onstage or in the audience is going to attract the favourable attention of fashion writers, people on the platform drop things, and the visual presentation is decidedly tacky, which is endearing up to a point and exasperating beyond it.

Surely when Toronto theatre gathers to pay tribute to itself, it should do better justice to its own sense of design and production - - which after all is what several of the awards are for.

The Dora organizers, unlike those of other awards, recognize that people other than actors work in the theatre and allow them to present some of the awards, which is a good thing.

This year, Toronto's favourite clowns, Mump and Smoot, whose stock-in-trade is sulphurous surprise at finding themselves wherever they are, were welcome presences as assistant presenters and emphatically outshone Allegra Fulton, the official emcee.

Certain things about the Doras puzzle me. The awards, now in their 20th year, are officially described as "celebrating excellence in Toronto's theatre and dance communities." That word "communities" always sets my teeth on edge; it's become a fuzzy synonym for "people": people who are often, in fact, fiercely competitive.

That apart, if dance is something separate from theatre, then why doesn't opera get similar individual treatment? As things stand, it gets lumped in with all other shows that sing. This means that Irina Mishura in Il Trovatore won for Outstanding Female Performance in a Musical. The male equivalent, meanwhile, went to Colm Wilkinson in Les Miserables, reprising the part he created in London umpteen years ago. It's difficult to see how either of these performances could be judged against those of colleagues who were playing by totally different rules.

We all know that awards are, by their nature, unfair and unreasonable, and involve comparisons between the strictly incomparable, but in this case apples and oranges seem to have been tossed together with astounding abandon.

The theatre, as opposed to dance, awards fall into two categories: General (meaning large) Theatre and Independent (meaning small) Theatre. However, the nominations are announced onstage in batches comprising both categories, which is extremely confusing.

The Outstanding General New Play was, uncontroversially, Michael Healey's The Drawer Boy; it could hardly have been anything else. As with the Oscars and the Tonys, one major award tends to pull others with it. So there were prizes for The Drawer Boy's producers, Theatre Passe Muraille, for its director, Miles Potter (shared with Soheil Parsa for Aurash), and for one of its actors, David Fox. These are more troubling. Mr. Potter's work on this delightfully approachable play was fine but -- comparing the incomparable again - - it wasn't as impressive as Robin Phillips' rigorous direction for the Soulpepper company of the forbiddingly unfamiliar Don Carlos, which, disgracefully, was not even nominated. Mr. Fox was brilliant (he has been excellent in many plays this season), but it would be hard to place him above Peter Donat's icy Philip II, also in Don Carlos.

Amazingly, Soulpepper -- the best thing to happen to Toronto theatre in a decade, a company that was formed to produce classics to the highest standard and managed to fill the house with them -- received nothing at all. One sensed some resentment in the air, as if the Toronto theatre community would rather go on being parochial.

Outstanding female performance in a play was Nancy Beatty in Risk Everything, who was charming and won all hearts by searching for her acceptance speech in her purse. (There are no women in The Drawer Boy.) Outstanding New Musical, beating out heavy-hitters such as Fosse and Needfire, was The Nutmeg Princess by Richardo Keens- Douglas. Outstanding production of a musical was the Canadian Opera Company's Norma; outstanding direction of a musical was Stephen Wadsworth's Xerxes, so opera pretty much cleaned up. Outstanding Choreography (not a dance award) was, perhaps inevitably, Fosse, shared between Ann Reinking and the ghost of Bob Fosse himself. Since Mr. Fosse's work was by definition not new, one wonders if it was Ms. Reinking's contribution that made it eligible.

Among the independents, the nod for Outstanding New Play/Musical went to Easy Lenny Lazmon and the Great Western Association, produced by Moriah Productions, in association with Go Chicken Go, which also got outstanding production and direction. The two dance awards -- for new choreography and for performance -- both went to Learie McNicolls in Armour.

There's a separate one-slot category of Theatre for Young Audiences; it was won by Roseneath Theatre for Dib and Dob and The Journey Home. It was probably wonderful (I didn't see it), but I wish there had been a prize for Theatre Direct's Waiting For Lewis, which was one of the year's best plays, even by grown-up standards.

The special Silver Ticket award, given for overall achievement, went to native playwright Tomson Highway.
