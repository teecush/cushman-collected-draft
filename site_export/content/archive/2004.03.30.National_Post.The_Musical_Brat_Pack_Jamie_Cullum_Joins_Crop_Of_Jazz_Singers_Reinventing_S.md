robertcushman@lycos.com

The Top o the Senator, in Toronto last Thursday night, was as packed as I have ever known the place. The occasion, a cross between musical set and media junket, was the Canadian debut of young British singer-pianist Jamie Cullum. Cameramen hunkered down at the front of the room obliterated the lower half of the stage, enabling the star to make a highly effective surprise entrance. There he suddenly was, standing atop the piano and giving out, unaccompanied (well, obviously), with the verse to Cole Porters I Get a Kick out of You.

Cullum is one of the current crop of musical juveniles singing standard songs (sometimes) in jazz settings (also sometimes); they might be described as a musical Brat Pack, especially considering that that phrase, in its original movie context, was a pun on the name of Frank Sinatra's bunch. Other members are American Peter Cincotti and Canadian Michael Buble; their godfather, who trod the same path a decade ago and has recently shown signs of returning to it, is Harry Connick Jr. Of them all, Cullum seems to me the best -- though I'd add the rider that hes the only one of the newcomers Ive seen live and that he's a more exciting act in person than he is on disc.

One thing I chauvinistically like about him is his un- Americanness. British singers attempting this repertoire often end up sounding mid-Atlantic -- which means from nowhere -- but there's a vestigial rasp and snarl in Cullums vocals, a sound that would once have been called Cockney but is now just classless-ubiquitous, and that lends humour and an edge.

It isnt hostile; Cullum himself comes across as a nice young man, fresh out of college. It's both self-aware and self- deprecating, and it comes in especially useful in the chat between songs, a part of the business in which very few American singers have managed to avoid sounding pompous. Cullum's laid-back introductions are a point of rest to set against his manic physical activity. When he gets around to actually playing the piano, hes liable to stand up and attack it, Jerry Lee Lewis fashion, or reach inside it to take prolonged solos on its strings. Physically slight, temperamentally impish, aggressively mobile, he suggests Mick Jagger cast as a supper-club Frodo.

When he gets into the chorus of I Get a Kick, hes a born-again post-rocker, pounding out a rhythm that could be called enraged swing, but sensitive to lyrics, especially the ones about cocaine; as he shruggingly says afterwards, the song might have been written yesterday. Later he does I Could Have Danced All Night, very convincingly, as the hangover from a rave. He includes the verse to this one, too, which is most unusual for someone who isn't Julie Andrews.

He also does more recent stuff, including Jimi Hendrix' The Wind Cries Mary and Jeff Buckleys Baby, You Shouldve Come Over, making a better musical case for the former than for the latter; he didn't do much for the lyrics of either. (He was much clearer on the standards, or maybe it's just that I know those words backwards anyway.)

But a lot of his show just merged, enjoyably. At one point he wandered away from the piano for an engaging saunter through Singin in the Rain. It seemed to follow on logically from what went before, but Ive still no idea how, musically, he got there.

His sidemen, Geoff Gascoyne (bass) and Sebastian DeKrom (drums), played superbly and were loyal foils.

The CD Twentysomething (Verve, to be released here May 11) unhelpfully adds a band, which sounds like an afterthought and, along with the lack of an audience, accentuates the cracks in the singer's voice; rough would be better. And most of those welcome verses are missing. There are several of Cullum's original compositions, which tend to start better than they finish; the title number has a terrific premise (what do you do with an arts degree?) but degenerates into an aimless list. The best is a song called Its About Time.

Like all his contemporaries, he has been hailed as being, and damned for not being, the new Sinatra. Its a pointless comparison, if only because the Sinatra who's being invoked never existed at any given time; he's an essence of the singer, distilled from a career of 50 years. None of the newcomers has the sweet sound of Sinatra in his band-singing twenties; theyve gone straight for the burned- out, world-weary voice without having gone through the years it took its owner to earn it. To put it another way, they sound like Bobby Darin, first and best of the post-Sinatras, but without the vocal energy. Cullum, despite reproducing a Sinatra interpolation on Kick, sounds the least like an imitator, which means he may have the best chance of emulating the original and making the songs his own.

Of the others, Cincotti is the one being marketed most simply as a jazzman; his self-titled CD (Concord), recorded last year when he was 19, features just bass, drums, sax and his own piano, on which he is strikingly clever and assured. (His Erroll Garner impression on Spinning Wheel is a warm musical joke.) His singing is boyish going on brash, best of all on Miss Brown to You.

Michael Buble, who also has a self-titled CD (Warner/Reprise), has the richest voice of the bunch -- certainly the smoothest -- but is really being sandbagged with Sinatra. Not only does he sing the songs (a Summer Wind thats here more of a feckless breeze), he even sings the arrangements; Billy May's familiar chart on Come Fly with Me is reproduced, so we can sing along with Buble singing along with Sinatra, a sort of double karaoke. It's fun to play in the car. Buble could probably deliver better; on his quiet concluding track (Thats All), he does. Maybe hell turn into Vic Damone. One song, the Latin perennial Sway, turns up on both discs. Buble's is the Arthur Murray version; Cincotti actually sounds like a kid longing shyly for a dance, which is unusual on this kind of song, and makes sense.

Connick, now the senior of the juniors, has a new CD, Only You (Sony), composed of songs from the '50s and '60s, a period less musically rich than he seems to believe, or maybe it's just that his choices are odd. (Who wants to hear more of More?) His singing is sleepier than ever -- Dean Martin on the wagon, perhaps -- but it yields some unexpected rewards, notably on a doggedly pleading Save the Last Dance for Me. His arranging -- his greatest talent, I think -- is excellent. For Once in My Life sounds fresh (for once in its life) with what appears to be the relentless plucking of whole banks of strings.
