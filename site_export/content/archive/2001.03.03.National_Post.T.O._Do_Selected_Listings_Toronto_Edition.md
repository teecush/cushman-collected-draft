Wit

Margaret Edson's Pulitzer Prize-winning play arrives from the United States brandishing rave reviews and wearing every conceivable garland. These facts, especially about the Pulitzer, are not automatically encouraging as the Pulitzer judges have a history of rewarding whatever ennobling drama is fashionable in a given year. Wit is not sentimental, except towards the end, but it is, however, cute. It doesn't have a single boring moment, and its Toronto presentation is ideal. I cannot imagine a tighter production than Glynis Leyshons or a more complete central performance than Seana McKennas whose effect is spellbinding. All the same, I didn't much like it. Robert Cushman

- Runs to March 10, $20-$60. Bluma Appel Theatre, 27 Front St. E. (416) 368-3110 or (416) 872-1111

Earshot

This is a one-character comedy, written and directed by Morris Panych. Randy Hughson performs it, Ken MacDonald designed it, and both their contributions are beyond praise. But everything stems from the author: The piece is a superbly mounted Panych attack. Panych has apparently suffered all his life from over-acute hearing, an affliction he has bequeathed to his protagonist, Doyle. A pain to Panych, the condition is a torment to Doyle. He lives in a spectacularly squalid one-room apartment, agonizingly aware of every audible detail in his neighbours' lives -- and it seems that most details are audible if you concentrate. His complaint, he explains to some imaginary interlocutor, "isnt deafness -- its DEAFENING. The play's metaphor is similarly deafening in its obviousness, and quite magnificent in its effectiveness. Hughson plays the physical comedy to the limit while vocally exploring the full range of the character's bemusement, anger and pain -- and his enjoyment of all of them. There is not a moment of dead air in his performance, and he has remarkable transformative powers. Its only February, and this, after Zadies Shoes, is already Hughson's second virtuoso performance of the year. Add in Slavs from last fall, and it's his third of the season. At this rate, we wont need any other actors. Panychs visual and verbal choreography of his own text is masterly. The visual I would have expected; the verbal is an exhilarating surprise. Robert Cushman

- Runs to March 18, Tuesday to Saturday 8 p.m., Saturday 2 p.m., $19-$24, Sunday 2 p.m. (PWYC). Tarragon Extra Space, 30 Bridgman Ave. (416) 531-1827

Clout

An all-star cast fills David Young's satire about men, sex and power ... and a woman who wins. A burnt-out left-wing journalist and a Machiavellian press baron are kidnapped and held hostage by a mysterious shape-shifting woman who claims to be an eco-terrorist. Directed by Richard Rose. Starring Waneta Storms, Eric Peterson and R.H. Thomson. See review on Page F4.

- Runs to March 25, Tuesday to Friday 8 p.m., Saturday 2 and 8 p.m., Sunday 2 p.m. Factory Theatre, 125 Bathurst St. (416) 504- 9971

The Vagina Monologues

As the title makes clear, this show deals with women's experiences with their genitalia. Excerpts from Eve Enslers book of the same name are read aloud, dealing with issues such as rape, orgasms and visits to the gynecologist. Krista Sutton and Sandi Ross hold the two anchoring roles, with various actors rotating through the third position: Jann Arden, March 5-10; Carole Pope, March 12- 25 and Jeanne Beker, March 26-April 6.

- Indefinite run. Monday to Saturday 8 p.m., $55, Saturday matinee 5 p.m., $45. New Yorker Theatre, 651 Yonge St. (416) 870- 8000

A Flea in her Ear

Laszlo Martin, who directs this Soulpepper revival of the George Feydeau classic farce, is too intelligent a director not to know that whats expected of him is to make it funny. And he must know, too, that the best way to make it at least seem new is to make it socially and psychologically real. Unfortunately, the methods he chooses contradict one another. The evening is an intermittently entertaining debacle. Marton begins by parading the characters before us. A piano accompanies their promenade, and the pianist remains to underline, silent-movie fashion, every turn in the action -- every suspicion, every discovery, every flight and pursuit. The actors are troupers, and the script is a masterpiece, but there are only so many times they can pick themselves up and carry on. There used to be signs hung in saloons that read: Don't shoot the pianist, he's doing his best." Im sure this one is, but hes killing the play stone-dead. His accomplices, who made me feel equally murderous, are the movement and set designers. The set is mainly composed of stairs, with the crucial bedroom shunted off to one side. The actors spend a lot of time executing debilitating steps on debilitating steps. Robert Cushman

- Regular run to April 8, Tuesday to Saturday 8 p.m., Wednesday, Saturday and Sunday matinees 2 p.m., $35-$65. The Elgin Theatre, 189 Yonge St. (416) 872-1212 or at the box office.

Lock, Stock and Two Smoking Barrels (1998)

What? You havent seen this movie? The one that made Guy Ritchie famous, and therefore worth courting to the Material Girl? Mr. Madonna the First's first feature is hilarious, bloody and at times nervewracking. A few critics in England nitpicked that it didn't do enough to examine the social factors motivating its Cockney protagonists, a group of four men who keep trying, and failing, to make it rich through illegal means. But so what. Who cares. This is cool moviemaking, not social commentary.

Paradise Cinema, 1006 Bloor St. W.

March 7 and 8, 9:25 p.m. both nights
