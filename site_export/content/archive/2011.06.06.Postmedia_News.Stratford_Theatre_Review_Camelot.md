Camelot

Festival Theatre, Stratford

Both of our major festivals this year are mounting famous musicals by Alan Jay Lerner and Frederick Loewe. Shaw has My Fair Lady, a show that always works and does so again, very nicely. Stratford has Camelot, a show that never works. Except that this time it does, magnificently.

Chief among its many glories is the King Arthur of Geraint Wyn-Davies, an actor who seems, like Arthur's teacher Merlin, to be aging backwards. In the past few Stratford seasons we have seen him nudging greatness as Polonius, Bottom, Julius Caesar and, just the night before this one, Falstaff. He reaches it as Arthur, who starts the show boyish, shy and idealistic, and retains those qualities even as they become battered and embittered by tragic experience. And for this show, "tragic" is not too high-flown a word. It deals with hopeless choices.

In Lerner's adaptation of T. H. White's adaptation of Thomas Malory and his predecessors, Arthur, born into a world of war, conceives the ideas of peace and civic justice and, for a time, makes them work. Then Guinevere, his queen and confidante, and Lancelot, his best friend and champion knight, fall in love. Their passion may not be consummated (the show's ambiguous on the point) but Mordred, Arthur's illegitimate son and ambitious antitype, makes sure that it looks like it was. Guinevere's condemned to death; Mordred gleefully points out Arthur's "magnificent dilemma" between law and love.

There are two major climaxes that Wyn Davies makes achingly real. At the end of Act 1, Arthur, recognizing the emotional facts on the ground, tries to argue himself into believing that everything will turn out for the best because everybody will behave well. At the end of the second, when they haven't and it hasn't, he prepares for his last battle and passionately enjoins a boy called Tom of Warwick (read, Tom Malory) to run away and keep the story of Camelot alive. This involves a reprise of the title song, miraculously transformed from cheerful jingle to desperate anthem.

Both these scenes are superbly written, and the finale, invoking failure and triumph and history and literature all at once, can bring a lump to the throat in even the crummiest productions. (Believe me, I know.) What's exceptional about them here is they're seamlessly connected to what has gone before, much of which is very funny. Arthur never believes himself clever, though he's always thinking things out; he has a gauche quick-wittedness that Wyn Davies makes supremely winning. Lancelot, questioned about his martial prowess, says, "my skill comes from timing," and the same is true of Wyn Davies' Arthur. Lancelot adds that his "strength comes from purity," and that suits for this Arthur, too. You truly believe that he's a good man. It both warms and breaks the heart

The tact and boldness of this performance are emblematic of Gary Griffin's production, which solves every one of the show's problems. It digs beneath much arch and awkward writing to get at what's real. Arthur's eccentric buddy King Pellinore talks with a forced joviality that suggests very bad imitation Wodehouse; Brent Carver downplays the mannerisms in favour of grit, loyalty and genuine humour. He also plays a truly unearthly Merlin, and this doubling of Arthur's two advisers adds up to his finest performance in years.

Similarly, Mike Nadajewski softpedals the failed wordplay of Mordred's self- revealing song, and plays it instead for a passionate, twisted delight in destructiveness.

Kaylee Harwood's Guinevere is plummy for my taste, but her relationship with Arthur rings true, and their duet of mutual consolation (What Do the Simple Folk Do?) is both amusing and revealing. Her relationship with Lancelot is more perplexing. Jonathan Winsby has a ringing voice and handsome presence, but it's still hard to see why Guinevere should fixate for years on a man who starts (C'est Moi) as a self-glorifying buffoon and then turns into cardboard. All this pair seem to do is sing heavy ballads at one another (though one, mercifully, has been cut).

Camelot is in fact unique among classic musicals in having a book that's far more interesting than its score; it seems somehow appropriate that Loewe's best tune, Follow Me, should be sung by a character we barely see to one we are about to lose. Griffin and his choreographer Warren Carlyle excel, however, at making the songs tell the story, especially the big concerted numbers like The Jousts, a medieval Ascot Gavotte that's here more exciting than I would ever have thought possible, and the insanely expository Guinevere.

The Lusty Month of May is rescued from coyness to become a feast of movement and - courtesy of Maria Blumenfeld's costumes - colour. Debra Hanson's sets speed, adorn and beautify the action without ever overpowering it. They include a magical forest and castle where dwells Lucy Peacock's deliciously languid Morgan le Fay, anachronistically hooked on chocolate. There's an equally magical prologue in which we see Arthur growing up while Merlin - what's the word? - youthens, and that incorporates a real hawk, a vital part of the hero's education.

Maybe the show's time has come; what was once known as the Kennedy musical may now be the Obama musical. Anyway, I've never heard of, and certainly never seen, a production of Camelot that came anywhere near matching this one.
