Rex Harrington injuring his Achilles tendon while leaping in the air was the worst of it, but not the first of it. The opening night of Andrew Lloyd Webbers Song and Dance, at the Music Hall on the Danforth, was dogged by disasters, not all of them inherent in the material.

The initial mishap was trivial enough. The theatres vaunted renovations seemed to have played havoc with the seat numbering, and the start of the show was delayed by 15 minutes while audience members, who'd probably had enough trouble parking outside, milled around looking for resting places. There must have been some equivalent trouble backstage as well, because the second half was also late starting. Some impatient patrons commenced a slow hand clap, a sound I haven't heard in a theatre in years.

By this time, wed already been through the Song half of the evening, near the end of which Louise Pitres mike went out. The malfunction was soon rectified, and though the sudden drop in volume was disconcerting, the effect, while it lasted, wasn't all bad. The voice remained fully audible and fully expressive; for a few moments we seemed to have plunged forward, or backward, to an age of Musicals Unplugged. Let us pray.

Pitre is the only singer here. Her part of the show is a song cycle recycled; or, in its belated Toronto premiere, rerecycled. Under the title Tell Me on a Sunday, it was a TV special and then a record album. In 1982, it reached the London stage, in accordance with the principle, even more prevalent then than now, that any Lloyd Webber creation might be a cash cow and had better be milked. The music, in fact, is good, if monotonously mopey; the title song exemplifies both qualities.

But the piece isn't very dramatic. Its the story of a North London woman who visits the United States and moves from New York to Los Angeles and back. She also goes from man to man to man: from a movie producer to somebody young and Bohemian to somebody married.

Don Blacks lyrics are full of superficial specifics: They are competent and occasionally witty, and they tell us something about each of the heroine's lovers, but only as she sees them and the lack of an outside perspective becomes very wearing. They tell us nothing about her, except that she's susceptible, ever hopeful and gets angry when thwarted. Well, who doesnt?

She gets especially vexed with a mysterious girlfriend and confidante who likes to be the first with bad news and who inspires the minor hit song Take That Look off Your Face. (Or maybe the protagonist sings it to herself. Thats possible. And a mite more interesting.)

The structure is off, too, since her most important love -- the one she beseeches to Tell Her on a Sunday -- is her second, so what follows is anti-climax. And to top it off, the ambience is too old to be contemporary and too new to be nostalgic. Pitre's last, supposedly defiant, words are, "You don't know me" -- and shes righter than her character thinks.

She also happens to be miscast. She sings truly and fiercely, with no more melismatic melodramatics than the score absolutely forces on her, and shes wryly funny when she gets the chance. But she is not the kind of girl to get so repeatedly and so helplessly into this kind of trouble, and certainly not the kind to write sad/ chirpy letters to Mum about it. She is too tough and, especially with her trademarked white hair proudly on display, too old.

Nor can she do the accent. The show's Britisher-abroad angle has always seemed to me pretty specious, but it's written in and has to be dealt with. In those letters home, which are the narrative glue, she never sounds as if she knows where or why Muswell Hill is or what Tizer might be. (Youre as puzzled as she is. Its a fizzy drink I remember from my childhood. I had no idea they were still making it.)

Trudy Moffatt's direction brings nothing to the material, and the set looks to have cost the minimum in money and imagination.

The Dance section is far livelier. The music consists of Webber's variations on a theme by Niccolo Paga- nini. Its bright and bracing, and suggests, not for the first time, that Webbers greatest musical talent is for orchestration. (He originally wrote the variations for his cellist brother, and the cello is thrillingly employed in both the show's segments.)

Wayne Sleep, who choreographed the original production, has redone his work, which complements the first part (though it doesn't illuminate it) by being basically a succession of male-female encounters.

There is a supporting corps of six, and I suppose the three men may be equivalents of the English girls three guys. That would probably make the diminutive Ryan Foley the doppelganger of her Greenwich Village guy; anyway, hes cheeky and delightful, especially when matched with the contrastingly towering Julia Juhas.

By this logic, Harrington and his partner, Evelyn Hart, would represent the ideal love we spent the first half in search of. They had great chemistry, and if he seemed a trifle sedate, she was lovely. Anyway she won all hearts, and a standing ovation when she returned, just after his accident, for what obviously should have been a crowning pas de deux and improvised, with perfect timing, a series of solo pirouettes.

There was once a Broadway production of Song and Dance that tried, ill-advisedly, to bring the two to a joint narrative closure. Here, Pitre simply comes back on stage and sings something very vague about love at the dancers. The tune is that of Webber's rather famous, though not very enlivening, Unexpected Song, and it's doubly unexpected here, since it originally was part of the Tell Me on a Sunday score but has here been cut from it. So we get a reprise of something we havent previously heard. Make that triply unexpected and singularly ineffective.

Still, the feelings of the cast at this point on the first night can only be guessed at. Robert Campanella will be subbing for Harrington.

CARE

Tarragon Theatre

30 Bridgman Ave. - 416-531-1827

A clear example of the theatrical gulf between accuracy and truth. Diane Flacks and Richard Greenblatt play a mature couple with their first baby, plus assorted friends, helpers, lovers and -- the most compelling character -- the wifes dying father. The issues are compelling and a few of the jokes good, but the effect is sketchy. Through May 28.

SPARTA

Theatre Passe Muraille

16 Ryerson Ave. - 416-504-8980

Back to the farm. Sarah Martyn's play has a good sense of family tensions, and of economic-agricultural realities, but no theme, story or believable characters to hitch them to. Layne Coleman's direction, on an awkwardly semi-stylized set, is of little help to either the play or the actors. Closes tomorrow.

BOYGROOVE

Diesel Playhouse

56 Blue Jays Way - 416-971-5656

An amusing and endearing deconstruction of the boy-band phenomenon -- groups whom nobody, including the participants, think are any good. Most of the songs, unsurprisingly, are filler, but the script, direction and the four boys (who play many other roles as well) are fast, astute and friendly-with-attitude. Through June 4.

WINGFIELDS INFERNO

Royal Alexandra Theatre

260 King St. W. - 416-872-1212

The sixth instalment of the Walt Wingfield saga might also be called Wingfields Insurance. The rural township's Orange Lodge burns down and stockbroker-turned-farmer Walt is put in charge of its reconstruction. Anecdotes, apparently self-contained and mostly farcical, come together at the end, like a team of shaggy-dog stories on a single leash. Rod Beattie, playing everybody, is reliably amazing. Through June 4.

10 DAYS ON EARTH

Berkeley Street Theatre

26 Berkeley St. - 416-368-3110

In Ronnie Burkett's new puppet-play, a mothers love is felt, overwhelmingly, through its forcible removal. Darrell, a 50-year- old mentally challenged man, is orphaned but doesnt know it. The effect is heartbreaking, though the supplementary material, despite Burkett's technical virtuosity, feels like padding. Through June 24.

ESCAPE FROM HAPPINESS

Factory Theatre

125 Bathurst St. - 416-504-9971

George F. Walker's 1992 play is funny, a bit suspenseful and generally more fun than most, with a tough mouth and a soft heart. It also has two separate plots -- family and crime -- that dont quite cohere. Irene Poole, in one of her killer-dog roles, gives a tornado of a performance, and Clare Coulter is triumphant as a kind of secular saint. Closes tomorrow.
