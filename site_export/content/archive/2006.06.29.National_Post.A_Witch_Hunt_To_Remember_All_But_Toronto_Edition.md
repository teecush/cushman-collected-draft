THE CRUCIBLE

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Tadeusz Bradecki's production of The Crucible is the Shaw Festivals most exciting production since the full-length Man and Superman. As is well known, Arthur Miller planned his play as an exercise in historical analogy. Writing in the McCarthy years, he drew a parallel between the political hysteria of his own times and the more literal witch-hunt of 1692, the memory of which has made Salem, Mass., into a tourist attraction. Millers play stands on its own, as a thrilling piece of storytelling -- one of the few in modern drama -- with an earned moral fervour behind it that is never likely to run short of contemporary applications. In 1953, when it was first unveiled, the line that hit audiences where they lived was Is the accuser always holy now? spoken by the desperate farmer John Proctor to the men engaged in condemning his wife for witchcraft. Now, though for opposite reasons, it's likelier to be one uttered by the senior judge: "A person is either with this court or he must be counted against it." Those words are speedily followed by a Manichean declaration about good and evil, from somebody who, of course, is thoroughly convinced that he is on the side of good.

The only person in the play who, from the beginning, acknowledges a flaw in himself is Proctor, the hero; and that humanity, oddly enough, ends up as a flaw in the play. The historical Proctor went to the gallows, though his wife survived: Miller pins their story on the fact that Abigail Williams, one of the young girls whose claims of being diabolically possessed touched off the town's hysteria, had been the Proctors servant. Added to this is the supposition that John took her to bed, thereby igniting his own guilt, his wife Elizabeths anger and the discarded Abigail's murderous jealousy. To prove his wife's innocence, Proctor has to confess his own adultery. When asked to confirm his guilt, Elizabeth, her love triumphing over her self-righteousness, denies it. So far, so superbly ironic. But then Proctor, condemned to death and already despising himself for his infidelity, agrees to save his skin by confessing to witchcraft, while balking at making his confession public; he wants to preserve the integrity of his name. This, after the tremendous public drama that has gone before, seems like a quibble. It also feels like Miller letting his own demons get the better of him; the play was conceived, of course, in the era of "naming names" by a man who was both a victim of McCarthys blacklist and the adulterous lover of a younger woman. In his case it was Marilyn Monroe, and I think most of us would be prepared to understand. And, even by 17th-century New England standards, Proctors sin seems dramatically slight compared to what is going on around him.

Bradecki's production has a weakness for menacing off-stage hymn- singing (very un-Puritanical) and for piercing sound effects employed to underline tensions that have already been established by proper methods having to do with acting. The staging of the orgy of denunciation that concludes the play's trial is tremendous; coldly calculated by Abigail and the others, it develops its own momentum, engulfing everyone on stage.

Benedict Campbell, doggedly passionate, and Kelli Fox, her anger and her charity both sharp as knives, play the Proctors as finely as I have ever known them to be played. Jim Mezon is tremendous as Deputy Governor Danforth, a terrifying image of a calm judicial mind turning itself into a one-man lynch mob. Miller susbsequently felt that he had gone too easy on Danforth but, no, he got it right the first time. Excellent too are Mary Haney as a multiply bereaved mother whose grief curdles into vengeful bitterness, and Norman Browning as her husband, who sees denouncing witches a fine way of buying up cheap land. The play has a single explanation of how the madness got started in Salem, but many -- greed, spite, fear, frustration and perverted idealism -- for how and why it was allowed to continue.
