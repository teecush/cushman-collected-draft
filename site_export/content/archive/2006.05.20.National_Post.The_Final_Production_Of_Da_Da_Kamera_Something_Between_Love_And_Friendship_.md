robertcushman@lycos.com; A Beautful View plays through May 21 at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, 416-975-8555.

The most celebrated stage direction in all of drama, and the most daunting, may be "Exit, pursued by a bear." Shakespeare threw it in at the halfway point of The Winter's Tale; directors, technicians, actors in skins and plain old animal trainers have been challenged by it ever since. In his new play, A Beautiful View, Daniel MacIvor goes the Bard one better (I once swore a mighty oath that I would never refer to Shakespeare as "the Bard," but in this sentence it seems somehow inescapable) in a way that I shouldn't reveal. Well, all right, he goes double. As his own director, MacIvor also provides a casual object lesson in how to stage such scenes. Again, no details, but it's done with lights.

A Beautiful View comes right at the end of the Toronto theatre season and is one of the best things to have happened in it. It concerns two unnamed young women, played by Tracy Wright and Caroline Gillis, who first meet in a camping-equipment store. In fact, Wright is emerging from a tent when Gillis gets her first sight of her. This shared interest in the outdoors is one of the things that leads them, by ways both serpentine and logical, to the finale.

Something else they sort of have in common is music. Wright claims to have a band and wonders if Gillis would like to join as second ukulele player. Both women are, like most people, defensive fantasists (nothing as grand as liars), and there is no band. Fantasies can pay off, though, and later on a band does come into existence. It sounds exactly as you would imagine a band with two ukeleles would sound.

The two also bond over their admiration of a more professional group and especially over its female leader. They sound disappointed when she hooks up with one of her male musicians. It isn't that they identify as lesbians; in fact, they don't identify with anything much, especially in the way of relationships. As Wright misanthropically puts it, in my favourite line of the past 12 months, "I'm not even hiring in the friends department."

Gillis does take what might be called an initiative in that she tracks Wright to her job at an airport bar, which, as Wright points out, is not the kind of place you drop into if you live downtown. And later on they do find themselves in bed together, though Gillis bails out and Wright doesn't seem especially bothered. Then one of them gets married, which doesn't cause much jealousy, and one of them is caught in bed with the musician lady, which does. And they do set up a relationship, which lasts until the show ends.

It ought, I suppose, to be a shameful confession that a week after seeing it, I can't remember the precise order of events, or even which does what. But I don't think it matters. It's the shared energy of these characters that matters rather than their differences. It's tempting to say that Wright is the edgy, assertive half of the couple and Gillis the shy one, but their qualities merge and fluctuate, which strikes me as pretty true to life. One can, I think, identify a dynamic between them as performers, with Wright, the quirkier and more presentational of the two, bringing out the extrovert in Gillis, who is generally more apt to be the unobtrusive anchor of a show (and tends to get cast in that kind of role). They and MacIvor give us something between love and friendship that might not want to call itself either. It must be significant that, though the two women freely drop the names of the other people in their lives, they never give their own; they don't want to be pinned down. The show takes amorphous feelings and, without ever breaking faith with them, makes a concrete theatrical statement out of them.

It begins with a kind of negative teaser, a silent series of approaches and avoidances that resemble a Samuel Beckett mime-play and make you fear the worst. Once that's past, the play is MacIvor at his best, meaning at his least portentous. Even the revelation that the title refers to death fails to weigh the play down. It is even, in a jaunty kind of way, quite haunting. There's also a country-type song about someone being the one in only, the two in together and the four in forever. It's sung twice and it's terrible, even apart from the fact that it couldn't come up with a word for three, but the second time around it's rather touching: the "something appealing, something appalling" that's celebrated in A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum. MacIvor's staging, which boils down to the spatial relationships between the two actresses and a few select physical objects, is about as simple and as rich as it could be.

The show is the final production of MacIvor's company, Da Da Kamera, and enables it to go out in style. It also marks another good show for the host theatre, Buddies in Bad Times, which has been having its best season in living memory, or at least in mine; something that, with the possible exception of the Lorrane Kimsa, I couldn't say for any other house in Toronto.

MATHILDE

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

55 Mill St. - 416-866-6666

The depth and texture of a marriage are present in this French play (by Veronique Olmi, excellently translated by Morwyn Brebner) as in few others. In Kelly Thornton's flawless production, Martha Burns plays a writer who has served 15 months for seducing a minor, and Tom McCamus is her ruffled, apparently pragmatic oncologist- husband. Each is an expert at finding the other's tenderest points; tenderness flares up as well. Through May 27.

ARMS AND THE MAN

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Jackie Maxwell's production of Shaw's anti-romantic comedy is as cute as a whole row of buttons; this has its charms, but ends up selling the play short. Peter Hutt and Patrick Galligan give entertaining performances, but the exaggerated Balkan sets and costumes dominate the evening. In rep through Oct. 29.

THE HEIRESS

Royal George Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Tara Rosling gives an extraordinary performance, rising to tragedy, as Henry James's shy, plain heroine. Mike Shara, smooth and gauche at the same time, is the suitor who tantalizingly may not be faking it. Taut direction by Joseph Ziegler, superb design by Christina Poddubiuk. In rep through Oct. 7.

HIGH SOCIETY

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

The show of the movie musical of the play (that was also a movie). It never worked, and this busy production is particularly coarse. The augmented Cole Porter score is made up not of songs the characters just have to sing, but of songs it's not quite impossible they might; it's dehydrating. Dan Chameroy and the sets are good. In rep through Nov. 19.

SONG AND DANCE

Danforth Music Hall

147 Danforth Ave. - 416-778-8163

Piotr Stancyk (National Ballet) replaces Roberto Campanella, who sprained his ankle after replacing Rex Harrington, who injured his Achilles tendon. This has been a bad-luck show. But the dance half, with Evelyn Hart partnering whoever and with a fine corps, remains bracing. The song half, despite some of Andrew Lloyd Webber's better ballads and the presence of Louise Pitre, is not very dramatic. Through June 18.

CARE

Tarragon Theatre

30 Bridgman Ave. - 416-531-1827

A clear example of the theatrical gulf between accuracy and truth. Diane Flacks and Richard Greenblatt play a mature couple with their first baby, plus assorted friends, helpers, lovers and -- the most compelling character -- the wife's dying father. The issues are compelling and a few of the jokes good, but the effect is sketchy. Through May 28.

BOYGROOVE

Diesel Playhouse

56 Blue Jays Way - 416-971-5656

An amusing and endearing deconstruction of the boy-band phenomenon -- groups whom nobody, including the participants, think are any good. Most of the songs, unsurprisingly, are filler, but the script, direction and the four boys (who play many other roles as well) are fast, astute and friendly-with-attitude. Through June 4.

WINGFIELD'S

INFERNO

Royal Alexandra

260 King St West - 416-872-1212

The sixth instalment of the Walt Wingfield saga might also be called Wingfield's Insurance. The rural township's Orange Lodge burns down and stockbroker-turned-farmer Walt is put in charge of its reconstruction. Anecdotes, apparently self-contained and mostly farcical, come together at the end, like a team of shaggy-dog stories on a single leash. Rod Beattie, playing everybody, is reliably amazing. Through June 4.

10 DAYS ON EARTH

Berkeley Street Theatre

26 Berkeley St. - 416-368-3110

In Ronnie Burkett's new puppet-play, a mother's love is felt, overwhelmingly, through its forcible removal. Darrell, a 50-year- old mentally challenged man, is orphaned, but doesn't know it. The effect is heartbreaking, though the supplementary material, despite Burkett's technical virtuosity, feels like padding. Through June 24.

THE LORD

OF THE RINGS

Princess of Wales Theatre

300 King St. W. - 416-872-1212

At its best, it's wonderful; at its worst, it's far better than doubters ever thought it could be. Scenically and technically, it's a marvel; and the adaptation of Tolkien's trilogy is outstandingly intelligent. The musical component varies, in style and effect. Notable performances by Evan Buliung, Michael Therriault and assorted hobbits. Indefinite run.

LAST WILL AND VIDEOTAPE

Mysteriously Yours

2026 Yonge St. - 416-486-7469

The corpse in the latest interactive comedy-thriller from Mysteriously Yours dinner theatre is billionaire William Randolph, but he isn't a spoof Hearst or even a spoof Citizen Kane. The show parodies nothing but itself, which is a pity. It's coasting, but Jean Daigle (as a Newfie cop) has as much zest and authority as ever, and the dinner is much better than might be expected. Indefinite run.
