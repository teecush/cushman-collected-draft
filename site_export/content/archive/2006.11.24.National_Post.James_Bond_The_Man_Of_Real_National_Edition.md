Quentin Tarantino has said the sign of a good film is that it makes you want to go home, eat some pie and talk about it. With that in mind, our Popcorn Panel features film buffs feuding in this space each week.

THIS WEEKS PANEL

- Craig Courtice, a short filmmaker who isn't very tall

- Robert Cushman, the Post's theatre critic

- Benjamin Errett, Arts & Life editor

THIS WEEKS PIE Layer cake

THIS WEEKS SUBJECT Casino Royale

CRAIG I once said of Woody Allen that his films are like a yearly visit from your favourite neurotic uncle; you can see why people think he's crazy, but you grew up with him. I think Bond films might be similar, except they are like seeing the successful uncle with the hot aunt who flies your whole family down to Nassau for the holidays and gives you the latest cool gadget as a gift. That said, I didn't grow up watching Bond so I always wonder what everyone gets so excited about. I guess Daniel Craig is better than Pierce Brosnan, I just dont relate.

ROBERT I suppose I grew up with Bond. The movie cycle started when I was at the end of my teens, and it drew me to read some of the books. I saw most of the Sean Connery pictures; since then Ive checked in only infrequently. I remember thinking Dr. No was very cool when I first saw it; I tried watching it again on TV a few years ago, and found it unbearably slow. This new one throws a lot more action at you far quicker, but I have a feeling that TV -- I'm thinking 24 -- now does these things better; it can't afford such spectacular chases, or so many stunt doubles (there seemed to be more of those than actors in the credits) but it can take the time to tell you who is chasing whom and why, which is ultimately exciting. But I do like the Craig Bond; hes not only better than Brosnan -- hes a better actor (though not necessarily a better presence) than Connery was when he started.

BEN I'm with Robert on this one, Craig, so I imagine you're as befuddled as when you were talking with a supernerd about Superman Returns. Bonds different though, primarily because if an obsessed fan were to walk down the street dressed as Superman theyd look dumb, whereas a Brioni tux has the opposite effect. In other words, a fanboy obsession with 007 is completely legit.

As said enthusiast, watching these movies is like checking off a mental list of all the stuff I want to see: the blood-seeping opening montage, the first girl, the villain, the second girl, the drink order, the gadgets (missing but frankly unlamented), the jazzy theme, the double entendres as well as and a handful of groaners (If all that was left of you was your smile ..."). This movie covered the bases, and it gave us Jeffrey Wright as Felix Leiter, a much more inspired choice than John Cleese as Q.

As a non-nerd, Craig, did the take-this-job-and-shove-it angle do anything for you? I thought it gave him just enough pathos to humanize him, but not so much that he became a brooding mess (i.e., Timothy Dalton.) And Robert, speaking of the credits, I imagine you stayed to the end as I did to ensure that James Bond Will Return.

CRAIG You're right. Casino Royale manages to remove the super from the hero. Craig's Bond bleeds like a man, loves like a man and, rarely, even blunders like a man. He is not as much a caricature as the other Bonds seemed. But while director Martin Campbell and his entourage of editors, producers and studio execs deserve credit for reviving this franchise, they dont deserve a free pass. The chase in Africa made my girlfriends palms sweaty; by the time the third action sequence was over our asses were sweaty from sitting so damn long. And this is what separates Casino Royale from the great spy movies; story is always the star of, let's say, The Bourne Identity or Patriot Games.

There was plenty of potential for plot development with Wright's CIA agent, for example, but most of it ended up on the editing room computer. Instead, we were treated to yet another punch-up with undeveloped baddies and the too-long-by-half finale in a non- descript Venice building. But thats OK. When fanboys grow into full- fledged fanmen, they usually find out their favourite uncle has his own foibles.

ROBERT I think a lot of whats good about the movie is that it sticks pretty close to the original book -- the first and, by common consent, the best of the series. It shows us Bond becoming Bond. And this is wittily underlined at the beginning, with the first blooding scene being in black and white, and at the end when the familiar Bond theme only clicks in over the credits. We also see Bond's heart being broken for the first and last time. And they keep in the book's famous last line, which shows him closing up his feelings, though theyve softened it by adding a further action sequence.

I agree the Venetian chase went on too long, but at least I had some emotional investment in it. And its interesting too that one of the reasons Venice is sinking is because people keep having protracted shoot-outs there. As for the central gambling sequence; I'm usually terminally bored by poker games, on and off screen, but this one gripped me, because of Craig and his opponent, Mads Mikkelsen, even if he's younger and sleeker than I would have have imagined the Le Chiffre of the book. (The casting, by the way, is cosmopolitanism gone mad -- a French actress playing Bonds very English love interest?)

CRAIG strikes me as less likely to grow into Sean Connery than into Michael Caine in his Ipcress File days; a more plebeian style of deadpan than Connery. I also loved the sight of Judi Dench as M being woken from sleep with her oblivious husband (presumably) lying at her side. Theyre giving her a personality -- or maybe it's just that she's Dame Judi, at her most businesslike. As an attempt to re- invent the franchise, bring into line with current spy fiction while maintaining its old identity, Id say its two-thirds successful. I think it would be better if it got quicker to the main conflict; if it cut to the chase by cutting a chase.
