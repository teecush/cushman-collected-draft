If you suspect the title Cock of having anatomical significance, your suspicions are justified. It also has metaphorical significance. Mike Bartlett, the British playwright who has won awards for this and other plays, conceives of it as a sequence of battles, fought one-to-one, no quarter given: cockfights, in fact. His concept pretty much dictates the play's staging; the actors stalk one another around the playing area, which in Studio 180s production is a bright green square in the middle of the auditorium. Its exciting.

Meet John, who within this structure ranks as a doubly privileged figure; he's the only one who fights in every round, and the only one afforded the dignity of an actual name. Not that he seems to feel privileged - or dignified, for that matter. He's inclined to feel sorry for himself, wanting only to be left alone. He also, though, wants to be loved, thereby ensuring that those closest to him are both infuriated by him and protective of him. We meet two of these people. One is M, the man who has had John as his live-in partner for an unspecified but clearly substantial period. The other is W, the woman of whom John regularly catches sight on his way to work and with whom he has his first heterosexual experience. He enjoys this so much that it becomes a serious threat to his relationship with M, already under stress through Ms resentment of Johns general helplessness and John's resentment of that resentment. Exhibit A (this alphabetical stuff is catching): M complains that John can't cook while he himself is a whiz in the kitchen; you can bet, though, that both of them prefer it that way.

Flailing around in an attempt to mollify M, John rashly (and inaccurately) describes W as "manly," thereby inspiring M, who has never met her, to embark on fantastical descriptions of her as some kind of outsized Amazon. He gets quite specific about it; he might conceivably be accused of vagina-envy, but it seems likelier that hes just protecting his own turf - to use an image that he doesnt but very well might. However, as bold as the play's language and situations get, its staging is very discreet. Clothing is presumed to be shed, and sexual congress to be occurring, but the actors remain fully clad and hardly even touch. In fact when, near the end of the evening, two people actually kiss, it's a real shock effect. In the meantime Johns first nervous exploration of Ws body, conveyed in words alone, has been made both touching and hilarious.

I don't think the title word ever crops up in the actual dialogue, metaphorically or otherwise, and even the sporting image doesn't seem too exact; the encounters we witness early on are more like boxing matches, each round concluding with the ringing of a bell and the participants sneaking the occasional glass of water. As a device, this outstays its welcome; it gets us interested in the characters but not truly involved with them. We hunger for some relief from the duologues, and eventually we get it; W is invited to Ms place, so that John may choose between them (which of course is the last thing John wants to do). Sadistically, the author keeps us waiting for the longest time for W to cross the threshold. The wait is worth it; W may not be manly (she even wears a skirt) but shes a tough little bruiser whose confrontations with M up the play's stakes from mere squabbling to actual conflict. Then, having successfully broken his own mould, Bartlett proceeds with even greater effect to shatter it. We've noticed in the program that theres a fourth character, billed as F. This turns out to stand for father: Ms dad, a widower, whom he's invited over for moral support. F confesses to having been taken aback when M first came out; now, however, he's firmly in his sons corner, and wants only his happiness, which he translates as getting rid of W. And its between F and W that battle is finally and, in Joel Greenberg's production, most excitingly joined.

Challenged to decide who and what he is, John cries out that he doesn't want to be categorized: as straight or gay or, probably, as bisexual. Its a nice sentiment and a strong moment, but sexual identity, fixed or fluid, isnt really what's at issue. It's about what constitutes love, about whether domesticity can coexist with erotic excitement and in what proportions, and about how you choose. John of course wants everything, except responsibility; and hes charming enough almost to get away with it. As an acting role, hes something of a booby-trap: "I'm two different people" he says at one point but, as played by Andrew Kushnir, who excels at artless, tousle-haired likability, he's very much the same person whichever partner hes with: flustered, eager to please and apt to fall into a single apologetic rhythm. Kushnir is at his best when he too breaks his mould: when John is goaded into exploding. Much of the plays high tension depends on the audience's wanting John to decide (though I'd guess a poll would result in a sharp division on which way); much of its cleverness depends on its acknowledgment that any decision would seem, dramatically, like cheating. The uncertainty remains even after, in plot terms, John would seem to have made his choice; we leave him curled up on the floor, like a hedgehog in agony: the perfect position for him.

Jeff Miller, though somewhat self-conscious about his English accent, is fine and forceful as M, alternately upbraiding John and tempting him with cheesecake (his favourite). Jessica Greenberg is excellent as W, at first amused by John, then blisteringly aggressive; and shes matched by Ian D. Clarks F, solicitous turning to inquisitorial. John Thompson's set design is, obviously, neutral; it's the dialogue that sets the scenes, and its notable that, while the early locations have been fairly vague, in the last section were made very aware of the layout of the house. The play becomes a territorial struggle in the best Harold Pinter tradition, with a particular resemblance to The Collection, one of the best if least-known constituents of the Pinter canon. Cock has its limitations but it's tight within them. It's cocksure.
