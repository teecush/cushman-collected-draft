THEATRE REVIEW THE COST OF LIVING

Thursday, April 09, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY MORRIS PANYCH Starring Tim Battle At Young People's Theatre Rating: AIDS message is in the medium Morris Panych's, The Cost of Living is a brilliant and cleverly disguised lecture on AIDS using banks of video monitors to reach its teen audience BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THE Cost of Living is a brilliant play about growing up - brilliant in both writing and performance - disguised as a lecture on AIDS awareness. Or maybe its the other way round.

Produced by Vancouvers Green Thumb Theatre, Morris Panych's wittily- written script plays with its audience's dread of the moment when the message will be delivered. The piece is a monologue: an multi-media presentation by a teen-ager identified solely as Our Guy (played by Tim Battle) who is telling his classmates about himself and the assistant librarian he is in love with. She has promised him a birthday treat, which he assumes means she will go to bed with him. In fact she takes him to see an ex-lover of hers, now hospitalized with AIDS. Depressed and embarrassed he expects an awful warning. He gets one. Its something to do with the inadvisability of the shirt and tie he is wearing.

Great comic release though it is, this doesnt quite let Our Guy off the hook. A message is duly forthcoming, about placing a value on life, your own and everybody else's. If this is a let-down, it is because it seems terribly vague. What - in concrete terms and especially with reference to Angel (that's his girl-friends name) - is Our Guy going to do about it?

One asks, because up to that point the play has been exhilaratingly specific. For a start it conquered my prejudice against using video in the theatre. (Its like sex and violence; we can get all that at home.) Here it's an economic way of bringing in other characters, notably the hero's parents whose attitudes to his sexuality vary between embarrassing tolerance and embarrassing embarrassment.

Adolescence was always a minefield. Its now more booby-trapped than ever before. Battles performance, wonderfully fluent and engaging, a Catcher in the Rye for the 1990s, builds a bridge between the problems of now and the problems of always. For those of us who grew up in the 60s the return of the condom, with multiplied trauma, is one of life's more sourly ironic jokes. Not that the play is meant for us, but it's a tribute to the play that it can take us in along with its target audience: who, when I saw it, seemed enthralled.

This is the first AIDS play I have seen that has not degenerated into hospital-ward drama; and the first that has not presented it as an exclusively gay issue. It is also legitimately entertaining. Being on the side of life is easy. Bringing that life to life is an achievement. The Cost of Living will continue until April 12 at YPT; St. Catherines, April 21-24; Ottawa, April 27 to May 1 and London, Ont., from May 4 to 16.
