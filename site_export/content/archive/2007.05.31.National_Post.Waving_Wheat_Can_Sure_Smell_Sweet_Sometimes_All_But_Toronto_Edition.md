THEATRE REVIEW

OKLAHOMA!

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

I've never noticed it before, but I realized at the new Stratford production that we're three-quarters of the way through Oklahoma! before anybody mentions Oklahoma. And even at that, it only happens in the subplot. Ali Hakim, the itinerant peddler supposedly from Persia, having recently escaped a shotgun wedding to the flirtatious Ado Annie, bids her what he calls "a Persian goodbye." This seems to involve up-ending the recipient and implanting a smothering kiss on her lips that leaves her begging for more. At which point Will Parker, her actual affianced, gets mighty jealous and treats her to "an Oklahoma hello," which is the same sort of gesture, only with more gusto.

So, it starts as a joke. But in the next scene, which always plays like a finale ahead of its time, the entire company delivers another kind of Oklahoma hello by singing the title song, one of the great goosebump moments in the American musical. But this time, as the wind comes sweeping down the plain and Richard Rodgers' music sweeps right along with it, they're saying hello to Oklahoma itself, the "brand new state" that has yet to come into existence. Curly the cowboy and Laurey the rancher, the newlywed hero and heroine, are its symbols, their union prefigured in The Farmer and the Cowman, the challenge square dance that opens the show's second act and looks forward to "when this territory is a state / And joins the Union just like all the others." Up until then, we haven't heard a word about it.

Which just shows how cunning the apparently artless Oscar Hammerstein was.

Oklahoma!'s opening is a legendary paradigm of simplicity: A woman sits outside a farmhouse silently churning butter while a male voice approaches from offstage singing about a beautiful morning. It's one of those songs that's so much a part of our lives it's hard to believe anyone actually sat down and wrote it. The tableau develops into a love scene that's mostly tall tales and quarrelling. Then we get news of the outside world, i.e. loose-living, high- rising (seven storey) Kansas City. Both the serious and the comic love plots involve the tensions between the two ways of life that have to become one. The burning question that notoriously underpins the long first act -- will it be Curly or Jud Fry, the brooding and reclusive hired hand, who gets to take Laurey to the box social? -- is less trivial than it looks. It's literary shorthand for which of them will marry her. That has traditionally been enough to fuel a romantic comedy, and is here a further shorthand of its own. By the time the explosive square dance begins, an epic has snuck up on us.

Some of the surprise is necessarily lost on the Festival Theatre's stage, where everything looks epic to begin with. But the show can handle it -- after all, it was one of the first musicals to become a widescreen movie -- and Patrick Clark's sets combine the broodingly spacious with the comfortably particular. Donna Feore's direction and choreography are immensely spirited, although the acrobatic production numbers seem to exist for their own enjoyable sake rather than to further or deepen the story.

There isn't the subtlety or the pathos of the Trevor Nunn-Susan Stroman production that recently played in London and New York, though this staging does follow that one in dispensing with the traditional doubles for Laurey's dream ballet and letting the principals dance out their own conflicts. The result is a huge gain in power and coherence both for the sequence itself and for the show as a whole.

Blythe Wilson is still primarily a singing rather than a dancing Laurey, which is fine since she has the two loveliest songs: the not- quite-defiant Many a New Day and Out of My Dreams which, wistful and sweeping at the same time, may be the best of all Rodgers' waltzes, which is saying something.

She is also an acting Laurey, strong but troubled. Dan Chameroy's Curly is more problematic; as fine an actor as he's become, he looks and sounds strangely sneaky here. (And what one would give to hear Oh What a Beautiful Mornin' as the first audience did, unamplified and coming from somewhere rather than everywhere.) In fact, he seems rather less wholesome than David W. Keeley's Jud, a surprisingly open-faced outsider whose frustrations are not only powerful but sympathetic, both in his Lonely Room solo and in the mocking Pore Jud, a brilliantly sarcastic sequence that also finds Chameroy at his best.

But then Hammerstein's lyrics in this show are the funniest he ever wrote. His dialogue can sparkle, too, especially when shrugged through by Jonathan Ellul, whose Persian is unmistakably and unashamedly Jewish. Kyle Blair is a winning Will with, as the song says, plenty of room to spin a rope, and plenty of skill to take advantage of it. Lindsay Thomas, though, spoils Ado Annie by over a- doing her. The character, of course, has no subtlety: all the more reason for the actress to have some. Nora McLellan as Aunt Eller wraps the show up as surely as one of the ribboned boxes at the social.

Berthold Carriere's conducting makes the score a joy, which admittedly is no trick. Or, as the Broadway historian Ethan Mordden unanswerably asked, when debunking the myth of the show's failure at its Boston tryout, "How could an audience not like Oklahoma!?" - Oklahoma! is in repertory through Nov. 4.
